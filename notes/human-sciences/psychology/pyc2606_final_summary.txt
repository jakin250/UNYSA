# INTRODUCTION
## Contextualising the module
- Psychological assessment = development/administering of tests to quantify behavior.
- Psychological questionnaires measure behavior and predict actions.
- Widely adopted in multiple domains: occupational, educational, health, economic.
- Importance: Captures human behavior through scientific principles, enhancing validity and reliability.

## Controversial Use in Non-Western Contexts
- Psychological assessments often criticized in non-Western settings.
- Western-derived practices hegemonically imposed; neglect of cultural contexts.
- Culture and environment are key behavior determinants; traditions influence life orientation.
- In South Africa, questionnaires linked to historical racial agendas (Claasen, 1997; Laher & Cockcroft, 2014).
- Psychological assessments normed for white children; used unfairly with black children.
- Post-1994, push for culturally sensitive, fair, unbiased tests considering language, race, education.

## Important Considerations for Questionnaire Design
- Consider population characteristics for scientific soundness and fairness.
- Address discrimination history in South Africa's context.
- Avoid one-size-fits-all approaches; ensure questionnaires are reliable, valid, and context-sensitive.

## Decolonisation and Questionnaire Design in Psychology
- Decolonisation critiques Eurocentric ideologies, exposes power dynamics (Dudgeon & Walker, 2015).
- Promotes indigenous knowledge and equitable knowledge systems (Furo, 2018).
- Historical misuse of questionnaires for oppression in South Africa and globally.
- Module encourages awareness of psychology's history and decolonial perspectives in questionnaire design.

## Bias and Equivalence
- Minimise bias and achieve equivalence in questionnaire design.
- Bias = differences in performance not reflecting the construct measured (Van de Vijver & Tanzer, 2004).

### Types of Bias
1. **Construct Bias**
   - Partial overlap in meaning across cultures (Van de Vijver & Poortinga, 1997).
2. **Method Bias**
   - Inherent to the measurement process, including sample, instrument, and administration issues.
3. **Item Bias**
   - Different psychological meaning attached to items across cultures (Van de Vijver & Leung, 1997).

### Strategies to Reduce Bias
- **Construct Bias**
   - Decentering; convergence approach.
- **Method Bias**
   - Train administrators; use detailed protocols; assess response styles.
- **Item Bias**
   - Detection through judgmental and psychometric methods; item analysis.

# Study Notes on Questionnaire Design

## 2.1.2 What are you measuring?
- **Questionnaire Construct**: Focus on the constructs to measure.
- **Theory of Planned Behaviour**: Factors include:
  - **Attitude** towards behaviour (using alcohol).
  - **Subjective Norm** concerning behaviour.
  - **Perceived Control** regarding behaviour.
- **Validity Types**:
  - **Discriminant Validity**: Ensure items are specific and not interpretable as related constructs.
  - **Content Validity**: Measures the full range of the content domain and addresses criticisms regarding omissions.

## 2.1.3 Presentation and Sequence of Items
- **Item-Order Effect**: Order can influence responses. Example:
  - Life satisfaction item preceding dating frequency item showed a weak correlation (−.12).
  - Reversing the order showed a strong correlation (+.66).
- **Response Options Impact**: Framing of options can affect frequency reporting:
  - Options framed by major irritations yield infrequent reports.
  - Options framed by minor irritations yield more frequent reports.

## 2.1.4 Be Consistent in Response Options
- **Response Options**: Use consistent scales throughout.
- **Likert Scale**: A typical format presenting several statements for agreement-disagreement ratings (5-point scale).
  - Avoid mislabeling other rating scales as Likert scales unless used as originally intended.

## 2.1.5 Types of Items
- **Open-Ended Items**: Respondents answer freely.
  - Examples: 
    - Important lessons for children.
    - Experiences of discrimination.
- **Closed-Ended Items**: Respondents select from provided options.
  - Example includes age ranges (under 18, 18-34, etc.).

## 2.1.6 Combining Question Types
- **Appropriate Formats**: Use a mix of item types to enhance data validity.
- **Increased Coverage**: Example combining open-ended and closed-ended questions in library surveys to check information consistency.

## 2.1.7 Formatting the Questionnaire
- **Introduction**:
  - Explain survey purpose, sponsor, and importance of participation.
  - Establish informed consent, detailing topics, time commitment, and confidentiality.
- **Item Arrangement**:
  - Start with critical items when respondents are most engaged.
  - Group items by topic and item type for clarity.
  - Sensitive or personal questions are better placed at the end.
- **Closing Acknowledgment**: Express appreciation to respondents.

## 2.2 Glossary
- **Design of a Questionnaire**: Structured technique for collecting data, motivating respondents for detailed information.
- **Respondents**: Individuals who reply to surveys, providing essential data.
- **Questionnaire Items**: Questions or text used within the questionnaire, varying in reliance on interpretation and providing answer alternatives.
- **Item Scaling**: Uses scales for performance rating, indicating preferences, and expressing attitudes.
- **Layout of a Questionnaire**: Organizes cover page, completion instructions, question sequence, and overall layout.
- **Questionnaire Specification Document**: Lists characteristics and requirements for the questionnaire’s content, format, and purpose.

### 1. Cultural Considerations in Research

#### 1.1 Western vs. Non-Western Perspectives
- Avoid imposing Western cultural standards on non-Western communities.
- Cultures are not static; change should not be forced from external influences.

#### 1.2 Language
- Use simple, clear language in questionnaires; avoid jargon.
- Ensure clarity in questions to collect reliable data:
  - **Leading questions:** Direct respondent towards a certain answer (e.g., "Isn’t it safer being at school than at home?").
  - **Double-barrelled questions:** Require multiple answers (e.g., "Does the university consider race and gender in its admission policies?").
  - **Presumptions:** Assume responses that may not be accurate (e.g., "What type of cereal do you eat in the morning?").
  - **Ambiguous questions:** Allow for multiple interpretations (e.g., "Are you satisfied with the curriculum?").

- Recognize the linguistic diversity in South Africa (11 official languages).
- Importance of translating questionnaires to respect indigenous contexts and definitions of terms.

### Types of Reliability
1. **Split-Half Reliability**
   - Measures internal consistency by comparing two halves of a questionnaire.

   - Assesses consistency over time by administering the same test to the same subjects at different times.

---

### Categories of Validity
1. **Content Validity**
   - Ensures the questionnaire covers the entire scope of the concept being measured.

2. **Criterion-Related Validity**
   - Assesses how well one variable predicts an outcome based on another variable.

3. **Construct Validity**
   - Evaluates whether a test truly measures the theoretical construct it claims to measure.

---

### Key Concepts
- Reliability: The degree to which a questionnaire yields consistent results.
- Validity: The extent to which a questionnaire measures what it intends to measure.

# PYC2606/1

## 5.1 INTRODUCTION
- Questionnaire designed for specific purpose to obtain information on a concept or construct.
- Reliability Questions:
  - Will results be consistent over different occasions?
  - Will similar items yield the same results?
  - Do items consistently measure the same concept?
- Validity Questions:
  - Do questions represent relevant tasks, behaviors, attitudes?
  - Do results relate to current/future behaviors?
  - Does it measure the intended concept?
- Reliability indicates consistency; validity signifies accurate measurement of intention.

## 5.2 WHICH TYPE OF RELIABILITY IS APPROPRIATE?

### 5.2.1 Test-retest reliability
- Administer same questionnaire to same group on two occasions; correlate scores.

### 5.2.2 Alternate-forms reliability
- Administer two forms of the questionnaire to the same group; assess correlation.
- Criterion for reliability; similar items should have consistent difficulty.

### 5.2.3 Split-half reliability
- Measure internal consistency by dividing questionnaire into two parts; correlate scores.

### 5.2.4 Interpret information on the reliability
- Reliability coefficient (correlation coefficient) interpretation:
  - Close to 0: low reliability.
  - Close to 1: high reliability.
- Levels of reliability determined by questionnaire purpose.

**Summary of Reliability Types:**
- Test-retest: One form, two sessions, Coefficient of stability.
- Alternate-forms: Two forms, one/two sessions, Coefficient of equivalence/stability.
- Split-half: One form, one session, Coefficient of internal consistency.

## 5.3 WHICH CATEGORY OF VALIDITY IS APPROPRIATE?

### 5.3.1 Content validity
- Adequacy of questions representing the intended content domain.
- Expert validation ensures coverage of tasks and behaviors.

### 5.3.2 Criterion-related validity
- Measures comparison with actual outcomes (current & predictive).
- Correlation calculated between questionnaire scores and criterion performance.

### 5.3.3 Construct validity
- Assesses if the questionnaire accurately captures the theoretical construct.
- Involves comparisons between groups; correlates with other constructs.

### 5.3.4 Interpret information on the validity
- Content validity cannot be quantitatively indexed; evaluated qualitatively.
- Concurrent and predictive validity expressed as correlation coefficients.
- Expectations of strong relationships (close to ±1) for validity assessment.

**Summary of Validity Categories:**
- Content validity: Questionnaire should represent specific experiences/behaviors.
- Criterion-related validity: Should correlate with current/future outcomes (e.g., health).
- Construct validity: Correlates with existing constructs; differentiation between groups.

## 5.4 GLOSSARY
- **Alternate-forms reliability**: Correlation between two equivalent forms.
- **Concurrent validity**: Accuracy of questionnaire identifying current behavior.
- **Consistency**: Constancy in measurement outcomes.
- **Construct validity**: Extent to which the questionnaire measures intended constructs.
- **Content validity**: Questionnaire items covering the content domain adequately.
- **Correlation**: Relation between two variables.
- **Equivalence**: Having equal values/functions.
- **Predictive validity**: Accuracy predicting future behavior.
- **Split-half reliability**: Correlation between equivalent halves of questionnaire.
- **Stability**: Remains unchanged over time.
- **Test-retest reliability**: Correlation between two different time applications of the same questionnaire.

### Correlation Coefficient

1. **Using Correlation Coefficient in Item Analysis**
   - **Definition**: A statistic indicating the relationship between two psychological constructs.
   - **Correlations**:
     - Positive correlation: High scores on one construct associate with high scores on another.
     - Negative correlation: High scores on one construct associate with low scores on another.
     - No relation: Scores do not correspond.
   - **Value Range**:

     - Horizontal and vertical axes represent scores of constructs.
     - Dots represent individual scores of each individual.
   - **Patterns**:
     - Perfect positive correlation (r = +1): Dots form an upward sloping line.
     - Perfect negative correlation (r = -1): Dots form a downward sloping line.
     - No correlation (r = 0): Dots are scattered randomly.

## 3. Reliability  

### 1. Measurement Error and Reliability  
- Definition: Consistency in measuring intended constructs.  
- Sources of unsystematic variation (e.g., mood, context) relevant to results.  
- Reliability coefficient values range from 0 (unreliable) to 1 (reliable).

### 2. Different Types of Reliability  
#### 2.1 Test-Retest Reliability  
- Administer the same questionnaire to the same group on two occasions.  
- Assess correlation coefficient for consistency over time; ideal coefficient close to 1.  

#### 2.2 Alternate-Forms Reliability  
- Develop two forms to measure the same attribute.  
- Administer both forms to the same group and assess correlation for equivalence.  

#### 2.3 Split-Half Reliability  
- Administer once; divide questionnaire in half for scoring and correlation.  
- Common method: compare scores from odd/even items.  
- Reflects internal consistency; correction needed for reliability estimate.

### 3. Evaluating Reliability  
- Specify questionnaire and group when referencing reliability.  
- Acceptable reliability levels vary by purpose (e.g., >0.90 for critical tests, >0.70 acceptable for aggregative contexts).

- Definition: Validity measures the questionnaire's ability to assess the intended construct.

     - Evaluated through:
       - Differentiation between groups hypothesized to differ on the construct.
       - Correlation with other constructs (convergent and divergent validity).

## Writing Questionnaire Items  

### 1. General Principles for Item Construction  
- Items should reflect meaningful definitions of what is to be measured.  
- Requires knowledge (science) and creativity (art) in item design.

### 2. Guidelines for Using and Modifying Existing Items  
- Use established questionnaires where reliability and validity are verified for comparability.

### 3. Guidelines for Constructing New Items

#### 3.1 Content and Construct Relevance  
- Ensure items directly relate to the construct.  
- Do not include interesting but non-essential questions.

#### 3.2 Language Level  
- Match language to the target population's understanding.  
- Avoid academic jargon and complex syntax.  
- Pre-test items on a small group for clarity.  

#### 3.3 Contextual Relevance  
- Consider different cultural interpretations of items.  
- Avoid idiomatic phrases that may confuse different demographic groups.  
- Ensure translations are accurate.

- Separate multi-issue questions for clarity.  
- Prefer active voice and specific questions for reliability.

# Study Notes for PYC2606/1 - Research Methodology

## 3.6 Prerequisite Knowledge
- Construct items for factual data carefully; remember limitations of memory.
- **Dos:**
  - Limit time frame inquiries to the recent past (last week to six months).
  - Ensure respondents have necessary information before asking for opinions on policies.
- **Don'ts:**
  - Avoid inquiries extending beyond immediate memory capability to prevent unreliable responses.

## 3.7 Leading Questions
- **Definition:** Questions that suggest a particular response.
- **Dos:**
  - Avoid wording that guides respondents to desired answers.
- **Examples:** 
  - Inappropriate: "Discrimination in South Africa is horrific, isn’t it?"
  - Instead: Use neutral wording without context-provoking examples.

## 4. Problems Relating to Response Bias or Response Style
### 4.1 Social Desirability Response Bias
- **Definition:** Tendency to choose socially acceptable or favorable responses.
- Affects responses at various consciousness levels, either consciously or unconsciously.
- **Faking Factors:**
  - Occurs when respondents are aware of implications of their responses or contexts (job applications, personal assessments).
- **Reduction Strategies:**
  - Ensure anonymity of respondents to mitigate pressure for socially favorable responses.

### 4.2 Response Styles
- **Definition:** Patterns of response regardless of item context (e.g., favoring extremes or central responses).
- To mitigate:
  - Design balanced questionnaires with both positively and negatively stated items.
- **Challenges:**
  - Negatively stated items can confuse respondents; focus on recognizing positively stated items only.
- **Example:** 
  - Trust measurement: “I trust people” (positively stated and positively scored).
