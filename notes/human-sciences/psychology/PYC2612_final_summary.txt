## ORIENTATION
- Focus: Developmental psychology from conception to adulthood.
- Aim: To enhance understanding of normal, healthy development stages.
- Application: Use knowledge in everyday situations and professional contexts.

## Unit 1: Basic Concepts of Child and Adolescent Development
- Definitions of key terms: development, milestones, and stages.
- Theories of development: 
  - **Cognitive Development**: Piaget's Stages
  - **Psychosocial Development**: Erikson’s psychosocial stages.
- Influences on development: genetics, environment, culture.

## Unit 2: Prenatal Development and the Birth Process
- Stages of prenatal development: germinal, embryonic, fetal.
- Factors affecting prenatal development: nutrition, teratogens, maternal health.
- Birth process: stages, types of delivery (natural, cesarean).

## Unit 3: The Neonatal Phase and Infancy
- Characteristics of neonates and infants: physical, cognitive, and emotional development.
- Attachment theories: Bowlby’s attachment theory, secure vs. insecure attachment.
- Milestones: sensory development, motor skills, language acquisition.

## Unit 4: Early Childhood
- Developmental milestones: language, social skills, cognitive development.
- The importance of play: types of play, role in development.
- Early education and its impact on social and emotional growth.

## Unit 5: Middle Childhood
- Key characteristics: cognitive development, peer relationships, self-concept.
- Theories of intelligence: Gardner's Multiple Intelligences.
- Social influences: family dynamics, school environment.

## Unit 6: Adolescence
- Physical development: puberty and its effects on adolescents.
- Cognitive development: abstract thinking, moral development (Kohlberg).
- Social identity: peer influence, social media impacts, identity formation.

## Unit 7: Adversities, Resilience and Rights
- Factors contributing to adversity in child and adolescent development (abuse, neglect).
- Resilience: definition, importance, and factors promoting resilience.

### Key Concepts
- **Goals of Child Psychology**
  - Understanding behavior and development.
  - Promoting positive growth and resilience.

- **Developmental Stages**
  - Infancy, early childhood, middle childhood, adolescence.
  - Each stage has distinct characteristics and transitions.

- **Domains of Development**
  - Physical, cognitive, emotional, and social development.
  - Interrelatedness of domains impacts overall development.

- **Theories of Development**
  - **Cognitive Development Theory** (Piaget): Stages of cognitive growth affecting learning.
  - **Social Learning Theory** (Bandura): Emphasis on observation and modeling.
  - **Ecological Systems Theory** (Bronfenbrenner): Interaction between individual and environmental factors.

- **Quantitative methods**: Surveys, experiments.
- **Qualitative methods**: Interviews, observations.
- Importance of **research ethics** when conducting studies on children.

## 7.1 ADVERSITY

- **Adversity**: Trauma, hardship, suffering; toxic stress from prolonged exposure harms child development.
  
### 7.1.1 Family influences

- **Stability Importance**: Stable family unit crucial for child development.

#### 7.1.1.1 Divorce
- **Impact of Divorce**: Effects vary based on individual circumstances and multiple influencing factors.

#### 7.1.1.2 Stepfamilies
- **Remarriage Effects**: Research reviews effects on children from divorced parents becoming stepparents.

#### 7.1.1.3 Single-parent families
- **Single-parent Dynamics**: Discusses the prevalence and effects on children in South Africa.

#### 7.1.1.4 Adopted children
- **Adoption Insights**: One in five children in SA are orphans; covers adoptive identity and transracial adoptions.

#### 7.1.1.5 Child-headed households
- **Research Findings**: Examines challenges faced by children in these households.

#### 7.1.1.6 Same-sex parents
- **Relationship Impact**: Emphasizes children’s well-being tied to parent relationships over gender/sexual orientation.

#### 7.1.1.7 Street children
- **Research Overview**: Discusses the prevalence and challenges encountered by street children.
