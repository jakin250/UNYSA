## Social upheaval and multilayers of human experience

- Trauma from violence includes fear, pain, grief, and anxiety.
- Breakdown in systems leads to domestic and civil conflicts.
- Relationships between political and criminal violence noted (Swartz, 1998).

### Case Study: Ms Mbanga’s Story (Swartz, 1998)

- 35-year-old mother supporting four children in an informal settlement.
- Dropped out of school due to political upheaval; lost a friend in the uprisings.
- Experienced familial rejection after having a first child at 16.
- Entered into business selling vegetables, faced gun violence from police, resulting in permanent disability.
- Referral to human rights group for damages claim against the Minister of Law and Order.
- Psychological assessment revealed poverty, pain, disability fears, and some mild depression.
- Family relations improved post-shooting, later experienced further family trauma with daughter.

## Social and physical dislocation

- Distress may not be perceived instinctively by individuals facing severe circumstances.
- Violence, prolonged conflict leads to economic problems affecting mental health.
- Displacement disrupts food production and community ties, increasing trauma and poverty.
- Inadequate utility from psychological interventions observed in Ms Mbanga’s context over time.

## Tough questions for understanding hard-hitting problems

- Importance of questioning as a pathway to future solutions (Gaarder).
- Asking questions rather than providing answers to engage complex social issues.

### Asking questions that lead to transformation

- Kahane (2004) emphasizes rapid answers in modern society can be problematic:
  1. **Dynamic complexity**: Spatial and temporal distance between causes and effects.
  2. **Social complexity**: Conflicting perspectives on issues; demands participatory approaches.
  3. **Generative complexity**: Obsolete solutions necessitate creativity in problem-solving.

- Responses imposed from outside may face resistance; community ownership is crucial for effective solutions.

- Evaluate potential counseling contexts within social frameworks.
- Cultivate sensitivity towards culture, diversity, and socio-political contexts.

## A Brief Theoretical Discussion of Crisis Intervention

### Definition of Psychological Crisis
- Perceived intolerable difficulty surpassing normal coping resources.
- Subjective interpretation: one’s crisis may differ from another’s.
- Commonly time-limited and acute occurrences.

### Types of Crises
- **Developmental Crises:**
  - Transitions in life stages (e.g., adolescence).
  
- **Situational Crises:**
  - Human-made (violence, accidents) or natural (disasters).

### Aims of Crisis Intervention
- Ensure physical and emotional safety.
- Allow emotional ventilation.
- Validate emotional responses.
- Prepare for consequences and aftermath.

### Role of Helpers/Counsellors
- Traditional face-to-face counselling is less emphasized.
- **Psychological first-aid** is crucial.
- Provide empathic support, calm reassurance, objective concern, and practical assistance.

### Disaster Support Principles (NSW Institute of Psychiatry, 2000)
- Comfort and console distressed individuals.
- Protect from threat or distress.
- Provide immediate care for physical needs.
- Support reality-based tasks and goal orientation.
- Facilitate reunions and share experiences.
- Connect to ongoing support systems.
- Aid in fostering a sense of mastery.

## Page 121: Crisis Counselling Process

  - Quickly explore current issues; define crises to find solutions (e.g., in interviews).

  - If the client is **psychotic** (hallucinations, delusions, life danger): Community assistance needed (family, friends); may require transport to facilities (medical or psychiatric).
  - If the client is **rational**: Joint definition of problems and coping strategies; mobilize community resources; counsellor to maintain calm and empathic presence.
  - Aim to foster self-mastery and stabilization, avoiding portrayals of helplessness.

## Chapter 8: Termination of Counselling Relationships

### 8.1 Overview of Counseling Relationships
- Relationships can end by mutual decision or unilaterally.
- Counsellor emotions and client self-determination are critical in termination.
- **Key Case**: Abandonment is defined as a counsellor's act/omission that leaves a client without appropriate recourse (Allan, 2002).

### 8.2 Timing and Process of Termination
- Continuous monitoring of client's progress is essential.
- Evaluate if client problems have diminished (e.g., stress, coping abilities, understanding).
- Referral to another counsellor may be necessary if current counselling is ineffective or if value conflicts arise.
- **Concerns for Timing**:
  - Reduction of initial symptoms.
  - Dissipation of stress motivating counselling.
  - Improvement in coping and self-understanding.
  - Enhanced relational abilities.
  - Increased productivity, enjoyment of life.

### 8.3 Initiating the Termination Phase
- **Process Suggestions**:
  1. Summarise key points from counselling sessions.
  2. Review action plans post-treatment (referrals if needed).
  3. Highlight and commend client achievements.
  4. Validate mixed emotions (excitement vs. sadness).
  5. Conclude positively with affirmations and encouragement.

- **Guidelines from Patterson and Eisenberg (1983)**:
  - Awareness of client and counsellor needs.
  - Reflection on personal feelings regarding termination.
  - Encourage client expression of feelings about ending.
  - Honest sharing of the counsellor's experience and learnings.
  - Acknowledgement of client progress.
  - Address challenges encountered during sessions.

### 8.4 Report-Writing Guidelines
- **Characteristics**: Clear, concise, and comprehensive (Dunbar-Krige & Fritz, 2006).
- Balance between conciseness and clarity while including all relevant information.

### 8.5 Sections of a Counseling Report
- Reason for referral.
- Client demographic data: name, birth date, gender, etc.
- Socio-economic details: occupation, income, etc.
- Sources of information: individuals or documents contributing data.
- Presenting problem from client’s viewpoint.
- History of presenting problems leading to counselling.
- Current family and significant relationships outline.
- Testing data: instruments and results.
- Assessment observations: client presentation and demeanor.
- Summary and conclusions: integrated insights.
- Recommendations for future action.

### 8.6 Linking with Client’s External Support Structures
- Importance of considering client within a social context: family, friends, community.
- Encourages utilizing existing support systems to foster resilience.

### Conclusion
- Termination is a necessary outcome of counselling and requires careful management to avoid feelings of abandonment.  
- Responsible report writing and awareness of client contexts contribute to effective practice.
