# 6 THE SCIENTIFIC METHOD

- **Cognitive Psychology & Scientific Approach**
  - Scientific method: directions leading to answers and questions.
  - Historical misuse to justify racialized oppression (Dubow, 1995).
  - Contentious definition of science, especially in African context.

- **Euro-American Influence**
  - Psychological theories historically marginalize African ontologies (Curran, 2011; Ramose, 1999).
  - Indigenous psychology rooted in European Enlightenment; neglects holistic African worldviews.
  - Contemporary psychology must consider broader politico-ideological backgrounds.
  
- **Limits of Current Psychological Methodology**
  - Current methods inadequately capture African knowledge sources (folklore, idioms, etc.).
  - Traditional views such as Darwinian evolution may be limited (Nsamenang, 2007).
  - Holism intrinsic to African universe theory; encompasses matter, life, and mind.
  
- **Complex Adaptive Systems**
  - Emerging field, based on holistic principles, mimics evolutionary processes.
  - Limitations exist in the understanding of multifaceted realities.

- **Scientific Enquiry**
  - Begins with questions from systematic observations of phenomena.
  - Key standards for adequate data:
    - Accurately shows variable relationships.
    - Applicable beyond the individual studied.
    - Produces consistent and accurate information.
  
- **Provisional Nature of Theories**
  - Theories are not permanent; they evolve as scientific understanding matures.
  - Science as a process, encompassing systematic observation and experimentation.

- **Common Activities in Cognitive Psychology Research**
  - Observation of phenomena.
  - Development of theories.
  - Testing theories through hypotheses.
  - Evidence collection for hypotheses.

- **Karl Popper's Falsifiability Principle**
  - Scientific theories must be falsifiable; untestable claims are non-scientific.
  
- **Stephen Hawking's Perspective on Theories**
  - Theories are hypotheses; can only be disproven, never definitively proven.

- **Testing Theories**
  - Implications derived from theories dictate 'what-if' scenarios.
  - Example of neural-imaging techniques (fMRI) in cognitive research.

- **Continuous Process of Science**
  - Constant refinement of theories to generate new hypotheses.

# Searle’s Chinese Room Argument

## Page 18

### Overview
- **Strong AI**: The belief that a program can achieve intelligence akin to human understanding if it passes the Turing test.
- **Weak AI**: Searle's position that computers cannot truly understand language; they merely follow instructions without comprehension.

### Thought Experiment
- **Chinese Room**:
  - A man inside a room, without knowledge of Chinese, processes Chinese symbols received from outsiders using a manual of instructions.
  - The man generates responses that seem intelligent but lacks understanding.

### Conclusions
- The man in the room does not comprehend Chinese, similar to how computers process symbols without true understanding.
- **Syntactic Operations**: Computers perform on symbols, lacking semantic understanding (meaning).

### Rejections of Searle’s Argument
- Various responses from AI researchers, notably:
  - **The Systems Reply**: Asserts that the entire system (man, room, instructions) demonstrates understanding.
    - **Searle’s Rejoinder**: Memorizing rules does not grant understanding; individual components cannot confer meaning.
  
  - **The Robot Reply**: Suggests that embedding the program in a robot creates causal links to the environment, potentially providing true understanding.
    - **Searle’s Response**: Even a robotic embodiment doesn't bestow understanding; the operator merely follows syntactic rules without knowledge of the symbols' meanings.

  - Several brain areas specialized for different visual functions.

- **Higher Cognition**
  - Perception serves as a foundation for studying higher cognitive functions such as reasoning and problem-solving.

### 1. Memory Models

- **Atkinson and Shiffrin’s Model**
  - Sensory store: smallest capacity
  - Iconic store: visual sensory memory
  - Haptic store: tactile sensory memory
  - Distinction: Structures termed **stores**; information held is **memory**.

---

### 2. Types of Memory

- **Episodic Memory**: Personal experiences tied to specific time and place.
- **Semantic Memory**: General knowledge, meaning of words.
- **Working Memory**: Active processing of information.
- **Short-term Memory**: Holds information temporarily.

- **Amnesia Types**
  - **Retrograde Amnesia**: Loss of memory for events before trauma.
  - **Anterograde Amnesia**: Loss of memory for events after trauma.
  - Explicit vs. Implicit Memory
    - **Explicit Memory**: Declarative knowledge; conscious recall.
    - **Implicit Memory**: Procedural knowledge; unconscious recall.

---

### 3. Memory Processes

- **Encoding**: Transformation of sensory input into memory representation.
- **Storage**: Retaining information over time.
- **Retrieval**: Accessing stored information.

- **Transfer from Short-term to Long-term**
  - Attend to and understand information.
  - Make connections to existing knowledge.
  - Rehearse to enhance retention.

---

### 4. Memory Disorders

- **Impacts of Amnesia**
  - Explicit memory typically impaired; implicit memory often intact.
  - Performance on tasks requiring procedural knowledge is affected.

---

### 5. Memory Improvement Strategies

- **Deliberate Attention**: Focus on new information.
- **Use of Associations**: Link new information with prior knowledge.
- **Rehearsal**: Reinforces memory retention.
- **Reflective Practice**: Monitor understanding and information processing.
- **Distributed Practice**: Space out learning sessions.
- **Context Variation**: Study in different locations/times to enhance recall.
- **Sleep**: Positive effects on memory consolidation (REM stage).
- **Elaborative Rehearsal**: Integrate new knowledge meaningfully.
- **Categorical Clustering**: Group items into classes to aid memory.

# Study Notes

## Chapter 6: Reasoning and Decision-Making

### Learning Unit 6.1: Arguments

#### 6.1.1 Types of Arguments
- **Deductive Arguments**: 
  - Conclusions follow necessarily from premises. 
  - Examples: 
    - "All human beings are rational. You are a human being. Therefore, you are rational." 
      - **Type**: Deductive, certain.
    - "All pigs can fly. Porky is a pig. Therefore, Porky can fly." 
      - **Type**: Deductive, certain.

- **Inductive Arguments**:
  - Conclusions based on probabilities rather than certainties.
  - Evaluated for plausibility.
  - Examples: 
    - "Most Algerian AIDS cases have been reported among young men in their twenties. Ahmed has been diagnosed with AIDS. So, Ahmed is a young man in his twenties." 
      - **Type**: Inductive, fairly plausible.
    - "Of all the rats we have tested, only one has learnt to run this maze. So, the next rat we test will not be able to run the maze." 
      - **Type**: Inductive, quite plausible.

#### 6.1.2 Analyzing Arguments
- Validity: Premises support the conclusion.
- Circular reasoning concerns: 
  - Example: "It is important to talk about household problems because it is important to talk about them."
    - Justification: Reasoning is circular; conclusion repeats premises.

### Learning Unit 6.2: Common Logical Fallacies
- **Gambler’s Fallacy**: Misconception that past independent events affect future outcomes.
- **Framing Effect**: Influence of presentation on decision-making.
- **Confirmation Bias**: Tendency to search for information that confirms existing beliefs.

### Learning Unit 6.3: Decision-Making Theories
- **Subjective Expected Utility Theory**:
  - Decision-making based on individual perceptions.
  - Focuses on maximizing perceived personal benefits.

- **Heuristics**: Mental shortcuts for decision-making.
  - **Availability Heuristic**: Accessibility of information influences perception of probability.

### Glossary of Terms
- **Deductive Reasoning**: Logic deducing conclusions from general principles.
- **Inductive Reasoning**: Forming generalizations based on specific instances.
- **Categorical Syllogism**: Logical argument using two premises leading to a conclusion.
- **Framing Effect**: How context influences perception of choices.
- **Confirmation Bias**: Favoring information that supports existing theories.
