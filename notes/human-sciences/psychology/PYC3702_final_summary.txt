### Overview
- Importance of Community Psychology and Prevention of Psychopathology.
- Focus on primary, secondary, and tertiary prevention of abnormal behaviour.
- Historical context: Community psychology emerged from criticisms of clinical psychology post-1965 Boston Conference.
- Early community psychologists highlighted social issues: substance abuse, crime, etc.
- Emphasis on enhancing human well-being through systemic intervention rather than individual treatment.

---

### 2.1 Study unit: Community Psychology (Page 13)

- **Definition**: Community psychology seeks to improve community quality of life through systemic intervention.
- **Role of Community Psychologists**: 
  - Facilitate community interventions vs. individualistic clinical approaches.
  - Focus on public interventions with diverse participant involvement.
- **Contrast with Clinical Psychology**: 
  - Community psychologists: preventative focus.
  - Clinical psychologists: curative focus.

---

### 2.2 Study unit: Prevention of Psychopathology (Page 13)

- **Functions of Community Psychology**: Prevention methodologies to maintain mental health rather than merely treating illness.
- **Types of Prevention**:
  - **Primary Prevention**: 
    - Targets lowering incidence of disorders by enhancing resources and removing harmful community factors.
    - **Example**: Project Head Start (1964).
    - Community-wide initiatives: E.g., Munoz et al. study on televised programs reducing depression symptoms.
  - **Secondary Prevention**: 
    - Focuses on early detection and treatment to reduce severity and duration of disorders.
    - **Role of Educators**: Identify and support at-risk children.
  - **Tertiary Prevention**: 
    - Aims to readjust mentally ill individuals post-hospitalization.
    - Use of “passes” for gradual community reintegration.
    - Public education on mental disorders to alleviate stigma.

## 1. UNIT 4.1: Definitions
- **Culture**: Sum of living ways including values, beliefs, language, and norms, developed for survival in an environment (Pusch, 1981).
- **Ethnocentrism**: Viewing and interpreting others' behavior through one's own cultural lens (Matsumoto, 1994).
- **Stereotypes**: Fixed beliefs about people from different cultures; useful for categorization but can be damaging if applied rigidly (Matsumoto, 1994).

---

## 2. UNIT 4.2: Approaches to Understanding Culture and Mental Health

### Universalism
- Characterized as an **“etic”** approach with universally accepted definitions of abnormality.
- Assumes uniform treatment is applicable across cultures based on a universal human psyche.
- Diagnostic criteria of mental disorders are Western-centric.
- **Strengths**: Facilitates cross-cultural comparisons (e.g., international schizophrenia study).
- **Weaknesses**: Ignores environmental influences, stifles individual variability, risks stigmatization and mislabeling.

### Relativism
- Characterized as an **“emic”** approach emphasizing culturally relative definitions of abnormality.
- Argues understanding abnormal behavior requires cultural context.
- Example: Possession deemed appropriate in religious contexts can be misinterpreted in other settings (Kleinman, 1988).
- **Strengths**: Provides rich context for understanding behavior; attends to individual experiences.
- **Weaknesses**: Tends to overlook societal dynamics and may yield fragmented knowledge.

---

## 3. UNIT 4.3: The Cultural Context of Psychopathology
- Culture shapes the definition and perception of abnormal behavior.
- Various cultural factors (psychosocial stressors, syndromes, and community attitudes) impact mental health.
- Understanding these dynamics is crucial for effective mental health interventions.

### Key Points
- Cultural-specific syndromes may be prevalent in certain contexts (e.g., beliefs about possession).
- Social changes influence mental health trends (e.g., rises in drug abuse).
- Negative behaviors should be interpreted within cultural norms to avoid misdiagnosis.

---

## 4. UNIT 4.4: The African Worldview versus the Western Worldview
- Dual healthcare systems in Southern Africa: Traditional African systems vs. Western biomedical approaches (Stricklin, 1990).
- Traditional African healing is intuitive, culturally embedded, and increasingly relevant in urban settings.
- Individuals often navigate between these systems, facing confusion regarding health choices.

### Comparative Attributes
- **Western Approach**: Disease-oriented, focuses on symptoms, emphasizes rational decision-making independent of family consensus.
- **Traditional Approach**: Socially oriented, emphasizes community consensus regarding health perceptions.

*Important for practitioners to grasp these perspectives to effectively address and interpret psychopathology in varying cultural contexts.*

### 4.4 African and Western Worldviews

- **Outcomes**: 
  - Distinguish African vs. Western worldviews

#### African Worldview
- **Definition**: Perceives reality from an African perspective; emphasizes humanity (Ubuntu).
- **Attributes**:
  - Emphasis on interconnectedness of spirit, mind, and matter.
  - Importance of community; "I am because we are".
  - Extended family structures and rituals.
  - Belief in respect for ancestors, honoring rather than worshipping.
  - Temporal focus: past influences present and future.
  - Changing worldviews due to socio-economic factors such as urbanization and Western influences.

#### Western Worldview
- **Definition**: Relies on individualism; highly competitive society.
- **Attributes**:
  - Strong focus on individuality, privacy, and self-reliance.
  - Value placed on material possession and status.
  - Emphasis on future planning; time equated with money.
  - Separation between spiritual, personal, and analytical domains.
  - Nuclear family focus, with deviations viewed as abnormal.

### 4.5 Traditional African Perspective on Psychopathology

- **Overview**: 
  - Holistic view: no distinction between physical and mental health.
  - Health viewed as interconnected with environment and community.
  - Traditional beliefs impact interpretation and causation of illnesses.
  
- **Causation of Disorders**:
  - Disorders seen in the context of human relationships and ancestral influences.

  - Healing is community-oriented; significance placed on spirituality and rituals.

# Study Notes: Culture-Specific Syndromes

## Chapter 4: Culture-Specific Syndromes

### 4.1 Culture-Specific Syndromes
- Culture-specific syndromes refer to abnormal behaviors found exclusively in specific sociocultural contexts.
- Importance of culture in the expression and manifestation of psychological disorders.
- Evidence supports cultural relativism in abnormality through ethnographic reports and emic approaches (Mogale, 1999; Matsumoto, 1994).

### 4.2 Outcomes
- Ability to define and describe culture-bound syndromes.
- Distinction between **ukuthwasa** and **mafufunyana** and their adaptive/maladaptive aspects.

### 4.3 Study Topics
- Psychopathology classification based on perceived causes.
- Disorder categories in South Africa including:
  - **Ukufa kwabantu** (Disorders caused by ancestors)
  - **Magical causes** (Sorcery and witchcraft)
  - **Pollution** (Purity issues)
  - **Umkhuhlane** (Natural causes)

### 4.4 Culture-Specific Syndromes in South Africa
- **Sejeso**: Anxiety and belief of poisoning via food among Sotho-Tswana.
- **Moriti wa letswele**: Female psychogenic pain under the left breast among Sotho-Tswana.
- **Malombo and Malopo**: Spirit possession disorders among Venda and Pedi groups, involving trances and therapeutic music services.
- **Ukuthwasa**: Illness perceived as a call from ancestors, with a range of somatic and behavioral symptoms after a diagnostic process by traditional healers. Treatment involves rituals and potential further training.
  
### 4.5 Amafufunyane
- A spirit possession illness believed to arise from witchcraft, symptoms include psychosis, anxiety, and various psychopathic behaviors.
- Linked to social conflict or jealousy.
- Diagnostic differentiation from **thwasa**, which is considered a healthy ancestral calling, while **mafufunyana** stems from negative phenomena.

### 4.6 Case Study: Tladi
- Diagnosed with **amafufunyane**, showing typical symptoms of possession and witchcraft influence.
- His narrative reflects traditional African explanations for illness as being multifactorial, often linked to witchcraft and social dynamics.

### 4.7 Classification of Abnormal Behavior
- **Magical causes** categorize Tladi’s behavior stemming from perceived witchcraft and communal jealousy.
- Cultural framing influences diagnosis and treatment expectations, advocating for traditional healing consultations.

### 5.1 Study Unit: Assessment and Classification of Abnormal Behaviour (Page 45)
- DSM-5 guidelines are crucial for identifying abnormal behaviour.
- Emphasis on understanding the context of presenting symptoms.
- Effective identification of disorders for prevention and referrals.

### Background Information on the DSM-5 Classification System
- **Classification**: Establishes order in understanding and treating abnormal behaviour.
- Categories for mental disorders are influenced by social norms.
- Initial classifications stem from biological/medical traditions (e.g., Emil Kraepelin).
- DSM-5, published May 2013, continues the psycho-medical classification model.
- Abnormal behaviour correlates with emotional distress and impairment in functioning.
- Classification involves assessing presenting symptoms and contextual factors.
- DSM-5 suggests behaviour exists on a continuum rather than fixed categories.

### Guidelines for Classifying Abnormal Behaviour
1. **Identify Aetiological Medical Conditions**: Assess any medical conditions contributing to psychological symptoms (e.g., “Depressive Disorder due to cerebrovascular accident”).
  
2. **Evaluate Personality Disorders and Intellectual Disability**:
   - Consider history of symptoms and premorbid functioning.
   - Chronic disorders like Personality Disorders can complicate treatment.
   - Diagnosis of Personality Disorders usually requires observation and corroboration from others.

3. **Assess Children**:
   - IQ testing is crucial for diagnosing Intellectual Disability (IQ below 70).
   - Focus on categories relevant to children, such as Neurodevelopmental Disorders.
   - Apply severity and course specifiers only if all criteria are met per DSM-5 (APA, 2013).

# Study Notes on Differential Diagnosis and DSM-5 Classification

## Page 48-53

### Chapter 1: Understanding Mental Disorders

#### Learning Unit 1: Differential Diagnosis

- **Differential Diagnosis Process**:
  - Systematic elimination of possible disorders based on symptom analysis.
  - Focus on severity and duration of symptoms.
  - Rule out severe disorders first by analyzing symptom combinations.

#### Step 4: Neurocognitive Disorders
- **Key Features**:
  - Significant cognitive deficits represent a change from previous functioning.
  - Symptoms of delirium or dementia must be present for diagnosis.
- **Important Note**: Premorbid functioning is crucial as Neurocognitive Disorders imply decline from a previous level of cognitive function (APA, 2013, p. 591).

#### Step 5: Schizophrenia Spectrum and Other Psychotic Disorders
- **Core Symptoms**:
  - Delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, behavior, and negative symptoms.
  - Duration: Symptoms must persist for at least six months, with one month of active-phase symptoms.
- **Exclusion Criteria**: Psychotic symptoms should not relate to other mental conditions or substance use.

#### Step 6: Substance-Related and Addictive Disorders
- Examine individual’s substance use (e.g., tobacco, alcohol, drugs).
- Consider Gambling Disorder under non-substance addictive disorders.

#### Step 7: Mood and Anxiety Disorders
- **Mood Changes**:
  - Assess for changes in mood (elevated or low).
  - Criteria for Bipolar and Related Disorders if manic or hypomanic episodes are present.
- **Obsessive-Compulsive Symptoms**:
  - Obsessions: Recurrent intrusive thoughts.
  - Compulsions: Repetitive behaviors driven by obsessions.
- **Anxiety Disorders**: Consider if prominent feelings of anxiety are present.
- **Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders**: Identify traumatic events linked to current symptoms.
- **Dissociative Disorders**: Look for disruptions in consciousness and identity.

#### Step 8: Other Conditions That May Be a Focus of Clinical Attention
- Identify relational problems, abuse, neglect, socioeconomic issues affecting mental health.
- Multiple diagnoses possible; start with principal diagnosis.

#### Categories of Other Conditions
- **Relational Problems**: Family dynamics, parental issues, bereavement.
- **Abuse and Neglect**: Confirmed or suspected abuse scenarios.
- **Educational/Occupational Problems**: Academic underachievement, work-related stress.
- **Housing and Economic Problems**: Issues like homelessness or inadequate living conditions.
- **Social Environment Problems**: Life phase changes, social exclusion.
- **Legal System Issues**: Victimization, criminal records.
- **Healthcare Access Problems**: Availability of medical and support services.

### Case Study: Elaine
- **Background**:
  - 25-year-old female, socially reserved, fearful of criticism, exhibits depressive symptoms.
- **Symptoms Observed**:
  - Distress in response to loss, social withdrawal, sleep disturbances, weight loss, and suicidal ideation.
- **Diagnosis Process Considerations**:
  - **Differential Diagnoses**: Schizotypal, Schizoid, Avoidant, and Dependent Personality Disorders.
  - **Avoidant Personality Disorder (APD)**: Elaine meets criteria with pervasive social inhibition and fear of negative evaluation. 

#### Key Features of Elaine’s Case
- Long-standing stable pattern of symptoms (insecurity, social withdrawal).
- Consistent theme of self-doubt and fear of rejection throughout life.
- Exacerbation of symptoms post-trauma (loss of a significant figure).

### Conclusion
- Understanding complex mental health issues requires a systematic approach to diagnosis.
- The DSM-5 classification system aids in identifying and categorizing symptoms for effective treatment planning. 

**Note**: At the third-year level, focusing on key diagnostic categories like Feeding, Eating Disorders, Sleep-Wake Disorders, and Medication-Induced Movement Disorders is not emphasized.

## Chapter 6: Personality Disorders

### Learning Unit 6.1: Antisocial Personality Disorder

- **Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD)**
  - Known as psychopathy or sociopathy.
  - Characterized by:
    - Disregard for and violation of the rights of others.
    - Symptoms begin in childhood/adolescence and persist into adulthood.
    - Core features: deceit, manipulation, impulsivity, aggression, irresponsibility, lack of remorse.

- **DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria for ASPD (APA, 2013, p. 659):**
  - A. Pervasive pattern of disregard for rights of others since age 15, indicated by 3+ of:
    1. Nonconformity to social norms (repeated arrests).
    2. Deceitfulness (lying, aliases, conning).
    3. Impulsivity (failure to plan).
    4. Aggressiveness (fights/assaults).
    5. Recklessness (safety disregard).
    6. Irresponsibility (work and financial obligations).
    7. Lack of remorse (rationalizing harm done).
  - B. Individual age 18+.
  - C. Evidence of Conduct Disorder before age 15.
  - D. Not during Schizophrenia or Bipolar Disorder.

### Learning Unit 6.2: Case Study - Pieter

  - **Confirmed:**
    - ASPD, Cannabis Use Disorder, Cocaine Use Disorder.
    - Partner Violence (Physical & Psychological).
    - Educational problems (low literacy).

- **Justification for ASPD Diagnosis:**
  - Displays 3+ ASPD symptoms:
    - Engaged in illegal activities since age 15 (drug selling).
    - Consistent deceitfulness (lying/manipulating).
    - Impulsivity and aggression (violent behaviors towards partner).
    - Reckless endangerment of self and others.
    - Lack of remorse (no accountability for actions).

- **Personality Functioning (Multipath Model):**
  - **Biological Dimensions:** Genetic factors, CNS abnormalities.
  - **Psychological Dimensions:** Weakened superego (psychodynamic theory), extreme core beliefs (cognitive theory).
  - **Social Dimensions:** Hostile home environment, parental neglect.
  - **Sociocultural Dimensions:** Gender influences, cultural norms.

## 6.4 Study Unit: Cluster C - Disorders Characterised by Anxious or Fearful Behaviour

- **Disorders Included**:
  - Avoidant Personality Disorder
  - Dependent Personality Disorder
  - Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder

- **General Overview**:
  - Involves anxious or fearful behaviours.
  - **Avoidant Personality Disorder**: Fear of rejection and humiliation; reluctance in social relationships.
  - **Dependent Personality Disorder**: Fear of being independent; reliance on others.
  - **Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder**: Perfectionism, rigid behaviours, devotion to detail.

- **Outcomes**:
  - Knowledge of Cluster C disorders.
  - Definition and DSM-5 diagnostic criteria for each disorder.
  - Distinction between Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD) and Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD).

### **DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria for Avoidant Personality Disorder** (APA, 2013, pp. 672-673)
- A pervasive pattern of social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, and hypersensitivity to negative evaluation, indicated by four or more of the following:
  1. Avoids interpersonal activities (fears criticism/rejection).
  2. Unwilling to engage unless certain of being liked.
  3. Restraint in intimate relationships (fear of ridicule/shame).
  4. Preoccupation with criticism/rejection.
  5. Inhibition in new situations due to feelings of inadequacy.
  6. Self-view as socially inept or inferior.
  7. Unwilling to take risks due to fear of embarrassment.

### **DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria for Dependent Personality Disorder** (APA, 2013, p. 675)
- A pervasive and excessive need to be taken care of leading to submissive behaviour, indicated by five or more of:
  1. Difficulty making decisions without excessive advice.
  2. Needs others to assume responsibility for major life areas.
  3. Fear of loss of support affects disagreement with others.
  4. Difficulty initiating projects due to self-confidence issues.
  5. Excessive lengths for nurturance from others (e.g., volunteering for unpleasant tasks).
  6. Uncomfortable/helpless when alone (exaggerated fears).
  7. Urgent need for another relationship upon loss of close relationship.
  8. Preoccupation with fears of self-care.

### **DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria for Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder** (APA, 2013, pp. 678-679)
- A pervasive pattern of preoccupation with orderliness and perfectionism, beginning in early adulthood and present in various contexts, indicated by four or more of:
  1. Preoccupation with details/lists/order that loses the main point.
  2. Perfectionism that interferes with task completion.
  3. Excessive devotion to work at the expense of leisure/friendships.
  4. Rigidity about morality/values (culturally unaccounted).
  5. Difficulty discarding worthless objects.
  6. Reluctance in delegating tasks unless others comply with specific methods.
  7. Miserly spending style; hoarding mentality.
  8. Rigidity and stubbornness.

### **OCPD vs. OCD**
- OCPD: Personality Disorder; chronic rigid behaviours and perfectionism.
- OCD: Separate diagnosis under Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders; involves intrusive thoughts and compulsive actions.

# Study Notes on Anxiety Disorders

## 7.1 STUDY UNIT: Types of Anxiety Disorders

- **Anxiety Disorders**: Maladaptive anxiety/fear rooted in apprehensive future-oriented mood states.
  - **Anxiety**:
    - Symptoms: physiological arousal, feelings of tension, apprehension.
  - **Fear**:
    - Characterized by immediate alarm reaction.
- **Symptoms**:
  - **Affective**: Fearfulness, dread, terror.
  - **Cognitive**: Unrealistic worry, panic, concentration issues, hyper vigilance.
  - **Behavioural**: Avoidance, freezing, clinging, agitation.
  - **Somatic**: Increased heart rate, perspiration, muscle tension, gastrointestinal issues.

- **Main Subtypes of Anxiety Disorders**:
  - **Separation Anxiety Disorder**
  - **Selective Mutism**
  - **Specific Phobia**
  - **Social Anxiety Disorder**
  - **Panic Disorder**
  - **Agoraphobia**
  - **Generalised Anxiety Disorder**
  
- **Focus**: Panic Disorder, Agoraphobia, and Generalised Anxiety Disorder.

## 7.1.1 Characteristics

- **Anxiety Disorders**: Defined by excessive anxiety reactions, abnormal behaviour patterns, distress.
- **Distinction**: Normal anxiety vs. maladaptive anxiety; latter is intense, enduring, and limits functioning.

### 7.1.2 Separation Anxiety Disorder
- **Characteristics**:
  - **Symptoms** (at least three needed):
    - Distress when separated from attachment figures.
    - Worry about losing attachment figures.
    - Reluctance to leave attachment figures.
    - Nightmares about separation.
    - Persistent physical complaints when separation occurs.

- **Duration**:
  - Children: at least 4 weeks.
  - Adults: at least 6 months.

### 7.1.3 Selective Mutism
- **Definition**: Persistent failure to speak in certain social situations despite normal language abilities.
- **Duration**: At least 1 month, impacts educational/social performance.

## 7.2 STUDY UNIT: Agoraphobia and Panic Disorder

### 7.2.1 Agoraphobia
- **Definition**: Fear of being in situations where escape is difficult or help unavailable if panic-like symptoms occur.
- **Situations**: Avoid crowds, public transport, open/enclosed spaces, outside home alone.
- **Symptoms**: Persistent fear/anxiety lasting 6 months or more, significant distress or impairment in functioning. 

### 7.2.2 Panic Disorder
- **Definition**: Sudden episodes of intense fear with various physical symptoms.
- **Panic Attacks**:
  - **Types**:
    - *Expected*: Obvious cue present.
    - *Unexpected*: No identifiable trigger.
- **Characteristics of Panic Attack**:
  - At least four of the following:
    - Palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, choking sensations, chest pain, nausea, dizziness, chills, numbness, feelings of unreality, fear of losing control or dying.
  
- **Diagnosis of Panic Disorder**:
  1. Recurrent unexpected panic attacks.
  2. At least one attack followed by worry/behavior change for 1 month.

### Comparative Study of Panic Disorder and Generalised Anxiety Disorder
- **Commonality**: Both are classified as Anxiety Disorders under DSM-5.

- Cognitions and conditioning are core to panic disorder development.
- Individuals misinterpret physical sensations (e.g., rapid heartbeat) as heart attacks, which amplifies anxiety.
- Misinterpretation leads to a positive feedback loop of anxiety and physical symptoms, resulting in panic attacks.

# 7.4 STUDY UNIT: Types of Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders and Aetiology

## Overview of Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders
- Involuntary, intrusive, repetitive thoughts/actions.
- Associated with reduced quality of life, social/occupational impairment.
- High distress levels in affected individuals.

## Examples and Consequences
- **Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)**: 
  - Time-consuming obsessions/compulsions lead to impaired functioning (e.g., obsessing over symmetry).
- **Body Dysmorphic Disorder**: 
  - Significant impairment due to appearance concerns; potential for being housebound.
- **Hoarding Disorder**: 
  - Substantial occupational impairment; possible legal issues (eviction).
- **Trichotillomania**: 
  - Impairment and health risks (irreversible hair damage, bowel obstruction).
- **Excoriation (Skin-Picking) Disorder**: 
  - Social/occupational impairment; medical complications may arise (infections).

## Diagnostic Criteria for Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder
- **Obsessions**: 
  1. Recurrent, intrusive thoughts/urges causing anxiety or distress.
  2. Individual attempts to ignore/suppress or neutralize thoughts.
- **Compulsions**:
  1. Repetitive behaviors/mental acts performed in response to obsessions.
  2. Aimed at reducing anxiety or preventing feared events, but are excessive/irrational.
  
- **Criteria**:
  - Time-consuming (>1 hour/day) or significant functional impairment.
  - Not attributable to substances/another medical condition.
  - Not explained by another mental disorder.

- **Specifiers**:
  - Insight levels (good/fair, poor, absent).
  - Tic-related history.

## Diagnostic Criteria for Hoarding Disorder
- **A**: Difficulty discarding possessions, regardless of value.
- **B**: Urge to save items with associated distress.
- **C**: Accumulation leading to clutter that compromises living areas.
- **D**: Causes functional impairment or distress.
- **E**: Not attributable to a medical condition.
- **F**: Not better explained by another mental disorder.

- **Specifiers**:
  - Excessive acquisition of items.
  - Insight levels (good/fair, poor, absent).

## Aetiology - Multipath Model
- **Psychological Factors**:
  - Cognitive-behavioral patterns contribute to disorder development.
  
- **Social Factors**:
  - Familial and peer influences affecting behavior and stress response.

- **Sociocultural Factors**:
  - Cultural attitudes toward cleanliness and possessions impacting disorder manifestation.
