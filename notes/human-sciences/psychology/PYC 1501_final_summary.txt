---

### 1. Functional Parts of a Neuron
- Identify six parts of a typical neuron:
  - **Dendrites:** Receive information signals
  - **Soma (Cell body):** Processes received signals
  - **Axon:** Conducts signals to other points in the nervous system
  - **Axon terminals:** Transmit signals to other neurons
  - **Myelin sheath:** Insulates the axon
  - **Neuron membrane:** Generates and transmits nerve impulses

### 2. Nerves and Nerve Tracts
- Recognize:
  - **Afferent Neurons:** Carry signals to the CNS
  - **Efferent Neurons:** Carry signals from the CNS
- Distinction between:
  - **Nerves:** Bundles of axons outside CNS
  - **Nerve tracts:** Bundles of axons within CNS

---

### 3. Relation Between Impulse Conduction and Behavior
- Conversion of environmental stimuli into nerve impulses for communication to the brain.
- Impulse conduction influences behavior; disruptions affect reactions.

### 4. Nature of Resting Membrane Potential
- Function: Prepares the neuron to respond to stimuli
- Cause: Results from uneven ion distribution across cell membranes.

### 5. Nature of Action Potential
- Cause: Sufficient stimulation of a neuron.
- Result: Changes resting membrane potential to conduct stimulus along the axon.

### 6. Concept of Refractory Period
- Definition: Period during which a neuron cannot fire after an action potential.
- Ensures: 
  - One-directional impulse conduction
  - Regulates stimulus intensity and impulse frequency.

### 7. Three Principles of Impulse Conduction
- Identify:
  - **All-or-Nothing Law:** Neurons either fire fully or not at all.
  - Relation of stimulus intensity to impulse frequency.
  - **Saltatory Conduction:** Rapid impulse transmission along myelinated axons.

# Study Notes on Impulse Conduction and Synaptic Transmission

## Chapter 1: Impulse Conduction in the Human Nervous System
### Page 48

#### 1.1 Characteristics of Impulse Conduction
- Action potentials are all-or-nothing events; either fired or not if threshold is met.
- Action potentials remain constant in amplitude along the axon; do not weaken.
- Myelinated axons conduct impulses faster than unmyelinated axons.

### Chapter 2: Synaptic Transmission of Impulses
#### Page 49

#### 2.1 Structure Involved in Synaptic Transmission
- Presynaptic membrane: before the synapse (axon terminal).
- Postsynaptic membrane: after the synapse (next neuron).

#### 2.2 Types of Postsynaptic Potentials
- **Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential (EPSP)**: can trigger an action potential.
- **Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential (IPSP)**: prevents action potential.

#### 2.3 Summation Concepts
- **Temporal Summation**: accumulation over time.
- **Spatial Summation**: accumulation from multiple sources.

### Chapter 3: Differences Between Action Potentials and Postsynaptic Potentials
#### Page 50

- Action potentials: All-or-nothing; propagate without decrement.
- Postsynaptic potentials: Graded responses; can diminish over distance.
- Both require summation to contribute to action potential firing.

## Chapter 4: Common Neurotransmitters and Their Effects
### Page 51

#### 4.1 Factors Influencing Neurotransmitter Action
- Nature and site of action of neurotransmitters.
- Quantity respective to enzymes that degrade them.
- Balance between excitatory and inhibitory influences.

#### 4.2 Classic Neurotransmitters and Associated Disorders
- **Alzheimer’s Disease**: Insufficient acetylcholine.
- **Schizophrenia**: Excess dopamine.
- **Parkinson’s Disease**: Low dopamine levels.
- **Seasonal Depression**: Serotonin deficiency.
- **Pain**: Low endorphin levels.

## Chapter 5: Effects of Drugs on Synaptic Processes
### Page 52

#### 5.1 Drug Classification
- **Agonists**: Mimic neurotransmitter action.
- **Antagonists**: Block neurotransmitter action.

# Structure and Functions of the Human Nervous System

## Chapter 1: The Nervous System Overview

### 1.1 Protection of the Spinal Cord and Brain
- **Bony Protection**: Spinal vertebrae and skull.
- **Membranes**: Dura mater, arachnoid mater, pia mater.
- **Cerebrospinal Fluid**: Surrounds brain and fills ventricular spaces; produced by the brain.
- **Blood-Brain Barrier**: Protects against toxic substances.

### 1.2 Spinal Cord Functionality
- **Motor and Sensory Pathways**: 
  - Motor and sensory roots identified.
  - Spinal cord facilitates reflexive (rapid automatic) and voluntary (brain-initiated) behavior.
- **Injury Effects**: Damage can cause paralysis, but reflex actions may persist.

### 1.3 Structures of the Hindbrain
- **Key Structures**: 
  - Medulla Oblongata: Controls vital functions.
  - Pons: Regulates sleep and wakefulness.
  - Cerebellum: Coordinates muscle movement.

### 1.4 Structures of the Midbrain
- **Tectum**: Reflex centers for visual and auditory systems.
- **Tegmentum**: Involved in motor movement.

### 1.5 Structures of the Forebrain
- **Key Structures**: 
  - Hypothalamus: Regulates internal environment (blood pressure, heartbeat).
  - Thalamus: Relay station for sensory information.
  - Limbic System: Emotion regulation.
  - Basal Ganglia: Motor behavior control.

### 1.6 Cerebral Cortex Lobes
- **Frontal Lobe**: Executive functions, voluntary motor movements, emotional regulation.
- **Temporal Lobe**: Sound perception (verbal/non-verbal).
- **Parietal Lobe**: Spatial information, sensory-motor integration.
- **Occipital Lobe**: Visual information interpretation and integration.
  
### 1.7 Peripheral Nervous System
- **Somatic Nervous System**: 
  - Comprises peripheral nerves communicating with skin and muscles.
  - Includes sensory (optic nerve) and motor components (skeletal muscle function).
- **Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)**:
  - **Sympathetic Division**: Activates fight or flight response (increased heart rate, blood pressure).
  - **Parasympathetic Division**: Promotes rest and digest responses (increase in saliva and tear secretion).

**Note**: The systems work together for homeostasis.

### 1. Types of Reasoning
- **Deductive Reasoning**
  - Truth of premises guarantees conclusion.
  - Example: 
    - Premise 1: Liquids that contain acid turn litmus paper red.
    - Premise 2: The liquid in the blue bottle does not turn litmus paper red.
    - Conclusion: The liquid in the blue bottle does not contain acid.
  
- **Inductive Reasoning**
  - Conclusion drawn from premises based on probability.
  - Identified by terms like "could" or "might."

### 2. Differences Between Formal and Everyday Reasoning
- **Formal Reasoning**
  - Follows explicit rules of logic.
  - Generally has one correct solution.
  
- **Everyday Reasoning**
  - Less structured, often includes personal relevance.
  - More prone to weaknesses.

### 3. Fallacies in Reasoning
- **Types of Fallacies**
  - Based on irrelevant premises to support conclusions.
  - Example: Ad hominem fallacy (discrediting the person, not the issue).

### 4. Critical Reasoning
- **Critical Reasoning Skills**
  - Evaluate problems by identifying them, maintaining an open mind, and considering all viewpoints.
  
### 5. Basic Problem-Solving Strategies

  3. Explore possible solutions.

- **Concepts**

## Concepts of Learning and Memory

### 01. Classical Conditioning
- **Definition**: Learning through association of stimuli.
- **Conditioned Stimulus (CS)**: A neutral stimulus that acquires significance through pairing with an unconditioned stimulus (US).
- **Key Phenomena**:
  - Reinforcement
  - Extinction
  - Spontaneous Recovery
  - Generalisation
  - Discrimination
  - Higher-order Conditioning

### 02. Operant Conditioning
- **Definition**: Learning based on the association between behavior and its consequences (rewards or punishments).
- **Operant Response**: The behavior enacted to achieve a desired outcome.
- **Reinforcement**:
  - Positive Reinforcement: Introducing a pleasant stimulus to increase behavior.
  - Negative Reinforcement: Removing an unpleasant stimulus to increase behavior.
- **Punishment**: Application of an unpleasant consequence to reduce behavior.

### 03. Cognitive Learning
- **Definition**: Learning that involves mental processes and understanding rather than direct experiences.
- **Types**:
  - Cognitive Mapping: Learning the layout/route based on mental representations.
  - Perceptual-Motor Skills: Learning skills through physical practice involving perception and movement.
  - Intellectual Skills: Acquiring knowledge through analysis and verbal understanding.

### 04. Social Learning
- **Definition**: Learning through observation and interaction within a social context.
- **Methods**:
  - **Operant Social Learning**: Behaviors learned through reinforcement in social environments.
  - **Social Observational Learning**: Learning by observing the actions of others and their consequences.
- **Influences on Behavior**: 
  - Social Needs
  - Motivation to Change
  - Reinforcement from others

### 05. Memory
- **Components of Memory**:
  - **Coding**: Translating sensory information into a form that can be stored.
  - **Storage**: Maintaining encoded information over time.
  - **Retrieval**: Accessing stored information when needed. 
- **Recognition**: Not a distinct process but part of retrieval.
