## Unit 1: Human Nervous System

### 1.1 Introduction
- Human nervous system essential for human functioning and behavior.
- Comparative studies of animal nervous systems enhance understanding of human behavior.
- Focus on structure of neuron, impulse conduction, and human nervous system organization.

### 1.2 Human Nervous System: Structure and Functions
- **Divisions**: 
  - Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
  - Central Nervous System (CNS)

### 1.2.1 The Peripheral Nervous System
- Carries signals for body survival; includes voluntary and involuntary actions.
- **Divisions**:
  - **Somatic Nervous System**:
    - Relays sensory and motor information.
    - **Efferent Neurons**: Carry signals from CNS to muscles.
    - **Afferent Neurons**: Carry sensory information to CNS.
  - **Autonomic Nervous System**:
    - Controls involuntary functions.
    - **Sympathetic Division**: Prepares body for stress (fight-or-flight).
    - **Parasympathetic Division**: Restores body to homeostasis.

### 1.2.2 The Central Nervous System (CNS)
- Comprises brain, brainstem, and spinal cord; performs information synthesis.
- Regulates organ function, high-level thoughts, and movements.
- Neurons: Approximately 100 billion in the brain, essential for communication.
  
### 1.3 Brain Structure and Functions
- **Brain**: Headquarters of the nervous system.
  - **Limbic System**: Regulates emotions (e.g., hippocampus, amygdala).
  - Influences fear, aggression, hunger, wake cycle, and memory.
- **Cerebellum**: Coordinates actions automatically.
- **Cerebrum and Cerebral Cortex**:
  - Gray matter (cell bodies) outside, white matter (myelinated axons) inside.
  - Each hemisphere manages sensory/motor functions contralaterally.
  - The **Corpus Callosum** connects hemispheres.

### 1. The Brain and Its Lobes
- **Frontal Lobe**: Cognitive functions (language, memory, decision making, problem solving).
- **Parietal Lobe**: Processes sensory inputs from skin and muscles.
- **Occipital Lobe**: Contains primary visual cortex; responsible for vision.
- **Temporal Lobe**: Contains auditory and olfactory regions; processes hearing and smell.

### 2. Internal Brain Structures
- **Limbic System**: Memory (hippocampus, fornix) and emotions (cingulate gyrus); connected to motivation.
- **Globus Pallidus**: Involved in motor movements and coordination.
- **Hypothalamus**: Regulates endocrine hormones with pituitary gland; drives and motivations.
- **Thalamus**: Traffic cues for sensory and motor processing.

### 3. Brainstem and Spinal Cord Functions
- **Midbrain**: Superior/inferior colliculi (visual/auditory processing); substantia nigra's role in Parkinson’s disease.
- **Reticular Formation**: Manages arousal, sleep, temperature regulation.
- **Hindbrain**: Pons (sensory/motor processing) and medulla oblongata (breathing, digestion, heart function).
- **Spinal Cord**: Connects brain to body; manages sensory and motor nerve impulses.

### 4. Neurons and Impulse Conduction
- **Neuron Structure**: Consists of dendrites (info collection), soma (cell body), axon (info transmission), terminal boutons (synapse info release).
- **Myelin Sheath**: Insulates axons, speeding up signal transmission.

### 5. Action Potential
- **Definition**: All-or-nothing event; requires reaching the threshold of excitation.
- **EPSP/IPSP**: 
  - **EPSP**: Depolarizing input; brings membrane potential closer to threshold.
  - **IPSP**: Hyperpolarizing input; moves membrane potential further from threshold.

### 6. Refractory Period
- **Definitions**: 
  - **Absolute Refractory Period (ARP)**: No new impulse possible; sodium channels inactive.
  - **Relative Refractory Period (RRP)**: Neuron can respond to strong stimuli post-ARP.

### 7. The Synapse
- **Synaptic Cleft**: Space for neurotransmitter exchange; crucial for neuron communication.
- **Neurotransmitters**: Chemical signals crossing synapse; facilitate neural transmission and communication among neurons.

## 1.3 Neural Communication

### 1.3.1 Neural Communication - The Role of Neurotransmitters
- **Neurotransmitters**: Variable shapes fit into receptor sites, influencing neuronal firing.
  - *Excitatory effects*: Increase likelihood of firing.
  - *Inhibitory effects*: Decrease likelihood of firing.
- Neurons can receive multiple neurotransmitters, leading to combined effects.
- Action potential: Triggered when excitatory effects exceed inhibitory.

### 1.3.2 Synaptic Process
- Neurons must remove excess neurotransmitters for subsequent stimulation via:
  - **Enzymatic breakdown**
  - **Reuptake**: Absorption back into the transmitting terminal.

### 1.3.3 Neural Communication - Electrical & Chemical Process
- **Electro-chemical process**: Electrical signals travel through neurons, chemical signals between neurons.
- Signals transmitted from dendrites to soma as electrical signals.
- If strong enough, the signal progresses to the axon and terminal buttons.
- **Resting membrane potential**: More negatively charged ions inside compared to outside.
- **Action potential**: When threshold reached, positively charged ions enter axon.
- **Node of Ranvier**: Segmented axon facilitating rapid transmission (up to 1000 times/sec).

### 1.3.4 Neurotransmitters
- Over **100 neurotransmitters** identified; vital for:
  - Emotion, cognition, behavior, appetite, memory, muscle action.
- **Table 1.1**: Key neurotransmitters and functions.
  - **Acetylcholine (ACh)**: Muscle contractions; low levels linked to Alzheimer's.
  - **Dopamine**: Motivation and emotion; schizophrenia and Parkinson's disease associations.
  - **Endorphins**: Natural pain relief; deficiency linked to depression.
  - **GABA**: Major inhibitory neurotransmitter; linked to anxiety.
  - **Glutamate**: Major excitatory neurotransmitter; excessive levels may lead to migraines/seizures.
  - **Serotonin**: Regulators for mood; low levels linked to seasonal affective disorders.
  - **Adrenaline & Noradrenaline**: Regulation of fight-or-flight responses; excess linked to high blood pressure/depression.

### 1.3.5 Drug Influence on Neurotransmitters
- **Agonists**: Mimic neurotransmitter effects (e.g., cocaine with dopamine).
- **Antagonists**: Block neurotransmitter effects (e.g., curare with acetylcholine).
- Certain drugs inhibit reuptake, increasing neurotransmitter availability at synapses.

## Unit 2: Sensation and Perception

### 2.1 Introduction
- Continuous interaction with environment; influences and responds.
- Sensory systems enable exploration, understanding, and survival.
- Focus on sensation and perception processes that provide meaning.

### 2.2 Defining Sensation and Perception
- **Sensation**: Awareness from sensory organ stimulation (biochemical, neurological).
- **Perception**: Organization, interpretation of sensory information; includes bottom-up and top-down processing.
  - **Bottom-up**: Perception built from sensory input.
  - **Top-down**: Interpretation influenced by knowledge and experiences.
- Goals: Detection (sensation) vs. creating useful information (perception).
- **Absolute Threshold**: Minimum stimulation to detect a stimulus.
- **Differential Threshold (Just Noticeable Difference - jnd)**: Minimum difference required to detect stimulus changes (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
- Sensory adaptation: Reduced response to unchanging stimuli.

### 2.3 The Five Sensory Systems
Refer to Figure 2.1: The sensory systems.

#### 2.3.1 Hearing (Auditory)
- Sound wave dynamics: Vibration in medium (e.g., air).
- Sound enters outer ear → auditory canal → eardrum → ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) → cochlea (hair cells detect sound) → auditory nerve (van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).
- Tonotopic organization of hair cells; signals processed in auditory cortex (Romani et al., 1982).
Refer to Figure 2.2: Diagram of the human ear.

#### 2.3.2 Smell (Olfaction)
- Chemical sense; interact with taste (gustatory).
- Odour molecules detected by olfactory receptor cells in nasal passage.
- Neuronal signals to olfactory bulb → limbic system and primary olfactory cortex (Lodovichi & Belluscio, 2012; Spors et al., 2013).
Refer to Figure 2.3: Smell receptors.

#### 2.3.3 Taste (Gustation)
- Basic tastes: Sweet, salty, sour, bitter, umami.
- Taste molecules interact with receptors on tongue; taste buds renew every 10-14 days.
- Neural impulses from taste transmitted to medulla, thalamus, limbic system, gustatory cortex (Maffei et al., 2012; Roper, 2013).
Refer to Figure 2.4: Structure of taste buds in the tongue.

## 2.3.4 Touch
- Somaesthetic system provides information about touch, pressure, heat, and pain.
- Receptors transduce pressure into neural activity in/on the skin.
- Somatosensation includes touch, temperature, and pain, converting stimuli into electrical potentials.
- Tactile stimuli processed by mechanoreceptors; heightened skin sensitivity in areas like lips vs. shoulders.
- Skin aids proprioception, sensing body position/movement through specialized neurons.

## 2.3.5 Vision (Seeing)
- Humans rely heavily on vision; light enters through the cornea, pupil, and lens, focusing on the retina.
- Sensation depends on light intensity (brightness) and wavelength (color).
- Photoreceptors (rods for low light, cones for color/details) convert light into electrical signals.
- Damage to the primary visual cortex can lead to agnosia or prosopagnosia.
- Visual recognition involves two pathways: ventral ('what') and dorsal ('where').

### Colour vision and colour blindness
- Normal vision relies on three cone types (trichromatic theory): red, green, blue.
- Opponent-process theory posits color is coded in pairs; negative afterimages occur post-stimulus removal.

### Depth perception
- Ability to perceive spatial relationships in 3-D.
- Binocular vision utilizes both eyes for depth; binocular disparity provides depth clues.
- Monocular cues (e.g., linear perspective) enable depth perception in 2-D images.

## 2.4 The principles of perceptual grouping
- Perceptual grouping is the tendency to organize stimuli into meaningful patterns.
- Gestalt psychology, led by theorists like Wertheimer, Köhler, and Koffka, explores how the brain forms a whole perception.
- Perceptual hypotheses are informed by personal experiences and expectations.

## 2.5 Wave amplitude and wavelengths
- Visual and auditory stimuli are wave-based, sharing characteristics relevant to perception.
- Wave amplitude: height of a wave; wavelength: length from peak to peak.
- Frequency correlates to the number of waves passing a point, expressed in hertz (Hz).

### 2.5.1 Light waves
- The electromagnetic spectrum includes all radiation, with the visible range (380-740 nm) critical for human color perception.
- Different species may perceive additional wavelengths (e.g., bees with ultraviolet light).

### 2.5.2 Sound waves
- Sound waves result from vibrations creating pressure in a medium, like air.
- Frequency relates to pitch; human audible range is 20-20,000 Hz, with varying sensitivity across frequencies.
- Loudness correlates to amplitude, measured in decibels (dB); potential hearing damage from 80 dB to 130 dB.

## Unit 3: Cognition: Thinking, Reasoning, and Problem-Solving

### 3.1 Introduction
- Thinking guides behavior; aids in problem-solving.
- Involves manipulating information: forming concepts, solving problems, reasoning, decision-making.
- Understanding thinking is key to learning, remembering, and communicating.

### 3.2 Defining Cognition
- **Cognition**: Mental processes including thinking, reasoning, and perceptions (Van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).
- Entails organizing, understanding, and communicating information.
- Involves mental imagery alongside verbal processes (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

### 3.3 Types of Mental Representations

#### 3.3.1 Mental Image
- Represents objects/events in mind, not merely pictorial (Shepherd & Metzler, 1971).
- Involves various senses; can mentally manipulate images (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

#### 3.3.2 Concepts
- Categories of objects/events/ideas sharing common properties (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- Types:
  - **Formal Concepts**: Defined by strict rules (e.g., a square).
  - **Natural Concepts**: Defined by experiences, not strict rules (e.g., birds).
- Importance:
  1. Generalization
  2. Association of experiences
  3. Memory facilitation
  4. Clues for reactions

#### 3.3.3 Prototype
- Best example of a natural concept possessing characteristic features (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- Prototypicality influences categorization (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).

#### 3.3.4 Proposition
- Expresses relationships between concepts; can be true/false (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- Dynamic; updates with new information.

#### 3.3.5 Schemas
- Set of propositions forming complex mental representations; generalizations about categories/events (Bernstein et al., 2012).

#### 3.3.6 Scripts
- Specific schemas outlining sequences for familiar activities (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- Example: restaurant dining sequence.

### 3.4 Reasoning
- **Reasoning**: Evaluating arguments and drawing conclusions (Bernstein et al., 2012).

#### 3.4.1 Types of Reasoning
- **Formal (Logical) Reasoning**:
  - Follows rigorous procedures for valid conclusions (Bernstein et al., 2012).
  - **Deductive Reasoning**: General to specific; definitive conclusions based on true premises (Santrock, 2003).
    - Example: Syllogism relating to angels.
  
- **Informal Reasoning**:
  - Assesses the credibility of conclusions; context-dependent (Bernstein et al., 2012).
  - **Inductive Reasoning**: Specific to general; conclusions are probable, not guaranteed (Santrock, 2003).

## 3.4 Reasoning

### 3.4.1 Critical Reasoning
- Reflective and productive thinking.
- Evaluates evidence, open to different perspectives.
- Encourages deeper understanding of concepts (Santrock, 2003).

### 3.4.2 Reasoning Fallacies
- **Errors in Thinking**:
  - Sympathy manipulation: E.g., student uses personal issues to request a pass.
  - Discrediting through personal attacks: E.g., witness's credibility questioned due to criminal history.
  - Group characteristics: E.g., advertising claims "real men drink king size beer."
  - False analogy: Assumes similarity suggests identity (e.g., roles of wives).
  - Misrepresentation: E.g., altering someone’s statement to support one’s agenda.

## 3.5 Problem-solving and Decision-making

- **Cognitive Tools**: Mental images, concepts, and prototypes.
- Problem-solving: Achieving a goal through thoughtful behavior (Santrock, 2003).
- Decision-making: Identifying alternatives and choosing the best option (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).

### 3.5.1 Steps in Problem-solving & Decision-making
1. **Find and Frame the Problem**: Clear recognition and understanding.
2. **Develop Strategies**: Effective methods include algorithms and heuristics.
3. **Evaluate Solutions**: Test effectiveness of solutions against criteria.
4. **Rethink Problems & Solutions**: Continuous improvement of understanding (Santrock, 2003).

### 3.5.2 Strategies for Problem-solving & Decision-making
- **Trial-and-error**: Testing solutions until one works (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).
- **Algorithms**: Step-by-step procedures leading to correct solutions (Coon & Mitterer, 2012).
- **Heuristics**: Educated guesses to narrow solutions (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
  - **Representative heuristic**: Classifying based on similarities.
  - **Availability heuristic**: Based on recall ease (Tversky & Kahneman, 1973).
- **Incubation**: Taking breaks can yield new insights (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
- **Analogy**: Drawing parallels from past solutions (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- **Insight**: Sudden mental reorganization resulting in an 'aha' moment (Durso et al., 1994).

### 3.5.3 Complexities in Decision-making
- Emotions impact preferences.
- Evaluations of options can fluctuate.
- **Uncertainty effect**: Preference for avoiding uncertainty.
- **Gut feeling**: Unconscious deliberation may guide choices.
- Brand and product factors influence decisions (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).

### 3.5.4 Barriers to Problem-solving & Decision-making
1. **Focus on Irrelevant Information**: Distinguishing between relevant and irrelevant data (Sternberg, 1986).
2. **Functional Fixedness**: Limited thinking to typical uses of objects (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).
3. **Mental Sets**: Reliance on past strategies even if they're suboptimal (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
4. **Confirmation Bias**: Seeking evidence that supports one's beliefs, ignoring contrary evidence (Santrock, 2003).

## 3.6 Language and Thinking
- Language as a system of symbols representing thoughts.
- Used for communication and inner speech (Van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).
- Common structural characteristics of languages:
  - Sounds, meanings, word order, and rules for sentence construction.

## 3.6 Language Structure and Use

- **3.6.1 Grammar**
  - Rules governing language structure.
  - Chomsky (2006): Innate Language Acquisition Device (LAD) for language understanding and production.

- **3.6.2 Phoneme**
  - Basic unit of sound affecting meaning in language.
  - Different languages have unique phonemes; changing them alters word meaning (Bernstein et al., 2012; Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

- **3.6.3 Morpheme**
  - Smallest meaningful unit of language (e.g., 'dog', 'un-', 'ed').
  - Combined to form phrases and sentences, governed by syntax rules (Bernstein et al., 2012).

- **3.6.4 Syntax**
  - Rules for combining words and phrases into sentences.
  - Syntax mix-ups can lead to misconstrued meanings (Lasnik, 1990; Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

- **3.6.5 Semantics**
  - Rules for determining meanings of words and sentences.
  - Different syntax can yield same semantic meaning (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

- **3.6.6 Pragmatic**
  - Practical aspects of communication (e.g., conversation turn-taking, gesturing).
  - Includes intonation, which varies when addressing different audiences (Yule, 1996).

## 3.7 The Relationship Between Language and Thinking

- Language vs. Thought Influence:
  - **Piaget (1962):** Concepts precede language development (collective monologue).
  - **Vygotsky (1987):** Language aids the formation of thoughts; private speech increases with social interaction.

### 3.7.1 The Role of Language in Thinking
- Language's influence on cognition and problem-solving.
- **Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis:** Language shapes thought patterns. Study by Lucy & Shweder (1979) supports color perception influenced by language.
- Cognitive universalism: Concepts are universal and independent of language (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

### 3.7.2 The Role of Thinking in Language
- Evidence shows cognition can exist independently of language.
- Studies on deaf children show cognitive equivalence to hearing peers in tasks despite language barriers (Furth, 1971, in Santrock, 2003).

## 3.8 Summary
- Cognition encompasses mental processes for knowledge acquisition (thinking, reasoning, problem-solving).
- Cognitive processes impact various life facets (school, work, relationships).

## Unit 4: Memory

### 4.1 Introduction
- Memory essential for organizing significant life events.
- Enables connections with people and continuity over time.
- Discusses the three-stage memory process: encoding, storage, retrieval.

### 4.2 Defining Memory
- **Memory**: Retention of information over time via encoding, storage, and retrieval (Santrock, 2003).
- Reflects various abilities: holding information briefly, episodic recall, general knowledge (McDermott & Roediger, 2021).
- Comparable to a computer: encoding → storage → retrieval (Coon & Mitterer, 2012).

### 4.3 Three-stage Process of Memory

#### 4.3.1 Encoding (putting it in)
- **Encoding**: Process of acquiring and transferring information into memory (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
- Types of encoding:
  - **Structural Encoding**: Shallow; focuses on physical characteristics (e.g., capitalization).
  - **Phonemic (Acoustic) Encoding**: Emphasizes sound (e.g., recalling song lyrics).
  - **Semantic Encoding**: Focuses on meaning; deeper processing leads to better retention.
  - **Visual Encoding**: Involves pictorial representation; easier to recall high-imagery words (Paivio, 1986).

#### 4.3.2 Storage (keeping it in)
- **Storage**: Retention of encoded information; varies from brief to permanent (Santrock, 2003).
- Stages: Sensory memory, short-term memory, long-term memory.

#### 4.3.3 Retrieval (getting it out)
- **Retrieval**: Accessing stored information for conscious awareness.
- Types of tasks:
  - **Recall**: Direct retrieval of learned information (e.g., essay test).
  - **Recognition**: Identifying learned items (e.g., multiple-choice questions).
- **Serial Position Effect**: Tendency to recall first and last items better than middle items (Santrock, 2003).

### 4.4 Types of Memory Systems

#### 4.4.1 Sensory Memory (SM)
- Brief retention of sensory information; less than a second.
- Types:
  - **Iconic Memory**: Visual information retention for a short period (e.g., fleeting images).
  - **Echoic Memory**: Auditory retention; short-lived echoing of sounds (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).

#### 4.4.2 Short-term Memory (STM)
- Information enters consciousness; selective attention is vital (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).
- Retains information for approx. 30 seconds; capacity is 7 ± 2 items (Miller, 1956).

#### 4.4.3 Long-term Memory (LTM)
- Permanent storage with unlimited capacity; includes personal experiences and factual information.
- Types:
  - **Declarative (Explicit) Memory**: Directly accessed; stores specific knowledge (e.g., factual information) (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).

### 5.4.1 Associative Learning
- Definition: Forming associations among stimuli and responses.

      - **Unconditioned Stimulus (US)**: Naturally elicits a response (e.g., food).

      - **Conditioned Response (CR)**: Learned response to the CS (e.g., salivation triggered by the whistle).

## Chapter 1: Learning Theories

### Section 1: Classical Conditioning
- **Definition**: Learning through association between a neutral stimulus and an unconditioned stimulus (US).
- **Process**:
  - **Contiguity**: Association formed through close temporal occurrence (Swartz et al., 2011).
  - **Acquisition**: Development of a conditioned response (CR) when CS and US are paired (Weiten, 2013).
  - **Extinction**: Gradual weakening of CR when CS is presented without US (Swartz et al., 2011).
  - **Stimulus Generalization**: CR triggered by stimuli similar to the conditioned stimulus (Swartz et al., 2011).
  - **Stimulus Discrimination**: Ability to differentiate between similar stimuli, responding accordingly (Schacter et al., 2012).
  - **Higher-order Conditioning**: New stimuli linked to previously conditioned stimuli to elicit CR (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
  - **Spontaneous Recovery**: Reappearance of CR after extinction; indicates incomplete elimination of CR (Plotnik & Kouyoumdjian, 2011).

### Section 2: Operant Conditioning
- **Definition**: Learning where behaviors are modified through consequences (Plotnik & Kouyoumdjian, 2011).
- **Core Concept**: Focus on consequences affecting behavior.
- **Key Researchers**: B.F. Skinner, Edward L. Thorndike.

#### Laws of Learning (Thorndike)
- **Law of Readiness**: Motivation impacts learning experience; satisfaction vs. annoyance.
- **Law of Exercise**: Repetition strengthens connections between stimulus and response.
- **Law of Effect**: Actions followed by rewards are more likely to be repeated (Plotnik & Kouyoumdjian, 2011).

### Section 3: Basic Principles of Operant Conditioning
- **Reinforcement**: Increases likelihood of behavior.
  - **Positive Reinforcement**: Adds pleasant stimuli (Swartz et al., 2011).
  - **Negative Reinforcement**: Removes unpleasant stimuli (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
- **Punishment**: Aversive stimuli decrease behavior.
  - **Positive Punishment**: Adds unpleasant stimuli (Swartz et al., 2011).
  - **Negative Punishment**: Removes pleasant stimuli, reducing behavior (Swartz et al., 2011).

#### Problems with Punishment
- May lead to avoidance of the punisher over the behavior.
- Encourages deceit and fear instead of addressing behavior.
- Can model aggression and emotional distress.

## 5.4 Learning Theories

### 5.4.1 Operant Conditioning

- **Punishment Principles**:
  - Timely: Punishment must follow undesired behavior immediately.
  - Consistency: Must be applied every time the behavior occurs.
  - Pairing: Punishment should coincide with reinforcement of desired behavior.

- **Schedules of Reinforcement**:
  - **Fixed Ratio (FR)**: Reinforcer follows a fixed number of responses.
    - Example: Piecework payment system.
    - Refer to Figure 5.11: Reinforcement in construction projects.
  
  - **Fixed Interval (FI)**: Reinforcer occurs after the first response following a fixed time interval.
    - Example: Scheduled bus arrivals.
    
  - **Variable Ratio (VR)**: Reinforcer follows an unpredictable number of responses.
    - Example: Casino slot machines.
  
  - **Variable Interval (VI)**: Reinforcer appears after varying time intervals.
    - Example: Periodic checks by a boss.
  
- **Reinforcement Effectiveness**:
  - Immediate reinforcement is crucial.
  - Only desired behaviors should be reinforced.

- **Comparison: Classical vs. Operant Conditioning**:
  - **Classical Conditioning**: Involuntary behaviors; stimulus precedes behavior.
  - **Operant Conditioning**: Voluntary behaviors; behavior influence by consequences.

| Association   | Based on antecedent stimuli                  | Based on consequences                          |

## 6.4 The nature of sleep

- Sleep: low physical activity, reduced sensory awareness.
- Biological regulation: fluctuations in heart rate, blood pressure, body temperature (Siegel, 2008).
- EEG patterns: 
  - Awake = beta waves.
  - Pre-sleep = alpha waves.
  - Deep sleep = delta waves (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).
  
- **Evolutionary Psychology Perspective**: sleep restores resources expended (Frank, 2006; Rial et al., 2007).
- Cognitive function & memory formation during sleep (Brown, 2012; Huber et al., 2004).

### 6.4.1 Non-Rapid Eye Movement (NREM) sleep

**Stages of NREM Sleep**:
1. **Stage 1**:
   - Alpha waves → theta waves.
   - K-complexes possible.
   - Reflex muscle twitches ("hypnic jerk").
   
2. **Stage 2**:
   - Sleep spindles appear.
   - Transition to deep relaxation.

3. **Stage 3**:
   - Delta waves signal deeper sleep.
  
4. **Stage 4**:
   - Largest, slowest delta waves; deep oblivion.

- REM sleep: brain activity resembles wakefulness; vivid dreams.
- Significant for cognitive functions like memory retention and emotional processing.

## 6.6 Sleep Disorders

### 6.6.1 Insomnia
- Most common sleep disorder.
- Characterized by trouble falling asleep, waking up at night, or waking too early.
- Associated with daytime fatigue, cognitive impairment, anxiety, health problems.
- Short-term treatment with sleeping pills; long-term use can interfere with sleep.

### 6.6.2 Narcolepsy
- Sudden, uncontrollable sleep episodes.
- Episodes last from 1 minute to 30 minutes; individuals feel refreshed upon awakening.
- Can hinder job performance and pose risks (e.g., driving).
- Narcoleptics enter REM sleep immediately.
- Treatment may include counseling.

### 6.6.3 Catalepsy
- Muscle paralysis akin to REM sleep.
- Person is awake but unable to move; paralysis ceases when the person is touched.
  
### 6.6.4 Sleep Apnoea
- Periods of halted breathing during sleep, leading to abrupt awakening.
- Typically accompany loud snoring; more prevalent in men.
- Common among older adults, postmenopausal women, and the obese.
- Associated with increased fatigue and exacerbation of cardiovascular diseases (Sánchez-de-la-Torre et al., 2012).

### 6.6.5 Somnambulism (Sleepwalking)
- Occurs during NREM stages 3 and 4.
- Individual wanders during sleep, typically within the first three hours; may not recall the event.
- Causes unknown; potential genetic predisposition.

### 6.6.6 Sleep-talking
- Involves speaking during sleep; responses may be inaccurate.
  
## 6.7 Hypnosis
- Systematic procedure inducing heightened suggestibility (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
- Used therapeutically to alter thoughts and perceptions; can facilitate retrieval of information from memory.
- Distinction between therapeutic use and entertainment hypnosis is important.
- Generally, individuals retain clear memories and control during hypnosis.
- Four steps to induce suggestibility (National Research Council, 1994):
  - Focus on one object.
  - Comfort and relaxation.
  - Openness to hypnosis.
  - Encourage imagination.
- Variation in susceptibility; most individuals are moderately hypnotizable (Kihlstrom, 2013).
- Effective for pain management, depression, anxiety, smoking cessation, weight loss (Alladin, 2012; Elkins et al., 2012).

## 6.8 Substance Use Disorders
- Dependency and drug use are classified under DSM-5.
- Compulsive drug use despite adverse consequences; characterized by physical and psychological dependence.
- Drug tolerance leads to increased dosage and potential overdose.

### 6.8.1 Psychoactive Drugs
- Interaction with neurotransmitter systems; can act as agonists or antagonists.
  
- **Depressants**: 
  - Suppress CNS activity (e.g., barbiturates, benzodiazepines).
  - Alcohol as an agonist of GABA; effects include euphoria, sedation, impaired reaction time.
  - Risks of physical dependence and withdrawal; potential for FAS in pregnant women.
  
- **Stimulants**: 
  - Stimulate CNS; commonly agonists of dopamine.
  - Examples include caffeine, cocaine, amphetamines, nicotine.

- **Hallucinogens**: 
  - Alter sensory experiences; include marijuana and LSD.
  
- **Inhalants**: 
  - Chemicals inhaled for a high (e.g., household cleaners, glue).

- **Opioids**: 
  - Include heroin, morphine, methadone; analgesic properties.
  - High potential for abuse with intense euphoria and addiction risks.

### 7.6.1 Freudian Structures of Personality  
- **Id**: Primitive, pleasure-seeking; operates on the pleasure principle.

- **Superego**: Moral component; imposes societal standards and values; can lead to guilt if overly critical.

- **Neurosis**: May occur from imbalances among id, ego, and superego, causing anxiety or negative emotions.
- **Defence Mechanisms**: Unconscious behaviours to protect against negative feelings (see Figure 7.5).

### Psychosexual Stages of Development  
- **Stage 1: Oral (0-1 year)**  
  - Focus: Mouth; conflict over feeding/weaning.  
  - Fixation leads to dependency, addiction, etc.  
- **Stage 2: Anal (2-3 years)**  
  - Focus: Anus; conflict over toilet training.  
  - Fixation can result in control issues, orderliness, or messiness.  
- **Stage 3: Phallic (3-6 years)**  
  - Focus: Genitals; develop Oedipus or Electra complexes.  
  - Resolution is crucial for healthy identification.  
- **Stage 4: Latency (6 years-puberty)**  
  - Sexuality suppressed; focus on skills and social contacts.  
- **Stage 5: Genital (puberty onward)**  
  - Focus on mature relationships; influenced by earlier conflicts.

## 8.1 Introduction
- Human intelligence concepts debated historically in psychology.
- Key qualities associated with intelligence include memory, quick thinking, and knowledge acquisition.

## 8.2 Defining Intelligence and Creativity
- **Intelligence Definition**: Global capacity to act purposefully, think rationally, and deal effectively with the environment (Weschler, 1955).
- **Creativity Definition**: Ability to think in novel ways and produce unconventional solutions (Santrock, 2003).

## 8.3 The Nature of Intelligence
- **Alfred Binet**: Developed Binet-Simon test; Terman adapted it to create Stanford-Binet test (Terman, 1916).
  - Used normal distribution for scoring intelligence.
- **Francis Galton**: Studied psychological measurement; nature vs nurture debate; highlighted heritability in intelligence.
- **David Wechsler**: Created WAIS to measure multiple intellectual abilities rather than a single general intelligence (Wechsler, 1955).

## 8.4 Influences on Intelligence

### 8.4.1 Genetic Influences on Intelligence
- Brain volume correlates with intelligence; specific brain areas linked to IQ (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
- Genetic factors contribute to IQ similarity among family members (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
- **Heritability Concept**: Variability in group intelligence attributable to genetics (Santrock, 2003).
- Twin studies: Identical twins show higher IQ similarity compared to fraternal twins (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).

### 8.4.2 Environmental Influences on Intelligence
- Adoption studies demonstrate environmental impact on IQ (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
- Studies indicate children raised by the same mothers show similar IQs regardless of genetic relationship (Weinberg, 1989).
- Socioeconomic status influences intellectual stimulation opportunities for children (Santrock, 2003).
  
### 8.4.3 Cultural Influences on Intelligence
- Culture shapes definitions of intelligence; e.g., Western vs. African perspectives (Santrock, 2003).
- Challenges with standardized intelligence tests across different cultures; development of culture-fair tests (Coon & Mitterer, 2012).
- Intelligence is context-specific; varies with cultural values and practices (Nisbett, 2003).

## 8.5 Standardization and the Intelligence Quotient (IQ)
- Intelligence tests aim to measure general intelligence (g) accurately.
- Tests must demonstrate reliability (consistency) and validity (accurately measure intelligence).
- **Standardization**: Testing diverse age groups to compute average scores; essential for norm creation.
- **Flynn Effect**: Observations of increased global intelligence test scores over time (Flynn, 1999). 
- **IQ Formula**: IQ = (mental age ÷ chronological age) × 100.
- Modern IQ tests evaluate relative performance against peers; used in personnel selection in organizations (Schmidt & Hunter).

## 8.6 Types of Intelligence
- Various scholars contribute to understanding of intelligence.
  
### 8.6.1 Crystallised and Fluid Intelligence
- **Crystallised Intelligence**: 
  - Specific learned knowledge (e.g., vocabulary).
  - Problem-solving skills.
  - Remains stable throughout adulthood (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
  
- **Fluid Intelligence**: 
  - Logical thinking without previously learned knowledge.
  - Involves memory capacity and information processing speed.
  - Peaks in young adulthood, then declines (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).

### 8.6.2 Social and Emotional Intelligence
- **Social Intelligence**: Ability to manage interpersonal relationships.
- **Emotional Intelligence (EQ)**: 
  - Monitor own and others' emotions.
  - Use emotions to guide thinking and actions (Santrock, 2003).
  - Goleman (1995) identifies aspects: emotional awareness, managing emotions, reading emotions, handling relationships.

## 8.7 Theoretical Models of Intelligence

### 8.7.1 Spearman’s General (g) Factor Theory
- **Spearman (1904)**: Intelligence is a general ability underlying multiple skills.
- Developed **factor analysis** to identify common factors among intelligence tests.
- Recognized specific (s) and group factors influencing test performance (Bernstein et al., 2012).

### 8.7.2 Sternberg’s Triarchic Theory
- **Three components**:
  - **Analytical Intelligence**: Information processing, abstract reasoning; measured by IQ tests (Santrock, 2003).
  - **Creative Intelligence**: Ability to generate new ideas and solve novel problems (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
  - **Practical Intelligence**: Everyday problem-solving abilities (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).

### 8.7.3 Gardner’s Theory of Multiple Intelligences
- **Eight types** identified, including:
  - **Verbal-linguistic, Logical-mathematical, Visual-spatial, Musical-rhythm, Bodily-kinesthetic, Interpersonal, Intrapersonal, Naturalistic** (Gardner, 1999).
- Each type can be damaged by brain injury; unique cognitive skills.

### 8.7.4 Thurston’s Multiple Factors of Intelligence
- **Seven primary mental abilities**: 
  - Verbal comprehension, General reasoning, Word fluency, Memory, Number ability, Spatial ability, Perceptual speed (Thurston, 1947).

### 8.7.5 Carroll's Model of Intelligence
- **Three levels (strata) of intelligence**:
  - **Stratum III**: general intelligence (g).
  - **Stratum II**: broader categories (e.g., fluid intelligence, processing speed).
  - **Stratum I**: specific components (e.g., visual memory) (Carroll, 1993).

## Unit 9: Emotions

### 9.1 Introduction
- Emotions combine physiological sensations and subjective feelings.
- Non-verbal communication (e.g., expressions, body language) predates spoken language (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).

### 9.2 Defining Emotion
- Emotion = intense, subjective feeling with physiological arousal.
- Mood = prolonged, less intense affective state with no specific cause (Santrock, 2003).

### 9.3 Theories of Emotions
- Emotions involve physiological arousal, psychological appraisal, and subjective experience.
- **Cultural factors** influence emotional experiences.

#### 9.3.1 James-Lange Theory
- Emotions result from physiological arousal (e.g., heart racing from fear).

#### 9.3.2 Cannon-Bard Theory
- Physiological arousal and emotional experience occur simultaneously but independently (Lang, 1994).
- Research shows emotions can occur without distinct arousal.

#### 9.3.3 Two-Factor Theory
- Emotions = physiological arousal + cognitive appraisal (Schachter & Singer, 1962).
- Context determines emotional interpretation.

#### 9.3.4 Cognitive-Mediational Theory
- Emotions arise from cognitive appraisal of stimuli (Lazarus, 1991).
- Zajonc & LeDoux suggest different pathways for emotional processing.
  - Zajonc: Some emotions precede cognitive interpretation (e.g., startled by loud sound).
  - LeDoux: Fear processed with minimal cognition (Cunha et al., 2010; LeDoux, 1996).
