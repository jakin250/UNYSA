## Chapter 1: HIV and Aids: A Brief History

### 1.1 Nobel Prize Discovery
- Nobel Prize awarded in Physiology and Medicine 2008.
- Luc Montagnier and Françoise Barré-Sinoussi (HIV discovery).
- Harald zur Hausen (HPV vaccine).
- Controversy surrounding Robert Gallo's absence in the prize discussion.

### 1.2 The Origin of HIV
- Questions about Aids origin: historical presence or recent emergence?
- **Schoub (1999: 13)** indicates limited knowledge about human virus origins.
- Speculations included:
  - Aids present in central Africa for centuries, undiagnosed.
  - Polio vaccine linked to Aids causation.
  - Anti-malaria medication as a potential cause.
- Conclusion: HIV likely crossed species barrier from primates (simians).

#### 1.2.1 Crossing the Species Barrier
- HIV related to SIV (simian immunodeficiency virus).
- Possible multiple SIV strains transmitted at different times to humans:
  - **HIV Type 1**: Dominated by group M; divided into subtypes A-K.
  - **HIV-1 subtype C** prevalent in southern Africa.
  - **HIV Type 2**: Limited to West Africa; less pathogenic than HIV-1.
  - Origin researches implicated chimpanzees around the 1930s (Williamson & Martin, 2010).

#### 1.2.2 Urban Legends
- Urban legends reflect societal fears about HIV’s origin.
- Conspiracy theories assert blame on various entities.
- Importance for educators: countering misconceptions with scientific facts.

### 1.3 The Global Aids Epidemic
- Aids remains a critical health issue.
- Reduction in new HIV infections from 3.5 million (1996) to approximately 2 million annually (2016).
- Sub-Saharan Africa significantly affected; women and children are disproportionately impacted.

#### 1.3.1 Incidence and Prevalence of HIV Infection
- Differentiation:
  - **HIV incidence**: new infection cases in a time period; expressed as a percentage.
    - Example: 3.6% incidence if 1 million new cases in 27.8 million uninfected.
  - **HIV prevalence**: proportion of HIV-infected individuals at a specific time.
    - Example: 10.2% prevalence if 5.51 million out of 54 million population.

##### 1.3.1.1 HIV Incidence
- Difficult to measure; often obtained via statistical modelling.
- Longitudinal studies required to determine new infections.

##### 1.3.1.2 HIV Prevalence
- Measured via: antenatal prevalence surveys, community surveys, key population studies.
- Includes anonymous blood testing during antenatal checks.

#### 1.3.2 Measuring HIV Prevalence
- National estimates derived from various surveys.
- Antenatal surveys since 1990 for trend assessment.
- National surveys provide demographic insights.

### 1.4 The World’s Response to the Aids Epidemic
- Initial responses marked by denial and stigma.
- Mischaracterization of Aids as a "gay disease."
- Division between "innocent victims" (e.g., haemophiliacs) and those perceived as responsible for spreading the disease.

## 1.4  South Africa’s response to the Aids epidemic  
### 1.4.1  Inaction and denialism  
- Initial response marked by government inaction, denialism; Aids seen as a ‘gay’ disease.
- 1985: First Aids Advisory Group established; ineffective without government support.
- 1993: National Aids Convention of South Africa (NACOSA) formed; unification of government and activists.
- 1994: ANC presidency (Nelson Mandela) brought hope but faced numerous challenges including TB co-infection.
- Notable scandals: Sarafina II (1996), Virodene (1997) undermined credibility.
- 1998: Government refusal to provide AZT to pregnant women post-USA approval; case highlighted increasing HIV in pregnant women (4.3% to 17% 1994-1998).
- 1998: Treatment Action Campaign (TAC) led by Zackie Achmat formed for treatment rights; nominated for Nobel Prize 2004.

### 1.4.2  Our darkest days  
- Mbeki era (1999-2008): characterized by denialism and refusal of ARVs for pregnant women.
- 2002: South African High Court ordered provision of nevirapine after TAC activism; HIV prevalence in pregnant women at 24.5%.
- 2003: Antiretroviral therapy made available for free; criticized as ‘too little too late’ (330,000 lives potentially lost).
- Continued embarrassment due to support of untested remedies (vitamin supplements, etc.) by health minister.
- 2006: Minister's promotion of alternative remedies at the International Aids Conference escalated controversies.
- Changes initiated post-2006 with Deputy Minister Madlala-Routledge; dismissal by Mbeki in 2007 amidst crises.

### 1.4.3  A new beginning?  
- Jacob Zuma's election raised concerns but eventually positive responses emerged.
- Commitment to five-year National Strategic Plan for Aids and TB.
- 2009: Announced interventions on World Aids Day; aimed to improve access to ARVs for vulnerable groups.
- 2010: New clinical guidelines implemented for HIV management; major HIV testing campaign launched (18 million tested).
- 2015: Shift to treat HIV-infected at higher CD4+ counts; lifelong treatment for pregnant women initiated.
- 2016: Removal of CD4+ count requirement for ARV treatment announced; expansion of PrEP availability.

## 1.4.4  Positive worldwide responses  
- HIV has prompted significant research and advancements in treatment and prevention.
- 1994: AZT recommended for pregnant women in the USA, reduced transmission to infants.
- 1995: Introduction of combination antiretroviral treatment (HAART).
- 2001: WHO/UNAIDS’ '3 by 5' initiative aimed at treating 3 million people in low- and middle-income countries.
- 2013: WHO guidelines for earlier ARV treatment adoption; escalating treatment access from 7% (2003) to 42% (2008).
- Ongoing vaccine research; 2016: Experimental vaccine study began in South Africa.

## HIV and the immune system

### The immune system
- **Defenses against pathogens**: The immune system protects the body from pathogens through:
  - **First line of defence**: Physical barriers (e.g., skin).
  - **Second line of defence**: Innate immune response through inflammation and phagocytosis.
  - **Third line of defence**: Adaptive immune system with specific responses.

- **Functions of the immune system**: Detects pathogens, kills them, and adapts for future protection.

- **When the immune system fails**: Compromised immune responses can lead to infections. 

### The human immunodeficiency virus (HIV)
- **General viruses**: Pathogens that replicate within host cells.
- **Specificity of viruses**: Viruses target specific cells; HIV targets CD4+ T cells.
- **Uniqueness of HIV**: High mutation rates due to errors in replication; leads to variability and different subtypes (e.g., clades).

### 2.1 The immune system
#### 2.1.1 The first line of defence
- **Physical barriers**: Skin and mucous membranes prevent pathogen entry.
- **Non-specific defences**: Act against all pathogens indiscriminately.

#### 2.1.2 The second line of defence
- **Innate immune response**: 
  - Inflammatory response: Redness, pain, swelling, heat signals the immune system.
  - Phagocytes: Engulf and digest pathogens.
  - Example cells: Macrophages, neutrophils, eosinophils, natural killer cells.

- **Plasma proteins**: Complement proteins assist in destroying pathogens by forming pores and marking them for phagocytosis.

#### 2.1.3 The third line of defence
- **Adaptive immune system**: Highly specialized and specific immune responses developed upon exposure to pathogens.
- **Phases of immune response**:
  1. **Recognition and warning**: Distinguishing between self and non-self.
  2. **Mobilisation and battle**: Activation of specific lymphocytes and antibodies.
  3. **Demobilisation**: Return to homeostasis post-infection.
  4. **Active and passive immunity**: Long-term immunity via memory cells and short-term immunity through antibodies from other sources.

## 2.1. Cellular Biology and the Immune System

### 2.1.1 Overview of Biological Cells
- **Outer Membrane:** Controls fluid/nutrient exchange.
- **Organelles:** Each cell contains functional structures.
- **Genome:** Genetic material, primarily double-stranded DNA, serves as a building plan.
  
### 2.1.2 Immune System Recognition
- **Unique Genetic Codes:** Pathogen identification through distinctive proteins (antigens).
- **Antigens:** Unique to each species and signal foreign invaders.
  
### 2.1.3 Immune Response Phases
#### 2.1.3.1 Phase 1: Recognition
- **Known vs. Unknown Pathogens:** Known pathogens trigger a swift immune response via memory cells.
- **Memory Cells:** T and B cells store history of antigens for quick reactions.

#### 2.1.3.2 Phase 2: Mobilisation and Battle
- **Lymphocytes:** Key white blood cells: T (thymus) and B (bone marrow).
  - **CD4+ T Cells:** Regulatory, stimulate immune response.
  - **CD8+ T Cells:** Cytotoxic, kill infected cells via cytotoxins.
- **Antigen-Presenting Cells (APCs):** Dendritic cells and macrophages present epitopes to T cells.

#### 2.1.3.3 Phase 3: Demobilisation
- **Suppressor T Cells:** Instruct CD4+ T and CD8+ T cells to cease actions after infection is eradicated.

#### 2.1.3.4 Phase 4: Active and Passive Immunity
- **Active Immunity:** Long-term immunity from memory T and B cells post-infection; generated via vaccination.
  
- **Passive Immunity:** Short-term immunity transferred from mother to infant (IgG across placenta, IgA in colostrum); injectable antibody-rich serum for adults.

- **Phases of Immune Response:** Summary is illustrated in **Figure 2.5**.
- **Protection Mechanisms:** Antibodies prevent infections by neutralizing pathogens and recruiting immune cells.

### 2.1.5 When the Immune System Fails
- **Normal Functionality**: The immune system typically addresses infections; severe pathogens (e.g., rabies) can bypass this.
- **Weakened Immunity**: Aging, stress, poor diet, or inherited factors can impair immunity, making individuals vulnerable to non-lethal infections.
- **Acquired Deficiency**: HIV, being an acquired condition, attacks immune cells, notably CD4+ T cells, leading to immunodeficiency.
- **Medication**: No cure for HIV; ARVs delay replication, assisting immune recovery.

### 2.2 The Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)

#### 2.2.1 Viruses in General
- **Pathogen Classification**: Includes bacteria, viruses, protozoa, and fungi; viruses require host cells for replication.
- **Mechanism**: Viruses hijack host cell machinery to replicate, using integration of genetic material into host DNA.

#### 2.2.2 The Specificity of Viruses
- **Infection Criteria**: Viruses can only infect cells with matching receptor types (e.g., HIV targets CD4+ T cells).
- **Antibody Production**: The immune system uses virus-specific antigens to generate antibodies targeting viral proteins.

#### 2.2.3 The Uniqueness of HIV
- **Retrovirus Characteristics**: Circular structure (0.0001 mm), contains RNA and enzymes (reverse transcriptase, protease, integrase).
- **Core Proteins**: p24 crucial for diagnosis; infections lead to direct attack on CD4+ T cells.
- **Implication**: Reduction in CD4+ T cells leads to impaired immune response and opportunistic infections.

#### 2.2.4 HIV Infection and Replication

##### 2.2.4.1 How HIV Enters the Body
- **Entry Mechanism**: HIV can infect through intact mucous membranes via Langerhans cells that transport the virus into the body.

##### 2.2.4.2 Steps of Infection and Replication
- **Step 1 – Attachment**: Gp120 attaches to CD4 receptors on T cells. Binding requires additional co-receptor (CCR5 or CXCR4) binding for entry.
- **Co-Receptors**: 
  - **CCR5**: Found on activated lymphocytes, macrophages, dendritic cells.
  - **CXCR4**: Present on resting T cells and monocytes.

### 2.2.5 Structure and Genetic Information of HIV
- **Gene Classification**:
  - **gag gene**: Inner core proteins (e.g., p24).
  - **env gene**: Envelope proteins responsible for host binding (e.g., gp120, gp41).
  - **pol gene**: Virus enzymes for replication (e.g., reverse transcriptase).
- **Regulatory Genes**: 
  - Positive (tat, rev, vif, vpu) and negative (nef) regulatory genes manage virus replication and protein synthesis.

### 2.2.4 Steps of HIV Infection
- **Step 1 – Attachment**: HIV (gp120) binds to CD4 receptors on CD4+ T cells.
- **Step 2 – Fusion**: Membranes of the virus and host cell merge.
- **Step 3 – Injection**: HIV injects its RNA and enzymes (reverse transcriptase, integrase, protease) into the CD4+ T cell.
- **Step 4 – Reverse Transcription**: Viral RNA is converted to proviral DNA using reverse transcriptase.
- **Step 5 – Integration of Genetic Material**: Proviral DNA integrates with host cell DNA, aided by integrase.
- **Step 6 – Replication of Genetic Material**: Utilizes host cell machinery to replicate viral RNA for new viruses.
- **Step 7 – Production of New Viruses**: Protease assembles viral RNA and proteins into new viruses, killing host cells and infecting others.

### 2.2.5 Variability and Subtypes of HIV
- **Genetic Diversity**: High mutation rates complicate immune recognition and vaccine development.
- **Mutation Causes**:
  - Errors during genetic copying (reverse transcriptase is error-prone).
  - Recombination between viral genomes (new recombinant viruses formed).
  
#### 2.2.5.1 HIV Groups and Subtypes
- **Group M**: Main group causing global HIV epidemic, subdivided into subtypes (A, B, C, D, F, G, H, J, K).
- **Subtype Distribution**:
  - Subtype C: Southern and Eastern Africa, Nepal, India.
  - Subtype A: West Africa.
  - Subtype D: Parts of Eastern and Central Africa.
  - Subtype B: Europe, North America, Australia, Thailand, Japan.

- **HIV-2**: Less pathogenic, mainly in West Africa; classified into nine groups (A to I) with only groups A and B epidemic.

### 2.2.6 Different Responses to HIV Infection
- **Median Survival Time**: 
  - 10.5 years for females, 11.5 years for males; shorter in Africa due to various factors.
- **Progression Rates**: 
  - Rapid progressors (HIV to AIDS in 2-3 years) vs. slow progressors (long-term disease-free).
  
### Conclusion
- Understanding HIV's impact on the immune system is crucial, yet complete eradication remains unachieved. Preventing transmission is key, highlighting the need for awareness of infection methods. The focus will shift to transmission dynamics in the next chapter.

## 3.1 Sexual Transmission of HIV Infection
- Primary transmission methods: unprotected vaginal/anal intercourse, and certain oral sexual acts.
- High risk in sub-Saharan Africa predominantly via heterosexual intercourse.
- HIV enters bloodstream through body fluids (semen, vaginal fluid, blood, breastmilk).
- **Cell Interaction**: Requires connection to CD4 receptors and co-receptors on macrophages, dendritic cells, and CD4+T cells.
- **Langerhans Cells**: Act as antigen-presenting cells that facilitate HIV transport to CD4+T cells.

### 3.1.1 Contributing Factors Influencing the Spread of HIV
#### 3.1.1.1 Gender
- Women/girls more susceptible to HIV (e.g., prevalence rates: 14.4% women vs. 9.9% men in 2012).
- Biological factors: prolonged exposure to male semen, larger mucosal exposure, STIs, and vaginal conditions.
- **Menstrual Cycle**: High risk during menstruation.
- **Cultural Practices**: ‘Dry sex’ exacerbates risk; women often lack knowledge of reproductive health.

#### 3.1.1.2 Age
- Vulnerability peaks in reproductive years (15-24 age group).
- **Age-disparate Relationships**: Increased risk due to relationships with older men contributing to higher infections in young women.

## Prevention of HIV
- Education on transmission is crucial.
- **Behavioural Interventions**: Address multiple risks through education and empowerment.
- **Biomedical Interventions**: Focus on medical solutions (e.g., antiretroviral therapy).
- **Structural Interventions**: Address socioeconomic conditions fueling transmission.

# HIV and Aids education, care and counselling: A multicultural approach

## 3.1 Transmission and Prevention

### 3.1.1 Factors Affecting HIV Transmission

#### 3.1.1.1 Age-Disparate Relationships
- Higher HIV prevalence among youth (15-24 years) in age-disparate sex (Shisana et al., 2014).
- Reasons for early sexual activity: low self-esteem, peer pressure, sexual coercion, financial exchange (transactional sex).
  
#### 3.1.1.2 Terminology
- 'Prostitution' outdated; preferred terms: **sex work**, **sex worker**, **transactional sex**.
- Refer to children involved as **commercial sexual exploitation of children** (UNAIDS, 2011: 2).

#### 3.1.1.3 Other Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs)
- Presence of STIs (e.g., syphilis, herpes) increases HIV susceptibility (average fourfold).
- STIs create breaches for HIV entry and attract immune cells (CD4 receptors).
- Importance of timely STI treatment when symptomatic.

#### 3.1.1.4 Socioeconomic and Cultural Conditions
- Socioeconomic factors contribute to vulnerability: high unemployment, low status of women, extreme poverty, limited access to health services, disempowerment.
- Issues needing address: community upliftment, women's empowerment, improved working conditions for HIV prevention.

### 3.2 Modes of HIV Transmission

#### 3.2.1 Contaminated Blood
- Transmission via blood transfusions, shared needles.
- **Window period**: time between HIV infection and detectable antibodies (potential false negatives in tests).

#### 3.2.2 Blood Transfusions and Blood Products
- 98-100% infection risk from transfusions of infected blood.
- Recommendations for blood safety (WHO, 2015):
  - National blood systems; low-risk voluntary donors; quality-assured screening.
  
#### 3.2.3 Injecting Drug Use
- High risk from sharing needles.
- Drug users often engage in sex work to fund habits, increasing transmission risk.

#### 3.2.4 Blood-Contaminated Needles and Instruments
- Risk through poor medical hygiene, accidental exposure, tattooing, piercing, or traditional practices.
- Education for practitioners on instrument hygiene is essential.

### 3.2.5 Occupational Exposure
- Risk of HIV from needlestick injuries or contact with infected blood is approximately **0.3%** (CDC).
- Transmission mainly occurs in healthcare settings from accidental exposure.

### Glossary
- **Self-esteem**: Personal value assessment.
- **Urbanisation**: Migration from rural to urban areas.
- **Viral Load**: Quantitative measure of virus detected in blood.
- **Adherence**: Compliance with medication regimen for viral suppression.

### Frequently Asked Questions
- **Single Exposure Risk**: Infection risk after one sexual encounter is under 20 per 100,000 contacts (Morris & Cilliers, 2010: 93).
- **Infectivity Phases**: Most infectious shortly after infection and during the AIDS phase.

**Post-exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)**
- Provides antiretroviral medication to healthcare workers after exposure to HIV-infected blood.
- Risk of transmission varies: 
  - Needlestick injury: 0.3%
  - Mucous membrane exposure: 0.1%
  - Skin exposure: <0.1%

**Infectious Body Fluids**
- Amniotic fluid, cerebrospinal fluid, peritoneal fluid, pericardial fluid, and synovial fluid are potentially infectious.

**Occupational Risks**
- **Hepatitis B (HBV)**: More infectious than HIV (transmission risk ranges from 6% to 30%).
- Vaccination against HBV is crucial for healthcare workers.

**Universal Precautions**
- Essential measures for anyone in contact with blood and body fluids to prevent HIV and other pathogens.

## Mother-to-Child Transmission (MTCT) of HIV

- Accounts for 90% to 95% of HIV infections in young children.
- MTCT can occur during pregnancy, delivery, or breastfeeding.
- Without precautionary measures, 25%-45% risk of transmission to infants.

**Risk Factors for Transmission**
- High maternal viral load, severe symptomatic HIV disease, recent maternal infection.

**Preventative Interventions**
- Antiretroviral therapy during pregnancy, elective Caesarean section, safe delivery, and safe infant feeding.

### 3.3  Pregnancy
- **Prevent New Infections**: Maintain low viral load.
- **ART Initiation**: Lifelong ART regardless of CD4+ counts (option B+).
- **Nutritional Support**: Provide iron, folate, and calcium supplements.
- **Regular Monitoring**: Frequent clinic visits to monitor mother's health.

### 3.3.1 Labour and Delivery
- High risk of MTCT during vaginal delivery.
- Use of ART during labor recommended.
- Avoid prolonged membrane rupture and invasive procedures.

### 3.3.2 Breastfeeding
- Presence of HIV-infected cells in breastmilk.
- Significant reduction in transmission risk with ART for mother or infant.
  
**Duration of Breastfeeding**
- Optimal duration: **6 months exclusive breastfeeding** followed by complementary feeding until 12 months.
  
**Feeding Guidelines**
- Avoid mixed feeding; it can increase MTCT risk.
- Discuss safe alternatives for replacement feeding if chosen.
  
### Conditions for Safe Replacement Feeding
- Access to safe water and sanitation.
- Reliable provision of sufficient formula milk.
- Ability to prepare formula safely.
- Exclusive formula feeding in first six months.

**Mixed Feeding Risks**
- Increases vulnerability to infections.

- **CDC Guidelines**: Referencing risk assessments and universal precautions.
- **WHO Recommendations**: Exclusive breastfeeding and feeding practices tailored for HIV-positive mothers.

## 3.5.2.1 Male Circumcision
- Protective effect against HIV acquisition in heterosexual men (Siegfried et al., 2009).
- Three trials (2002-2007, South Africa, Kenya, Uganda) show a 60% reduction of HIV infection risk (Auvert et al., 2005; Bailey et al., 2007; Gray et al., 2007).
- Voluntary medical male circumcision (VMMC) adopted as HIV prevention strategy in 14 sub-Saharan African countries (Wamai et al., 2015).
- Mathematical models indicate millions of HIV infections could be averted with widespread circumcision.

### Key Considerations
- **Not a cure**: Reduces, but does not eliminate HIV risk.
- Increased sexual partners (>25%) can negate benefits (Gray et al., 2007).
- Must be part of a comprehensive prevention package: safer sex counselling and education.
- Risks associated: surgical complications (bleeding/infection); education for providers is crucial.

### Methods and Devices for VMMC
- **Surgical Options**: Traditional circumcision requiring anaesthesia and trained professionals.
- **Non-surgical Methods**: Forceps Guided Method, Sleeve Resection Method, Tara Klamp, PrePex device (approved by WHO in 2013).
  
## 3.5.2.2 Microbicides
- **Definition**: Substances designed to kill microorganisms (e.g., bacteria, viruses).
- Microbicides can be applied vaginally or rectally to prevent HIV/STIs.
  
### Potential Mechanisms (Abdool Karim & Baxter, 2010)
- Support natural vaginal defenses.
- Kill or immobilize pathogens.
- Create barriers to pathogen entry.
- Prevent fusion and inhibit replication of viruses.

### Research Highlights
- **CAPRISA 004 trial**: Tenofovir gel reduced HIV infection rates by 6%-39% (Abdool Karim et al., 2015).
- **FACTS 001 study**: No differences in HIV infection rates among users and placebo; adherence issues noted for young women (Rees et al., 2015).
- **Dapivirine vaginal ring**: Effective for women over 25; studies showed a 27%-31% reduction in HIV infections.

### Limitations
- Microbicides may not provide 100% protection; condoms generally offer better protection.

## 3.5.2.3 Antiretroviral Treatment as Prevention (TasP)
- **Definition**: Use of ART to lower the risk of HIV transmission.
  
### Key Applications
- **Mother-to-child transmission**: ART reduces risk to ~98%.
- **Heterosexual discordant couples**: HPTN 052 study showed 96% reduction in transmission risk through ART (Monater, 2011).
- **Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP & nPEP)**: Short-term ART after potential exposure to HIV.

### Pre-exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)
- **Definition**: ARVs taken before exposure to prevent HIV (e.g., Truvada®).
- Efficacy: 44%-73% reduction in HIV incidence among MSM with high adherence.

### Treatment as Prevention (Enrichment)
- High viral load correlates with higher transmission risk; treating all HIV-positive individuals reduces community transmission.

## 3.5.2.4 HIV Vaccines
- Essential for eradicating HIV; vaccines build immunity against infections.

### Vaccine Development
- **Mechanism**: Uses harmless substances to invoke immune response.
- Historical successes: Jenner's smallpox vaccine, Pasteur's vaccines for cholera, anthrax, rabies.
  
### Strategies for Vaccine Development (Kahn, 2005)
- **Live attenuated pathogens**: Weakened live pathogens.
- Other unspecified strategies continue to evolve alongside ongoing research. 

### Conclusion
- Global efforts focus on multi-faceted prevention strategies comprising circumcision, microbicides, ART, and vaccine research.

### 4.2.2 Clinical Stage 1: Asymptomatic
- Majority show no symptoms; unaware of HIV infection.

- Mild symptoms begin; CD4+T cell count: 350-499 cells/mm³.

  - Moderate unexplained weight loss (<10% body weight).

  - **Angular Cheilitis** (fungal cracks in lips).
  - **Oral Ulcers** (recurrent).
  - **Papular Pruritic Eruptions (PPE)**.
  - **Seborrhoeic Dermatitis**.
  - Chronic itchy skin (**Prurigo**).
  - Fungal nail infections.

- Signs of severe HIV-related diseases as immune system deteriorates; CD4+T cell count: 200-349 cells/mm³.
- Symptoms:
  - Severe unexplained weight loss (>10% body weight).
  - Chronic diarrhoea >1 month.
  - Persistent fever/night sweats >1 month, unresponsive to treatment.

  - **Oral Hairy Leukoplakia**.

## 4.4.2.3 Tuberculosis Prophylaxis (Isoniazid Preventive Therapy or IPT)
- Tuberculosis (TB) is a critical opportunistic infection in HIV-positive individuals.
- IPT reduces TB risk by 33% in HIV-infected individuals, with a 64% reduction in those TST positive (Date et al., 2010).
- **WHO Recommendation**: Offer IPT to HIV-infected individuals only if TST positive; TST must be performed within one month if not done prior to IPT initiation. 
- Negative TST results suggest IPT should cease unless the individual is on ARVs (continue for 12 months), with annual reassessment of TST status.
- **CD4+ T cell counts are no longer used** to initiate TB preventive therapy.

### Glossary
- **Active TB**: Symptomatic TB; infectious.
- **Latent TB**: Dormant TB, non-symptomatic, non-infectious.

### Eligibility for IPT
- Refer to ‘National Consolidated Guidelines’ for eligibility criteria.

### Frequently Asked Questions
- **TST Explanation**: Tuberculin test for TB exposure determination; positive test indicates possible TB exposure, not active disease. 
- **Positive TST Implications**: Indicates exposure to TB bacilli.
- **Negative TST Implications**: Indicates no prior TB exposure but does not rule out TB; false negatives can occur due to various conditions.

### Isoniazid Preventive Therapy (IPT) Considerations:
- IPT **NOT** for active TB as it encourages drug resistance.
- Screening for TB in all HIV patients is essential during healthcare visits.
- Symptom-based screening is recommended.

### TB Symptoms for Screening (Refer to Table 4.3):
- Adults: Current cough, persistent fever, unexplained weight loss, fatigue, drenching night sweats.
- Children: Current cough, persistent fever, fatigue, poor weight gain.

### Recommended IPT Regimen:
- **Adults**: Isoniazid (INH) 5 mg/kg/day (max 300 mg/day), plus Pyridoxine 25 mg/day.
- **Children**: Isoniazid 10 mg/kg/day (dissolved), Pyridoxine per age/malnutrition status.
- **Secondary Prevention**: IPT for adults post-bacteriological cure of previous TB.

### Hepatitis Concern:
- Discontinue IPT if nausea, vomiting, or jaundice develops.

### 4.5 Tuberculosis (TB) 

#### 4.5.3 Symptoms of TB
- Main symptoms of pulmonary TB (see Table 4.3):
  - Persistent cough (>2 weeks; any duration in HIV-infected individuals).
  - Fever (>2 weeks).
  - Unexplained weight loss (>1.5 kg/month).
  - Drenching night sweats.
- Screening: Each patient should be screened at every contact.
- Additional symptoms:
  - Sputum production (may be blood-stained).
  - Loss of appetite, fatigue.
  - Shortness of breath, chest pains.
  - Palpable lymphadenopathy.
- Pediatric features indicating TB to investigate:
  - 3 or more TB symptoms.
  - Physical signs of TB.
  - Positive Tuberculin Skin Test (TST).
  - Chest X-ray findings suggestive of TB.

#### 4.5.4 Diagnostic Tests for TB
- Commonly used tests in sub-Saharan Africa:
  - **Smear Microscopy:**   
    - Sputum examination for acid-fast bacilli (AFB).
    - Staining methods: Ziehl-Neelsen or fluorescent auramine.
    - Results in ~48 hours; low sensitivity, especially in HIV+ individuals and children.
  - **Culture Methods:**
    - More sensitive than smear; detects higher TB case proportions.
    - Involves sputum inoculation on culture medium; results in ~4-6 weeks.
  - **Molecular Testing:**
    - **GeneXpert (GXP):** Rapid TB diagnosis and Rifampicin resistance screening.
    - **Line Probe Assay:** Confirms drug resistance.
    - WHO recommends GeneXpert as first-line test.
  - **Tuberculin Skin Test (TST):**
    - Identifies previous infection; positive does not confirm active TB.
    - Not reliable in high TB prevalence areas (false positives common).

#### 4.5.5 Treatment of TB
- Curable with complete antibiotic treatment.
- **Standard Drugs:**
  - Isoniazid (H), Rifampicin (R), Pyrazinamide (Z), Ethambutol (E), Streptomycin (S).
- **Treatment Protocol:**
  - **Intensive Phase:** 2 months (RHZE).
  - **Continuation Phase:** 4 months (RH).
- **Extra-pulmonary TB:** Treated similarly; may extend duration to 9 months if complicated.

#### 4.5.6 TB and HIV
- HIV+ individuals at higher risk of TB.
- TB can accelerate HIV progression.
- False negatives common in HIV patients, complicating diagnosis.
- TB treatment same for HIV+ and HIV-; all require antiretroviral therapy.

#### 4.5.7 Side Effects of TB Medicines
- Monitor for:
  - Numbness, tingling, joint pains, nausea, abdominal pain, skin rash, changes in urine color, vision impairment, yellowing of eyes, confusion.
- Major side effects require immediate hospital referral.

#### 4.5.8 Adherence to Treatment
- Essential for successful TB treatment:
  - Non-adherence leads to prolonged illness, community transmission, drug resistance, and death.
- Implement Directly Observed Treatment (DOT) to enhance adherence:
  - Supporters ensure completion of treatment and monitor side effects.

## 4.5.9 Drug-resistant Tuberculosis
- Development of resistance in **M. tuberculosis** leads to inability to use standard drugs.
- Human error contributes to **multidrug-resistant tuberculosis (MDR-TB)**: non-adherence, management issues, etc.
- Categories of drug-resistant TB:
  - **Rifampicin resistant TB (RR-TB)**: Resistance to rifampicin, potentially mono- or multi-drug resistant.
  - **MDR-TB**: Resistance to both rifampicin and isoniazid.
  - **Extensive drug-resistant TB (XDR-TB)**: Resistance to fluoroquinolones and one of three injectable second-line drugs.
  - **Mono-resistant TB**: Resistance to one first-line medicine.
  - **Polydrug-resistant TB**: Resistance to more than one first-line drug, excluding rifampicin and isoniazid.
- Utilization of **Xpert MTB/RIF** for expedited diagnosis of RR-TB and MDR-TB.
- Patients with MDR-TB require referral to specialized treatment and hospitalization until non-infectious.
- **XDR-TB**: High mortality (>90%); prevention crucial due to treatment difficulty.

## 4.6 Sexually Transmitted Infections
- STIs significantly relate to HIV risk; high rates prevalent in Southern Africa.
- Patients with STIs, particularly severe forms, are highly susceptible to HIV.
- Early STI diagnosis and treatment are vital to prevent complications and transmission.

### 4.6.1 Diagnostic vs. Syndromic Management of STIs
- **Diagnostic Approach**: Ideal but impractical in resource-limited settings due to lack of laboratory support.
- **Syndromic Approach**: Recognizes symptoms to prescribe treatment (advantage: timely care; disadvantage: potential overtreatment).

  - **Bacterial Vaginosis**: Grey, sticky discharge; often fishy odor.

- Caused by infections spreading to pelvic organs; characterized by severe pain, bad-smelling discharge, fever.

### 4.6.4 Male Urethritis Syndrome (MUS)
- Most common causes: **Gonorrhoea** and **Chlamydia**.
- Symptoms include urethral discharge, dysuria, and potential complications from rectal and pharyngeal infections.

### 4.6.5 Scrotal Swelling (SSW)
- Often indicates **epididymo-orchitis**; treated with antibiotics and supportive measures.

### 4.6.6 Genital Ulcer Syndrome (GUS)
- Caused by syphilis, genital herpes, and chancroid.

- **LAP**: Antibiotics for gonorrhoea and chlamydia; HIV testing emphasized.
- **MUS**: Treatment for gonorrhoea; partner treatment essential.
- **SSW**: Antibiotic treatment for underlying STIs.
- **GUS**: Combination antibiotics plus acyclovir for herpes.

## 5.2 HIV-antibody tests
- **Testing mediums**: Blood (whole blood, serum, plasma), saliva, urine.
- **Key tests**:
  - **Rapid HIV-antibody test**
  - **ELISA (EIA)**
  - **Western Blot** (used mainly as confirmatory).

- **Function**: Tests detect anti-HIV antibodies, not the virus. Positive results occur post-seroconversion.

### 5.2.1 The ELISA antibody test
- **Characteristics**:
  - Common, inexpensive, reliable, high sensitivity.
  - Detects HIV-1 and HIV-2 antibodies.
- **Window period**: 
  - First-gen: 45 days
  - Third-gen: 22 days
  - Fourth-gen: 16 days
- **Confirmation**: Positive results require a second test; repeat negative tests 3-4 weeks post-exposure if HIV exposure is suspected.

### 5.2.2 Rapid HIV-antibody tests
- **Usage**: Conducted outside laboratories; results in ~10 minutes.
- **Fluid types**: Whole blood, serum, oral fluid.
- **Advantages**: Less invasive, affordable, stable at various temperatures. 
- **Confirmation**: Positive results should be confirmed with a different manufacturer's test.

#### 5.2.2.1 Home test
- **Caution**: Requires proper counselling pre- and post-test; results may be unreliable without adherence to instructions or if improperly stored.

#### 5.2.2.2 Saliva test
- **Advantages**:
  - Easy to use, non-invasive, painless.
  - Collects saliva using absorbent pads.
- **Usage**: Requires conventional ELISA testing for confirmation of positive results.

### 5.2.3 The Western Blot antibody test
- **Use**: Confirmatory test, not for screening.
- **Cost**: More expensive than ELISA, less availability.
- **Confirmatory alternatives**: Combination of ELISA and rapid tests.

---

## 5.3 HIV virus tests
- **Function**: Detect actual HIV particles in blood.
- **Types**:
  - Detection of viral antigens (e.g., p24).
  - Detection of viral nucleic acids (RNA or DNA).
  - Virus culture (rarely used for diagnosis).

### 5.3.1 The HIV p24 antigen test
- **Detection**: Detects p24 antigen within 16 days post-infection.
- **Useful for**: Early detection in specific clinical settings (e.g., newborns).

#### 5.3.1.1 The dried blood spot (DBS) test
- **Method**: Blood blotted on filter paper from newborn's heel.
- **Advantages**: Inexpensive, low invasiveness, easier logistics and storage, longer lifespan.

### 5.3.2 Proviral DNA detection
- **Testing method**: Qualitative PCR detects proviral DNA.
- **Applications**:
  - Early diagnosis (post-exposure prophylaxis, vertical transmission screening).
  - Estimate window period: ~16 days.

### 5.3.3 Viral RNA detection
- **Testing method**: Quantitative PCR (RNA PCR).
- **Purpose**: Mainly for monitoring after diagnosis; indicative of viral load and treatment efficacy.
- **Timing**: Can identify infection within five days post-exposure.

### 5.4 HIV Counselling and Testing Algorithms  
- National Department of Health's recommended HIV testing algorithms for:  
  - Adolescents and adults (including pregnant and breastfeeding women)  
  - Children younger than 18 months  
  - Children older than 18 months  

#### 5.4.1 HIV Counselling and Testing for Adolescents and Adults  
- **Group Information Session**: Outline HCT benefits; address benefits for pregnant women.  
- **Individual Information Session**: Clarify questions and offer HIV test.  
- **Testing Procedure**: Obtain consent, rapid HIV-antibody test via finger prick.  
- **Negative Test Results**: Considered uninfected; provide post-test counselling; encourage repeat testing after three months.  
- **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding**:  
  - Negative women enter PMTCT programme and receive routine repeat testing and TB screening.  
  - Pre-consultation: Recognising TB symptoms and safe feeding.  
- **Repeat Testing for General Population**: Every 6-12 months based on risk behavior.  
- **Positive Test Results**:  
  - Conduct confirmatory rapid test.  
  - Provide results and post-test counselling in client's preferred language.  
  - Immediate TB screening and necessary tests (CD4, clinical staging).  
  - Pregnant/breastfeeding women to start ART immediately if positive.  
- **Indeterminate/Discordant Results**: Blood for ELISA test, results to be returned in five days.  
- **Refusal of Testing**: Offer post-refusal counselling and continuous non-coercive testing offers.  

#### 5.4.2 HIV Counselling and Testing for Children Younger Than 18 Months  
- **Check HIV Status**: Every health visit. Mother is HIV negative, repeat testing every three months.  
- **Mother HIV Positive**:  
  - Baby tested with HIV PCR at birth, 10 weeks, or 18 weeks.

- **Negative PCR**: Test repeated if breastfeeding persists, confirmatory rapid test at 18 months for HIV-exposed infants.

- **Check Exposure Status**: Every health visit; repeat testing for breastfeeding mothers every three months.  
- **If Mother HIV Uninfected**: No test needed unless symptomatic.  
- **If Mother HIV Positive**:

  - Submit HIV ELISA if confirmatory test is negative.  
- **Initiate ART**: For all HIV-positive children, irrespective of age or CD4 count.  
- **Additional Testing**: On parental request or in case of increased risk factors (death of HIV-positive family member, symptomatic presentation).

## 6.1 Clinical assessment
- Full clinical assessment post-HIV diagnosis critical.
- Regular check-ups (every six months if healthy; more often if symptomatic).
- Key assessments at each visit (Aurum Institute & CDC/FEPFAR, 2015: 5):
  - **WHO staging** (Table 4.1, p. 95)
  - **CD4+T cell count**
  - Screening for: 
    - TB symptoms
    - STIs
    - Pregnancy
    - Mental health
    - Comorbidities (hypertension, diabetes)
    - Pap smears (as needed)
  - Assess ART eligibility and monitor adherence.

- Management of health issues:
  - Prescribe IPT (if eligible)
  - Manage comorbidities
  - Family planning counseling
  - Nutritional guidance
  - Support for disclosure and partner notification.
  - Encourage return for health problems.

## 6.1.1 CD4+T cell count and viral load
- Importance of CD4+T cell counts:
  - Assess immune system status.
  - Indicate opportunistic infection treatment timing.
  - Guide ART initiation.
- Importance of viral load testing:
  - Assess infection severity.
  - Measure ART response.
  - Detect antiretroviral resistance.
- Aim for undetectable viral load after six months of treatment.

## 6.2 Goals of ART
1. **Virological goal**: Reduce HIV viral load to undetectable levels.
2. **Immunological goal**: Restore/improve immune function, reduce opportunistic infections.
3. **Therapeutic goal**: Improve quality of life for HIV-infected patients.
4. **Epidemiological goal**: Reduce morbidity/mortality and HIV transmission impact.

- ART administration leads to an increase in CD4+T cell counts, reducing morbidity and mortality (Southern African HIV Clinicians Society, 2012: 114).
- Main uses of ART:
  - Treatment of established HIV infections.
  - Prevention: PMTCT, post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP), pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP).

## 6.3 Classes of ART and their mechanisms of action
- HIV lifecycle interruption through antiretroviral drugs targeting viral enzymes:
  - **Reverse transcriptase** (converts RNA to DNA)
  - **Protease** (assembles new viral components)
  - **Integrase** (integrates viral DNA into host genome).
  
- Classes of ARVs:
  - NRTIs (Nucleotide Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors) and NtRTIs
  - NNRTIs (Non-Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors)
  - PIs (Protease Inhibitors)
  - Integrase inhibitors
  - Entry inhibitors.

## 6.3.1 Reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs and NNRTIs)
- Interrupt HIV lifecycle, blocking reverse transcriptase enzyme.
- NRTIs: mimic DNA building blocks.
- NNRTIs: inhibit reverse transcriptase directly.

## 6.3.2 Protease inhibitors (PIs)
- Inhibit protease enzyme, preventing new virus assembly and release.

## 6.3.3 Integrase inhibitors (InSTIs)
- Block HIV DNA integration into host cell nucleus, hindering replication.

## 6.3.4 Entry inhibitors
- Prevent HIV entry into host cells by blocking viral protein interactions with CD4+T cells and CCR5 co-receptors.

## 6.7 How to Know if ART is Effective
- **Viral Load Monitoring** (National Department of Health, 2015: 75):
  - VL <400 copies/ml: 
    - Monitor every 12 months.
    - Intensified adherence for VL >50 copies/ml.
  - VL 400 - 1,000 copies/ml: 
    - Assess/manage adherence.
    - Repeat VL in 6 months.
  - VL >1,000 copies/ml:
    - Assess adherence, intense support.
    - Repeat VL in 2 months.
    - Check HBV status/Hb; switch to second-line if VL remains >1,000 copies/ml after addressing adherence.

- **Glossary**:
  - **Virological Suppression**: Viral load reduced to undetectable levels (<50 copies/ml).
  
- **Important Note**: CD4+T cell count not a reliable indicator of ART effectiveness; adhere to viral load monitoring.

## 6.8 When to Change ART
- Change ART under these conditions:
  - Intolerance despite appropriate treatment.
  - Drug toxicity occurrence.
  - Virological failure (VL increase despite adherence).

- **Recommendations (SAHIV Clinician’s Society, 2014: 128)**:
  - Switch to second-line if VL >1,000 copies/ml after two measurements, preferably taken 2-3 months apart.
  - Expedite switching if CD4+T cell count <100 cells/mm3 or persistently low VL >200 copies/ml for over a year.

## 6.9 The Development of Drug-Resistant Viruses
- **Glossary**:
  - **Drug Resistance**: Occurs if ART is not taken as prescribed.

- Factors contributing to drug resistance:
  - High genetic variability of HIV.
  - Variants' survival and replication in the presence of ARVs.

- **Mechanism of Resistance**:
  - Mutations lead to fitness advantage over the wild-type virus in inadequate ART suppressing conditions.

- **Consequences**: Treatment complications, potential cross-resistance within drug classes.

### 6.9.1 Non-Adherence and Drug Resistance
- Failure to adhere allows mutant viruses to dominate and become resistant.
- High ARV blood levels necessary for effective suppression; lapses create resistance.

- **Enrichment: Resistance Testing**:
  - **Genotypic Assay**: Examines viral genetic material for resistance.
  - **Phenotypic Assay**: Assesses virus response to ARVs under controlled conditions.

## 6.10 Adherence to ART
- Minimum adherence of 90% crucial to prevent mutation and resistance.

### 6.10.1 Reasons for Non-Adherence
- **Barriers**: 
  - **Psychosocial**: Treatment fatigue, forgetfulness, denial.
  - **Patient-Provider Relationship**: Lack of support, insufficient information.
  - **Practical Problems**: Transport issues, insufficient food.
  - **Work-Related**: Inflexible clinic hours.
  - **Service-Related**: Stock shortages, poor clinic organization.
  - **Stigma**: Fear of rejection, non-disclosure.
  - **Cultural**: Using traditional medicine alongside ARVs.
  - **Conflict of Opinion**: Misinformation from healthcare or alternative providers.

### 6.10.2 Strategies for Improving Adherence to ART
- Evaluate patient readiness and intention to adhere.
- Simplify regimens with fixed-dose combinations (FDCs).
- Identify and address personal, social, or cultural challenges.
- Train healthcare workers to manage adherence-related issues.
- Employ effective teaching techniques (repeat information, use visual aids).
- Provide skills training for managing ARV intake and disclosure.

### Glossary
- **Treatment Fatigue**: Exhaustion from continuous medication leading to non-adherence.
- **Locus of Control**: Individual belief in control over life events; external vs. internal.

- Antiretroviral prophylaxis at birth is vital for reducing MTCT.
- Guidelines based on the mother's ART status and viral load:
  - VL <1,000 copies/ml: Nevirapine (NVP) for six weeks.
  - Late ART initiation or diagnosis: NVP for 12 weeks.
  - VL > 1,000 copies/ml: NVP plus AZT for six weeks.

- Newborns to HIV-positive mothers receive prophylaxis; PCR testing to confirm HIV-exposure status.
- **Questions Addressed:**
  - If mother refuses ART for baby, professional intervention is essential (Children’s Act, No. 38 of 2005).
  - ARVs lower maternal viral load, reducing infant infection risk during pregnancy and breastfeeding.

### Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) After Occupational Exposure
- Low risk (.3%) for contracting HIV post-exposure; higher risk for Hepatitis B and C.
- PEP can significantly lower HIV infection risk post-exposure; treatment must start within 72 hours.
- PEP Protocol:
  - Clinical assessment of exposure type.
  - Rapid HIV test after pre-test counseling; ELISA for laboratory settings.
  - Treatment with TDF + 3TC/FTC and RAL recommended for 28 days if baseline HIV negative.

- If HIV positive, refer for counselling and testing post-exposure; PEP is contraindicated.
- Provide follow-up testing and support for healthcare workers who receive PEP.

#### Eligibility for PEP
- **Exposures warranting PEP:** 
  - Percutaneous or mucous membrane exposure; bodily fluids including blood and breast milk.
- **Non-significant exposures:** 
  - Known HIV negative sources, already HIV-positive individuals, and low-risk fluids (e.g., tears, non-blood-stained saliva).

- PEP guidelines applicable to healthcare and non-healthcare workers at risk of exposure; ART should not replace general safer sex practices.

#### Enrichment: ‘Starter’ Packs for PEP
- Starter packs contain 3-day supply of antiretroviral drugs (ARVs) for post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP).

### 6.13 PEP After Rape or Sexual Assault
- High risk of HIV post-rape influenced by:
  - Rapist's HIV status
  - Viral load
  - Coexisting STIs (especially ulcerations)
  - Physical trauma significance (higher in violent rape and child victims)
- Urgent initiation of ART recommended within 72 hours, preferably within 1-2 hours post-incident.
- ART recommendations:
  - Same regimen as occupational exposure (Section 6.12).
  - Preferably a combination of three drugs for 28 days.
- Testing protocol:
  - If HIV positive: No PEP (previous infection), refer for lifelong ART preparation.
  - If HIV negative: Start PEP; emphasize importance of completing 28 days.
  - If waiting for test results beyond 72 hours, consider initiating PEP.
- Counselling requirements:
  - Educate on side effects, adherence strategies.
  - Schedule HIV antibody testing at 3 months for seroconversion.
  - Encourage safer sex practices for 3 months post-incident.  
- STIs prophylactic treatment and hepatitis B vaccination recommended.
- Free ART available at public facilities in South Africa; Aids Helpline for ART referrals.

### Frequently Asked Questions
#### Can children take antiretroviral drugs after sexual assault or rape?
- Children can receive ARVs for PEP within 72 hours if HIV-negative.
- HIV-positive children referred for counselling and follow-up.
- Legal consent requirements depend on age and maturity for testing and treatment.

#### How does ART work to prevent HIV infection after needlestick injuries or rape?
- ART must be administered within 72 hours to possibly eradicate the virus before CD4+T cell infection occurs.

- Truvada® (FTC and TDF) recommended as part of combination prevention approaches alongside condom use.

### Notes to Classes of ARVs
- **NRTIs**: Associated with lactic acidosis, hepatic steatosis, lipodystrophy.
