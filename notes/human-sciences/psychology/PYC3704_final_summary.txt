## 5. TOPIC 2: PROBABILITY

### OVERVIEW OF THE RESEARCH PROCESS
- **Components**: Theory → Hypothesis → Population → Sample → Probability → Results.

### DEFINITIONS
- **Probability**: Measure of uncertainty; likelihood of specific event outcomes.
  
**Formula**:
- \(P(E) = \frac{\text{Number of favourable events}}{\text{Total possible outcomes}}\)

- **Relative Frequency**: Approximation of event occurring.

### Characteristics and Rules
- **Independent Events**: Previous outcome does not affect next outcome.
- **Mutually Exclusive**: Only one outcome can occur (e.g., coin flip).
- **Sample Space (S)**: All possible outcomes (e.g., {heads, tails}).

### Law of Large Numbers
- Repeated experiments yield results closer to theoretical probability over time.

### P-Value and Probability Characteristics
- Expressed as percentage, fraction, or decimal; represents proportion of outcomes supporting an event.

### Probability Concepts
- **Proportion**: Decimal number between 0 and 1.
- **p-value range**: \(0 \leq p \leq 1\).
  - \( p = 0 \): Event will NOT happen.
  - \( p = 1 \): Event WILL happen.
- **Probability of NOT occurring**: \(1 - P(E)\).
- **Sample space (S)**: All possible outcomes of a statistical experiment (e.g. \( S = \{heads, tails\} \)). Sum of \( S = 1\).

### Rules for Combining Probability (Independent Events)
1. **Additive Rule**: 
   - If mutually exclusive: \( P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) \).
   - If NOT mutually exclusive: \( P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \text{ and } B) \).
  
2. **Multiplicative Rule**: 
   - \( P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B) \) (Events occurring together).
  
3. **Conditional Probability**: 
   - \( P(A | B) \): Probability of A given B occurs.

### Probability Distributions and the Normal Curve
- Transition from normal distributions to probability.
  
#### Example: Kids' Scores out of 15
- A: Probability of score \(9/15\): \(P(9/15) = f/p = 4/36 = 0.11\).

#### Cumulative Probability
- Probability of events equaling or exceeding a certain point.
- Data represented 'under a curve' for continuous variables.

### Standard Normal Distribution
- Mean \(= 0\), standard deviation \(= 1\).
  
#### z-Score Formula
- \(Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\) for population.
- For sample: \( \overline{Z} = \frac{X - \overline{X}}{s} \).

- **z-Scores**: Indicate standard deviations from the mean.
- All z-scores' distribution has mean \(= 0\), standard deviation \(= 1\).

### Example: Calculating z-Score
1. Scores: \(X = 56\%\), class average \(= 52\%\), standard deviation \(= 4\).
   - Formula: \(Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \rightarrow Z = \frac{56 - 52}{4} = 1\).

2. Alternative example:
   - Scores: \(X = 56\%\), class average \(= 65\%\), standard deviation \(= 6\).
   - Calculation yields \(Z = -1.5\).

### Comparing z-scores
- Positive z-scores indicate values above the mean; negative indicate values below.

## TOPIC 1: Sampling and Sampling Distributions

### Study Unit 2.3.1 Sampling and Sampling Distributions - Overview 

- **Sampling Distributions**  
  - Estimate population values; represent the best guess for unknown population parameters.  
  - **Sampling Error:** Difference between sample results and true population values.

- **Mean of All Means:**  
  - Identical to population mean (µ); best estimate of unknown population mean.

- **Standard Error:**  
  - Measure of sampling error; variability of scores; standard deviation of sampling distribution of the mean.  
  - **Formula:**  
    - Population: \[ σ̄_x = \frac{σ}{\sqrt{n}} \]  
    - Sample: \[ S̄_x = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]

- **Sample Means & Possible Errors:**  
  - Total of sample means (M̄s) equals the population mean (µ).  
  - Factors affecting sampling error:  
    - Sample size  
    - Nature of the variable

- **Z-test Statistic for a Sample Mean:**  
  \[ Z̄_x = \frac{M̄ - µ̄_x}{σ̄_x} \] 
  - Symbols:  
    - \(σ\) = Population Standard Deviation  
    - \(n\) = Sample Size  
    - \(M̄\) = Sample Mean  
    - \(μ̄_x\) = Population Mean under H0  

### Study Unit 2.4 Sampling Distributions and the Central Limit Theorem 

- **Definitions:**  
  - **Sampling:** Using small cases to infer about larger population.  
  - **Population:** Larger group under study.  
  - **Sample:** Group involved in research.

- **Purpose of Sampling:**  
  - Saves time/money while keeping accuracy close to full population studies; influenced by law of diminishing returns.

- **Random Sampling:** Ensures each individual has equal chances; connected to sampling error.

- **Parameters vs. Statistics:**  
  - Parameters: Summary values for populations (unknown).  
  - Statistics: Summary values for samples (known).

- **Sampling Distribution of a Statistic:**  
  - Set of all possible values of a statistic from fixed size samples drawn from a population.

- **Central Limit Theorem:**  
  - Distribution of sample means approaches normality as sample size increases (n) regardless of population distribution shape.
  - **Formula:**  
    \[ z_x = \frac{X - µ_x}{σ} \]

- **Standard Error of the Mean:**  
  - Indicates expected deviation of sample means from population mean.
  
- **Key Takeaways:**  
  - Central limit theorem underpins hypothesis testing, determination of mean, variance, and shape for sample means.  
  - Importance in hypothesis tests with means.

## TOPIC 2: General Principles of Statistical Hypothesis Testing

### Study Unit 3.1: Translating Research Hypothesis into Statistical Hypotheses

- **Process:**  
  - Develop testable theory; refine into operational hypothesis detailing constructs, population, and research design.  
  - Translate operational hypothesis into statistical hypotheses (H0 and H1).  
  - **Null Hypothesis (H0):** Always state first; non-directional (≠) or directional (<, >).

### Study Unit 3.2: Obtaining Sample Data and Results

- **Sample Data Collection:**  
  - Random sample from target population; perform measurements (e.g. intelligence tests).  
  - Calculate sample mean for hypothesis evaluation.

- **Hypothesis Testing:**  
  - Determine significance through p-values; reject H0 if statistical significance doesn’t align with practical significance (psychological importance).

### Study Unit 3.3: Decision-making Regarding Null and Alternative Hypotheses

- **Cut-off P-value:**  
  - Significance level (α) established before testing (commonly 0.05 or 0.01).  
  - Decision rule: reject H0 if p-value < α.

- **Errors in Testing:**  
  - Type I Error: Rejecting true H0.  
  - Type II Error: Not rejecting false H0.  
  - Strategies to minimize error probabilities include increasing sample size or reducing measurement errors.

- **Power of a Statistical Test:**  
  - Probability of rejecting H0 when it is false (1 - β); increased by larger samples or decreased errors.

## Topic 3: BEFORE YOU CAN APPLY THE DECISION RULE

### Alternative Hypothesis and P-Values
- Match hypotheses: both must be one or two-tailed.
- Change p-value to match the Alternative Hypothesis; **do not change H1**.

### Z-Tables
- Provide **directional (ONE-TAILED)** p-values.
- Non-directional hypothesis: e.g. **H1: µ≠110**; multiply p-value by 2 (x 2) for **TWO-TAILED** p-value.

### Computers
- Provide **non-directional (TWO-TAILED)** p-values.
- Directional hypothesis: e.g. **H1: µ < 110** or **H1: µ > 110**; divide p-value by 2 (÷ 2) for **ONE-TAILED** p-value.

### Decision Rule Summary (p. 82/83 UNISA Study Guide)
1. Use "REJECT" or "FAIL TO REJECT" for Ho.
2. Alpha (α) preset by researcher (0.01 or 0.05).
3. Test Ho, aiming to **reject Ho** to accept H1.
4. For TWO-TAILED/non-directional (x2 p-value), apply decision rule.
5. Calculate test statistic (e.g., Zp, tx) to check observed relationship deviance.
6. If p-value < α, **reject Ho**; if not, **fail to reject Ho**.

## Topic 3: Type 1 and Type 2 Errors
### Type 1 Error
- Rejecting Ho when it is true.
- Probability of occurrence determined by p-value.
- To avoid: set α to a small value (e.g. 0.01).

### Type 2 Error
- Accepting Ho when it is false.
- Probability indicated by **beta (β)**, which cannot be calculated; relies on α.
- To avoid: set α to a large value (e.g., 0.20).

## Topic 3: Summary of Research Steps
**10 Steps When Doing Psychological Research**
1. **Theory**: Understand human behavior aspect.
2. **Hypothesis**: Research hypothesis (e.g., gender performance differences).
3. **Operational Hypothesis**: Ability to measure constructs/variables.
4. **Statistical Hypothesis**: Define Ho (e.g., µ=110) & H1 (e.g., µ > 110).
5. **Draw a Sample**: Large sample size to reduce error.
6. **Get Test Results (Data)**: Assume normal distribution.
7. **Face Value Step**: Evaluate if sample supports hypothesis.
8. **Choose a Test Statistic**: Use appropriate test statistic from flowchart.
9. **Apply the Decision Rule**: Ensure p-value aligns with H1 definition and compare to α.
10. **Accept or Reject H1**: Conclude regarding Research Hypothesis.

## Topic 3: Example 1 (p. 93 UNISA Study Guide)
- Psychologist measures impact of sleep deprivation on performance.
- Sample Size: **n=16**; Population Mean: **µ=1200**, Standard Deviation: **σ=200**.
- Sample Mean: **X=1050**; α=0.05.
- Null hypothesis: **Ho: µ = 1200**; Alternative: **H1: µ < 1200**.
- Standard Error: **Sₓ = σ/√n = 200/√16 = 50**.
- Z-statistics: **z = (X - µₓ) / σₓ = (1050 - 1200) / 50 = -3.00, p = 0.0013**.
- **Decision Rule**: Reject Ho; sleep deprivation negatively affects performance.

## Topic 4: tx - Statistical Test for Means: Single Sample Groups Design
- Note: t-test p-values are computer-generated, thus **NON-DIRECTIONAL/TWO-TAILED**.
- Directional hypothesis requires division of p-value by 2.
- Group Design: Compare to two or more populations.
- Y-variable (dependent) measured on **interval/ratio scale**.
- Single sample analysis; population standard deviation (σ) is unknown. 
- Use **tx**:
  - Formula: **tx = X - µₓ / Sₓ**.
  - Standard error calculation: **Sₓ = s / √n** (use sample standard deviation, s).

## TOPIC 5: tc AND td TEST STATISTICS: TWO SAMPLE GROUPS DESIGN

### 1. GROUPS DESIGN
- **Concept:** Comparing two or more population groups.
- **Y-VARIABLE (DEPENDENT):** Measured on a RATIO or INTERVAL SCALE (e.g., IQ, height).
  
### ASSUMPTIONS:
1. Normal distributions for both groups.
2. Equal variance (σ²) for both groups.
3. Set α in advance.
4. Statistical hypotheses focus on population means (µ).
   - E.g.: **Ho:** µ = 104 / **H1:** µ > 104.
5. Select TWO SAMPLES based on:
   - **Independent Groups** (no systematic relationship).
   - **Dependent Groups** (matched on characteristics).

### 7. TEST USAGE:
- **Independent Groups:** Use tc.
- **Dependent Groups:** Use td.

## TOPIC 6: T-TEST FOR THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN TWO MEANS OF TWO DEPENDENT GROUPS (td̃)

### DEPENDENT SAMPLES:
- One sample matched based on a characteristic to another.
- Example: Same students tested at two different times.

### STATISTICAL HYPOTHESES:
- **Ho:** D = 0
- **H1:** D ≠ 0 (or greater/less than 0).

### DIFFERENCE SCORE:
- **Formula:** d̄ = X2 - X1 (difference between sample means).
- **S̄d:** Sample standard deviation of "difference" scores calculated by subtracting standard deviations of results.

## TOPIC 7: CORRELATIONAL DESIGNS

### 1. CORRELATIONAL DESIGN:
- **Variables:** Both Y (dependent) and X (independent) are continuous (Interval/Ratio).

### USE:
- **Pearson’s correlation coefficient (r):**
  - Range: -1 to 1.
  - Relationships:
    - r = 1 (Perfect positive).
    - r = 0 (No relationship).
    - r = -1 (Perfect negative).

### SCATTER PLOT:
- Data plotted on x and y axes; straight line indicates linear relationship.

### STATISTICAL HYPOTHESES:
- **Ho:** ρ = 0 (No relationship).
- **H1:** ρ ≠ 0 (Relationship exists).
  - Variants: ρ > 0 (positive), ρ < 0 (negative).

### CATEGORICAL VARIABLES:
- **Use:** Pearson’s chi-squared test (X²).
  - Conditions: Expected frequency should be ≥ 5.
  - **Formula:** X² = Σ((O - E)² / E).

## ADDITIONAL NOTES: DEGREES OF FREEDOM
- **Definition:** Number of values that can vary; important for calculations.
- **T-Test Degrees of Freedom:**
  - One sample: df = n - 1.
  - Two samples: df = n1 + n2 - 2.

### CORRELATION DESIGN:
- **Pearson’s r:** df = N - 2.

### CHI SQUARE:
- **Degrees of freedom:** (r-1)(k-1).
  - r = number of rows; k = number of columns.

## EFFECT SIZE
- **Definition:** Quantifies difference between groups; emphasizes difference size.
- **T-Test Effect Size (d):** 
  - **Formula:** d = mean difference / standard deviation.
  - Interpretation: 
    - d = 0.2 (small), d = 0.5 (medium), d = 0.8 (large).
  
- **Pearson’s r Effect Size (r²):** 
  - Interpretation: 
    - r² = 0.01 (small), r² = 0.09 (medium), r² = 0.25 (large).
    - Example: r = 0.65, r² = 0.42 (42% variance shared).
