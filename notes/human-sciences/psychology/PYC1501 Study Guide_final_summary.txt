# Unit 1: Human Nervous System

## 1.1 Introduction
- The human nervous system is crucial for human functioning and behavior.
- Comparative studies of animal nervous systems help in understanding human brain functions.
- Covers neuron structure, impulse conduction, and overall nervous system coordination.

## 1.2 Human Nervous System: Structure and Functions
- The mammalian nervous system is complex and facilitates coordinated function.
- Evolves to show similarities in adaptive physiological and behavioral functions across species.
- Divided into **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)** and **Central Nervous System (CNS)**.

### 1.2.1 The Peripheral Nervous System
- Carries necessary survival signals, involving voluntary and involuntary functions.
- Divided into somatic and autonomic nervous systems.

#### The Somatic Nervous System
- Manages conscious and voluntary activities.
- Relays sensory/motor information to/from CNS.
- **Efferent neurons** carry information away from CNS to muscles.
- **Afferent neurons** convey sensory information to CNS.

#### The Autonomic Nervous System
- Controls involuntary functions of organs and glands.
- Divided into sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions.
  
  (i) **Sympathetic Nervous System**: 
  - Prepares body for stress (fight-or-flight).
  
  (ii) **Parasympathetic Nervous System**: 
  - Calms the body, restores homeostasis.

### 1.2.2 The Central Nervous System (CNS)
- Comprises the brain, brainstem, and spinal cord.
- Main functions include synthesizing information and regulating organ function and movements.
- Relays information via **neurons** (approx. 100 billion in the brain).

#### The Brain
- Largest CNS component, managing sensation, perception, thinking, and emotions.
- **Limbic system**: Regulates emotions; includes structures like the hippocampus and amygdala.

#### Brain Regions
- **Cerebellum**: Coordinates actions unconsciously.
- **Cerebrum (Cerebral Cortex)**: Advanced brain functions; houses gray and white matter.
- **Cerebral Hemispheres:** Control sensory and motor functions; connected by the **corpus callosum**.

### Brain Lobes
- **Frontal Lobe**: Cognitive functions (language, memory, problem-solving).
- **Parietal Lobe**: Processes body touch inputs.
- **Occipital Lobe**: Primary visual processing.
- **Temporal Lobe**: Auditory and olfactory processing.

### Limbic System Structures
- Processes memory (hippocampus) and emotions (cingulate gyrus).
- **Hypothalamus and thalamus**: Regulate drives and sensory/motor information.

## The Brain and Brainstem

- **Thalamus**: Processes sensory information.
- **Midbrain**: Includes 
  - **Superior and Inferior Colliculi**: Visual and auditory processing.
  - **Substantia Nigra**: Involved in movement and implicated in **Parkinson’s Disease**.

- **Hindbrain**: Contains

  - **Medulla Oblongata**: Regulates autonomic functions (breathing, digestion, heartbeat).
  - **Cerebellum**: Controls coordination, balance, and muscle tone.
- **Spinal Cord**: Connects brainstem to body's major nerves; 
  - **Dorsal nerves (afferent)**: Receive sensory info.
  - **Ventral nerves (efferent)**: Send motor signals.

## Impulse Conduction in the Neuron

### Structure of Neuron
- **Dendrites**: Collect information.
- **Cell Body (Soma)**: Contains nucleus; maintains cell.
- **Axon**: Transmits info; can be long (e.g., from spinal cord to limbs).
- **Terminal Boutons**: Release neurotransmitters at the synapse.
- **Myelin Sheath**: Insulates axon, speeds up signal transmission.

### Action Potential
- **Definition**: All-or-nothing event triggered by reaching the **threshold of excitation**.
- **Fluctuations**: 
  - **EPSP (Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential)**: Depolarizes membrane, moves closer to threshold.
  - **IPSP (Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential)**: Hyperpolarizes membrane, moves away from threshold.
- **Result**: Action potential fires if threshold is reached.

### Refractory Period
- **Description**: Period that limits neuron firing.
- **Types**: 
  - **Absolute Refractory Period (ARP)**: Neuron cannot fire; sodium channels inactive.
  - **Relative Refractory Period (RRP)**: Neuron can fire but requires stronger stimulus.

---

## The Synapse

- **Synaptic Cleft**: Space between neurons for neurotransmitter transmission.
- **Neurotransmitters**: Chemicals that relay signals; released when action potential reaches terminal buttons.
- **Receptor Sites**: Dendrites accept neurotransmitters if they fit; effects can be excitatory or inhibitory.

### Neural Communication
- **Process**: Electrical within neurons; chemical between neurons.
- **Action Potential**: Change in charge leads to signal propagation.
- **Nodes of Ranvier**: Gaps in myelin where electrical signal jumps; aids rapid transmission.

### Removal of Neurotransmitters
- **Methods**: 
  - **Enzymatic Breakdown**: Degrading neurotransmitters.
  - **Reuptake**: Reabsorbing neurotransmitters into the sending terminal.

### 1.3.5 The Neurotransmitters

- Over 100 identified neurotransmitters influence emotion, cognition, and behaviour.
- Regulate appetite, memory, emotion, muscle action.

#### Table 1.1: Neurotransmitters and Their Functions
- **Acetylcholine (ACh)**: Stimulates muscle contractions; associated with Alzheimer’s disease (undersupply).
  
- **Dopamine**: Involved in movement and pleasure; linked to schizophrenia (overproduction) and Parkinson's disease (underproduction).
  
- **Endorphins**: Natural pain relievers; deficiency linked to depression.
  
- **GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid)**: Major inhibitory neurotransmitter; low levels lead to anxiety and involuntary motor actions.
  
- **Glutamate**: Released in >90% brain synapses; excess can cause migraines and seizures.
  
- **Serotonin**: Regulates mood, appetite, sleep; low levels associated with seasonal depression.
  
- **Adrenaline**: Increases heart rate, blood vessel contraction, metabolism; excess can cause rapid heartbeat and high blood pressure.
  
- **Noradrenalin (NA)**: Excites neurons; deficiency causes depression, excess results in mania.

- **Drug Interactions with Neurotransmitters**:
  - **Agonists**: Mimic neurotransmitter effects (e.g., cocaine with dopamine).
  - **Antagonists**: Reduce normal neurotransmitter effects (e.g., curare with ACh).
  - **Reuptake inhibitors**: Increase neurotransmitter action by blocking reuptake.

### 2.1 Introduction
- Interaction with the environment involves constant information processing.
- Sensory systems allow unique experiences through stimuli interpretation.
- Focus on sensation (detection of stimuli) and perception (interpretation of stimuli).

### 2.2 Defining Sensation and Perception
- **Sensation**: Awareness from stimulation of sensory organs (e.g., eyes, ears).
- **Perception**: Organization and conscious experience of sensory information.
  - **Bottom-up Processing**: Building perceptions from sensory input.
  - **Top-down Processing**: Filtering sensory input through prior knowledge and experiences.
- Goals: Sensation aims for detection; perception aims to create meaningful environmental information.
- **Absolute Threshold**: Minimum stimulation required for detection; measured by signal detection (Schiffman, 1990).
  - **Table 2.1**: Absolute thresholds for the five senses.
- **Differential Threshold (Just Noticeable Difference - jnd)**: Ability to detect differences in stimuli, changes with intensity (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
- Experiences shape the brain's information processing.
- **Sensory Adaptation**: Decreased response to unchanging stimuli.

---

### 2.3 The Five Sensory Systems
#### Figure 2.1: The Sensory Systems

#### 2.3.1 Hearing (Auditory)
- Sound waves: Pressure from vibrations in a medium (e.g., air).
- Pathway: Outer ear → Auditory canal → Eardrum → Ossicles (Malleus, Incus, Stapes) → Cochlea → Auditory nerve (van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).
- Sound waves detected by hair cells in the cochlea → Transmitted to auditory cortex (Romani et al., 1982).

#### 2.3.2 Smell (Olfaction)
- Chemical sense: Interaction of taste and smell.
- Odour molecules detected by olfactory receptor cells in the nasal passage.
- Signals sent via olfactory nerve to limbic system and olfactory cortex (Lodovichi & Belluscio, 2012; Spors et al., 2013).
  
#### Figure 2.3: Smell Receptors

#### 2.3.3 Taste (Gustation)
- Basic tastes: Sweet, salty, sour, bitter, and umami (Kinnamon & Vandenbeuch, 2009).
- Taste buds: Grouped taste receptor cells; neural impulses transmitted to medulla, thalamus, limbic system, gustatory cortex (Maffei et al., 2012; Roper, 2013).

#### Figure 2.4: Structure of Taste Buds

#### 2.3.4 Touch
- **Somaesthetic System**: Senses touch, pressure, heat, pain (Van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).
- Mechanoreceptors convert pressure into neural activity.
- **Somatosensation**: Transduces physical stimuli; feeds information to primary somatosensory cortex.
- Skin's sensitivity varies by body areas.

#### 2.3.5 Vision (Seeing)
- **Visual System**: Light enters the eye; focused by lens onto retina.
- Sensory information sent via optic nerve to primary visual cortex (van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).
- Light intensity and wavelength influence perception.
  - Light intensity determines brightness; wavelength affects color perception (Bernstein et al., 2012).

### 1. Vision

- **Photoreceptors**
  - Retina converts light into electrical signals via photoreceptors: **rods** (dim light) and **cones** (colour, detail).
  - Rods concentrated in periphery; cones in **fovea** (central focus).
  - Issue: Direct gaze at dim stars leads to perceived disappearance (insufficient rods).

- **Damage to the Visual Cortex**
  - Possible effects include:
    - **Agnosia**: inability to perceive visual stimuli.
    - **Prosopagnosia**: inability to recognize faces.
  - Visual processing pathways:
    - **Ventral pathway**: "what" pathway (object recognition).
    - **Dorsal pathway**: "where" pathway (location/movement).

- **Colour Vision and Colour Blindness**
  - **Trichromatic Theory**: three cone types (red, green, blue) combine to produce all colours.
  - **Opponent-Process Theory**: colour encoded in opposing pairs (black-white, yellow-blue, green-red). 
    - Negative afterimages occur post-stimulus removal.

### 2. Depth Perception

- Ability to perceive spatial relationships in **3-D** space (front/back, above/below).
- Utilizes cues for depth:
  - **Binocular Cues** (both eyes):
    - **Binocular disparity**: differences in images on retinas.
  
  - **Monocular Cues** (one eye):
    - **Linear Perspective**: parallel lines converge.
    - Others: interposition (overlap), relative size, horizon proximity.

### 3. The Principles of Perceptual Grouping

- **Perceptual Grouping**: tendency to organize stimuli into coherent patterns.
- Originates from **Gestalt Psychology**: perception as more than sensory input sum.
  - Key concepts:
    - Hypotheses inform perception.
    - Influenced by personality, experiences, expectations.

### 4. Wave Amplitude and Wavelengths

- **Physical Characteristics**: wave amplitude (height) and wavelength (length from peak to peak).
  - Higher amplitude = greater intensity/brightness; wavelength frequency correlates inversely.

#### 4.1 Light Waves

- **Electromagnetic Spectrum**: includes wavelengths of visible light (380-740 nm).
  - **Colour Perception**: linked to light wavelength (long = red, intermediate = green, short = blue/violet).
  
#### 4.2 Sound Waves

- Sound caused by vibrations in a medium. Properties:
  - **Frequency**: relates to pitch (20-20,000 Hz).
  - **Amplitude**: correlates with loudness (measured in decibels).
  
- Timbre: sound purity dependent on the interplay of frequency, amplitude, timing.

# Unit 3: Cognition: thinking, reasoning, and problem-solving

**3.1 Introduction**
- Thinking guides behavior and addresses daily problems (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
- Involves manipulating information through concepts, problem-solving, critical thinking, reasoning, and decision-making (Santrock, 2003).
- Understanding thought processes is key for learning and communication.

**3.2 Defining cognition**
- Cognition encompasses mental processes such as reasoning and perception (Van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).
- Involves organization, understanding, and communication of information.
- Includes mental representations, not limited to verbal processes (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

**3.3 Types of mental representations**

**3.3.1 Mental image**
- Represents an object or event; has a picture-like quality (Shepherd & Metzler, 1971).
- Includes various sensory modalities; used for mental rotation of images (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

**3.3.2 Concepts**
- Categories of objects/events with common properties (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- Formal concepts: defined by strict rules (e.g., square).
- Natural concepts: defined by experiences, fuzzy boundaries (e.g., birds).

**Importance of concepts** (Santrock, 2003):
1. Generate generalizations.
2. Associate experiences.
3. Aid memory retention.
4. Provide clues for reactions.

**3.3.3 Prototype**
- A member representing common features of a natural concept (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- Prototypical examples facilitate quick identification.

**3.3.4 Proposition**
- Mental representation expressing relationships between concepts.
- Propositions can vary based on new information (Bernstein et al., 2012).

**3.3.5 Schemas**
- Sets of propositions that generalize categories and frameworks for understanding (Bernstein et al., 2012).

**3.3.6 Scripts**
- Schemas for familiar activities outlining expected sequences (Bernstein et al., 2012).

**3.4 Reasoning**
- Process of generating and evaluating arguments (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- Closely related to critical thinking (Santrock, 2003).

**3.4.1 Types of reasoning**
- **Formal reasoning**: Rigorous methods to draw valid conclusions (Bernstein et al., 2012).
  - **Deductive reasoning**: General to specific (e.g., syllogism).
  
- **Informal reasoning**: Assessing conclusion believability based on evidence (Bernstein et al., 2012).
  - **Inductive reasoning**: Specific to general; conclusions are probable but not guaranteed (Santrock, 2003).

- **Critical reasoning**: Reflective thinking evaluating evidence and differing perspectives (Santrock, 2003).

**3.4.2 Reasoning fallacies**
- Fallacies undermine the logical structure of arguments:
  - Exploiting sympathy for unreasonable outcomes (e.g., failing student).
  - Discrediting proponents rather than arguments (e.g., courtroom witness).
  - Group characteristics misused to support conclusions (e.g., advertisements).
  - False analogies yielding inaccurate comparisons (e.g., work vs. housewife analogy).

## 3.5 Problem-solving and decision-making

- Problem-solving: Finding an appropriate way of attaining a goal not readily available (Santrock, 2003).
- Decision-making: Process of identifying alternatives and choosing the best option (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).

### 3.5.1 Steps in problem-solving and decision-making
- **Find and frame the problem:** Clearly define and understand the problem.
- **Develop good problem-solving strategies:** Use algorithms and heuristics.
- **Evaluate solutions:** Test solutions to assess effectiveness.
- **Rethink and redefine:** Continuously improve problem and solution (Santrock, 2003).

### 3.5.2 Strategies for problem-solving and decision-making
- **Trial-and-error:** Trying various solutions until successful (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).
- **Algorithms:** Step-by-step rules leading to a correct solution (Coon & Mitterer, 2012).
- **Heuristic method:** Educated guesses based on experience (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
  - **Representative heuristic:** Categorizing based on shared characteristics (Kahneman et al., 1982).
  - **Availability heuristic:** Making judgments based on recall of information (Tversky & Kahneman, 1973).
- **Incubation:** Taking a break to gain new insights (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
- **Analogy:** Finding similarities with past problems for solutions (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- **Insight:** Sudden realization of solution; "aha" moment (Coon & Mitterer, 2012).

### 3.5.3 Complexities associated with decision-making
- Emotions impact preferences.
- Evaluations of options are inconsistent.
- Uncertainty effect leads to avoidance of decision-making.
- Unconscious deliberation influences choices.
- Brand names and product characteristics affect judgments (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).

### 3.5.4 Barriers to problem-solving and decision-making
- **Focus on irrelevant information:** Distracting information hampers problem resolution (Sternberg, 1986).
- **Functional fixedness:** Overlooking alternative uses of objects (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).
- **Mental sets:** Relying on previous strategies even when better ones exist (Bernstein et al., 2012).
- **Confirmation bias:** Seeking evidence that supports existing beliefs while ignoring contrary evidence (Santrock, 2003).

## 3.6 Language and thinking

- **Language**: System of symbols representing thoughts; used for communication and inner speech (Van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).
  
### 3.6.1 Grammar
- System of rules governing language structure. Language Acquisition Device (LAD) facilitates understanding and production (Chomsky, 2006).

### 3.6.2 Phoneme
- Basic sound unit affecting meaning; variations in phonemes change word meanings (Bernstein et al., 2012; Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

### 3.6.3 Morpheme
- Smallest meaning unit in language; includes standalone words and affixes (Bernstein et al., 2012).

### 3.6.4 Syntax
- Rules for combining words into grammatically correct sentences; critical for clear communication (Lasnik, 1990).

### 3.6.5 Semantics
- Rules for determining meaning in language; sentences can differ in syntax yet share the same semantics (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).

### 3.6.6 Pragmatics
- Practical aspects of communication, including turn-taking and intonation (Yule, 1996).

## 3.7 The Role of Language and Thought
### 3.7.1 The Role of Language in Thinking
- Relationship between language and thought is debated.
- **Piaget (1962)**: Concepts precede language; egocentric speech (collective monologue) decreases with social involvement.
- **Vygotsky (1987)**: Language aids concept development; egocentric speech helps in thought formation and behavior control.
- Language influences problem-solving, memory, and cognition.
- Language as a tool for representing ideas (**Santrock, 2003**).
- **Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis**: Language shapes thought processes and concepts.
  - Study by **Lucy & Shweder (1979)** supports hypothesis; language affects color perception.
  - Cognitive universalism asserts concepts are universal despite linguistic differences (e.g., Zulu language).
- Criticisms: Studies (e.g., **Rosch, 1973**) indicate language influences rather than determines thinking.
- Complexity in multilingual contexts like South Africa.

### 3.7.2 The Role of Thinking in Language
- Cognition may underpin language development.
- Close relationship expected between language ability and intellectual capacity.
- Mental retardation does not always correlate with poor language skills (e.g., **Williams Syndrome**).
- Studies on deaf children show similar problem-solving abilities to peers without hearing impairment (**Furth, 1971, in Santrock, 2003**).
- Evidence suggests language and thought evolved separately but interactively influence each other.

## 3.8 Summary
- **Cognition**: Mental processes for knowledge acquisition; includes thinking, reasoning, and problem-solving.
- Cognitive functions affect daily life—from academics to relationships.
- Interplay of cognition with actions and thoughts influences behavior and interactions.

## 4.3 Three-stage process of memory
- Memory processes: encode, store, retrieve information.
  
### 4.3.1 Encoding (putting it in)
- **Definition**: Acquisition of information and transfer to memory (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
- **Automatic vs. Effortful Processing**: Requires attention for effective encoding (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
- **Types of Encoding**:
  - **Structural Encoding**: Focus on physical structure (e.g. uppercase vs. lowercase letters).
  - **Phonemic (Acoustic) Encoding**: Emphasis on sound of words; aids recall through auditory links.
  - **Semantic Encoding**: Emphasizes meaning; deeper processing leads to longer-lasting codes (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
  - **Visual Encoding**: Involves creating mental images; high-imagery words are easier to remember (Paivio, 1986).

### 4.3.2 Storage (keeping it in)
- **Definition**: Retention of encoded information; duration varies from seconds to a lifetime (Santrock, 2003).
- Information flows through:
  - **Sensory Memory**: Brief retention (<1 sec).
  - **Short-term Memory (STM)**: Limited duration (~30 sec); connects with long-term memory.
  - **Long-term Memory (LTM)**: Permanent storage; unlimited capacity.

### 4.3.3 Retrieval (getting it out)
- **Definition**: Accessing stored information.
- **Recall vs. Recognition**:
  - **Recall**: Retrieve without cues (e.g., essay tests).
  - **Recognition**: Identify from options (e.g., multiple-choice tests) (Santrock, 2003).
- **Serial Position Effect**: Better recall of first (primacy) and last (recency) items on a list.

## 4.4 Types of memory systems
### 4.4.1 Sensory memory (SM)
- Holds vast amounts of sensory data briefly (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
- **Types**:
  - **Iconic Memory**: Visual recollection (e.g., after-image).
  - **Echoic Memory**: Auditory recollection; short retention of sounds (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).

### 4.4.2 Short-term memory (STM)
- Acts as working memory; requires selective attention (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).
- Average capacity: 7 ± 2 items (Miller, 1956).

### 4.4.3 Long-term memory (LTM)
- Permanent storage; includes general knowledge and personal experiences.
- **Types**:
  - **Declarative (Explicit) Memory**:
    - Easy retrieval; includes facts, names, events (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
    - **Semantic Memory**: Meaningful information about the world.
    - **Episodic Memory**: Personal experiences (e.g., significant life events).
  - **Non-declarative (Implicit) Memory**:
    - Affects behavior subconsciously.
    - Includes procedural memories for actions and skills (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).
    - **Priming**: Enhanced retrieval due to preceding similar information (e.g., word fragment completion).

### 5.3 Theories of Learning

#### 5.3.1 Behaviouristic Learning Theories

- Founded by **John B. Watson**; behaviorism focuses on observable behavior (Ciccarelli & White, 2017).
- Key assumptions of behaviorism:
  - Focus solely on observable behavior, ignoring internal processes.
  - Environment shapes learning more than individual characteristics.
  - Principles of contiguity and reinforcement are central to learning (Heffner, 2014).

- **B.F. Skinner**: Expanded on Watson's findings, recognizing influence of internal states on behavior. He emphasized behavior training for survival (Heffner, 2014).

#### 5.3.2 Cognitive Learning Theories

- Emerged in response to behaviorism; focus on the mind's influence on behavior.
- Cognition involves internal processes (perceptions, thoughts, etc.) that impact observable behavior (Ciccarelli & White, 2017; Coon, 2005).
  
- **Gestalt psychologists**: Critiqued behaviorism for its reductionist approach; provided alternative theories (http://www.lifecircles-inc.com/Learningtheories/learningmap.html).
  
- Cognitive theorists view knowledge acquisition as key to learning; internal processes like organizing and storing information are critical. Jean Piaget linked cognitive shifts to developmental changes and experiences (Van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).

## 5.4 Types of Learning

- Learning types often interrelate and overlap.
- Cognitive and social learning elements can intersect with conditioning principles.

### 5.4.1 Associative Learning

- **Definition**: Involves forming associations between stimuli and responses.
- **Key Terms**:
  - **Antecedents**: Events preceding a response.
  - **Consequences**: Effects following a response.

- **Types**:
  - **Classical Conditioning**: Event associations lead to anticipatory responses.
    - **Application**: Pavlov's dogs learn to associate a stimulus with a reflexive response.

### Classical Conditioning Elements
- **Unconditioned Stimulus (US)**: Naturally elicits a response (e.g., food).
- **Unconditioned Response (UR)**: Natural response to US (e.g., salivation).
- **Neutral Stimulus (NS)**: Does not naturally elicit a response (e.g., whistle).
- **Conditioned Stimulus (CS)**: Initially NS that, through conditioning, elicits a response (e.g., whistle triggers salivation).
- **Conditioned Response (CR)**: Learned response to CS (e.g., salivation after hearing the whistle).

### Classical Conditioning Process
- **Acquisition**: Learning occurs through repeated pairing of CS and US.

### Basic Principles of Classical Conditioning
- **Contiguity**: Proximity of stimulus and response determines association strength.
- **Acquisition**: Conditioned response emerges when stimuli are paired.

## 6.3 The nature of consciousness

  - Waking
  - Daydreaming

  - Hypnotic states
  - Sleep deprivation

## 6.4 The nature of sleep
- Sleep: low activity, reduced sensory awareness; regulated by biological factors like heart rate and body temperature (Siegel, 2008).
- Brainwave activity: 
  - **Beta waves** (alertness, awake)
  - **Alpha waves** (precede sleep, relaxation)
  - **Theta waves** (light sleep)
  - **Delta waves** (deep sleep, slow wave).

- Evolutionary psychology: suggests sleep restores resources expended during the day; however, evidence is lacking (Frank, 2006; Rial et al., 2007).
- Cognitive functions and memory formation: 
  - Sleep deprivation impacts cognition (Brown, 2012).
  - Slow-wave sleep aids in task performance (Huber et al., 2004).
  - Areas controlling sleep: 
    - Thalamus
    - Hypothalamus (suprachiasmatic nucleus - biological clock)
    - Pons (regulates REM sleep).

### 6.4.1 Non-Rapid Eye Movement (NREM) sleep
- NREM consists of four stages distinguished by specific brainwave patterns.
  
  - **Stage 1**: 
    - Alpha waves transition to theta waves.
    - K-complexes appear, may react to environmental stimuli.

  - **Stage 2**: 
    - Sleep spindles and K-complexes indicate deeper relaxation.
  
  - **Stage 3**:
    - Delta waves begin, transitioning to slow-wave sleep.
  
  - **Stage 4**:
    - Deepest sleep: pure delta waves, difficulty waking.

### 6.4.2 The Rapid-Eye Movement (REM) sleep
- REM sleep stimulates brain development in infants; occurs approximately 85% of the time with vivid dreams.
- Associated with: 
  - EEG patterns like Stage 1 sleep.
  - Memory consolidation (Coon & Mitterer, 2012).
  
- Benefits: 
  - Creative thinking and language learning linked to sleep (Cai et al., 2009; Wagner et al., 2004).

## 6.6.5 Somnambulism (sleepwalking)

## 6.7 Hypnosis

- Defined as a systematic procedure inducing heightened suggestibility (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).
- Involves extreme self-focus; minimal attention to external stimuli.
- Employs relaxation and suggestion for therapeutic outcomes; can enhance memory.
- Distinction necessary between therapeutic hypnosis and entertainment hypnosis; hypnotized individuals generally retain control (Raz, 2011).
- Four steps to induce hypnosis (National Research Council, 1994):
  - Focus on a single point (e.g., hypnotist's words).
  - Achieve comfort; induce relaxation.
  - Openness to hypnosis and trust in hypnotist.
  - Encourage utilization of imagination.
- Variability in susceptibility to hypnosis; most are moderately hypnotisable (Kihlstrom, 2013).
- Therapeutic applications include pain management, depression, anxiety, smoking cessation, weight loss (Alladin, 2012; Elkins et al., 2012; Golden, 2012).

## 6.8 Substance Use Disorders

- Dependency and drug use disorders alter consciousness; defined by **DSM-5**.
- Characterized by compulsive drug use; may result in significant adverse consequences.
- Physical dependence: withdrawal symptoms upon cessation.
- Psychological dependence: emotional need, drug use for psychological relief.
- Drug tolerance: increased amounts required for previous effects.

### 6.8.1 Psychoactive Drugs
- **Mechanisms**: Interact with neurotransmitter systems as agonists (facilitates) or antagonists (hinders).

- **Depressants**:
  - Suppress CNS, e.g., barbiturates, benzodiazepines; act as GABA agonists.
  - Alcohol effects: euphoria at low doses, sedation at higher doses; associated with FASD/FAS.

- **Stimulants**:
  - Enhance CNS activity; many act as dopamine agonists (e.g., caffeine, cocaine, amphetamines).
  - High abuse potential due to dopamine system involvement.

- **Hallucinogens**:
  - Profound sensory and perceptual alterations; include marijuana and LSD.

- **Inhalants**:
  - Chemicals inhaled for a high; commonly found in household products.

- **Opioids**:
  - Includes heroin, morphine; analgesic properties; high potential for abuse.
  - Mimic endogenous painkillers; substantial risk for addiction and health consequences.

## 7.6 Personality Theories

### 7.6.1 Freud’s Theory of Personality Development
- **Defence Mechanisms**: Unconscious behaviors to reduce anxiety caused by id-superego conflict.
- **Psychosexual Stages of Development**: Five stages influence personality:
  - **Stage 1: Oral (0-18 months)**  
    - Pleasure: Mouth; Conflict: Weaning

  - **Stage 2: Anal (2-3 years)**  
    - Pleasure: Anus; Conflict: Toilet training  
    - Traits: Controlling, orderly, preoccupied with cleanliness.
    
  - **Stage 3: Phallic (3-5 years)**  
    - Pleasure: Genitals; Conflicts: Oedipus/Electra complex  
    - Traits: Flirtatious, jealous, competitive.

## 7.6 Cultural Influences on Personality

- **Culture definition**: Beliefs, customs, art, and traditions of a society.
  - Influences personality development and traits.
- **Personality psychology focus**: Relationship between culture and personality traits.
  - Investigates if traits are universal or culture-specific.

### Importance of Cultural Influences
- Western personality concepts may not apply universally (Benet-Martinez & Oishi, 2008).
- Varying strength of personality traits across cultures:
  - **Individualistic Cultures** (e.g., USA, Europe): 
    - Emphasize independence, competition, personal achievement (Oyserman, Coon, & Kemmelmeier, 2002).
    - Exhibit more personally oriented traits (Yang, 2006).
  - **Collectivistic Cultures** (e.g., Africa):
    - Value social harmony, respectfulness, group needs.
    - Display socially oriented traits.
    - Philosophical perspective encapsulated in the proverb: *Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu* (Lassiter, 1999).

### African Perspective on Personality
- Emphasizes interdependence; individuality viewed within community context.
- Clan and group survival as primary focus.
- Concept of aggression framed as a personal attack against community.
- Extended family cohesive structure; caregiving is communal responsibility (Khoapa, 1980).

**Comparison of Perspectives** (Table 7.5: Adapted from Van der Walt, 1997):

  - Developed by Henry Murray and Christiana Morgan (1930s).

## 8.4 Influences on Intelligence

### 8.4.1 Genetic Influences on Intelligence
- Intelligence correlates with brain volume and cortex thickness, especially in prefrontal lobes.
- Positive association with white matter health; fluid intelligence impacted by frontal lobe damage.
- Gender differences noted: 
  - Men: IQ linked to gray matter in frontal/left parietal lobe.
  - Women: IQ linked to gray matter in right frontal lobe/Broca’s area, and white matter volume.
- Moderate similarity in intelligence among biological relatives; genetic markers identified on chromosomes.
- **Jansen (1969, in Santrock, 2003)**: Intelligence primarily inherited; minimal role for environment.
- **Heritability**: Measures population variance due to genetics, not individual differences; relevant for group comparisons.
- Twin studies show:
  - Monozygotic twins raised together exhibit higher IQ correlation than dizygotic twins.
  - Monozygotic twins raised apart show higher similarity than dizygotic twins together.
- Adopted children more similar in IQ to biological than adoptive parents, but studies inconclusive on environmental influence.

### 8.4.2 Environmental Influences on Intelligence
- Adoption studies indicate environment shapes intelligence.
- Biological children resemble parents more than adopted children if raised together.
- Evidence:
  - Children from orphanages improved by adoption; those stayed show IQ drop.
  - Foster children exhibit IQ similarity to foster parents; unrelated children in same home also show similarities.
- Socioeconomic status (SES) impacts intellectual stimulation; communication from middle-income parents correlates with higher IQ.
- Complex interplay; wealth does not guarantee success, motivation from disadvantaged backgrounds can lead to achievement.

### 8.4.3 Cultural Influences on Intelligence
- Definitions of intelligence vary by culture.
- Western cultures emphasize reasoning; African cultures integrate social participation.
- **White (2006, in Coon & Mitterer, 2012)** criticized standardized tests as culturally biased; solution involves culture-fair tests.
- Intelligence context-specific; Nisbett (2003): Western cultures prioritize order/rational debate, Eastern cultures prioritize understanding/relationships.

## 8.5 Standardisation and the Intelligence Quotient (IQ)
- Psychologists have designed reliable/intelligent tests to measure the general intelligence factor (g).
- Key characteristics: **Reliability** and **Validity**.
- Standardisation process: tests administered to a diverse sample to establish norms.
- **Flynn effect**: average IQ increases globally; roughly 3 IQ points per decade.
- Mental age derived from standardised scores influences IQ calculation:
  - **IQ Formula**: IQ = mental age ÷ chronological age × 100.
- Intelligence tests applied in personnel selection for job performance prediction.

### 8.7 Theoretical Models of Intelligence

#### 8.7.1 Spearman’s General (g) Factor Theory

- Understand the concept of emotion.
- Explain major theories of emotion.

### 9.1 Introduction
- Emotions combine physiological sensations and conscious feelings.
- Not solely human; expressed by other primates (Darwin).
- Enhance survival and communication; predate spoken language (Cacioppo & Freberg, 2013).

---

### 9.2 Defining Emotion
- **Emotion vs. Mood**: 
  - Emotion: Intense, subjective state in response to experiences (Santrock, 2003).
  - Mood: Prolonged, less intense, not directly response-related.

---

### 9.3 Theories of Emotions

#### 9.3.1 James-Lange Theory
- Emotions arise from physiological arousal; feeling occurs after arousal.
- Distinct arousal patterns correspond to different emotions.

#### 9.3.2 Cannon-Bard Theory
- Physiological arousal and emotional experience are simultaneous but independent events (Lang, 1994).
- Challenges James-Lange; supports simultaneous emotional experience.

#### 9.3.3 Two-Factor Theory
- Emotions consist of physiological arousal and cognitive interpretation (Schachter & Singer, 1962).
- Context of arousal crucial for emotional experience.

#### 9.3.4 Cognitive-Mediational Theory
- Lazarus (1991): Emotions stem from the appraisal of stimuli, which occurs before cognitive labeling.
- Zajonc: Emotions can occur independently of cognitive processing (Zajonc, 1980).
- LeDoux (1996): Some emotions, like fear, may bypass cognition and rely on neurological pathways.

---

### 9.4 Components of Emotions

#### 9.4.1 Cognitive Component
- Knowledge component with interpretive and evaluative aspects.
- Determines emotional response based on memory and prior experiences (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).

#### 9.4.2 Behavioral Component
- Outward expressions through verbal/non-verbal behaviors (Van Deventer & Mojapelo-Batka, 2013).
- Universal expressions of basic emotions guide action tendencies (e.g., fight or flight).

#### 9.4.3 Physiological Component
- Close ties to physiological processes governed by the autonomic nervous system.
- Response intensity varies with emotional intensity (Weiten & Hassim, 2016).

---

### 9.5 Functions of Emotions

#### 9.5.1 Intrapersonal Functions
- Emotions act as rapid information processors for decision-making (Tooby & Cosmides, 2008).
- Prepare behavior by organizing perception, attention, memory, and physiological reactions (Cosmides & Tooby, 2000).
- Influence memory recall, affecting how emotions color our memories (Wang & Ross, 2007).

## 9.5 Functions of Emotions

### 9.5.2 Interpersonal Functions of Emotions
- Emotions impact relationships and interact through:
  - **Verbal** (words) and **non-verbal** (facial expressions, gestures) cues.
- Emotional expressions have **signal value** affecting social interactions (Hwang & Matsumoto, 2020).
- They provide insight into feelings, intentions, and relationships.
- Emotions serve to:
  - Solve social problems.
  - Evoke responses, indicating relationship nature (Keltner, 2003).
- **Facial expressions** are universal signals affecting social interaction:
  - **Fear** prompts approach behaviour; **anger** prompts avoidance (Marsh et al., 2005).
- Emotional expressions inform about **interpersonal relationships** and emotional states.

### 9.5.3 Sociocultural Functions of Emotions
- Emotions contribute to societal and cultural functions, shaping:
  - Communication and interaction norms.
- Cultural variations influence emotional expression and perception.
- **Cultural transmission**:
  - Shapes worldviews and emotional expectations (Matsumoto & Hwang, 2013).
- **Display rules** regulate emotional expression frequency and appropriateness across cultures (Friesen, 1972).
- **Norms** related to emotion control ensure social order and effective functioning:
  - Influence emotional management and societal behaviour.
- Emotional expressions serve as cues for desired social behaviours (e.g., social referencing - Klinnert et al., 1983).

## 9.6 Emotional Intelligence (EI)

### 9.6.1 Perception and Expression of Emotion
- Ability to **identify** emotions via facial expressions, tone, and body language (Brackett et al., 2013).
- Involves expressing emotions and communicating needs effectively.

### 9.6.2 Use of Emotion to Facilitate Thinking
- Emotions can enhance cognitive functioning and adaptiveness.
- Skillful individuals use emotions to achieve specific outcomes:
  - Certain emotions may boost performance while others may hinder it (Mayer & Salovey, 1997).

### 9.6.3 Understanding of Emotion
- Ability to differentiate between emotional states and their causes.
- Recognizes emotional trajectories and mixed emotions, facilitating effective handling (Feldman et al., 2001).

### 9.6.4 Management of Emotion
- Involves openness to a range of emotions and applying strategies to control them (Gross, 1998). 
- Understanding when to experience emotions and managing responses to prevent negative outcomes is crucial.

## 9.7 Summary
- Overview of emotions’ roles highlights theories and functions.
- Emotional intelligence encompasses various abilities aiding communication and interaction.

### 9.8 Glossary

- **Cannon-Bard Theory of Emotions**: Physiological arousal and emotional experience occur simultaneously and independently.
  
- **Cognitive-Mediational Theory of Emotions**: Emotions are determined by immediate, unconscious appraisal of stimuli.

- **Cultural Display Rules**: Culturally specific standards governing types and frequencies of emotional displays.

- **Culture**: Shared ideas (values, beliefs, attitudes) reflected and reinforced by institutions, products, and rituals.

- **Emotion**: A feeling involving physiological arousal, conscious experience, and behavioral expression.

- **Emotional Intelligence (EI)**: Ability to monitor one's and others' feelings/emotions, discriminate among them, and use this information to guide thinking/actions.

- **Evaluative Aspect of Emotion**: Characterization of emotions as pleasant or unpleasant.

- **Gut Feeling**: Instantaneous and unexplainable feeling experienced for someone or something.

- **Interpretive Aspect of Emotion**: Concerned with the interpretation of situations based on memory.

- **James-Lange Theory of Emotions**: Emotions arise from physiological arousal.

- **Mood**: Prolonged, less intense affective state not linked to specific experiences.

- **Polygraph (Lie Detector)**: Measures physiological arousal in response to questions.

- **Social Referencing**: Seeking information from others to clarify situations and guide actions.

- **Two-Factor Theory of Emotions**: Considers physiological arousal and cognitive content of emotional experiences.
