## Chapter 1: Volunteerism and Community Engagement

### 1.1 Importance of Volunteering
- Volunteering fosters connections and combats feelings of isolation.
- Personal experiences shape motivations to volunteer.
- Quote by Barack Obama emphasizes action over despair.

### 1.2 Motivations for Volunteering
- **Akintola (2011)**: Identified motivations among AIDS caregivers:
  - Altruistic concerns for others
  - Employment or career benefits
  - Avoiding idleness (especially among the unemployed)
  - Learning skills or utilizing existing skills for personal growth
  - Seeking community recognition or responding to a religious call
  - Addressing personal experiences related to AIDS
  
### 1.3 Implications of Volunteer Motivations
- Poor alignment between organizational goals and volunteer motivations leads to volunteer dissatisfaction and attrition.
- Understanding volunteer motivations is crucial for effective recruitment and retention strategies.

### 2.1 Types of Communities
- **Geographical**: Neighborhoods (e.g., Soshanguve)

### 2.3 Considerations for Community Exploration
- Acknowledge potential physical limitations or safety issues during exploration.

## How volunteering differs from employment

### Differences between volunteering and employment
- **Time Limitation**: 
  - More restricted time commitment; volunteers communicate availability upfront.
  
- **Role Uncertainty**: 
  - Ambiguity in duties; volunteers may lack clear expectations.
  
- **Disciplinary Challenges**: 
  - Absence of formal contracts makes volunteer discipline complex; volunteers should still commit fully.

**Source**: Farmer, M & Fedor, DB. (1999). Volunteer participation and withdrawal: A psychological contract perspective on role of expectations and organizational support. *Nonprofit Management & Leadership*, 9(4), 349-367.

## Six Decolonial Axioms

- **Community Psychology's Colonial Roots**
  - Community Psychology as a response to colonial past.
  - Calls for decolonisation of society, universities, and psychology.

### 1. People are shaped by history, politics, economics, and culture
- Individual experiences and perceptions are context-specific.
- Psychology must engage with historical and political realities.
- Consider how personal and organizational understandings reflect these themes.

### 2. Decolonisation happens in institutions, relationships, and individuals
- Decolonisation transcends state power; it encompasses mindset and social constructs.
- Reflect on organizational responses to colonial and apartheid legacies regarding culture, race, class, and gender.

### 3. “Psychology” and “Community Psychology” may not be useful categories
- Disciplines are historically limited; potential for reimagining their frameworks.
- Explore ideas about reclassifying Psychology and Community Psychology within broader contexts (e.g., politics, Development Studies).

### 4. Knowledge can come from many sources
- Knowledge transcends academia; includes local community knowledge and perspectives from the Global South.
- Identify various knowledge sources within volunteer organization.

### 5. Knowledge becomes known and accepted in different ways
- Scientific validation is not the only criterion for knowledge acceptance.
- Promote respectful listening and openness to diverse forms of knowledge.

### 6. Knowledge products can take many forms
- Emphasize non-traditional forms of knowledge (arts, media) beyond academic writing.
- Recognize the complexity of knowledge dissemination amidst potential misinformation.

## Appendix D: Stakeholder Analysis Matrix

- **Stakeholder Name**
- **Impact**: Determines how much the project affects them (low, medium, high).
- **Influence**: Assesses their power over the project (low, medium, high).
- **Important to Stakeholder**: Identifies stakeholder priorities.
- **Contribution**: Ways the stakeholder can aid the project.
- **Blocking the Project**: Potential actions to impede the project.
- **Engagement Strategy**: Plan for stakeholder involvement.

### Examples
- **Nurses & Midwives Union**
  - Impact: High
  - Influence: High
  - Important: Maintaining working conditions
  - Contribution: Agree to implement reforms
  - Blocking: Strike
  - Strategy: Monthly round-table discussions

- **Sunday Times Newspaper**
  - Impact: Low
  - Influence: High
  - Important: Good story coverage
  - Contribution: Print supportive stories
  - Blocking: Print opposing stories
  - Strategy: Quarterly press meetings

## Appendix E: Principles of Community Psychology

### Overview 
- Key principles reflect the foundations of Community Psychology and guide research and action.

### Principles
- **Empowerment**
  - Critical to understand political dimensions; addresses disempowerment and promotes self-determination.
  - Individual and collective empowerment are vital for access to resources and participation.
  
- **Respect for Diversity**
  - Emphasizes mutual respect for varied identities; individual-environment fit is crucial.
  - Challenges oppressive norms and values differences among community subgroups.
  
- **Social Justice**
  - Engages in movements against inequality; focuses on resource distribution and workers' rights amid global capitalism.
  
- **Caring, Compassion, and Sense of Community**
  - Aligns with humanistic psychology, viewing community connections as integral to wellbeing and shared commitment.
  
- **Risk Prevention and Health Promotion**
  - Focuses on preemptive measures to modify conditions that increase community risks.
  - Implements programs to address factors jeopardizing community wellbeing.
