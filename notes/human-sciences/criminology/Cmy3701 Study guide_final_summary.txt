## STUDY UNIT 1.1: The classical school: the offender as calculator  

### 1.1.1 INTRODUCTION  
- Classical school of criminology emerged during the 17th and 18th centuries' Age of Enlightenment.
- Cesare Beccaria: Father of classical criminology; advocated preventing crime rather than punishing.  
- Shift from tradition/religion to rationality and individual responsibility.  
- Opposition to ineffective and inhumane criminal justice systems.  
- Essential elements of an effective criminal justice system:  
  - Predictability  
  - Non-discriminatory  
  - Humane and effective punishment  
- Principles of punishment (Eassey & Boman, 2015: 1–2):  
  - **Certainty**: Assurance of punishment for illegal behavior.  
  - **Swiftness**: Prompt punishment to maintain the crime-punishment association.  
  - **Severity**: Proportional punishment to deter criminal acts.  
- Jeremy Bentham: Contributed hedonistic calculus idea, viewing punishment as a deterrent.

### 1.1.2 ASSUMPTIONS OF THE CLASSICAL SCHOOL OF CRIMINOLOGY  
- **Human nature: Rationality**  
  - Individuals are self-interested, rational, and capable of free will.  
  - Criminal behavior viewed as a failure of rational choice, leading to prison for reform.  

- **Conception of society or social order**  
  - Chaos results from conflicting self-interests.  
  - Criminal laws protect individual rights under a social contract; violations justify state punishment (Matsueda, 2013: 286).  

- **Causes of crime**  
  - Rational beings weigh pleasure against the pain of punishment; irrational decisions possible due to misleading information or insufficient penalties.  

- **Implications for policy**  
  - Strict adherence to due process; well-defined and consistently applied legal codes.  
  - Minimization of judicial discretion in sentencing; known and just punishments necessary to deter crime (Coleman & Norris, 2000: 9).  

### 1.1.3 LIMITATIONS OF THE CLASSICAL SCHOOL OF CRIMINOLOGY: NEOCLASSICAL SCHOOL OF THOUGHT  
- Flaws in identical punishments for identical crimes; recognized need for consideration of circumstances.  
- Acknowledgement that free will can be limited by factors like poverty or mental state.  
- Revisions led to neoclassical criminology, which still holds individuals accountable but allows for context-based considerations (Netterville, 2014: 1).  
- Introduced non-legal experts into the criminal justice system for individual assessments and more appropriate sentencing (Burke, 2017: 40–41).  
- Central attributes (Burke, 2017: 43–44):  
  - Focus on criminal law and its definition.  
  - Punishment fitting the crime, not the offender.  
  - Free will as a guiding doctrine.  
  - Lack of empirical research in methodologies.  

### 1.1.4 SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION  
- Classical theorists: Criminal behavior results from rational decisions weighing risks and rewards.  
- Serious criminals deserve punishment; deterrence relies on the severity, certainty, and swiftness of punishment.  
- Neoclassical school maintains the core ideas of free will and rationality while acknowledging factors influencing choice.  
- Contributions sparked ongoing debate about understanding crime and offending; results led to theories in Theme 2 (positivist perspective).

## STUDY UNIT 1.2: Contemporary rational choice theories

### 1.2.1 Introduction
- Classical ideas influence modern criminology, emphasizing rational behavior of offenders.
- Shift from classical to positivist theories in 1870s, and back to classical ideas post-1970s.
- **Routine Activities** (Cohen and Felson, 1978) and **Rational Choice** (Clarke and Cornish, 1985) theories focus on offender decision-making and opportunity.

### 1.2.2 Routine Activities Theory
- Developed by **Cohen and Felson**; states crime requires:
  - Motivated offender
  - Suitable victim
  - Lack of capable guardians
- Changes in lifestyle lead to opportunities for crime due to absence of guardianship.
- Elements include:
  - **Suitable targets** (criteria: VIVA - Value, Inertia, Visibility, Accessibility).
  - **Absence of guardians** (police, homeowners, etc.).
  - **Presence of motivated offenders**.

### STUDY UNIT 1.2: Contemporary rational choice theories

#### 1.2.1 Routine Activities Theory
- **Convergence of Crime Elements:**
  - **Motivated Offender**
  - **Suitable Target**
  - **Absence of Capable Guardian**
- **Crime Example:** Young women drinking excessively seen as easy targets for date rape (Burke, 2005:46). 

#### 1.2.2 Evaluation of the Routine Activities Theory
- **Primary Goal:** Identify environmental triggers for crime (Cote, 2002:297).
- **Focus:** Crime of place vs. crime of person.
- **Critics' Views:**
  - Ignores offender motivation.
  - Potential victim-blaming (McLaughlin, 2006:366).
- **Insights for Crime Prevention:** 
  - Surveillance and urban planning strategies by Felson (Cote, 2002:298).

#### 1.2.2.2 Concise Summation of the Routine Activities Theory
- Focus on **Opportunity** and **Lifestyle Influences**.
  - **Factors Affecting Victim Risk:**
    - Proximity to criminals
    - Time exposed to criminals
    - Attractiveness as a target
    - Protection ability (Siegel, 2004:94)
- **Key Theory Outcome:** Reduce intersection of offenders and unguarded targets to prevent crime.

#### 1.2.3 Rational Choice Perspective
- **Cornish and Clarke's Model (1986):** 
  - Crime involves background factors influencing criminal motivations (Lilly et al, 2018:361).
- **Crime Decisions:** Involves a sequence of choices, not just motivations.

#### 1.2.3.1 Propositions of the Rational Choice Perspective Theory
1. **Deliberate Acts:** Committed to benefit offenders.
2. **Decision-Making:** Affected by risks and uncertainties.
3. **Variation in Decisions:** Depends on crime nature.
4. **Involvement vs. Event Decisions:** Involvement decisions (initiation, habituation, desistance) differ from specific act decisions.
5. **Stages of Involvement:**
   - **Initiation:** Commitment readiness.
   - **Habituation:** Continuation of offending.
   - **Desistance:** Decision to stop.
6. **Event Decisions:** Involves specific choices during the criminal act (preparation, target selection, execution).

#### 1.2.3.2 Bounded Rationality
- **Rational Behavior:** Limited understanding and decision-making due to constraints (Newburn, 2007:280).
- **Criminal Choice Process:**
  - Involvement influenced by personal learning experiences and situational context.

#### 1.2.3.4 Evaluation of the Rational Choice Perspective Theory
- **Promise for Crime Reduction:** Focus on situational crime prevention (e.g. alarm systems, increased security).
- **Danger of Oversimplification:** Treat offenders purely as rational decision-makers ignores social contexts, potentially leading to harsh policies (Wortley, 2013:259).

### 1.2.4 SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION
- **Routine Activities Theory:** Focuses on convergence of offender, target, and guardian.
- **Rational Choice Theory:** Highlights personal choice and deterrence concepts, with underlying assumption that opportunities will lead to crime if not prevented.
- **Commonalities:** Both theories emphasize circumstances shaping criminal acts.

### 2.1.1 INTRODUCTION

- Theoretical frameworks are essential for studying crime and criminal behavior.
- Diverse criminological perspectives exist; none are entirely accurate but each provides insights into crime.
- Integration of theories bridging classical and positivist criminology is encouraged.
- Focus shifts from theorizing to understanding variables related to crime.

### 2.1.2 DEFINITIONS OF KEY CONCEPTS

  - A deterministic approach viewing offenders as influenced by uncontrollable biological, psychological, or sociological forces.

- **Psychological Positivism**:
  - Examines internal factors like neurological disorders and personality disturbances of the offender.

## STUDY UNIT 2.1: The positivist school: the offender as predestined actor

### 2.1.3 THE POSITIVIST SCHOOL OF THOUGHT
- Late 19th century origins; Italian scientists.
- Focus on measurable interactions and social processes.
- Emphasis on scientific treatment vs. punishment of offenders (Reid, 2018; Siegel, 2018).
- Key figures: **Cesare Lombroso** (1835–1909), **Raffaele Garofalo** (1852–1934).
- Emerged in opposition to classical school's harsh views; addressed causes of criminal behavior (Hunter & Dantzker, 2002).
- Classical school: Free will, punishment to deter crime; limited effectiveness in addressing crime.
- Changes post-Industrial Revolution; questioning traditional knowledge sources (Glick 2005).
- **Positivists** reject legalism, favor determinism; focus on constitutional aspects influencing behavior (Reid, 2018).
- Advocated individual, scientific treatment based on physical/social sciences (Reid 2018).
- Shift from speculative philosophy to factual, scientific study of human behavior (Glick, 2005).
- **Auguste Comte** (1798–1857): Founder of sociology; emphasized external/internal forces in human behavior.
- Advocated empirical study of crime origins (Frampton, 2013).
- Positivism views behavior as influenced by external social forces and internal capabilities.

### 2.1.4 ASSUMPTIONS OF THE POSITIVIST SCHOOL OF THOUGHT
- Character and personal backgrounds explain criminal behavior, focusing on offender characteristics.
- Scientific determinism exists; crime determined by prior causes, rejecting free will.
- Offender fundamentally different from non-offender; identified factors influencing criminality.
- Role of experts (psychologists, social workers) in diagnosing conditions leading to criminality.
- Individualized treatment necessary; arguments for indeterminate sentences based on offender assessment.

### 2.1.5 EVALUATION OF THE POSITIVIST SCHOOL
- Contributed to scientific study of criminal behavior; emphasized empirical research and determinism.
- Suggests individuals lack free will/control (Joyce, 2006); implies passive behavior impacted by biology/environment (Moyer, 2001).
- Criticism: Neglects human rationality and choice; focuses on treatment over individual responsibility (Newburn, 2007).
- Methodological flaws: non-select samples, limited follow-up studies, unclearly defined concepts (Reid, 2006).
- Positivism shaped contemporary criminology; recognized need to study individual and societal crime causes (Cote, 2002).

### 2.1.6 CONCLUSION
- Emergence of positivism addresses deficiencies in classical approaches to crime control.
- Positivism emphasizes individual and social factors in criminality.
- Rejects free will; views crime causation as complex, incorporating societal factors.
- Three formulations of predestined actor model: biological, sociological, psychological positivism.

### 2.2.1 INTRODUCTION  
- Focus on biological positivism as resurgence in studying biology and psychology’s relationship to criminal behaviour.
- Biological positivism’s foundations lie in Lombroso, Ferri, and Garofalo's work; posits that criminality has a biological basis.

### 2.2.2 DEFINITIONS OF KEY CONCEPTS AND TERMS  
- **Adoption studies**: Study children's behaviour in relation to biological and adoptive parents; results inconclusive.  
- **Biological positivism**: Claims crime is due to physical aspects beyond individual control (Burke, 2005:281).  
- **Biosocial theories**: Examines interactions of biology, behaviour, and environment to explain criminality (Akers & Sellers, 2013:11).  
- **IQ (Intelligence Quotient)**: Measures cognitive abilities relative to peers.  
- **Genetic factors**: Heritability of traits and behaviours across generations (Winfree, Jr & Abadinsky, 2017:42).  
- **Personality**: Descriptive of consistent emotional and temperamental attributes influencing behaviour (Jones, 2001:398).  

### 2.2.3 EARLY THEORIES OF BIOLOGICAL POSITIVISM  
- Two periods identified:  
  - **First Period**: Nature vs. nurture debate; behaviour as a product of genetics.  
  - **Second Period (sociobiology)**: Interaction of biology and environment acknowledged.  
- Early biological theories focused on physical attributes, associating criminality with biological inferiority; **Franz Joseph and Lombroso** focused on physical traits and their behavioral implications.

### 2.2.4 LOMBROSO AND THE POSITIVISTS  
- **Lombroso’s Theory**: Proposed "born criminals" identifiable by physical stigmata (e.g., lower jaw, nose shape).  
- Criminals as genetic throwbacks, indicating evolutionarily inferior qualities.  
- Introduced concepts of **“born criminals,” criminaloids**, and recognized environmental factors alongside biology.  
- Contributions included a shift in focus towards individual offenders and advocacy for scientific methodology in criminal studies.   
- Continued by **Ferri and Garofalo**:  
  - **Ferri**: Three categories of criminals—born bad, insane, or circumstantially motivated; incorporated socio-environmental factors.  
  - **Garofalo**: Emphasized scientific methodology; defined criminals as developmentally deficient regarding social concern.

## STUDY UNIT 2.2: Biological Positivism

### 2.2.4.1 Physique and Crime
- **William H. Sheldon**: Proposed body types: 
  - Ectomorph: Tall, skinny 
  - Endomorph: Short, fat 
  - Mesomorph: Athletic 
- Aimed to link body type with temperament and criminal behavior (Reid, 2018:68).
- Criticisms:
  - Sample selection issues in Sheldon's research.
  - **Glueck Research**: Studied 500 delinquents vs. 500 non-delinquents, concluded links between body type and delinquency.
  - Critiques focused on:
    - Not astonishing findings of delinquency and body type correlation.
    - Limited generalizability from "persistently delinquent" samples.
    - Vague measurement techniques.

### 2.2.5 Biosocial Theories
- *Four biosocial theories outlined in Figure 2.2.2*.

#### 2.2.5.1 Eysenck's Biosocial Theory of Crime
- **Hans Eysenck**: Believed genetics and environment interact to shape human behavior (Healey, 2012:6).
- Biosocial theories assert:
  - Biological factors increase likelihood of criminal actions without determining them (Glick, 2005:88).
  - Emphasize interaction of biological and social environments.

#### 2.2.5.1.1 Recent Interest in Biological Theories
- Distinguishing features of recent biosocial theorists:
  - Focus on a broad array of biological factors (e.g., genetics, toxins).
  - Stance that biology influences, but does not dictate, behaviour (Cote, 2002:36).
  - Recognition of social environment’s role in behaviour development.

#### 2.2.5.1.2 Biosocial Theory Principles (Siegel, 2004:141)
- Genetic makeup significantly influences behavior.
- Unequal potential among humans for learning and achievement.
- Individual uniqueness (exceptions being identical twins).
- Combination of genetics and environment shapes behavior patterns.
- Categories: Genetic, Biochemical, Neurophysiological.
- Importance of understanding genetic and environmental interplay (Glick, 2005:88).

### 2.2.5.2 Genetic Factors
- Criminality considered hereditary like physical traits (Burke, 2005:8).
- **Sources of Evidence**:
  - Family studies.
  - Twin studies.
  - Adoption studies.
- Critiques of early methodology questioned rigor of findings (Reid, 2018:69).

#### 2.2.5.2.1 Studies of Families

### 2.3.1 Freud’s Id, Ego, and Superego
- **Id**: Represents primitive impulses and drives, seeking pleasure without morality or concern for others. Uncontrolled libido linked to deviant behaviours. 
- **Ego**: The conscious and rational segment that mediates between id impulses and the superego's demands, operates on the reality principle. 
- **Superego**: Represents social conscience and morality. Emerges from internalizing parental and societal values, leading to self-criticism and moral judgment.

### 2.3.2 Explanation of Criminal Behaviour
- **Conflict**: Unresolved childhood conflicts may lead to future problem behaviour; these conflicts are often kept unconscious via defense mechanisms.
- **Defense Mechanisms**: Can prevent anxiety and guilt, but overreliance may lead to issues like interpersonal conflict or mental disorders. 
  - Examples include **displacement** and **sublimation** as responses to guilt.
- **Freud's View on Criminals**: Criminals may unconsciously desire punishment due to guilt, and an overdeveloped superego can result in aggressive energy being expressed violently.

### 2.3.3 Evaluation of the Psychoanalytic Perspective on Criminal Behaviour
- Criticisms include lack of testability and reliance on interpretative case studies post-behaviour occurrence.
- Few modern psychologists utilize Freudian concepts due to their weak empirical support, though early life experiences are noted for their impact on delinquency.

### 2.3.4 BEHAVIOURAL PERSPECTIVE
- Originating in the 1800s; highlighted by B.F. Skinner's work.
- Focus on observable behaviour rather than unconscious drives, with behaviours shaped by learning experiences and social interactions.

#### 2.3.4.1 Classical Conditioning
- **Definition**: Involves learning through stimulus pairing; automatic responses to stimuli develop.
- **Example**: Pavlov’s dogs salivating in response to a bell due to conditioning.
- Minimal relevance to crime since punishment does not immediately follow criminal acts.

#### 2.3.4.2 Operant Conditioning
- **Definition**: Involves behaviour modification through reinforcement (increased behaviour) or punishment (decreased behaviour).
- **Key Elements**: Antecedent conditions, behaviour itself, and consequences must be considered.
- Behaviour shaping occurs via rewards or punishments, emphasizing the interaction between individual and environment.

#### 2.3.4.3 Social Learning Theory
- Variations include expectancy theory, imitation, and modeling.
- **Proponents**: Albert Bandura.
- Key Principle: Aggressive behaviour is learned through observation and imitation of role models in the environment.

- **Key references**: **Brown et al. (2015)**; the frustrations and distortions in lower-class aspirations lead to illegitimate means for goal attainment.

- Low socioeconomic neighborhoods develop **sub-cultures** with distinct value systems reacting to social isolation and deprivation (Siegel, 2019:192).

- **Strain theories** highlight societal pressures causing frustration leading to crime.

  - Dominance of **social factors** (poverty, inequality) in criminal behavior.

## STUDY UNIT 3.3: Anomie and Strain

### 3.3.1 Introduction
- Merton’s strain theory, derived from Durkheim’s concept of *anomie*.
- *Anomie* refers to normlessness leading to social malaise when control over individual needs wanes.

### 3.3.2 Definitions of Key Concepts
- **Anomie:** Normative deregulation; lack of societal rules (Walsh & Hemmens, 2008).
- **Anomie Theories:** 
  - **Durkheim's view:** Normlessness occurs in rapid change.
  - **Merton's view:** Individuals pursue illegitimate means for socially desired ends (Burke, 2005).
- **Mechanical Solidarity:** Exists in pre-industrial societies; strong common values (Walsh & Hemmens, 2008).
- **Modes of Adaptation:** Merton's classifications (conformity, ritualism, innovation, retreatism, rebellion).
- **Organic Solidarity:** Modern societies with specialization and weakened norms (Walsh & Hemmens, 2008).

### 3.3.3 Durkheim’s View on Crime
- Shift from individual to socio-cultural explanations of crime.
- **Key Assertions:**
  - Crime is normal in societies.
  - Crime serves a functional role.

#### (a) Crime as a Normal Phenomenon
- Diversity in societal values leads to normative breaches (e.g., drug abuse).
- Identifying norm violators fosters community bonding (Tibbetts, 2019).
- Crime helps validate societal norms and boundaries of behavior.

#### (b) Crime is Functional

## STUDY UNIT 3.3: Anomie and Strain  

### 3.3.1 Introduction  
- **Merton's Argument**: Social conditions exert uneven pressure on different classes, leading some individuals to commit crimes due to environmental influences (Fattah, 1997:237).  
- **Nature of People**: People are inherently good; deviations from norms reflect social environment failure (Cullen & Wilcox, 2010:613).  
- **Anomie**: Breakdown in cultural beliefs (e.g., American Dream) leading to normlessness.  

### 3.3.2 Merton's Theory  
- **Legitimate Means**: Society remains non-anomic when all members use legitimate means for advancement.  
- **Strain Theory**: Pressure to achieve desires leads to various personality adaptations resulting from the strain between desires and available means (Chamberlain, 2015:80).  

### 3.3.3 Assumptions of Merton’s Theory  
- **Cultural Goals vs. Institutional Means**:  
  - Cultural Goals = Values society aims to achieve (e.g., wealth, status).  
  - Institutional Means = Resources and methods to achieve these goals (Triplett, 2018:143).  
- **Imbalance**: Greater emphasis on success than means leads to anomie (Siegel & Senna, 2000:145).  

### 3.3.4 Patterns of Crime  
- **Lower-Class Crime Rates**: Higher crime incidence among lower classes due to restricted access to legitimate means; results in strain and use of illegal means (Siegel, 2018:196–197).  

#### 3.3.4.1 Crime as a Lower-Class Phenomenon  
- Lower classes seek wealth like higher classes but lack legal opportunities, resulting in anomic strain and illegal means of income.  

#### 3.3.4.2 Reactions to Anomie  
- **Conformity**: Acceptance of both goals and means; largest group, maintaining societal stability (Cullen & Wilcox, 2010:17).  
- **Innovation**: Deviant pursuit of goals by using any means, often illegal (Cullen & Wilcox, 2010:17).  
- **Ritualism**: Acceptance of means but rejection of goals; no ambition for improvement, merely following rules (Cullen & Wilcox, 2010:17).  
- **Retreatism**: Rejection of both goals and means, leading to withdrawal from societal norms (Cullen & Wilcox, 2010:17).  
- **Rebellion**: Complete rejection and replacement of goals and means; may involve activism or violent rebellion (Williams, 2004:308).  

#### 3.3.4.3 Evaluation of Merton’s Theory  
- **Sociological Insight**: Explains crime concentration in lower classes and minorities; relevant beyond American context (e.g., South Africa).  
- **Limitations**: Does not explain individual offenses or motivations; overlooks white-collar crime, implying lawlessness is a lower-class issue.  

### 3.3.5 Summary and Conclusion  
- **Definition of Anomie**: Normlessness resulting from weak normative societal orders (Krohn et al, 2019:162).  
- **Societal Control**: Anomie leads to a breakdown in controlling behavior; high crime rates may indicate anomic conditions.  
- **Consensus of Values**: Uneven distribution of means and overemphasis on goals lead to disorganization and anomie.  
- **Structural Inequality**: Causal risk factor for deviance and crime.

### The Victimized Actor Model
- **General Introduction**
  - **Rational Actor Model**: Individuals possess free will and make rational choices, including criminal behavior.
  - **Predestined Actor Model**: Crime arises from uncontrollable biological, psychological, or social factors.
  - **Victimized Actor Model**: Proposes that societal structures target behaviors of the disadvantaged while ignoring misconduct of the powerful (Burke, 2005:138).
  - **Foundations**: Based on labelling and conflict theories, focusing on institutional roles in crime.

## STUDY UNIT 5.1: The Labelling Perspective
### Definitions of Key Concepts
- **Labelling Perspective**: Describes offending as a reaction to societal labels, which can create self-fulfilling prophecies among offenders (Burke, 2014:14).
- **Primary Deviance**: Initial acts of deviance not altering self-identity.
- **Secondary Deviance**: Offender accepts deviant label, leading to identity transformation (Siegel, 2018:585).

### Interactionist Approach
#### Edwin M. Lemert
- Labelling triggers a stigma affecting self-image and behavior.
- **Primary Deviance**: Undetected deviant acts lacking significant impact on identity.
- **Secondary Deviance**: Offender internalizes negative labels, often leading to group formation.
  
### Social Response Approach
#### Howard Becker’s Contributions
- Focus on societal reactions to behavior rather than the behavior's causes.
- Analyzed subcultures and processes of deviance (Becker, 1963).

### Segments of Becker’s Contribution:
1. **Labelling as a Cause of Crime**
2. **The Process of Labelling**
3. **Typology of Deviants**

### Notes:
- Labelling theory emphasizes the role of social interactions in the definition and perception of deviance.
- The theory critiques traditional approaches by focusing on societal reactions rather than the deviant acts themselves (Bernburg, 2019:1).

#### 5.1.4.1 Labelling as a cause of crime
- **Becker's Theory:** Differentiates legislative rules versus marginal subculture norms.
- **Impact of Labelling:** Limits law-abiding opportunities, fostering deviant choices.
- **Successful Offender:** Requires techniques/resources (e.g., knowledge of drug use).
- **Societal Perception:** Criminal acts as deviant push offenders towards crime.

#### 5.1.4.2 The process of labelling
- **Focus of Becker's Study:** How individuals are labeled as outsiders. Deviance seen as a social product.
- **Phases of Labelling:**
  - **Phase 1:** Deviant action performed (unconscious).
  - **Phase 2:** Being caught leads to new status/label; stigma generalizes.
  - **Phase 3:** Achieves master status; deviant label overshadows positive traits (self-fulfilling prophecy as per Bernburg, 2019).
  - **Final Phase:** Joins organized deviant groups for rationalization and support (Bernburg, 2019).

#### 5.1.4.3 A typology of deviants
- **Deviant vs. Non-deviant (Becker/Siegel 2018):** 
  - **Pure Deviant:** Engages in norm-violating actions recognized as such.
  - **Falsely Accused Deviant:** Innocent yet misjudged, leading to negative self-image.
  - **Conformist:** Accurately perceived as non-deviant; no norm violations.
  - **Secret Deviant:** Engages in norm violations that go undetected.

- **Table 5.1: Becker’s Typology of Deviant and Non-Deviant Behavior**
  - Conforming Behavior: Conformist, Secret Deviant
  - Norm-Violating Behavior: Pure Deviant, Falsely Accused

#### 5.1.5 Evaluation of the labelling perspective
- **Labelling Focus:** Stigmatization by society is key; behavior less important than reactions.
- **Key Questions:**
  - Who labels whom?
  - Who decides on labels?
  - What creates these labels?
  - How are labels attached?
- **Agents of Control:** Primarily the criminal justice system; powerful groups dictate what's deviant.
- **Critique of Traditional Theories:** Traditional views argue deviancy leads to control; labelling argues the opposite.
- **Broader Application:** Labelling theory extends beyond crime to mental illness and other deviances.

#### 5.1.6 Summary and Conclusion
- **Two Components of Labelling Theories:**
  - **Social Response:** Focus on responses to deviance.
  - **Secondary Deviance:** Development and impact of labels.
- **Becker's Contribution:** Illustrates the social process of becoming an outsider.
- **Critical Perspective:** Challenges middle-class definitions of deviance; highlights humane treatment of offenders.
- **Link to Conflict Theories:** Labelling perspective as precursor to conflict theories.

### 5.2.1 Introduction
- Conflict theories emerged mid 20th century, paralleling labelling theories.
- Focus on political nature of crime and legislation.
- Conflict theories analyze power dynamics in societal interactions.
  
### 5.2.2 Definitions of Key Concepts
- **Conflict Model of Crime**:
  - Crime definitions vary by group power and social status.
- **Conflict Perspective**:
  - Society comprises conflicting groups; criminal definitions serve those in power.
- **Social Classes**:
  - Defined by shared positions in production systems.

### 5.2.3 Assumptions of Conflict Theory
- Conflict characterizes society, not consensus.
- Majority groups’ power influences the law and its enforcement.
- Key elements of conflict perspective:
  - Diverse social groups with varying moral standards.
  - Natural conflicts arise from significant social distinctions.
  - Legal structures favor those in power.
- Dominance leads to systematic suppression of dissent (Marx & Engels).
- The struggle for resources exists alongside institutional mechanisms to mediate conflict.
  - Example: Rape legislation reflects power imbalances (Barlow & Kauzlarich).

#### 5.2.4 THE RADICAL (MARXIST) CONFLICT PERSPECTIVE

- **Introduction**
  - Radical criminology emerged in the 1970s, influenced by 19th-century utopian thinkers, especially **Karl Marx**.
  - Focuses on conflict as inherent to capitalism (Schmalleger, 1996).

- **The Legacy of Marx and Bonger**
  - **Marx's View**:
    - Economic production is core to societal structure and consciousness (Brown et al, 2013).
    - Society divides into two classes: **proletariat** (working class) and **bourgeoisie** (capitalists).
    - Conflict leads to class consciousness and potential revolution towards communism (Barlow & Kauzlarich, 2010).
  
  - **Bonger's Contribution**:
    - Higher crime rates in capitalist societies; crime increases with capitalism.
    - Self-interest is encouraged, leading to greed and crime, especially among lower classes (Barlow & Kauzlarich, 2010).
    - Criminal law functions to protect ruling class interests; emphasizes exploitation and systemic bias.
    - A socialist society could reduce crime by promoting collective welfare.

#### 5.2.5 EXPONENTS OF THE RADICAL CONFLICT PERSPECTIVE

- **William Chambliss**
  - Work: *Law, Order and Power* (1971).
  - Propositions:
    - Life situations influence values; societies are composed of conflicting norms.
    - Dominant groups enforce laws aligning with their interests.
    - Middle/upper class offenders more likely to evade justice.

- **Richard Quinney**
  - Describes crime's definition as a tool of the dominant class in capitalist societies (McCaghy, 2016).
  - Key propositions:
    - Crime definitions are shaped by the ruling class.
    - Behaviour patterns develop based on crime definitions.
    - Social reality of crime is constructed through communication and enforcement (Quinney, *Class, State and Crime*, 1977).

#### 5.2.6 RADICAL CRIMINOLOGY AND POLICY IMPLICATIONS

- Current radical criminology seeks practical reforms within the existing system:
  - Focus on equity in justice, addressing corporate crime, employment opportunities.
  - Emphasizes gradual transition to socialism and systemic change to reduce crime.

- Criticisms:
  - Overemphasis on social change vs. theoretical clarity.
  - Ignores broader public consensus against crime.
  - Simplifies crime causes, neglecting multiple contributing factors.

#### 5.2.7 SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION

- Conflict and competition for scarce resources drive crime, particularly in capitalist societies.
- Both Marx and Bonger oversimplify crime origins by neglecting middle class influences.
- Chambliss and Quinney continue to emphasize class struggle and survival, advocating for socialist solutions.
- Contemporary radicalists focus on reforms rather than revolution, recognizing complexity in crime causation while still being critiqued for ignoring public consensus on crime's inadvisability.
