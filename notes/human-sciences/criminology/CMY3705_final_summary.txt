## LEARNING UNIT 1: Brief History

### 1.1 OVERVIEW
- Emergence of **Victimology** as a sub-discipline of **Criminology** in the 1940s by **Von Hentig** and **Mendelsohn**.
- Victim marginalization in the criminal justice system; early focus on victim responsibility for crime.

### 1.2 DEFINITION OF CONCEPTS
- **UN Declaration (A/RES/40/34, 1985)** defines victims as individuals or groups suffering harm due to violations of criminal laws.
- **Crime victim**: Any entity suffering injury or loss from illegal activity (harm can be physical, psychological, or economic).
- **Legal definition of victim**: Individuals or entities suffering direct/threatened harm due to crime.
- **Primary victims**: Direct victims of crimes.
- **Secondary victims**: Individuals affected indirectly (e.g., intimate partners).
- **Tertiary victims**: Individuals experiencing harm vicariously (e.g., through media).
- Self-identification as "survivors" due to negative connotations of "victim."

### 1.3 HISTORY OF VICTIMOLOGY
- Evolution documented by **Van Dijk (1997)**; further details in cited sources.

### 1.4 HISTORICAL SOURCES ON VICTIMOLOGY
- **Von Hentig, H. (1948)**: *The criminal and his victim*.
- **Karmen, A. (1992)**: *Crime victims*.
- **Mendelsohn, B. (1963)**: *The Origin of the Doctrine of Victimology*.
- **Naudé, C.M.B., et al. (2006)**: *Experiences of crime in thirteen African countries*.
- **Newman, O. (1972)**: *Defensible space*.
- **Schwartz, M. & Pitts, V. (1995)**: Study on sexual assault.

## LEARNING UNIT 2: Victim Support and Empowerment

### 2.1 OVERVIEW
- **Secondary victimisation** in the **Criminal Justice System (CJS)**.
- Courts disregarding witness needs lead to secondary victimisation, potentially more traumatic than the initial crime.

### 2.2 DEFINITION OF CONCEPTS
- **Secondary victimisation**: Insensitive or blaming treatment of victims by CJS officials (Vetten, 2001).
- Forms of blaming, influencing the victim's experience negatively.

### 2.3 HOW DOES SECONDARY VICTIMISATION OCCUR?
- Starts when victims report crimes; particularly affecting vulnerable victims (e.g., women in sexual assault cases).
- Insensitivity and disbelief from police initiate secondary victimisation.
- Community and familial resistance exacerbates victim isolation and trauma during the court process.
- Victims often unaware of legal procedures, contributing to distress (Vetten, 2001).

#### Processes contributing to secondary victimisation:
- Lack of private facilities for victims.
- Lack of information on court procedures and updates on case progress.
- Potential rights infringements during court processes, including inappropriate questioning by defence.

## 2.3 WHAT IS VICTIM EMPOWERMENT AND SUPPORT? 
- **Definition**: Process aiding individuals affected by conflict/violence to overcome grief, fostering positivity and proactivity.
- **Purpose**: Equip communities with skills, confidence, and advocacy tools for victims’ rights amidst institutional discrimination.

## 2.4 VICTIM SUPPORT MODELS 

### 2.4.1 The Care Model 
- **Characteristics**: 
  - Services delivered as welfare/charity.
  - Governed by the ideology of care.
- **Examples**: 
  - State compensation schemes (historically introduced 1965-1980).
  - Rape crisis centers, shelters for spouse abuse victims, victim support schemes.
- **Limitations**: 
  - Limited eligibility, burdensome claim processes.

### 2.4.2 The Criminal Justice Model
- **Incorporation of Victims**: Victim involvement in the criminal justice process, including:
  - Notifications of investigation outcomes.
  - Victim Impact Statements rights.
  - Right to restitution and involvement in offender sentencing.
  
### 2.4.3 The Prevention Model 
- **Emphasis**: Integration of victim services in crime prevention.
- **Importance**:
  - Victims’ cooperation is crucial for effective police investigations.
  - Improving self-protection among potential victims.
  - Encouraging socio-economic interventions for at-risk groups.

## 2.5 SOUTH AFRICAN PERSPECTIVE ON VICTIM EMPOWERMENT AND SUPPORT 

### 2.5.1 South African Scenario Regarding Models of Care
- **High Crime Rates**: Due to economic deprivation and suitable targets availability.
- **Victim Dissatisfaction**: Many victims express a need for specialized help not provided.
- **Recommended Policies**: 
  - State compensation schemes.
  - Nationwide victim support networks.
  - Drafting a comprehensive Bill of Rights for crime victims.

### Development of the Victim Empowerment Programme 
- **Framework**: Part of National Crime Prevention Strategy (NCPS), approved in 1996.
- **Objective**: Reduce crime levels and provide a victim-centered approach to crime prevention.
- **Key Outputs**: 
  - Integrated service delivery models to victims.
  - Multi-agency cooperation (e.g., Welfare, SAPS, Justice).

### 3.1 Definition of Domestic Violence 
- **Gelles' Violence Definition**: Distinguishes between "normal" and "abusive" violence:
  - *Normal Violence*: Generally acceptable acts (e.g., slaps, pushes in discipline).
  - *Abusive Violence*: Serious acts (e.g., punches, shootings) with potential for severe injury.
- **Impact of Emotional Violence**: Emotional and psychological violence may have more profound consequences than physical abuse.
- **Tshiwula (1998)**: Defines violence as unlawful physical force, including emotional/spiritual injury; includes patterns of coercive behavior in domestic violence.

### 3.2 Types of Domestic Violence
#### 3.2.1 Physical Abuse
- **Gosselin (2003)**: Encompasses force/threat resulting in bodily harm.
- Signs include bruises, burns, and internal injuries.
- **Moody & McLeod-Butler (2000)**: Physical abuse can cause fear of imminent peril.

#### 3.2.2 Sexual Abuse and Rape
- **Johnson (2006)**: Differentiates physical and sexual violence into three categories involving coercion.
- **South African Legislation**: Rape defined in the *Sexual Offences Act*, Section 3(1) (2003) as unlawful sexual penetration.

#### 3.2.3 Acts of Abusive Sexual Contact
- **National Coalition of Free Men (1990)**: Defines sexual abuse as using another's sexuality outside mutual consent.
- **Davis & Snyman (2005)**: Discuss male victims; sexual assault is about power and control, not sexual attraction.

#### 3.2.4 Economic Abuse
- Definition involves controlling victim's financial resources, preventing education/employment.

#### 3.2.5 Spiritual Abuse
- Involves manipulation of religious beliefs, preventing practice, ridiculing beliefs.

### 3.3 Systems Theory Perspective on Abusive Relationships
- **General Principles**:
  - Systems interact with environments; defined by components—objects, attributes, relationships, and their environment.
  - Open systems adapt and develop; closed systems risk attrition.
- **Family as a System**:
  - Includes interacting individuals; internal dynamics define stability/changes.
  - Violent acts may regulate closeness and distance between partners.
- **Giles-Sims (1986)**: Patterns of conflict may stabilize abusive relationships.

### 3.4 Women as Victims
#### 3.4.1 Women as Victims of Sexual Violence
- **Davis & Snyman (2005)**: Daily threats of violence common for women; most attacks by known individuals.
- Victims often self-blame and fear police disbelief; domestic violence spans all demographics.

## Learning Unit 3: Domestic Violence

### 3.4.2 Psychological Dynamics of Domestic Abuse
- Effects of domestic abuse vary based on individual psyche.
- Key theories explaining intimate partner violence:
  - **Battered Woman’s Syndrome**
  - **Learned Helplessness**
  - **Traumatic Bonding**
  - **Psychological Entrapment**
  - **Separation Assault and Anxiety**
  - **Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder**
- No singular pattern for all abusive relationships (Davis & Snyman, 2005:190–192).

### 3.4.3 Battered Woman’s Syndrome
- Characteristic behaviors of prolonged abuse victims.
- **Cycle of Violence** (Walker, 1984:55–70):
  - **Tension Building Phase**: Victim tries to appease abuser.
  - **Explosion/Acute Battering Phase**: Peak of violence.
  - **Calm, Loving Respite/Honeymoon Phase**: Abuser seeks forgiveness.
- Victims experience disbelief, emotional breakdown, and learned helplessness (Davis & Snyman, 2005:190–192).

### 3.5 The Theory of Traumatic Bonding and Psychological Entrapment
- Abused victim develops strong attachment to abuser due to inconsistent abusive behavior.
- Victim feels powerless and increasingly dependent on the abuser (Davis & Snyman, 2005:190–192).

### 3.6 The Theory of Separation Assault
- Explains victim's return to abuser or reluctance to leave due to fear of retaliation.
- Declares that the abuser’s violence intensifies during separation attempts (Mahoney, 1991:6–7).
- Highlights that victims may actively seek escape but face real-life obstacles (Davis & Snyman, 2005:190–192).

### 3.7 Men as Victims/Husband Battering
- Husband abuse is more common than perceived (Litman, 2003:772).
- Men often refrain from reporting due to societal myths.
- Debate exists on understanding violence in marital relationships (Gelles & Cornell, 1986:79).
- Requires acknowledging female perpetrators in domestic violence contexts (George, 1994:137–159).

### 3.8 The Nature of Abuse
- Perceptions of female violence are often underestimated.
- Evidence shows men suffer serious injuries from domestic violence, often exacerbated by weapons (Smith, 1992).
- Reports indicate mutual violence and involvement of children in domestic situations.

### 3.9 Children as Victims
- Children reflect domestic violence and can be victims themselves in abusive environments (Davis & Snyman, 2005:175–176).
- Vulnerability to abuse in their homes leads to severe emotional and physical consequences.

### 3.10 Legislation
- **South African Constitution, Section 28**: Children’s rights to protection from abuse.
- **Child Care Act** includes provisions for children's courts and mandates reporting of suspected abuse.
- Despite legal safeguards, children often face ongoing victimization (Davis & Snyman, 2005:175–176).

### 3.11 The Elderly as Victims
- Elder abuse is a growing issue globally (Twain, 2008:1).
- Abuse occurs across diverse demographics.
- Responsibility for elder abuse prevention extends beyond welfare services to communities.

### 3.12 Definition of Concepts
- **Elder Abuse** includes:
  - Physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse, financial exploitation, neglect, and self-neglect.
- O’Malley (2006:98) frames abuse in context of inadequate care.
- Definitions of elder abuse and neglect vary among researchers.

### 3.13 Elder Abuse in South Africa
- Abuse and neglect of the elderly recognized in various forms.
- Lack of clarity in definitions complicates the understanding of elder abuse (Bennett).

# 3.14 THE ELDERLY AS VICTIMS IN SOUTH AFRICA

- **1968 Aged Persons Act**
  - Regulated homes for elderly white individuals
  - Aimed to address exploitation and unjust treatment
  - Required registration and inspections of homes (Assembly debates 21 April 1967 – 4639)

- **Developments in Elder Abuse Awareness**
  - 1981: SA Council for the Aged condemned elderly abuse (Eackley & Vilakazi, 1995)
  - 1992: Formation of Friends of Abused Frail Elderly
    - Workshop differentiated mistreatment (verbal, neglect, financial) from abuse (physical, psychological, sexual)

- **SACA Initiatives**
  - Adopted Declaration on the Rights of Elderly (1993)
  - Negotiated for establishment of an ombudsman for elderly rights

- **Research Findings (Eckley & Vilakazi, 1995)**
  - Correlation between abuse reports and advanced age; more incidents among elderly females
  - Distinction in living preferences: white elderly prefer independent living, black elderly often live with families
  - Over 80% of black elderly reported serious issues due to lack of health care and overcrowding

- **Media Coverage and Complaints**
  - August 1, 1995: "You" Magazine article on abuse in white elderly homes
  - Concerned Friends of the Frail and Aged reported 300 complaints over two years
  - Focus on Elderly Abuse formed in Cape Town to lobby for better conditions

- **1999 Health Department Report**
  - Quoted survey indicated:
    - 69% heard of elderly abuse
    - 32% reported ill-treatment
    - 37% emotional/psychological abuse
    - 36% financial abuse
  - Noteworthy breakdown of abusers: 55% grandchildren, 16% children, 20% spouses

- **Proposed Strategies to Address Abuse**
  - Enforce laws protecting elderly
  - Monitor abuse
  - Educate community on respect and care for elderly
  - Inform elderly about rights and reporting abuse

- **2000 Study by Keikelame and Ferreira**
  - Focus on elder abuse in black townships (Cape Flats)
  - Found emotional/verbal abuse more common than physical, linked to witchcraft accusations
  - Highlighted systemic abuse in health clinics and pay points
  - Identified government marginalization and insufficient response from authorities as issues

- **Analysis of Contributions to Abuse**
  - Elders viewed as part of broader domestic violence spectrum
  - Contributory factors: weakening family structures, urbanization, lack of government support

- **Race and Ethnicity in Elder Abuse**
  - Early-stage study: two hypotheses regarding ethnic/racial factors
    - Reduced risk of abuse with aging
    - Increased risk for elderly in black areas due to discrimination and poor social status

  - Eight forms of exploitation identified, including commercial sexual exploitation and forced marriage

## 4.2.1 Internet Exploitation
- Increased child trafficking leads to premature halts in socialisation and education.
- Trafficking effects: 
  - Psychological: fear, depression, low self-esteem, self-worth, anger, trust issues, social skills deficits, PTSD, isolation, substance abuse.
- Child trafficking recognized as a violation of human rights.

## 4.2.2 Youth Internet Victimisation
- Technological developments have created new forms of victimisation: cybercrime, identity theft.
- Virtual violence impacts psycho-social wellbeing.
- "Screenagers" exhibit deep engagement with electronic media facilitating new socialisation forms.
- Risks include:
  - Access to inappropriate content.
  - Exposure to hate and violence.
  - Being misled by advertisement.
  - Online bullying opportunities.

## 4.2.3 Child Sex Tourism
- Exploitation prevalent during adult-oriented tourism, especially in Asia.
- Rationalisation by exploiters: children's supposed agency and cultural acceptance of early sexual experiences.
- No justification for the abuse of power.

## 4.2.4 Child Labour
- Defined as work exploitative to minors; linked to minimum lawful employment age.
- Types of harmful child labour (Unicef):
  - Slavery and forced labour.
  - Commercial sexual exploitation.
  - Illicit activities.
  - Hazardous work threatening health or morals.

## 4.2.5 Child Soldiers
- Definition: any person under 18 involved in armed conflicts, including non-combat roles.
- Estimated 300,000 child soldiers globally (UN).
- Recruitment vulnerabilities:
  - Socioeconomic disadvantages.
  - Conflict zone residency.
  - Family instability.
- Recruitment methods for girls often include sexual exploitation.

## 4.3 Consequences of This Type of Exploitation
- **Educational**: Loss of schooling; systemic disadvantage.
- **Physical**: High injury/death rates; health issues due to STDs and poor hygiene.
- **Psychological**: 
  - Nightmares, flashbacks, depression, suicidal tendencies, irritability.
- Ongoing recruitment persists despite NGO efforts, leading to lasting trauma.

## 4.4 Street Children in Africa
- Street children viewed as products of socio-political changes, e.g., post-apartheid.
- Terminology derived from children's self-identification and societal perceptions (e.g., malunde, skadukinders).
- Literature fragmented; predominately focused on survival and developmental issues, lacking cohesive discourse on childhood.
- Institutional research often constricted by programmatic frameworks rather than theoretical understanding.

## 4.4.1 Definition of Street Children
- Identification through self-descriptors highlights societal labels and perceptions.
- Common evidence of abuse includes: 
  - Child labour.
  - Institutional abuse.
  - Psychological distress.
  - Abandonment.
  - War involvement.

## Chapter 5: Victims of Harmful Cultural Practices

### 5.1 Females

#### 5.1.1 Introduction
- **Democracy in South Africa**: New Constitution and Bill of Rights aimed at protecting vulnerable populations.
- **Cultural Diversity**: Sections 30 and 31 of the Constitution support customary law if not conflicting with constitutional rights.
- **Children’s Act**: Established to prevent child abuse in cultural contexts.

### Legislation
- **Children’s Act 2005, Sec 12**: Protects children from harmful cultural practices (e.g., genital mutilation, virginity testing, circumcision under 16).
- **UN Convention on the Rights of the Child**: Article 24(3) encourages abolishment of harmful practices.

#### 5.1.2 Ukuthwala
- Described as a "romantic" custom for marriage negotiation (Bekker).
- **Case Illustrations (Koyana & Bekker)**: Tacit consent and cultural views of marriage practices.
  
### Concepts
- **Traditional Marriage Custom**: Legal abduction (“thwala”) with consideration for community norms (Nosilela et al).
- **Rights Violation Debate**: Tension between cultural practices and constitutional rights (Ntlokwana, 2009).

#### 5.1.3 Ukuthwala in South African Context
- Media scrutiny has increased awareness.
- **Abduction vs. Custom**: Shift in perception from consensual tradition to abduction (Maluleke, 2009).

### Case Study: Nombulelo
- Nombulelo, a victim of Ukuthwala, illustrates the severe consequences of harmful cultural practices.

#### 5.1.4 Female Genital Mutilations (FGM/C)
- FGM/C involves the removal of external genitalia, primarily affecting girls aged 4-14.
  
### Reasons for FGM/C
- **Sexual Control**: Reduction of female sexuality.
- **Sociological Integration**: Rite of passage into womanhood.
- **Hygienic Beliefs**: Perceived cleanliness.
- **Health Misunderstandings**: False beliefs about fertility.
- **Misinterpreted Religious Beliefs**: No legitimate religious basis for practice.

### Consequences of FGM/C
- Severe health risks, including infection, trauma, and lifelong psychological effects.
- **Violation of Rights**: Contradicts rights to health, freedom from violence, and harmful practices.

### 6 LEARNING UNIT 6
#### Victims of Farm Attacks
---

#### 6.1 Overview
- Focus on issues related to farm attacks, particularly victims.

#### 6.2 Definition of Farm Attacks
- No specific crime labeled as "farm attacks" in law.
- Manifestations include robbery, house-breaking, murder, rape, assault, etc.
- **Approved Definition** (NOCOC): Attacks on farms aimed at residents, workers, visitors to murder, rape, rob, or inflict bodily harm, disrupting farming activities motivated by various ideologies or grievances.

#### 6.2.1 Historical Background of Farming and Land Reform Policy
- Government land reform policies and illegal invasions viewed as factors in violence against farmers.
- **19th-century Agriculture**: Large white farms with black labor; severe limitation on African land ownership.
- By late 1980s, 90% of agricultural land owned by whites; most rural population consisted of Africans.
- **Job Statistics (2001)**: 1.4 million employed in agriculture, significant job creation from 1996-2001.
- Unions: Agri South Africa (AgriSA), TAU, NAFU.
- **Policy Principles**: Redistribution, restitution, land tenure reform.

#### 6.2.2 Characteristics and Modus Operandi of Farm Attacks
- Attacks usually well-planned, sometimes facilitated by insider knowledge (former/current employees).
- Crimes can include murder, severe violence (burning, strangulation).
- Notably chaotic scenes post-attack; perpetrators often steal vehicles for escape.
- Emotional impact on the farming community; affects both black and white farmers.

#### 6.2.3 Potential Causes of Farm Attacks
- **6.2.3.1 Culture of Violence**: Legacy of conflict perpetuates a normalization of violence.
- **6.2.3.2 Poverty and Unemployment**: Justifications for crime rooted in historical injustices and disparities.
- **6.2.3.3 Revenge and Hate**: Potential for revenge attacks, often linked to labor disputes.
- **6.2.3.4 Illegal Immigrants**: Contributing to socio-economic strain and crime.
- **6.2.3.5 Easy Availability of Weapons**: Contributes to violence against farmers.
- **6.2.3.6 Inflammatory Statements**: Certain political rhetoric perceived as inciting violence (e.g., "Kill the boer kill the farmer").

#### 6.2.4 The Victim Profile of Farm Attacks
- **Demographics (2001)**: 61.6% white, 33.3% black, 4.4% Asian, 0.7% coloured.
- Victims include both farmers and farm workers; elderly often seen as soft targets.
- Investigators note patterns of victimization across different provinces, with rising attacks on black farmers noted.

#### 6.2.4.1 The Victims’ Perception of Farm Attacks
- Varying reactions including shock, anger, and feelings of targeted political motivations.
- Conflicting views on whether attacks are political or racially motivated.

#### 6.2.4.2 The Nature of Trauma on Victims After a Farm Attack
- **Reactions**:
  - Uncertainty about future due to loss of longstanding farming context.
  - Loss of control over personal and environmental security.
  - Emotional challenges include fear, a sense of degradation, and feelings of revenge.
  - **Financial Damage**: Loss of property or incurred medical expenses.

#### 6.2.5 The Prevention of Farm Attacks
- Investigating officers suggest proactive security measures:
  - Ensure thorough background checks and documentation of workers.

  - Encourage farmers to vary routines and enhance vigilance.

- The **Rural Protection Plan** initiated in late 1997 aimed at coordinated crime-fighting efforts.
- Post-launch, an increase in recorded farm attacks observed; success varies geographically.

  - Area Crime Combating Units
  - Sector Policing
- Ongoing evaluation of initiatives required (source: ISS, page 437).

### Chapter 7: Victims of Hate Crimes

#### 7.1 Introduction and Definition of Concepts
- **Hate Crimes**: Crimes based on prejudice (race, ethnicity, religion, etc.). May include:
  - Verbal abuse
  - Physical assault
  - Vandalism (religious properties, etc.)
- **Legislation**: State laws define hate crimes involving threats/harassment motivated by biases (e.g., US Legal, 2011).
- Notable inclusion of crimes against persons/property under hate crime laws.
  
#### 7.2 Race

##### 7.2.1 Xenophobia
- **Definition**: Unreasonable fear or hatred of foreigners.
- Impacts: Police victimization, assaults, ethnic cleansing.

##### 7.2.2 Phenomenon of Xenophobia in South Africa
- 2007-2008: Surge of xenophobic attacks; South Africans face immigrant integration challenges.
- Research indicates negative public attitudes towards immigration, despite constitutional rights.
- Support for restrictive immigration policies evident; general opposition to amnesties.
- Attitudes reflect fears related to crime, economic threats, and disease.
  
- **Public Attitudes**:
  - Conditional support for rights of migrants.
  - Many believe unauthorized migrants forfeit rights; varied views on refugee protections.
- **Impact of Social Contact**: Personal interaction with foreigners positively influences attitudes towards them.
- Majority sentiment combines hostility with reluctance to act against foreigners; termed **latent xenophobia**.

- Need for public education and advocacy to foster understanding and acceptance of refugees and immigrants, aligning with South Africa's obligations under international conventions.

### 7.3 Sexual Orientation-Based Hate Victimisation

- **7.3.1 Sexual Orientation-Based Hate Victimisation**
  - Many LGBT individuals face victimisation (Davis & Snyman, 2005:247–250).
  - Apartheid laws criminalised homosexuality until 1996.
  - New Constitution includes protections against discrimination based on sexual orientation.
  - Proposed anti-hate crime legislation recognizes LGBT individuals as potential victims.
  - Continued oppression and discrimination of LGBT persons in the “new” South Africa.
  
- **7.3.2 Corrective Rape**
  - Defined as the rape of lesbian women by men to "cure" them of their sexual orientation.
  - Despite progressive laws, incidents of corrective rape have increased (Global Voices, 2010).
  - Foundation of charity Luleki Sizwe in 2007 after a victim's experience.
  - Reports indicate significant numbers of victims in Cape Town, with average 510 corrective rapes annually.

- **7.3.3 Impact of Hate Crime Victimisation**
  - Victimology typically focuses on individual victims; hate crimes require broader consideration.
  - Emotional and psychological impacts affect not only individuals but their communities and society.

- **7.3.4 Individual Impact**
  - Discrimination manifests differently per individual; generally seen as an injury to dignity (Amnesty International, 2001:vii).
  - Victims display symptoms of trauma: depression, anger, PTSD, etc.
  - Healing from hate crime trauma can take up to five years, compared to two for non-bias crimes.

- **7.3.5 Societal Impact**
  - Emotional impact extends to targeted groups, leading to feelings of isolation and vulnerability.
  - Fear and renewed conflict among community members result from hate crimes, polarising society.

### 7.4 Conclusive Remarks on Hate Crimes
- Focuses on hate crimes in South Africa, including xenophobic attacks and corrective rape.
- Cultural issues exacerbate victimisation; need to treat these acts as crimes, not cultural practices.
- Bill of Rights enshrined equal rights; Sections 30 and 31 promote respect for cultural diversity, contingent on non-conflict with the Constitution and non-criminality.

## Chapter 10: Victimology in Practice

### 10.1 Victim Impact Statements (VIS)

#### 10.1.1 Definition of a Victim Impact Statement (VIS)
- Document by the victim detailing harm suffered due to an offence (Victims of Crime Bureau, NSW).
- **South African Law Commission**: A VIS reflects the impact of an offence on the victim, covering physical, psychological, social, and financial consequences.
- **Differentiation**: VIS personalizes crime beyond general victim testimony, allowing victims to express pain and anguish.
- **Inclusion**: Direct victims & indirect victims (e.g., family members) can be represented.

#### 10.1.2 Purpose of the VIS
- Growing acceptance in Western legal systems for victim empowerment and representation.
- Vital for informed sentencing decisions.
- Provides insight on physical, psychological, and economic impact on victims.
- Empowers victims and their communities by giving them a voice in the criminal justice system.

#### 10.1.3 Legal Provision for Victim Impact Statements in South Africa
- **South African Law Commission Recommendations**:
  - Incorporation of VIS in the **Sentence Framework Bill**.
  - Evidence from victims to assist the **Correctional Supervision and Parole Board**.
  - Emphasis on victim safety and well-being when determining parole conditions.
  - Judicial use of uncontested VIS as admissible evidence.
  - Introduction of clauses mandating consideration of VIS in sentencing.

#### 10.1.4 When and Who Can Prepare a VIS
- Submitted to court post-conviction, prior to sentencing.
- Victims or authorized representatives must notify State Prosecutor of intent to present a VIS.
- Preparation can involve self-preparation or assistance from professionals (counselors, criminologists).
- Guidelines from **New South Wales** indicate eligibility for direct victims, family members, or representatives.

## Chapter 11: The Way Forward

### 11.1 Future Developments in Victimology
- Emerging field since the 1970s, focused on victim needs and support.
- Shift from theoretical approaches to practical applications, with an emphasis on victim empowerment.
- Critical need for research to inform and influence policy and legislation.
- Collaboration between researchers and practitioners essential for effective victim support services.
