# Criminology Study Notes

## 1. What is criminology about? (7)

### 1.1.4 The criminologist’s role
- A criminologist studies crime phenomena and criminal behavior, focusing on causes, legal aspects, and solutions (Brown, Esbensen & Geis, 2001; Gibbons in Bezuidenhout & Little, 2011).
- Functions include:
  - Teaching at universities/colleges
  - Researching at institutions (e.g., ISS)
  - Community service (support for victims of violent crimes)
- Criminology aims to study, define, and develop theories on crime and victimization (Glick, 1995).
- Theoretical explanations for crime, including women's abuse, involve factors like substance abuse and socialization.
- Criminologists influence criminal justice policy, aid in court, and provide victim impact statements.

### 1.1.5 Is criminology a discipline in its own right?
- Criminology is viewed as a dynamic social science with its own methods and knowledge (Du Preez & Neser, 1993).
- Criteria for independent discipline (Johnson, 1978):
  - Unique object of study
  - Demarcated area of study
  - Specific methodologies
  - Theoretical foundation 
  - Societal applicability 
  - Educational provision
- Criminology fulfills these criteria, asserting its legitimacy as a discipline.

### 1.1.6 The fluctuating parameters of crime
- Definitions of crime vary significantly across cultures and time (Walsh, 2015).
- Justice perception differs; actions viewed as heroic in one context may be criminal in another (e.g., McBride's bombing).
- Definitions of acceptable behavior are influenced by power dynamics and community standards.
- Laws reflect societal values and can be unjust, requiring critical examination by criminologists.

### 1.1.7 Various approaches to the study of crime
- Criminologists hold personal definitions of crime shaped by their experiences and societal context.
- Study perspectives: 
  - **Juridical (legal)**: Focuses on law violations.
  - **Nonjuridical (social)**: Contextualizes behavior within societal norms and impacts.
- South African criminal justice has roots in Roman-Dutch and British law; indigenous law largely overlooked.

### 1.1.8 The juridical definition of crime
- Juridical approach emphasizes explicit legal violations punishable by state law (Bezuidenhout & Little, 2011).
- Criminal actions must be legally defined, observable, and intentional.
- Legal concepts:
  - **Act**: Must be voluntary and human-controlled.
  - **Wrongfulness**: Conflicts with legal norms; exceptions apply (self-defense, consent).
  - **Guilt**: Encompasses intent (dolus) and negligence (culpa) (Kemp et al., 2012).
  - **Punishment**: State-defined consequences for illegal acts, which can include various penalties (imprisonment, fines, etc.).

# 1 Schools of thought in criminology

## 1.1 Key Theories Overview (19)
- **Rational Choice Theory**: Criminal behavior results from a rational decision-making process weighing costs vs. benefits (Burke 2014:63).
- **Postmodernism**: Claims there is no singular theory of crime; understanding is eclectic and provisional (Burke 2014:369).
- **Social Process Approach**: Emphasizes interactions with societal organizations and processes as drivers of criminal behavior (Walsh 2015:127).
- **Social Structure Approach**: Focuses on the significant role of disadvantaged economic status in crime causation (Walsh 2015:105).

## 1.2 Purpose of Criminological Theory (20)
- Theories consist of propositions that logically explain relations among observed phenomena, providing coherent patterns (Walsh 2015:16).
- Resistance to theory is common, yet theory explains "how" and "why" behaviors occur.
- Theories connect with empirical research through testable hypotheses. Simple or complex theories inform understanding of human behavior (Walsh 2015:17).
- Theories are universally applicable but must be contextualized to specific settings.

## 1.3 Classical Criminology (20-21)
- Emerged in late 18th century through Cesare Beccaria and Jeremy Bentham’s work; rooted in Enlightenment ideals (Eamonn et al. 2015:49; Walsh 2015:73).
- **Social Contract**: Concept that individuals agree to surrender freedoms for societal protection (Van der Westhuizen 2011:126).
- Humans are viewed as rational, hedonistic beings making decisions to maximize pleasure and minimize pain. Fear of punishment is necessary for behavioral control.
- Beccaria’s **On Crimes and Punishments** (1764) outlines the utility-focused nature of punishment; it should be proportionate and not excessive (Burke 2014:35).
- Key principles:
  1. Public, prompt, necessary punishment.
  2. "Swift and certain" punishment enhances deterrence (Burke 2014:44).
  3. Focus on individual accountability, rejecting mitigating circumstances.

## 1.4 Limitations of Classical Model (22)
- Presents an overly rational view of humanity; underestimates complexities of human behavior.
- Assumes equal freedom and choice across individuals, ignoring societal inequalities.
- Fails to address root causes of crime, often leading to administrative and legal perspectives that overlook individual circumstances.

## 1.5 Neo-Classical School (23)
- Adjusts classical assumptions to allow for extenuating circumstances affecting responsibility (Brown et al. 2001:279–280).
- Continues to rely on free will but recognizes factors like mental illness that can limit accountability (White & Haines 2001:46).
- Represents a continuum of free will-based deterrence, maintaining classical ideas of rationality.

## 1.6 Crime Control and Due Process Models (23)
- **Crime Control Model**: Advocates strong law enforcement and penalties; prioritizes public safety over civil liberties (Einstadter & Henry 1995:43).
- **Due Process Model**: Emphasizes protection of individual rights; aims to minimize wrongful imprisonment over simply convicting suspects (Burke 2015:44-45).

## 1.7 Critiques of Classical School (23-24)
- Assumes rational, self-serving behavior fails to capture human complexities.
- Generalizes that individuals have equal reasoning capabilities which undermines concepts of "equal justice."
- Lacks exploration of crime’s social causes, often leading to unjust legislative applications.

## 1.8 Positivist Criminology (24)
- Emerged in the late 19th century, focusing on scientific study of crime through biological, psychological, and social lenses (Van der Westhuizen 2011:128).
- **Cesare Lombroso**: Advocated biological determinism in his work "Criminal Man" (1911) identifying "born criminals" through physical traits (Curran & Renzetti 1994:222).
- Positivists shifted focus from rationalism to studying external factors influencing crime, marking a departure from classical perspectives.

# Schools of Thought in Criminology

## 1. Positivistic School
- Founded on the belief in societal progress and the use of scientific methods to understand and address social problems (White & Haines 2001:31).
- Key characteristics:
  - **Determinism**: Focus on criminal behavior rather than legal issues.
  - Emphasis on the treatment and rehabilitation of offenders.
  - Human behavior influenced by external social forces, psychological factors (psychological determinism), and biological makeup (biological determinism) (Williams & McShane 2010:171–2; White & Haines 2001:38).
- Three assumptions:
  - Causes of criminal behavior linked to a person's character, background, genetics, and social factors.
  - Rejection of the notion of rational decision-making; emphasis on intellectual and emotional deficiencies.
  - Criminality driven by biological factors, psychological impulses, and an indifferent social environment (White & Haines 2001:48).
- **Clusters**: Divided into individual positivism (biological/psychological) and sociological positivism.
  
### Limitations of the Positivist Model
- Assumes objectivity in research; fails to acknowledge biases.
- Accepts the existing social/economic order and standard definitions of crime.
- Scientific method viewed as the sole path to knowledge.
- Lacks exploration of alternatives to the current social order.

---

## 2. Critical Criminology
- Encompasses various theories focusing on conflict and power relations as central to societal dynamics (Lynch & Michalowski, 2000:78).
- Critiques earlier criminologies for neglecting class, race, and gender’s roles in crime definition and law labeling (Chamberlain 2015:233).
- Emphasizes crime as a product of oppression experienced by marginalized groups (e.g., working class, women, ethnic minorities) (Burke 2014:245).
- Notable themes:
  - Criminal acts labeled as deviant by dominant societal groups.
  - Media portrayal and sensationalism leading to moral panics and misrepresentation of crime statistics.
  - Edwin Sutherland introduced "white collar crime," highlighting inequalities in societal treatment (Sutherland).

### Proposals for Addressing Crime
- Strategy of **social empowerment**: Direct involvement in decision-making and redistribution of resources (Walsh 2015:149).
- Critique of power relations within societal structures leading to unpunished elite crimes overshadowed by the focus on lower-class offenses.
- Advocates for anti-racist and anti-sexist campaigns, protection of human rights, and public accountability of state officials.

### Critiques of Critical Criminology
- Overlooked harsh realities and public concerns regarding crime, failing to engage scientifically with crime issues (Currie 1997:149).

---

## 3. Feminist Criminology
- Emerged in the late 1960s, critiquing traditional criminology's bias against women (Burke 2014:227).
- Recognizes assessment of crime from gendered perspectives, highlighting the dominance of patriarchal structures in society.
- Various feminist perspectives:
  - **Liberal Feminism**: Focus on legal reform and discrimination in social practices.
  - **Radical Feminism**: Emphasizes the power relations of patriarchy and its exploitation of women.
  - **Marxist Feminism**: Links women's subordination to capitalist exploitation of their domestic roles.
  - **Socialist Feminism**: Integrates radical and Marxist views to analyze oppression from both systems.
  - **Black Feminism**: Examines oppression through intersectionality of race, gender, and class.
  - **Postmodern Feminism**: Advocates for a more inclusive understanding of feminism, addressing multiple identities.

### Contributions of Feminist Criminology
- Critiques malestream criminology; emphasizes women's roles in crime and victimization.
- Introduces new areas of study and forefronts the role of gender in crime dynamics.
- Highlights the implications of masculinity in crime (Eamonn et al. 2014:106–7).

### Limitations of the Feminist Model
- The various facets and complexity of feminist theory can lead to divergent interpretations and deficiencies in cohesive policy formulation.

# Study Notes on Schools of Thought in Criminology

## 1. Schools of thought in criminology (31)

### 1.2.9 The Social Milieu School of Thought
- Proponents: Tarde, Guerry, and Quetelet.
- **Two Approaches**:
  - **Social Structure**:
    - Focus on community organization, economic systems, social disparities, family dysfunction, and geographical crime distribution.
    - Aims to identify structural flaws contributing to crime (Brown, Esbensen, and Geis 2007).
  - **Social Process**: 
    - Examines individual law-violator interactions and socialization processes.
    - Sometimes labeled as psychosocial theories.
- **Premises**:
  - Based on the juridical conception of crime.
  - Emphasis on the criminal's social environment.
  - Assumes improved social conditions will prevent crime.
  - Generally ignores the criminal and victim.
  - No focus on punishment or rehabilitation.

### 1.2.10 Postmodern Criminology
- Emerged late 1980s, from critical criminology.
- References: 
  - Schwartz & Friedrichs (Madifs 2014).
  - Burke (2014), Schmalleger (1996).
- **Characteristics**:
  - Challenges rationality, reason, and science in social explanations.
  - Focus on integrated, individualistic, and voluntaristic perspectives.
  - Critiques conventional concepts (e.g., norms, values) needing deconstruction.
  - Emphasizes interdisciplinary views, focusing on various complex factors influencing crime (socioeconomic, sociopolitical, etc.).
  - Incorporates perspectives such as rational choice, feminist crime theories, and more.
- **Universal Factors Contributing to Crime** (Van Dijk 1998):
  - Economic constraints, low education, gun circulation, urbanization issues, multiculturalism, high substance abuse, unemployment, and youth population challenges.

### 1.2.11 Criminology in Africa
- Lack of literature due to unreliable crime statistics and political instability (Mushanga 1992).
- **Contributors to Crime**:
  - Colonial imposition of capitalism disrupted indigenous law; focus shifts from individualism to community well-being (Rotimi & Oloruntimehin 1992).
  - Contemporary issues: political oppression, lack of victim support, urban squatting.
- **Theories**:
  - Critical criminology for crime explanation; positive theories exist (Muga 1973).
  - Rapid urbanization and cultural shifts leading to increased crime rates.
- **Crime Prevention Proposals**:
  - Education, poverty eradication, community restoration (Mushanga 1992; Kibuka 1980).

### 1.2.12 Criminology in South Africa
- Divergent views between positivist and critical criminology reflecting crime definitions (Venter 1977; Cloete & Stevens 1990).
- Early criminologists influenced by economic disparities (Bonger).
- Contemporary influences: race, political culture of violence, socio-economic conditions, youth marginalization, etc.
- **Specific Factors Identified by National Crime Prevention Plan**:
  - Deterioration of social control, political violence, economic deprivation, and vigilance culture.

- Overview of various criminological theories (classical, positivist, critical, feminist, social milieu, and postmodern).

### 2.1.3 The Classification of Crime (333)
- Classification organizes crime data to identify patterns and inform preventive measures.
- Categories include:
  - **Crimes of Violence:** e.g., murder, assault.
  - **Social Fabric Crime:** e.g., rape, ordinary assault.
  - **Property-related Crime:** e.g., burglary, theft.
  - **Commercial Crime:** e.g., fraud, embezzlement.
  - **Violence Aimed at Property:** e.g., arson.

# The Classification and Measurement of Crime (67)

## 2.2.1 Victim Surveys
- **Purpose**: Address the "dark figure" of unreported crime; compare with self-report studies.
- **Focus**: Victims’ experiences, attitudes towards crime, fear of crime, measures taken for safety, attitudes to the criminal justice system.
- **Example**: **UNICRI** conducts international victim studies since 1989; South African surveys highlight crime victimization rates.
- **Findings**:
  - 1 in 5 households experienced property crime.
  - 1 in 10 experienced violent crime.
  - Variations in victimization by demographic: whites more often victims of property crime; blacks and coloureds more victims of violence.

## 2.2.2 Limitations of Victim Surveys
- **General Limitations**:
  - Inability to capture crimes without identifiable victims (e.g., corporate crimes).
  - Cultural perceptions may influence responses.
  - Victims may forget or not report certain crimes.
  - Indirect victims report less often than direct victims.
  - Surveys reflect crimes, not offenders (same offender may appear multiple times).
- **Statistical Data Gaps**: Must interpret with caution; differences may arise from definitions rather than actual crime incidence.
  
## 2.2.3 Self-Report Studies/Surveys
- **Definition**: Surveys where respondents report violations of criminal law anonymously (Siegel 2013; Singh 2011).
- **Strengths**:
  - Measure attitudes, values, personal traits and behaviors.
  - Correlate respondent characteristics with admitted offences.
- **Weaknesses**:
  - Often focus on petty offenses; limited representation.
  - Typically target juvenile delinquency over adult criminality.
  - Reliability concerns; respondents may underreport or forget offences.

### 2.2.3.1 Accuracy of Self-Report Studies
- **Critiques**: Unreasonable to expect candidness; underreporting by males and chronic offenders.
- **Verification Techniques**: Costly and time-consuming (e.g., polygraph tests).
- **Representation Issues**: Institutionalized youths often underrepresented.

## 2.2.4 Data Mining
- **Definition**: Use of computational methods to analyze large datasets for patterns undetected by traditional methods (Siegel 2013).
- **Applications**: Helps identify crime patterns (e.g., armed robberies) through diverse data sources.

## 2.2.5 Crime Mapping 
- **Definition**: Use of technology (GIS) for visualizing and analyzing crime patterns (Walsh 2015).
- **Benefits**: Resource allocation based on geographic crime trends; enhances police patrol effectiveness in hotspots.

## 2.2.6 Conclusion
- **Concern**: Crime and violence are significant issues in South Africa.
- **Statistics Importance**: Despite gaps, they are crucial for understanding crime magnitude, guiding criminological theories, and judicial policy.
- **Reliable Crime Statistics**: Fundamental for effective crime prevention and evaluating intervention programs.

- **Mental Disorders**: Range from mild emotional distress to severe insanity affecting coping abilities (Hunter & Dantzker 2002:81).

  - **Acceptance of Responsibility**: Emphasizes societal and familial duties.

  - Aims to improve firearms possession control, enacted in July 2004.
- High incidence of crimes committed with firearms persists.
- Acne of gun-running, especially post-Mozambique war.

## 4.2.4 Specific Factors Contributing to Crime in South Africa

### 4.2.4.1 The Negotiated Transition and the Destruction of Social Control
- Post-apartheid societal changes led to a breakdown of existing social controls.
- Increased criminal activity due to lack of community and criminal justice system controls.

### 4.2.4.2 Political Culture, Violence and Crime
- Historical violence acceptance in South Africa; violence regarded as a means to resolve conflict (e.g., taxi violence).

### 4.2.4.3 Political Rivalry as a Factor Contributing to Crime
- Violence during apartheid extended to political rivalries post-transition.
- Notable incidents: Shobashobane massacre (1995).

### 4.2.4.4 Absence of National Political Consensus on Crime Issues
- Crime becomes politicized, leading to simplistic public policy solutions.
- Calls for an integrated interdepartmental approach to crime prevention.

### 4.2.4.5 Poverty, Unemployment and Relative Deprivation
- Crime prevalence correlated with economic deprivation, worsened by apartheid legacies.
- Economic distress may trigger violent crimes; the nuanced relationship between deprivation and crime.

### 4.2.4.6 Crime and Economic Development
- Mixed economy; well-developed sectors coexisting with vulnerabilities to crime syndicates.
- Poor border controls facilitate crime.

### 4.2.4.7 Youth Marginalisation
- Consequences of historical marginalization lead to gang involvement.
- Inferior education under the Bantu Education Act triggered resistance movements.

### 4.2.4.8 Inadequate Support for Victims of Crime
- Lack of comprehensive victim support undermines trust in the criminal justice system.
- Absence of state compensation for violent crime victims.

### 4.2.4.9 Vigilantism, Revenge and Self-Defence
- Perceived failures of the criminal justice system have led to informal justice forms.

### 4.2.4.10 Socio-psychological Factors
- Changes have fostered community fear and helplessness, leading to increased vigilantism and substance abuse.

### 4.2.4.11 Access to Firearms
- High availability of illegal firearms increases the severity and frequency of violence-related crimes.

# Learning Unit 4.3: Application of Theory (178-180)

## 4.3.1 Introduction (178)
- Theories explain natural phenomena and are evaluable through empirical findings.
- Daily use of theory for understanding events is common; crime theories must account for human complexity.
- Different theories exist for various categories of crime (e.g., violence vs. property).

## 4.3.2 Key Concepts (179)
- **Theory**: Central to science, explanations for crime.
- **Systematic Observations**: Methods for data collection from environments.
- **Generalisation**: Extrapolating findings from limited cases to larger populations.
- **Validity**: Truthful representation in research findings.
- **Structure Theories**: Focus on societal organization and its behavioral influence.
- **Process Theories**: Explain individual paths to offending through learning.
- **Conflict Theories**: Address human behavior shaped by social power struggles.
- **Consensus Theories**: Assume social agreement on norms and criminality impact societal good.

## 4.3.3 What is Theory? (179)
- A theory consists of interconnected propositions that explain relations among observed facts.
- Serves to answer "why" questions, deriving hypotheses for testing.
- Theoretical development involves systematic observation and data processing.
- Integral to criminology for understanding crime and guiding prevention and treatment efforts.

## 4.3.4 What is a ‘‘Good’’ Theory? (179-180)
- **Four Characteristics of Good Theory** (Maguire and Radosh, 1999):
  - **Plausibility**
  - **Evidence of Validity**
  - **Falsifiability**
  - **Predictability**

# Study Notes on Application of Theory (Page 181-186)

## 4.3.4 Components of a Good Theory
- **Plausibility (181)**
  - Must provide meaningful, logically consistent explanations of the relationship between facts.
  
- **Evidence of Validity (182)**
  - Should have empirical support and align with research findings and predictions.

- **Falsifiability (182)**
  - Must be disprovable; a theory cannot be considered valid if it cannot be tested or potentially proven false.

- **Predictability (182)**
  - Effective theories predict future behaviours and inform policy implications for crime prevention.

## 4.3.5 How to Study Theory
- **Background (183)**
  - The development of theory is influenced by the theorist’s social and intellectual context.
  - Differentiations arise from varying interpretations of facts based on psychological vs. sociological perspectives.

- **Key Premises (183)**
  - Key premises outline the essence of a theory and frame its arguments.

- **Responsibility for Actions (Free Will) (184)**
  - Examines individual agency in crime; considers biological, psychological, and social dimensions.
  - Balance between individual responsibility and external factors is vital in theoretical perspectives.

- **Policy Implications (184)**
  - A theory must influence social policies and practices if it effectively elucidates crime causes.

- **Evaluation (184)**
  - Theories assessed on adequacy (validity, plausibility), comprehensiveness (scope of explanation), and usefulness.

## 4.3.6 Usefulness of Theory (185)
- Assists in understanding criminal behaviour and developing control strategies.
- Changes in theories can influence governmental policies and the criminal justice system.
- Practical applications include police strategies, sentencing decisions, and rehabilitation programs.

## 4.3.7 Classification of Theories (185-186)
- **Classical vs. Positivist Theories (185)**
  - Classical: Focus on individual rights and legal structures.
  - Positivist: Focus on the pathology of criminal behaviour.

- **Structure vs. Process Theories (185)**
  - Structure: Examine societal influences on behaviour (e.g., strain theory).
  - Process: Explore the development of criminal behaviour through social learning.

- **Consensus vs. Conflict Theories (186)**
  - Consensus: Assume shared societal values; laws cater to the majority.
  - Conflict: Argue against consensus; highlight class-based value conflicts.

## 4.3.8 Conclusion (186)
- Theories explain crime by considering various factors, emphasizing both intrinsic and social influences.
- A "good" theory is valid, consistent, and predictive, aiding in practical applications for crime management.

# LEARNING UNIT 4.4: EXPLANATION OF CRIME (188)

## 4.4.1 Introduction (1347)
- Various theories explain crime but not all are comprehensive.
- Individual factors (personality) vs. environmental factors (social structure).
- **General theory** (Gottfredson & Hirschi, *General Theory of Crime* 1990) aims for comprehensive explanation.

## 4.4.2 Key Concepts (1348)
- **Self-control**: Established in childhood; the default is low self-control without proper socialization.
- **White-collar crime**: Crimes exploiting one's market status (theft, fraud, etc.).
- **Self-control theory**: Crime results from natural impulses; characterized by:
  - Present orientation
  - Risk-taking behavior
  - Lack of patience
  - Self-centeredness

## 4.4.3 Key Premises of the General Theory of Crime (GTC) (1351)
- Definitions of crime are broader than in other theories.
- Criminal acts and offenders are separate concepts.
- Self-control variations explain differences in crime tendency.
- Poor self-control roots in inadequate child-rearing practices.

## 4.4.4 The Criminal Act (1353)
### 4.4.4.1 Definition of Crime
- **Crime definition problematic**; varies across time and culture.
- **Mala in se** vs. **mala prohibita**: Universally condemned crimes vs. crimes forbidden by law.
- **Gottfredson & Hirschi's definition**: "Acts of force or fraud undertaken in the pursuit of self-interest."

### 4.4.4.2 Common Characteristics of Crime (1354)
- Immediate gratification of needs.
- Easy and simple satisfaction of desires.
- Excitement linked to risks.
- Minimal skill or planning required for many crimes.
- Crime's temporary benefits vs. long-term commitments.
- Negative consequences for victims.

## 4.4.5 The Potential Offender (1357)
- Immediate need satisfaction drives potential offenders.
- Minimal frustration tolerance; crime seen as easy solution.
- Excitement sought by those with poor self-image.
- Lack of long-term perspective influences crime participation.
- Traits: impulsiveness, insensitivity, short-sightedness, below-average intelligence.

## 4.4.6 Causes of Poor Self-Control (1364)
### 4.4.6.1 Poor Child-Rearing Practices (1365)
- Inadequate parenting leads to lifelong low self-control.
- Sociocultural factors (chronic stress, unemployment, poverty) influence parenting quality.
- Essential elements for effective child-rearing:
  - Monitoring of behavior
  - Identification and punishment of delinquency
- Effective education reduces impulse gratification and enhances sensitivity towards others.

## Explanation of crime (193)
- Parental neglect impacts children's behavior:
  - Lack of monitoring leads to delinquency.
  - Careful monitoring without disciplinary action encourages misbehavior.
  - Disinclination or inability to punish affects child development.
- **Outcome**: Neglected children may turn to crime, show poor academic performance, leave school early, and struggle in personal relationships.
- Conversely, watched and disciplined children tend to develop self-control and resilience.

## Criminal parental neglect scenario (194)
- The case of Nathan Walker Jr. highlights the consequences of parental neglect.
- Despite a troubled upbringing, the offenders received life sentences for serious crimes.
- Judge Marx acknowledged the influence of parental absence and poverty but emphasized personal accountability for their actions.

## Circumstantial factors (196)
- **Gottfredson and Hirschi**: Criminal propensity stems from human nature, elevated in absence of socialisation.
- Crime as an attraction—promising pleasure outweighing consequences.
- Crime necessitates:
  - Potential targets.
  - Perceived absence of unpleasant outcomes.
- Other influences include physical power, speed, and substance abuse.

## Evaluation (199)
- **Self-control theory** by **Gottfredson and Hirschi** claims it explains various criminal behaviors.

### Empirical support (199)
- Research links impulsiveness and low self-control to criminality.
- Notable findings:
  - Drunk drivers often display impulsive traits.
  - Violent recidivists show distinct impulsive personality structures (Siegel 1995).

### Shortcomings (200)
- Critique: Low self-control’s variability not solely from parenting—child behavior also impacts parental actions.
- Suggests genetic factors in self-control.
- Personality attributes alone do not distinguish criminals from non-criminals conclusively.
- Fails to consider socio-ecological patterns influencing crime rates.

## Conclusion (201)
- **Gottfredson and Hirschi** assert crime characterized by distinct traits tied to potential offenders.
- Proper child rearing combats poor self-control.
- Self-control is intertwined with situational circumstances affecting crime occurrence.
