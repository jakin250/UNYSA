# Study Unit: Crime Victims

## 1.1 Introduction
- Victim as a critical component of criminology alongside crime and criminals.
- Victims often overlooked in favor of criminals; focus on rights of the criminal.
- Purpose: Define "victim" and explore problems victims face post-crime.

## 1.2 Key Concepts
- **Fear of Crime**: Anxiety about crime or perceived victimization.
- **Posttraumatic Stress Disorders**: Emotional disturbances from traumatic stress.
- **Victimology**: Scientific study of crime victim causes and contexts.
- **Crime Victim**: Individual negatively affected by a criminal act.  

## 1.3 Definition of the "Victim" Concept
- "Victim": Someone who has suffered harm, not necessarily criminal (can be from disasters, neglect, etc.).
- **Positivist Victimology**: Restricts "victim" to harm from criminal acts. Distinction made between natural persons (individuals) and legal persons (entities).
- **United Nations Definition (1992)**: Victims facing harm through violations of criminal laws.
- **Radical Victimology**: Focuses on individuals harmed by systemic power structures (e.g., police brutality, state violence).  
- **Survivor**: Term for those who survive a criminal event; implies resilience.
- Fattah’s proposal for neutral terminology: "Participants in the crime conflict" recognizes complexity between victims and offenders.

## 1.4 Problems Crime Victims Experience
### 1.4.1 Economic Loss
- Victims incur loss of property (burglary, robbery).
- U.S. cost: ~$12 billion loss annually, much non-recovered.
- Productivity losses exceed $100 billion; long-term crime costs reach ~$450 billion.
- South Africa: Financial crimes yield R80-R90 billion; predatory crimes cost about R500 million.

### 1.4.2 Suffering
- Victims face injuries of varying severity; economic costs include medical treatment and lost work time.
- Impact of crime leads to avoidance of high-crime areas impacting businesses and tourism.
- Drug abuse linked to victimization, creating broader social costs.

### 1.4.3 Fear
- Prior crime experiences lead to reluctance to report or testify in court, detrimental to justice efficacy.
- Victims of violent crime may face heightened fear and potential for repeated victimization.
- **Revictimization**: Seen as trauma symptom; factors include high-risk behaviors and desensitization.
- Battered women show higher revictimization rates; society's views on domestic violence hinder acknowledgment.

### 1.4.4 Antisocial Behaviour
- Victims, especially from abuse, may later commit crimes; evidence from studies indicates higher arrest rates for victims of child abuse.
- Young males abused show increased likelihood of substance abuse compared to peers.

## 1.2.3 Factors Contributing to Victim Risk
- Various factors increase the risk of victimisation: age, gender, marital status, race and ethnicity, unemployment, income, neighbourhood, involvement in crime, and repeat victimisation.

### 1.2.3.1 Age
- Young people are more likely to be victimised; risk decreases after age 25 (Siegel 2004:86).
- Elderly (65+) are less victimised than youth; account for 1% of violent victimisation (NCVS).
- Teenagers (12-19) represent over 30% of victimisation cases.
- Children <12 are more likely to be assaulted by known individuals (Siegel 2004:86–87).
- Reasons for youth vulnerability: receptiveness to opportunities, lack of experience, carelessness (Van der Hoven & Maree 2005:58).

### 1.2.3.2 Gender
- Males are more victimised by violent crime, except for rape and sexual assault; men have higher assault rates (NCVS).
- Women experience higher rates of domestic violence and sexual offences (Siegel 2004:86).
- Female murder victims: 14.1% of total; female domestic violence victims: 89% (SAPS 2003:33).

### 1.2.3.3 Marital Status
- Divorced/never-married individuals are at greater risk than married counterparts (Siegel 2004:88).
- Young singles frequently visit high-risk public spaces, heightening exposure to violence.
- Widows/widowers generally face less risk due to age and lifestyle.

### 1.2.3.4 Race and Ethnicity
- Black groups face higher crime rates; African Americans have higher violent crime victimisation than whites (Siegel 2004:89).
- 80% of violent crime victims in South Africa are black.
- Contributing factors: high crime areas, unemployment, and poverty (Mawby & Walklate 1994:47).

### 1.2.3.5 Unemployment
- Unemployed individuals are more victimised; youth are particularly at risk (Siegel & Senna 2003:50).
- Employment allows better property protection; unemployed live in unsafe areas.
- Complex relationship between unemployment and violent/property crimes (Conklin 2004:144; Sheley 2000:221–223).

### 1.2.3.6 Income
- Victimisation does not directly correlate with income; victims of property crimes often higher income (Fattah 1991:123).
- Lower-income offenders, but higher-income individuals targeted for theft.

### 1.2.3.7 Neighbourhood
- Urban residents face higher victimisation rates than rural counterparts.
- Poorest neighborhoods are more crime-prone; affluent areas show lower rates (Mawby & Walklate 1994:45–46).

### 1.2.3.8 Involvement in Crime
- Previous victimisation increases likelihood of re-victimisation (Felson 2002:128).
- Significant overlap between profiles of murderers and victims in Soweto (Snyman 1994:118–125).

### 1.2.3.9 Repeat Victimisation
- Defined as multiple victimisations within a limited timeframe.
- Characteristics influencing repeat victimisation: personal traits, social situations, and offender relationships (Van der Hoven & Maree 2005:65).
- Past victimisation predicts future victimisation; higher risk for subsequent incidents (Van der Hoven & Maree 2005:66).
- Burglary pattern: high risk of repeat occurrences soon after initial incidents. 

### Notable Legislation and Cases

- **South African Police Service (SAPS) Reports**

# STUDY UNIT 2.1: VICTIM VULNERABILITY

## 2.1.1 Introduction
- Certain societal groups are more vulnerable to crime (rape, murder, robbery).
- **Vulnerability**: susceptibility to victimization from various external/internal factors (Clark 2005:173).
  
## 2.1.2 Key Concepts
- **Hate Crime**: Criminal acts motivated by hatred/bias against personal characteristics (race, gender, religion, sexual orientation).
- **Victim-Blaming**: Assigning guilt to an innocent victim/group.
- **Victim Precipitation**: Victim's behavior ignites the crime (e.g., verbal/physical provocation).
- **Victimization**: Characteristics increasing probability of becoming a crime victim.
- **Routine Activities Theory**: Crime results from the presence of a likely offender, suitable target, and lack of guardianship.

## 2.1.3 Definition of "Victim Vulnerability"
- **Victim Vulnerability**: Likelihood of becoming a crime victim, influenced by behavior, lifestyle, and offender interaction.

## 2.1.4 Vulnerability to Victimisation
- Factors influencing vulnerability encompass:
  - **Victim Precipitation and Provocation**
  - **Facilitation**
  - **Attractiveness of the Target**
  - **Lifestyle**
  - **Victim/Offender Interaction**

### 2.1.4.1 Precipitating Factors
- Victim's role in their victimization (passive/active) (Doerner & Lab 2002:9).
- **Active Precipitation**: Victim's aggressive behavior initiating the offence (Siegel 2009:73).
- **Passive Precipitation**: Individuals’ actions affecting their status, leading to such crimes (e.g., hate crimes).
- Controversy arises over victim responsibility; victim-blaming misplaces accountability away from offenders and societal issues (Van Der Hoven, 2007:24 & Howe, 2008:220).

### 2.1.4.2 Facilitating Factors
- Victim negligence leading to easier crime commission (e.g., leaving doors unlocked, valuables in sight) (Sheley 2000:173).
- Individuals under drug/alcohol influence are more vulnerable.

### 2.1.4.3 Attractiveness of the Target
- **Attractiveness of the Target**: Appeal of the target to criminals (Karmen 2009:409).
- Certain individuals or items (jewelry, electronic devices) are seen as easy and desirable targets for theft.

## 2.1.5 Conclusion
- Understanding victim vulnerability is crucial for preventing crime and fostering better societal responses.

# STUDY UNIT 2.1: VULNERABILITY TO VICTIMISATION

## 2.1.4.4 Lifestyle
- **Definition**: Lifestyle encompasses daily vocational and leisure activities (Hindelang et al 1978:241).
- **Influences**:
  - **Social Roles**: Certain roles increase victimisation risk (e.g., young people out at night).
  - **Social Structure Position**: Higher positions typically correlate with lower victimisation risk.
  - **Rational Component**: Choices can enhance safety, but young people often partake in high-risk activities (Williams & McShane 1994:224).
- **Predictability of Victimisation**: Certain occupations have higher risks; examples include police roles vs. office workers.
- **Influence of Structural Constraints**: Choices often constrained by factors like financial need (Karmen 2009:97).
- **Research Insights**: Deviant lifestyles and routine activities link to crime victimisation risk (Chen 2009:1084; 1101).

## 2.1.4.5 Interaction between the Victim and the Offender
- Emphasis on studying both victim’s and offender’s roles for a dynamic understanding of interactions.
- **Conflict Triggers**: Arguments, infidelity accusations, and insults can escalate quickly.
- **Homicide Studies**: 48% of suspects had prior relationships with victims (Cao et al 2008:665).
- **Human Trafficking Insights**: Victims often have close ties to their recruiters (Surtees 2008:52).
- **Sub-Intentional Death**: Victims may accidentally contribute to their own demise through risky behaviour, especially repeat victims (Karmen 2009:115).

## 2.1.6 Conclusion
- **Vulnerability to Victimisation**: Comprises five concepts:
  - Victim precipitation and provocation
  - Facilitation
  - Target attractiveness
  - Lifestyle
  - Victim/offender interaction

## 2.2.1 Child Abuse
- **Incidence**: Primarily occurs within familial relationships; prevalent cases of incest often involve father-daughter dynamics (Reid 2003:215).
- **Effects**: Victims experience severe mental health issues including:
  - Disrupted personality development
  - Guilt and shame
  - Posttraumatic stress disorder
  - Poor self-esteem
- **Link to Prostitution**: Studies suggest a connection between childhood sexual abuse and adolescent prostitution (Siegel, Welsh & Senna 2003:206).
- **Socio-environmental Factors**: Child abuse correlates with family dysfunction, parental substance abuse, and marital conflict.
  - Physical violence between parents affects children, increasing their risk.
- **Perpetrators**: Predominantly male (70%); more females involved in child neglect (Kauzlarich & Barlow 2009:85).
- **Parent Ignorance**: Lack of knowledge about child development leads to harsh interpretations of normal behavior (Lundahl, Nimer & Parsons 2006:258).
- **Parent Training**: Effective in reducing risks of child abuse.

### Notable Cases:
- Samantha: Abused by biological and foster parents.
- Wesley Smith: Died from severe abuse by mother's boyfriend.

## 2.2.2 Youthful Victims of Crime
- **Statistics**: Adolescents experience high rates of victimization; murder rates peak between 18-21 years (Conklin 2004:91).
- **Gang Involvement**: Youth gangs average ages 17-18; gang members face higher crime victimization risks (Bartollas 2003:294).
- **Reporting Issues**: Crimes against adolescents less reported; injuries often untreated.
  
## 2.2.3 Women as Victims of Crime
- **Vulnerability**: Women face higher risks of specific crimes (marital violence, sexual assault).
  - 4x more likely to be killed by husbands (Kauzlarich & Barlow 2009:72).
- **Cultural Factors**: Patriarchal systems in certain cultures further exacerbate vulnerabilities.
- **Victim Behavior**: Abuse impacts self-image and societal perceptions; ongoing victimization more common than single incidents.
- **Rape Statistics**: Young women (13-20 years) at highest risk; significant factors include socioeconomic status and living conditions.
- **Sexual Harassment**: Defined by the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act No 32 of 2007 as repeated, unwanted sexual advances; more prevalent in workplaces.

### Conclusion
- The interconnected issues of child abuse, youth victimization, and women as victims illustrate systemic societal failures that necessitate focused legal and social intervention to protect vulnerable populations.

# STUDY UNIT 2.2: Crime Victimisation Among Vulnerable Groups

## 2.2.6 Elderly Victims of Crime
- **Victimisation Rates**: Elderly have lower crime victimisation rates compared to other age groups; however, they experience a higher fear of victimisation (Cooper, Selwood & Livingston, 2008).
- **Risk Factors**: 
  - One in four elders at risk of abuse; detection rate is low.
  - Implications of criminal attacks are more traumatic for the elderly, including higher physical injury risk and economic loss.
  - Lifestyle changes due to fear include isolation and restricted activities (Glanz, 1992).
- **Vulnerability Factors**:
  - Age-related changes (vision, hearing, strength).
  - Isolation increases risk; often live alone.
  - Physical frailty makes injuries more severe and necessitates more medical attention.
  - Dependence on public transport increases exposure to crime.
  - Living in low-security, high-crime areas due to economic circumstances.
- **Abuse Types**:
  - Includes physical, psychological, and financial/material abuse (Kintz, Villian & Cirimele, 2008).
  - Mental abuse and neglect are more prevalent than physical and sexual abuse.
- **Family Abuse**:
  - Notable rise in abuse from family members; includes withholding food, medication, and finances (Reid, 2003).
  - Cooper et al. (2008) report percentages of psychological (25%) and financial abuse (6-18%).

## 2.2.7 Conclusion
- Studied vulnerable categories: children, youth, women, and the elderly.

# STUDY UNIT 2.3: VICTIM SUPPORT AND VICTIM EMPOWERMENT

## 2.3.1 Introduction
- Victimisation affects all South Africans; trauma from the Apartheid era remains.
- Awareness of trauma and support services essential for national mental health.
- Historically, victims were overlooked in criminal justice; now they have recognized rights.
- South Africa lags in service provision but requires better coordination among services.
- Emphasis on practical implementation of victim support and empowerment initiatives.

## 2.3.2 Key concepts
- **Empathy**: Understanding another’s feelings and experiences.
- **Empowerment**: Enabling victims to regain control of their lives post-victimisation.
- **Trauma**: Psychological condition from severe shock with long-lasting negative effects.
- **Identification parade**: Witness identification method for suspected criminals.

## 2.3.3 Definitions and aims of victim support and empowerment
- **Victim Support**: Empathetic assistance rendered post-victimisation.
- **Victim Empowerment**: Enabling victims to reclaim ownership of the crime event.

## 2.3.4 Victim support
- New field aimed at supporting crime/violence victims through emotional and practical help.
- **Objectives**:
  - Mitigate psychological trauma.
  - Identify post-traumatic symptoms.
  - Prevent secondary victimisation by the criminal justice system.
  - Guidance on preventive measures against re-victimisation. 
- Examples of support: 
  - Security improvements, claims assistance, court accompaniment, referrals to services.

## 2.3.5 Victim empowerment
- Facilitates management and control of personal lives post-victimisation.
- Includes services for individual and community empowerment.
- Focus on crime prevention and reduced victimisation.
- Revitalizes victim confidence in society.

## 3.1 Various approaches to crime prevention and control

### 3.1.1 Introduction
- Crime impacts the quality of life for citizens in South Africa, necessitating effective reduction strategies.
- Historically, crime prevention responsibility fell to individuals and families (Lab, 2004; Crawford, 2007).
- Contemporary approaches have evolved to incorporate diverse methodologies and community involvement.

### 3.1.2 Key concepts
- **Crime Prevention**: Defined as organized activities aimed at reducing or limiting crime (O’Block, 1991).
- **Crime Control**: Reactive measures preserving existing crime levels (Lab, 2004).
- Distinction between **crime prevention** (proactive) and **crime control** (reactive).
- Comprehensive definition: Measures by authorities and communities to prevent crime, excluding criminal justice systems.

### 3.1.3 Historical development of crime prevention and control
- Crime prevention is not a recent idea; has historical roots dating back to individual responsibility.
- Lex talionis principle and Code of Hammurabi signify ancient responses to crime.
- The rise of formal social control systems started with policing in cities like Classical Rome.
- England's 1066 practices involved communal responsibility for policing and infractions.
- The 18th century marked the emergence of official roles like magistrates and eventual establishment of the Metropolitan Police (1829).
- Shift toward community involvement in crime prevention acknowledged in the 1960s.
- Notable initiatives in the USA (NCPI, 1971) emphasized community engagement; similar efforts seen in Europe and Canada.

### 3.1.4 Factors crucial to successful crime prevention
- **Successful Crime Prevention Factors**:
  1. State and community collaboration on comprehensive policies.
  2. Multidimensional approaches incorporating various models.
  3. Models based on causative theories of crime.
  4. Attention to specific crime patterns and local conditions.
  5. Evaluation and planning of cost-effective programs.

### 3.1.5 Various crime prevention approaches
- Diverse crime prevention models exist, with classifications based on different criteria.
  
#### 3.1.5.1 The social crime prevention model
- **Social Crime Prevention Model**: A holistic, developmental strategy focusing on socio-economic conditions to prevent crime (Hawkins & Weis, 1985).

### 3.1.5.2 Limiting opportunities for crime
- Strategies aimed at reducing potential criminal opportunities through environmental design and public awareness.

### 3.1.5.3 The community-based crime prevention model
- Involvement of local communities in crime prevention, fostering cooperation among residents.

### 3.1.5.4 The biophysical prevention model
- Focuses on physical spaces and their design to deter crime (e.g., environmental design principles).

### 3.1.5.5 The psychological crime prevention model
- Considers individual and collective psychology in crime prevention strategies, targeting perceptions of safety and fear of crime.

## 3.1. Social Crime Prevention Model
- Combines **social development model**, **social control theory**, and **social learning theory**.
- Asserts family, schools, peers, and community influence behavior sequentially.
- Positive socialization leads to:
  - Development of social skills
  - Reward for desired behaviors
- Emphasizes attachment, commitment to conformity, and belief in conventional order.
- Social bonds inhibit delinquent behavior.

### 3.1.1. Importance of Socioeconomic Structures
- Key socialization structures (e.g., families, schools, churches) are vital for teaching norms and values.
- Programmes aim to assist vulnerable groups and reduce socioeconomic inequalities.

### Examples of Social Crime Prevention Programmes
- Urban cleanup initiatives
- Job provision policies
- Children-focused educational policies
- Family guidance policies
- Youth policies for responsibility
- Health and drug abuse policies

## 3.1.5. Crime Prevention Approaches

### 3.1.5.1. Limiting Opportunities for Crime
- Focuses on potential victims, community, and environment.
- Strategies include:
  - Urban planning and squatter area upgrades
  - Publicity for security awareness
  - Designing secure buildings
  - Physical security measures (e.g., locks)
  - Technological aids (e.g., surveillance cameras)
- Known as **Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design (CPTED)**.
- Particularly effective for property crimes and violent crimes.

### 3.1.5.2. Community-Based Crime Prevention Model
- Awareness of high capture risk deters potential offenders.
- Activities to change social conditions are crucial (Hope 1995).
- Examples include:
  - Neighborhood watches
  - Citizen patrols
  - Community policing
  - Emergency alarm systems

### 3.1.5.3. Biophysical Prevention Model
- Focuses on inner processes affecting humans.
- Early identification and treatment are essential for preventing crime.
- Treatments may include:
  - Medical and psychological techniques
  - Nutritional adjustments

### 3.1.5.4. Psychological Crime Prevention Model
- Addresses psychological needs and disorders linked to criminal behavior.
- Focus on early childhood experiences and family communication.
- Treatment methods include:
  - Psychological testing
  - Various therapeutic approaches

### 3.1.5.5. Multi-Factor Approach
- Developed by Lab (1988), aligning with **National Crime Prevention Strategy (NCPS)**.
- Divided into:
  - **Primary Crime Prevention**: Eliminate opportunities in the environment.
  - **Secondary Crime Prevention**: Focus on early identification and intervention for at-risk groups.
  - **Tertiary Crime Prevention**: Reactive approach targeting reduction of re-offending among already convicted offenders.

## 3.2.2 Key Concepts

- **Revenge**: Actions taken by victims against offenders for personal satisfaction.

## 3.2.3 Aims of the National Crime Prevention Strategy
- Establish a **comprehensive policy framework** for coordinated crime response.
- Promote a **shared understanding and vision** for tackling crime nationally and locally.
- Develop **national programmes** to focus government efforts on crime issues.
- Maximize **civil society participation** in sustainable crime prevention initiatives.
- Create an integrated crime prevention capacity for **ongoing research and evaluation**.

4. Focus on the **crime**, the **offender**, and the **victim**.
5. Emphasizes four **crime prevention models (pillars)** supplementary to existing government initiatives.
6. Responsibility lies with the **Minister** managing the National Growth and Development Strategy.
7. Continuous **evaluation** of programmes is necessary.

## 3.3.1 Introduction
- Importance of understanding **public attitudes** toward punishment and the criminal justice process.
- Aims of punishment post-conviction include:
  - Retribution
  - Incapacitation
  - Deterrence
  - Rehabilitation
  - Restitution

### 3.1 Elements of Punishment Objectives
1. **Retribution**
   - Punishment as deserved consequence (proportionality principle).
   - Reflects community's moral disapproval.
  
2. **Incapacitation**
   - Deprivation of ability, e.g., imprisonment to prevent further crime.

3. **Deterrence**
   - Discouragement of actions via fear (individual and general).
   - Deterrence functions on both levels to reduce crime.

4. **Rehabilitation**
   - Restorative approach to alter offenders’ behavior positively.
   - Aims at societal reintegration, but effectiveness varies.

5. **Restitution (Restoration)**
   - Compensation for victims; restoring relationships and enabling atonement.

## 3.3.2 Key Concepts
- **Sentence**: Criminal sanction by the court post-conviction.
- **Punishment**: Unpleasant consequence imposed for crime, includes court-sanctioned penalties.

### 3.1 Retribution Characteristics

# STUDY UNIT 3.3

### 3.3.1 Rehabilitation Challenges
- Mid-1970s: Robert Martinson's "nothing works" theory highlighted the minimal effect of rehabilitation on recidivism, sparking empirical research (Conklin 1995:513–516).
- Current rehabilitation philosophy emphasizes adaptation and reintegration models.
- Key operational criteria for rehabilitation:
  - Effectiveness measured during various sentencing stages.
  - Success evaluated through an "all or nothing" principle.
  - Comparative success rates of punishment forms.

### 3.3.2 Individual Deterrence
- **Definition**: Aims to teach individuals not to repeat criminal behavior.
- Rationale: Punishment's unpleasantness instills fear to deter future crimes (Brown et al 1996:45).
- Hedonistic risk/reward calculation: Punished individuals reassess risk/reward, reducing crime likelihood (Conklin 1995:441).
- Factors influencing behavior change post-punishment:
  - Rehabilitative effects of treatment.
  - Socialization (recognition of crime’s wrongness).
  - Natural aging out of criminal behavior.
- Difficulty in empirically distinguishing deterrence from other influences (Conklin 1995:442).

### 3.3.3 Incapacitation
- **Definition**: Deprivation of an individual’s natural powers to prevent crime (Webster’s New Collegiate Dictionary 1981:574).
- Forms include imprisonment, house arrest, or correctional supervision.
- Increased focus on incapacitation aligned with 1980s crime control ideology (Welch 1996:92).
- Distinctions:
  - **Collective Incapacitation**: Broad deterrence through lengthened sentences, with potential unfairness and counterproductiveness (Adler et al 1996:246).
  - **Selective Incapacitation**: Targeting high-risk individuals for incarceration without drastically raising prison populations (Bernard & Ritti 1991:33–54; Visher 1995:526).

### 3.3.4 General Prevention
- Assumption: Punishment deters potential criminals.
- **General Deterrence**: Punishment serves as a warning to deter crime, relying on rational choice and awareness of consequences.
- **Hedonistic calculus**: Individuals weigh pain against pleasure in criminal decision-making (Welch 1996:85; Schmalleger 1994:221).
- Effectiveness tied to:
  - Community stigma towards crime.
  - Visible suffering from punishment.
  - Certainty and promptness in punishment execution (Decker & Kohlfeld 1990:2–21).

### 3.3.5 Shortcomings
- Effectiveness of general deterrence questioned:
  - Absolute deterrence noted where motivations extend beyond punishment.
  - Sector policing may lead to geographic crime displacement (Brown et al 1996:184).
  - Restrictive deterrence defined as altering behaviors to reduce punishment risk (Conklin 1995:443).
- Rational actor theory’s assumptions scrutinized (Aker 1990:653–676).
- Difficulty in correlating crime decline directly to deterrence (Steffensmeier & Harer 1991:330–359).

### 3.3.6 Conclusion
- Shift from rehabilitation and individual deterrence to incapacitation and restorative justice practices.
- Incapacitation viewed as a more effective short-term prevention method than deterrence or rehabilitation.
- Recognition of limited confidence in punishment as a broad deterrent, with a need for more grounded approaches.
