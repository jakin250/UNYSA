# LEARNING UNIT 1.1

## 1.1 What is criminology about?
- **Definition of Criminology**
  - Focuses on law making, law breaking, and societal reactions.
  - Juridical concept: studies everything pertinent to crime.

- **Fields of Specialisation (Bezuidenhout & Little 2011:15)**
  - **Criminal Law:** Defines crime and outlines control, prevention, and punishment.
  - **Crime Theories:** Explanations for causes of criminal behaviour.
  - **Victimology:** Examination of crime victims and perceptions.
  - **Behaviour Systems:** Risk assessment for deviant behaviour.
  - **Criminal Justice:** Studies institutions managing offenders (courts, police, prisons).

## 1.1.4 The criminologist’s role
- **Definition (Brown et al. & Gibbons in Bezuidenhout & Little 2011:14)**
  - Criminologist: Professional focused on crime phenomena.
- **Main Roles**
  - Lecturing and research.
  - Community service for victims.
  - Policy development and crime prevention programmes.
- **Functionality**
  - Explain and analyze crime dynamics.
  - Research implications for criminal justice.
  - Submit court reports impacting sentence determinations.
- **Occupational settings:** Academics, law enforcement, correctional services, security, policy development.

## 1.1.5 Is criminology a discipline in its own right?
- **Defining Science (Du Preez & Neser 1993:62)**
  - Systematic knowledge collection, reliable and applicable.
- **Requirements for Independent Discipline (Johnson 1978:49–71)**
  - Unique object of study.
  - Clear study area and methodologies.
  - Theoretical foundation and societal application.
- **Criminology meets criteria:** Clear study of crime, unique methodologies, and global application.

## 1.1.6 The fluctuating parameters of crime
- **Contextual Nature of Crime (Walsh 2015:3)**
  - Variance in legal definitions based on culture and time.
  - Examples of varying definitions.
- **Subjectivity of Justice**
  - Inconsistent application of laws and definitions based on societal norms.
- **Power Relations:** 
  - Minority groups influence laws, reflecting societal values and biases.

## 1.1.7 Various approaches to the study of crime
- **Influences on Definitions (Siegel 2013:12–13)**
  - Personal and professional biases shape criminological perspectives.
- **Perspectives:**
  - Juridical (legal definition) vs. non-juridical (social definition).
- **Criminal Justice Evolution:**
  - Influenced by Roman-Dutch and British law; Constitutional Court upholds rights.

## 1.1.8 The juridical definition of crime 
- **Narrow Definition (Bezuidenhout & Little 2011:15)**
  - Focus on behavior violating state law, leading to punishment.
- **Key Elements:**
  - **The Act:** Must be human, voluntary, observable.
  - **Legal Recognition:** Criminal law must define act; punishment required.
- **Precept:** "No crime without law, no punishment without law" (Snyman et al. 1991:21ff).

### 1.1.8.1 The act itself
- **Criteria for Criminal Acts:**
  - Must be voluntary, intentional, and observable.
  - Exceptions for certain scenarios where acts may be deemed criminal despite non-observability.

- **1.1.8.1 Elements of a Crime**
  - Crime defined by deliberate intent to commit
  - Complicity requires conscious advancement of a crime
  - Human acts and their consequences must be punishable

- **1.1.8.2 The Wrongfulness of the Act**
  - Wrongful act: conflicts with legal norms (prescription/prohibition)
  - Objective requirement; exceptions: self-defense, emergencies, victim consent

- **1.1.8.3 The Element of Guilt**
  - Guilt: culpable frame of mind in committing a wrongful act
  - Forms of guilt: deliberate intent (dolus) and negligence (culpa)
  - Accountability factors: mental illness, age, intoxication, emotional stress

- **1.1.8.4 The Element of Punishment**
  - Only acts punishable by the state constitute crimes
  - Punishment specified by common law or legal prescriptions
  - Possible sanctions: prison, fines, correctional supervision, suspended sentences

- **1.1.9 The Nonjuridical Definition of Crime**
  - Scholars suggest broad understanding beyond legal definitions of crime 
  - Consideration of deviant behavior and societal norms crucial

- **1.1.9.1 Crime as a Violation of Behavioural Norms**
  - Sellin's focus on social norms violation alongside legal definitions
  - Behavioral norms influenced by tradition, religion, law

- **1.1.9.2 Crime as Social Harm**
  - Sutherland's definition includes illegal behavior harming society
  - Focus on white-collar crime and civil injustices

- **1.1.9.3 Crime as a Violation of Human Rights**
  - Schwendingers advocate for human rights violations as crime
  - Examples include restrictions on freedom of movement, speech, and imperialism

- **1.1.9.4 Crime as a Form of Social Deviance**
  - Social deviance: deviation from accepted societal norms with punishments
  - Variability in definitions of criminal behavior over time and culture

# LEARNING UNIT 1.2 

## LEARNING OUTCOMES 
- Discuss schools of criminological thought: classical, positivist, critical, feminist, social milieu, and post-modern criminology in Africa and South Africa.

### 1.2.1 Introduction 
- Development of influential schools of criminological thought.
- Schools of thought are also called approaches, ideologies, movements, or paradigms.

### 1.2.2 Key Concepts 
- **Classical Criminology** (Burke 2014:37)
  - Free will and hedonism; human behavior is pleasure-pain based.
  - Fixed punishments indelibly written in law, equal application to all citizens.
  - Punishment must exceed the pleasure from crime without diminishing overall happiness.

- **Positivism** (Wash 2015:72–3)
  - Focuses on the criminal's lack of control over crime (determinism).
  - Emphasizes treatment over punishment.

- **Critical Criminology** (Walsh 2015:147–148)
  - Defines crime through oppression; crimes of the powerful go unpunished.

- **Feminist Theory** (Burke 2014:227–228)
  - Critiques male-centered criminology; highlights significance of gender roles.

- **Rational Choice** (Burke 2014:63)
  - Criminals weigh costs and benefits; deterrence through punishment.

- **Postmodernism** (Burke 2014:369)
  - Rejects a single theory of crime; embraces eclecticism.

- **Social Process Approach** (Walsh 2015:127)
  - Crime is influenced by social interactions and institutions.

- **Social Structure Approach** (Walsh 2015:105)
  - Crime results from disadvantaged economic class positions.

### 1.2.3 The Purpose of Criminological Theory 
- Theory as interconnected propositions explaining observed facts (Walsh 2015:16).
- Theory helps to understand and make sense of complexities (Walsh 2015:17).
- Theories connect to empirical research through hypotheses.
- Theories should be universally applicable, contextually fluid.

### 1.2.4 Classical Criminology
- Emerged in late 18th century (Beccaria, Bentham) (Eamonn et al. 2015:49).
- Draws from Enlightenment thinkers like Locke, Rousseau, Montesquieu, and Hobbes.
- Social contract emerged from the Renaissance, humans are rational beings.
- Fear, especially of punishment, is key to behaviour control.
- Beccaria’s "On Crimes and Punishments" (1764); supports utilitarianism.
- Punishments must be public, prompt, necessary, and proportionate (Vold & Bernard 1986:25).
- Emphasis on deterrence; potential criminals weigh consequences of actions.
- Key assumptions of the classical school:
  - Humans are self-seeking.
  - Social contracts require sacrifice of freedoms for safety.
  - Punishment deters crime.
  - Punishment must fit the crime, not aim at rehabilitation.
  - Individuals are accountable for actions.

### 1.2.5 Limitations of the Classical Model
- Overly rational view of human nature, ignoring complexities.
- Assumes equal freedom to make choices.
- Does not address the relationship between inequality and crime.

### 1.2.6 The Neo-Classical School
- Modifies classical assumptions; recognizes extenuating circumstances (Brown et al. 2001:279–280).
- Free will may be inhibited by individual circumstances (White & Haines 2001:46).
- Continues the deterrence doctrine but includes individual differences.

### 1.2.7 Crime Control and Due Process Models 
- **Crime Control Model** (Einstadter and Henry 1995:43)
  - Emphasizes law enforcement, strict penalties, prioritizes public safety over civil rights.

- **Due Process Model** (Burke 2015:44-45)
  - Protects individual rights, emphasizes fairness, limits discretion of criminal justice personnel.

### 1.2.8 Criticisms of the Classical Approach 
- Simplistic view of rational decision-making.
- Ignores social inequalities impacting crime.
- Fails to explore causation of crime, focusing on crime control instead (Taylor, Walton & Young 1973:2).

### 1.2.9 Neoclassical Responses 
- Recognized the rigid nature of classical laws (e.g., French Code of 1791).
- Advocated for individual circumstances in justice administration (Vold & Bernard 1986:26).
- Consideration of offender backgrounds and the notion of free will.

# Schools of Thought in Criminology

## 1.2.6 Positivist Criminology (Page 24-25)
- **Origins**: Emerged in late 19th century; influenced by physical and natural sciences.
- **Focus**: Criminal behavior, treatment, rehabilitation.
- Key Figures: 
  - *Adolfe Quetelet* (Belgian mathematician)
  - *Cesare Lombroso* (1876 publication "Criminal Man") – emphasized biological determinism.
- **Shift from Classical Thinking**: Recognized hereditary, psychological, and social factors; redirected focus from rationalism and punishment.

### Key Principles:
- Emphasis on observable facts and scientific methods.
- Individual behavior influenced by social, biological, psychological factors.
- Crime defined more broadly than individual actions.

### Assumptions of Positivism:
- Criminal behavior caused by genetic, biological, psychological features and social context.
- Individuals lack complete rationality; decisions influenced by various constraints.
- Offenders driven by external and internal factors beyond their control.

### Divisions:
- **Individual Positivism**: Biological and psychological explanations.
- **Sociological Positivism**: Focus on social factors.

### Limitations:
- Assumes pure objectivity in research; overlooks biases.
- Accepts status quo; limits critique of social and economic structures.
- Over-reliance on scientific methods for knowledge.

## 1.2.7 Critical Criminology (Page 25-27)
- **Nature**: Encompasses various theories critiquing power and class conflict dynamics in society.
- **Definition of Crime**: Seen as a function of oppression; negatively impacts marginalized groups (e.g., working class, women, ethnic minorities).
  
### Key Critiques of Previous Criminological Theories:
- Previous theories lacked acknowledgment of class, race, and gender in defining crime and deviance.
- Radical criminology highlights the meaningful context of deviant behavior versus societal labels.

### Edwin Sutherland's Contribution:
- Introduced "white-collar crime"; emphasized inequalities in the treatment of crimes committed by different societal classes.

### Arguments:
- Working-class crime is often less consequential than white-collar and corporate crimes, which incur significant societal costs.
- Calls for redistribution of resources and direct participation in decision-making as responses to crime and inequality.

### Critiques:
- Overlooks harsh realities of crime affecting vulnerable communities.
- Tends to minimize criminal pathology among oppressed groups.

## 1.2.8 Feminist Criminology (Page 28-29)
- **Development**: Evolved in late 1960s-1970s as a critique of male-centered criminology.
- **Main Tenet**: Gender and class inequalities significantly influence crime perceptions and responses.

### Perspectives on Female Criminality:
- Rejects generalization of women as mere victims; portrays them as survivors.
- Criticism of male-dominated narratives which trivialize or mislabel female criminality.

### Varied Feminist Views:
- Some advocate for radical systemic change (capitalism and patriarchy); others seek reforms.
- No singular feminist theory; diverse interpretations of female oppression and criminality.

### Impact on Criminology:
- Challenges traditional criminological explanations and emphasizes a need for gender-sensitive perspectives in criminal justice research and policy.

## Contemporary Variants of Feminism

- **Liberal Feminism** (Burke 2014:229–230)
  - Focuses on legal reform to achieve equality.
  - Analyzes social structures and discrimination in public spheres.

- **Radical Feminism**
  - Emphasizes patriarchy as the dominant force controlling women.
  - Attributes practices like prostitution to patriarchal societal structures.

- **Marxist Feminism**
  - Links subordination of women to capitalist exploitation.
  - Critiques the ideological portrayal of women as primary caregivers.

- **Socialist Feminism**
  - Merges radical and Marxist viewpoints.
  - Recognizes both capitalism and patriarchy as oppressive systems.

- **Black Feminism**
  - Investigates the intersectionality of race, gender, and class.
  - Highlights a "matrix of domination" affecting black women.

- **Postmodern Feminism**
  - Advocates for gender equality, encompassing race and sexuality.
  - Questions universal feminist theories.

## Contributions of Feminist Approach
- Critiques malestream criminology regarding women's representation.
- Suggests new areas of study within criminology.
- Focuses on gender issues, especially men's roles in crime (Eamonn et al. 2014:106–7).

## Limitations of Feminist Criminology
- Fails to explain root causes of gender inequalities.
- Radical feminism criticized for universal male dominance assumptions.
- Overemphasis on crimes against women while neglecting other forms of violence.

## Social Milieu School of Thought
- **Proponents**: Tarde, Guerry, Quetelet
- **Approaches**:
  - **Social Structure**: Focuses on community organization (economic systems, family dysfunction, etc.) (Van der Westhuizen 2011:134).
  - **Social Process**: Examines individual law-violating behavior through social interactions (Brown, Esbensen, Geis 2007:319).

### Premises of Social-Environment School
- Based on a juridical conception of crime.
- Focus on social environment of criminals.
- Assumes improved social conditions can reduce crime.
- Lacks emphasis on punishment or rehabilitation.

### Differential Association Theory 
- **Edwin H. Sutherland (1883–1950)**:
  - Criminal behavior learned through interaction with others.
  - Emphasizes external social influences while recognizing individual responses differ.

## Postmodern Criminology
- Emerged in the late 1980s, challenging rationality and science (Schwartz & Friedrichs; Burke 2014:507).
- Not confined to specific crime perspectives.
- Questions conventional definitions of crime and advocates for interdisciplinary approaches.

### Universal Factors Contributing to Crime (Van Dijk 1998:8)
- Economic constraints and income inequality.
- Low education levels.
- High firearm availability.
- Inadequate urban infrastructure.
- Youth population.
- High rates of substance abuse.
- Repeat victimization.

## Key Focus Areas of Postmodern Criminology
- Embraces both juridical and nonjuridical crime definitions.
- Rejects limited definitions of social concepts.
- Integrates diverse factors in crime explanation.
- Prioritizes victim rights and community-based approaches.

## Criminology in Africa
- Limited literature due to political instability.
- Crime largely attributed to colonial capitalism (Mushanga 1992:78).
- Shifted focus from community protections to contemporary victims of various crimes.

### Contributing Factors to Crime in Africa
- Rapid urbanization and cultural transition from ubuntu to individualism.
- Youth population growth and inadequate state support contribute to rising crime rates.
- Need for poverty eradication and community empowerment (Mushanga 1992:x; Kibuka 1980:14).

## Criminology in South Africa
- Criminology aligns with either positivism or critical criminology, depending on crime definitions.
- Early criminologists influenced by Bonger focused on economic disparities.
- Contemporary criminologists address both universal and unique local factors affecting crime.

### Key Factors from National Crime Prevention Plan (1996):
- Destruction of social control post-democratization.
- Political violence and conflict.
- Political justifications for crime.
- Social marginalization and inadequate victim support.
- Socio-psychological influences unique to South Africa.

  - Only crimes reported are included.  
  - Statistics relative to total population can misrepresent the data (e.g. children under seven excluded).

## Racial and Ethnic Factors in Crime

- **Differences in Crime Incidence** (Sykes & Cullen 1992:109)
  - Social position, discrimination, and urban decline contribute to variations in crime rates among ethnic groups.
- **Disputed Claims of Police Prejudice** 
  - J Philippe Rushton attributes racial crime rate differences to genetic factors (Conklin 2004:92).
  - Criticism: Overlooks socio-economic factors; Jerome Neapolitan's analysis shows minimal correlation between race and cross-national homicide rates when controlling for income inequality, living standards, etc. (Conklin 2013:133).

- **Crime Correlates** 
  - Social and economic disadvantages correlate with high crime rates (Siegel et al. 2003:42).
  - Young African American males often receive harsher judgments by the justice system.
  - Chronic exposure to violence in impoverished neighborhoods fuels violent behavior.

- **Socio-economic Conditions** 
  - Conventional crime linked to systemic issues: poverty, unemployment, and unstable family environments (Sykes & Cullen 1992:111).
  - Study of 52 countries indicates economic inequalities strongly correlate with murder rates (Conklin 2004:147).

- **Unemployment and Crime** 
  - No definitive relationship; unemployment is not a primary cause for most crimes (Siegel 2004:56).
  - Relative deprivation may heighten feelings of frustration, linking perceived economic inequities to criminal behavior (Conklin 2013:132).

- **Urbanization Effects** 
  - Migration from rural areas leads to diminished family structure and fewer job opportunities in urban centers, exacerbating crime (Sykes & Cullen 1992:111).
 
- **Sentenced Offenders in South Africa (2014)** 
  - Disproportionate racial representation in prisons raises questions about socio-economic conditions:
    - Black: 79%
    - Coloured: 18%
    - White: 2% (Jules-Macquet 2014:9).

- **Economic Development** 
  - High crime rates associated with modernization; traditional values help maintain lower crime rates in societies like Japan (Conklin 2004:144).

## Gender Differences in Crime

### General Trends

- Males commit more crimes than females; gender differences vary across societies (Conklin 2013:84; Siegel 2013:53–54).
- Female incarceration rates in South Africa: 2% of total prison population, focusing on economic offenses (Jules-Macquet 2014:8).

### Crime Patterns

- **Arrest Trends** 
  - Male arrest rates significantly higher; female rates increase at a quicker pace (Siegel 2004:69).
  - Females often serve as accomplices, resulting in lower detection and prosecution rates.

- **Masked Behavior** 
  - Pollak's concept of "masked criminality": females may engage in more criminal behaviors than reported (Dastile 2011:291).
  - Chivalry hypothesis suggests women receive lighter sentences, reflecting societal biases (Siegel 2013:53).

### Aggression and Socialization

- **Biological vs. Socialization Theories** 
  - Aggression innate in males; females may become aggressive under specific circumstances (Siegel et al. 2003:175).
  - Differences in crime may stem from varying socialization practices for boys and girls, with stricter control over daughters leading to less delinquency (Siegel 2004:69; Sykes & Cullen 1992:108).

- **Power-Control Theory**
  - Delinquency linked to family structure; greater male-female differences in delinquent behaviors observed in patriarchal families (Burke 2014:204–205).

### Opportunities for Crime

- Women have fewer opportunities for large-scale crime, often driven into different criminal roles (e.g., prostitution) (Conklin 2004:161).
- Changes in women's economic and social status have led to increased opportunities for certain types of crime, such as theft.

### Feminist Perspectives

- Liberal feminist theory links women's lower crime rates to historical socio-economic status; however, the influence of feminism on crime rates appears minimal (Siegel 2004:69).
- Women's crime rates not significantly influenced by feminism; more related to economic marginalization (Mears et al. 1998:253).

### Peer Influence

- Exposure to delinquent peers varies by gender; males significantly more likely to engage with delinquent friends, while females are less influenced by such dynamics (Mears et al. 1998:263).

## 3.1.3 Age

- **Inversely related to criminality**: Young offenders have the highest crime rates; rates decline as offenders mature (Siegel et al. 2003:43).
- **Universal correlation**: Younger people commit crimes more than older individuals, reflecting the **age-crime curve** - peaks in adolescence, declines in early adulthood (Walsh 2015:212).
- **Peak age for crime**: Most transgressions peak between 16-20 years (Sykes & Cullen 1992:111). In the USA, arrests peak at age 17 (Conklin 1995:108).
- **Crime types**: Property crime peaks earlier than violent crime; sophisticated theft peaks in early twenties. Gambling and white-collar crimes appear at a later age.
- **South African context**: Majority of prisoners aged 18-25 years; those over 50 comprise only 2% of the total (Jules-Macquet 2014:9).
- **Universal patterns**: Serious crimes (murder, rape, robbery) peak in adolescence and decline with age (Sheley 2000:106).
- **Violent crime statistics**: In the USA (2014), juveniles responsible for 19% of violent crime. In South Africa, conviction rates for 18-25 age group exceed those for adults.
- **Reasons for juvenile crime**:
  - **Developmental factors**: Physicality and mental maturity impact crime commission.
  - **Social factors**: Poor life goals, unsuccessful job searches push juveniles to crime (Sheley 2000:108).
  - **Substance abuse**: Alcohol and drug involvement facilitate delinquency.
  - **Youth culture**: Reduced parental supervision, peer influence, and materialistic advertising drive crime (Cullen & Sykes 1992:114).
  - **Life transition**: Social pressures shift from crime to work/marriage (Siegel et al. 2003:44).

### Developmental Theories
- **Agnew’s super traits theory**: Interaction of low self-control and irritability with life domains impacts offending probability.
- **Farrington’s ICAP theory**: Antisocial potential and cognition shaped into pro or antisocial behaviors; distinguishes between long-term and short-term potential.
- **Moffit’s dual-pathway model**: Differentiates between adolescent-limited offenders (AL) and life-course persistent offenders (LCP); LCPs have predispositions to antisocial behavior.
- **Sampson and Laub’s age-graded theory**: Emphasizes informal social control; turning points (marriage, job) vital for desistance.

## 3.1.4 Conclusion

- **Criminal profile**: Higher crime rates among blacks due to social conditions (poverty, unemployment) and political factors (discrimination, urbanization).
- **Gender differences**: Fewer women commit crimes; when they do, they are less serious. Explains factors include social pressure, supervision, and opportunity.
- **Age factors**: Criminal behavior peaks 16-20; influenced by physical capacity, parental supervision, peer pressure. As individuals age, adherence to social norms increases, reducing crime's allure.

## 3.2.4 Distinction between motive and intent
- **Motive**: reason behind an action; causative factor driving action (Drives individual to act).
- **Intent**: aimed goal; conscious deliberation and purposefulness (Tebit 2005:169).
- Importance of distinguishing motive and intent:
  - Example 1: Doctor administering lethal medication out of love vs. wife motivated by greed to murder.
  - Judge determines motive and intent to inform crime type (e.g., murder vs. culpable manslaughter).
- Intent indicates desired outcome from action; motive explains reason for desire (Tebit 2005:169).
- Case Reference: Taxi driver conviction for murder (CMY1501, 2007:94–95).
  - Intent evidenced by deliberate actions and indifference to consequences.

## 3.2.5 The process of motivation
- **Motivation Theories**:
  - **Instrumental Theories**: behavior influenced by reward or punishment.
  - **Content Theories**: focus on fulfilling needs.
  - **Process Theories**: psychological processes affecting motivation (Armstrong 2003:218).
- **Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs**:
  - Ranges from basic physiological needs to self-actualization.
  - Basic needs must be met before higher-order needs (Dreyer 2008:8).
- **Components of Motivation** (Armstrong 2003:216):
  - **Direction**: objective of the person's action.
  - **Effort**: intensity of the attempts.
  - **Persistence**: duration of attempts.
- **Motivation Process**:
  - Initiated by recognition of unsatisfied need, creating wants.
  - Goals established to satisfy needs, leading to selected behaviors (Armstrong 2003:217).
- **Cognition**: Influences judgment and strategy, informed by primary and secondary appraisal (Reeve 2009:346–348).
- **Social Setting**: Emotional socialization impacts motivation and may catalyze crime.

## 3.2.6 Motivation and criminal behaviour
- **Key Questions**: What causes criminal behavior? Why do people commit crime?
- **Theoretical Frameworks**:
  - **Rational Choice Theory**:
    - Behavior motivated by hedonistic calculus; free will to choose actions (Walsh 2015:89).
    - Crime is both offence-specific and offender-specific; individual considers risks and benefits.
  - **Trait Theory**:
    - Originated from Lombroso; considers biological/psychological traits influencing behavior (Siegel 2009:122).
  - **Social Structure Theories**:
    - Links crime to poverty and unemployment; focuses on social institutions affecting behavior (Walsh 2015:105).
    - Subcategories include:
      - **Social Disorganisation Theory**: Examines urban conditions impacting crime.
      - **Strain Theory**: Crime arises from conflicts between goals and means of achievement.
      - **Cultural Deviance Theory**: Addresses unique strains in lower-class cultures (Siegel 2009:166).
  - **Social Process Theories**:
    - Emphasizes socialization as determinant of criminal behavior; includes:
      - **Social Learning Theory**: Crime techniques learned through social interactions.
      - **Social Control Theory**: Natural motivations toward crime curbed by societal bonds.
      - **Labelling Theory**: Labels affect self-identity and behavior (Siegel 2009:202; Walsh 2015:131).
  
- **Motivation Factors in Rape** (Cohen et al. in De Wet 2008:19; Bezuidenhout & Klopper 2011:232–3):
  - **Power**: Rapist expresses dominance and inadequacy.
  - **Anger**: Attack driven by frustration and hostility.
  - **Sexuality/Sadism**: Enjoyment derived from victimization and control.

### Types of Rape Motivation

  - Power-Reassurance: Attempting to overcome doubts about masculinity.
- **Anger Rape**:
  - Anger-Retaliation: Expression of hostility.
  - Anger-Excitation: Pleasure derived from the act.
- **Sexually Motivated/Sadistic Rape**: Power and anger eroticized; sexual gratification through torment.

## I. Moral Development Stages  

- **Stage One: Obedience and Punishment Driven**  
  - Focus on consequences for self; actions deemed "wrong" due to punishment.  
  - Egocentric and lacks recognition of different perspectives.  
  - Deference to authority prevails.  
   
- **Stage Two: Self-Interest Driven**  
  - Morality defined by personal gain; "What's in it for me?" attitude.  
  - Limited concern for others, based on reciprocity.  
  - Moral relativism dominates at this stage.  

## II. Conventional Level  

- **Overview**  
  - Typical of adolescents and adults, morality judged by societal norms.  
  - Includes Stages Three and Four; adherence to rules and expectations is rigid.  

- **Stage Three: Good Intentions as Determined by Social Consensus**  
  - Individuals conform to societal standards for approval.  
  - Actions evaluated based on relationships and societal views.  

- **Stage Four: Authority and Social Order Obedience Driven**  
  - Importance of obeying laws for societal function; moral reasoning beyond seeking approval.  
  - Laws perceived as obligations; duty to uphold societal rules denoted here.  

## III. Post-Conventional Level  

- **Overview**  
  - Individuals recognize personal moral principles over societal standards.  
  - Changes to laws are perceived as necessary for justice.  

- **Stage Five: Social Contract Driven**  
  - Views laws as social agreements that should reflect the general welfare.  
  - Acknowledges diversity in opinions and values.  

- **Stage Six: Universal Ethical Principles Driven**  
  - Moral reasoning grounded in justice and ethical principles.  
  - Commitment to justice may necessitate disobeying unjust laws.  
  - Action driven by intrinsic morality rather than external factors.

# Learning Unit 4.1: Purposeful Behavior and Intention

- **Purposeful Behavior**  
  - Free will, choice, conscious and voluntary actions.

- **Volition and Intention**  
  - Intention comprises:  
    - Cognitive element: Knowledge of circumstances and means.  
    - Conative element: Willingness towards a specific act (Snyman 1995:168).  
  - Legal definition of intention includes “evil intention” with awareness of the act’s unlawfulness (Snyman 1995:168).  
  - Three forms of intention:  
    - Direct intention (dolus directus)  
    - Indirect intention (dolus indirectus)  
    - Foreseeing consequences (dolus eventualis).

- **Theories of Free Will**  
  - **Classical School Perspective**  
    - Assumes total free will and rational behavior (Beccaria).
  - **Hard Determinism**  
    - Behavior determined by external factors (Lombroso).  
  - **Soft Determinism**  
    - Limited free will influenced by biological and environmental factors (Blackburn 1993:25).

- **Assessment of Intention**  
  - Requires determination of knowledge and capability to appreciate the wrongfulness of actions.  
  - Factors influencing capability include mental illness, immature age, non-pathological criminal incapacity, provocation, and intoxication (Snyman 1995:153).

- **Case Study: Dr. Manuela Costa**  
  - Injected children and set fire to home; context of severe personal distress (cancer, financial issues).  
  - Claimed lack of control during actions.  
  - Found not guilty due to inability to discern her actions.

## 4.1.5.1 Family Environment

- **Parental Influence on Delinquency**  
  - Traits formed in childhood; positive or negative parenting shapes self-image.

  - **Parental Conflict and Broken Homes**  
    - High parental conflict correlates with delinquency (Bartollas 2003:222).  
    - Divorce linked to economic hardship, loss of role models.

  - **Parental Rejection**  
    - Emotional deprivation leads to deviant group associations (Trojanowicz et al. 2001:143).  
    - Links between parental love and delinquent behavior; hostile parenting harms emotional development.

  - **Parental Control Methods**  
    - Ineffective or harsh disciplinary styles contribute to delinquency.  
    - Lax or erratic discipline leads to antisocial behavior (Hollin 1992:80).  
    - Importance of active parental engagement for socialization.

#### **4.1.5.2 School Environment**
- Schools as socialization agents can foster delinquency (Trojanowicz et al.).
- Poor school performance and negative attitudes linked to delinquency (Bartollas).
- High school failures and association with delinquent peers strongly correlate with delinquency (Trojanowicz & Morash).
- South African schools rank lowest in safety globally (Plusnews 2008).
- School violence influenced by:
  - Lack of conflict resolution skills among staff.
  - Poor control over external social issues (disrespect, family discipline).
  - Failure to address internal negative attitudes.
- Schools in inner cities suffer from poverty and crime, affecting educational focus.
- Delinquency stems from unmet social skills and negative self-images due to academic failure.

#### **4.1.5.3 Peer Group Influences**
- Peer groups gain significance during adolescence; help shape behavior (Kassin).
- Youth from deprived backgrounds more susceptible to peer influence.
- Delinquent peer groups form from shared interests and fulfill basic needs for belonging.
- Relationship between delinquent peers and individual delinquency is strong (Trojanowicz et al.).
- Gang classification (Yablonsky):
  - Social: Individual social goals.
  - Delinquent: Material profits from criminal acts.
  - Violent: Emotional gratification through violence.
- Gang attraction includes security, fun, and social interaction.
- Gang activities often include drug use and are influenced by socio-economic factors, particularly in South Africa.

## 4.3.1 Theories of Crime

### Structure Theories
- Focus on societal organization and its influence on behavior
  - Individual offender not emphasized.

### Process Theories
- Explain offender development through learning mechanisms.

### Conflict Theories
- Argue behavior shaped by interpersonal conflict; power dynamics influence needs.

### Consensus Theories
- Based on shared societal ideals; crimes are acts against majority rules.

## 4.3.2 What is Theory?
- A theory consists of logically interconnected propositions explaining relationships within a domain.
- Provides answers to "why"; serves as a basis for crime understanding in criminology.  
- Historical viewpoints:
  - **Biological:** Crime as hereditary.
  - **Psychological:** Crime as acquired by individuals.
  - **Sociological:** Crime linked to societal structure/function.

## 4.3.3 Characteristics of a "Good" Theory
- **Components of a good theory (Maguire & Radosh 1999):**
  - **Plausibility:** Provides logical and meaningful explanations.
  - **Validity:** Supported by empirical research.
  - **Falsifiability:** Must be able to be proven false.
  - **Predictability:** Ability to anticipate future behaviors.

## 4.3.4 How to Study Theory

### Background
- Theorists' social and intellectual environments influence theory development.
- Different orientations (psychological vs. sociological) lead to different crime prevention strategies.

### Key Premises
- Framework of theory reduced to essential arguments; theorists' own terminologies.

### Responsibility for Actions (Free Will)
- Critical to establish individual agency under various influences (biological, psychological, social).

### Policy Implications
- Theories influencing social policy based on empirical evidence, guiding strategies for crime control.

### Evaluation
- **Theoretical Adequacy:** Assessed via plausibility, empirical support, falsifiability, predictability.
- **Comprehensiveness:** Able to explain all types of crime across classes and types.


## 4.3.5 Usefulness of Theory
- Understanding crime leads to controlling behavior; theories influence policy.
- SA examples include preventative measures by SAPS and treatment guidelines from criminological assessments.

## 4.3.6 Classification of Theories

### Classical vs. Positivist Theories
- **Classical:** Focus on laws, rights of individuals.
- **Positivist:** Concentrate on the pathology of criminality.

### Structure vs. Process Theories
- **Structure Theories:** Focus on societal organization; strain theory as a subfield.
- **Process Theories:** Focus on individual development of criminal behavior.

### Consensus vs. Conflict Theories
- **Consensus Theories:** Assumption of shared societal values.
- **Conflict Theories:** No societal consensus; conflicting values exist across classes.

## 4.3.7 Conclusion
- Theories must balance individual and societal influences; a good theory is meaningful, valid, and adaptable for predictions.

# LEARNING UNIT 4.4: EXPLANATION OF CRIME

### 4.4.1 Introduction
- Theories vary in comprehensiveness; some focus on individual factors (biological, psychological), while others on social structures.
- Michael Gottfredson & Travis Hirschi's **General Theory of Crime** (1990) aims for comprehensive explanation.

### 4.4.2 Key Concepts
- **Self-control**: Established early; moral sense that inhibits harm.
- **White-collar crime**: Illegal acts exploiting one's market status (e.g., embezzlement, fraud).
- **Self-Control Theory**: Resultant of natural human impulses, leading to crime; linked to personality traits.

### 4.4.3 Key Premises of the General Theory of Crime
- Crime definition is expansive, encompassing various acts unlike traditional theories.
- Distinction between the criminal act and the offender.
- Individual differences in criminality correlate with levels of self-control.
- Poor child-rearing practices as root cause of low self-control.

### 4.4.4 The Criminal Act
#### 4.4.4.1 Definition of Crime
- Crime varies by culture/time; core crimes (mala in se) consistently condemned.
- Gottfredson & Hirschi's definition: "Acts of force or fraud for self-interest."

#### 4.4.4.2 Common Characteristics of Crime
- Immediate gratification of desires.
- Easy, simple, and exciting satisfaction of needs; minimal effort required.
- Crime leads to negative consequences for victims, such as harm and distrust.

### 4.4.5 The Potential Offender
- Traits of potential offenders:
  - Impulsiveness and minimal frustration tolerance.
  - Preference for immediate solutions without perseverance.
  - Attracted to crime due to poor self-image and lack of long-term outlook.
- Traits include self-centeredness and lack of sensitivity; propensity for physical harm.

### Evaluation of the Theory
- **4.4.7.1 Empirical Support**: Supported by evidence; needs further investigation.
- **4.4.7.2 Shortcomings**: The theory has limitations; not comprehensive for all crime forms.

## 4.4.6 Causes of Poor Self-Control

- **4.4.6.1 Poor Child-Rearing Practices**
  - Ineffective parenting is the main cause of low self-control; early inability to instill self-control difficult to remedy later.
  - Behavioural problems in childhood often correlate with delinquency and adult offending.
  - **Gottfredson and Hirschi**: Traits contributing to poor self-control are established early and persist throughout life.
  - Quality of early parenting affects self-control development: neglectful or lax parenting leads to impulsive and risk-prone behavior.
  - **In South Africa**: Chronic stress, unemployment, and poverty contribute to lax parenting.
  - **Research Findings**: Juvenile delinquents lack discipline, supervision, and affection. Poor parental conduct often seen (e.g., substance abuse, criminal records).
  - Minimum requirements for effective child-rearing:
    - Monitoring and supervision of child's behaviour.
    - Ability to identify and address delinquency.
    - Punishment for misbehaviour.
  - Affectionate investment by supervisors enhances educational outcomes and social sensitivity.

- **When Education Goes Wrong**:
  - Parents' neglect or failure to supervise impacts child development.
  - Lack of appropriate punishment contributes to poor self-control in children.

- **Summary**: Children lacking self-control may struggle academically, socially, and in employment. Conversely, effective parenting fosters self-control, supporting educational and relational success.

- **4.4.6.2 Circumstantial Factors**
  - Poor self-control often conflated with social circumstances; difficult to distinguish inherent traits from environmental influences.
  - **Gottfredson and Hirschi**: Crime potential intrinsic to human nature; higher risk without socialisation.
  - Crime requires rewarding motives and absence of detection or consequences.
  - Manifestations of poor self-control include antisocial behaviour; patterns vary among individuals.

## 4.4.7 Evaluation

- **4.4.7.1 Empirical Support**
  - Research supports self-control theory; correlations between impulsiveness/self-control and criminality observed in studies (e.g., impulsivity in drunk driving).

- **4.4.7.2 Shortcomings**
  - Criticism of attributing self-control variability solely to parental influence; child behaviour also affects parental actions.
  - Some studies indicate genetic factors contribute to low self-control.
  - Personality alone cannot conclusively differentiate criminals from non-criminals.
  - Theory insufficient to explain regional crime rate variations amidst ecological factors (e.g., poverty, unemployment).

## 4.4.8 Conclusion

- **Gottfredson and Hirschi**: Crime is characterized by distinct traits; disciplined child-rearing practices vital in fostering self-control.
- Consideration of circumstantial factors alongside self-control essential for understanding crime and related behaviours like substance abuse.
