#### STUDY UNIT 1.1
1.1.1 **Introduction**
- Victim as key component in criminology.
- Victims often overlooked in favor of focus on criminals.

1.1.2 **Key Concepts**
- **Crime victim**: A person injured or harmed due to crime (Dussich 2006:118).
- **Victimology**: Discipline studying data on victimisation (Dussich 2006:118).
- **Victimisation**: Significant damage to individuals, communities, or organizations (Dussich 2006:118).

1.1.3 **A Brief Historical Background**
- Established in the 1940s by Mendelsohn, Von Hentig, Schafer, and Wertham.
- Mendelsohn as the father of victimology (Rock 2007:41; Dussich 2006:116).

1.1.4 **Definition of the Concept ‘Victim’**
- **Three paradigms**:
  - **Conservative/Positivist Victimology**: Clear definitions of victims and offenders; blame placed on offenders (Snyman 2005:8-9).
  - **Radical Victimology**: Reform-centric, blurring roles of victim and offender; acknowledges context of victimisation (Snyman 2005:9).
  - **Critical Victimology**: Challenging current criminal justice systems; views social control as a contributing factor to victimisation (Snyman 2005:9).

- **Vianno's Definition** (2000:9-10):
  - A victim is someone harmed/damaged by another and recognized by society; four stages of victimisation:
    - **Stage 1**: Experience of injury or suffering.
    - **Stage 2**: Perception of injury as unjust.
    - **Stage 3**: Recognition of victimhood; seeking help.
    - **Stage 4**: Acknowledgment by self and society; barriers hinder reporting (e.g., societal beliefs).

1.1.5 **Problems That Crime Victims Experience**
- 1.1.5.1 **Physical loss**
- 1.1.5.2 **Economic loss**
- 1.1.5.3 **Emotional/Psychological loss**
- 1.1.5.4 **Social loss**

1.1.6 **Conclusion**
- Victim impacts important in understanding criminal behaviour and societal responses.

### Study Unit 1.1

#### 1.1.5 Problems Crime Victims Experience

- **Areas of Impact**: 
    - Physical
    - Economic
    - Emotional/Psychological
    - Social relationships

##### 1.1.5.1 Physical
- Physical effects include:
    - Pain and suffering; heightened adrenaline, increased heart rate, hyperventilation.
    - Physical injuries: categorized as minor (bruises), moderate (broken bones), or severe (stabbing).
    - Some injuries are visible (e.g., bruises), while others (e.g., internal injuries) may not be apparent.

##### 1.1.5.2 Economic
- Financial impact from crime:
    - Direct: loss or damage (theft).
    - Indirect: includes costs for medical attention, legal advice, and loss of income.
    - Other financial burdens:
        - Property repairs/replacements.
        - Increased insurance premiums.
        - Security measure installations.
        - Court-related travel expenses.
        - Counseling costs.

##### 1.1.5.3 Emotional/Psychological
- Psychological reactions to crime:
    - **Shock and Fear**: Initial responses include shock, fear, anger, guilt.
    - **Disorientation**: Resulting in distress, nightmares, depression.
    - **Acceptance**: Reconstruction of the event; victims seek to understand and decide on personal growth from the experience.
    - **Normalization/Adjustment**: Victims work to reclaim their lives and return to normalcy.

##### 1.1.5.4 Social Relationships
- Impact on social relationships:
    - **Intimate Relationships**: 
        - Disruptions may arise from childhood traumas leading to difficulties in forming attachments.
        - PTSD symptoms (avoidance, hyperarousal) can hinder intimacy.
    - **Occupational Functioning**: 
        - Inability to maintain employment due to physical or psychological effects of victimization.
        - Victims may face absenteeism, decreased productivity, or job loss due to safety concerns.
    - **Social Functioning**: 
        - Victimization disrupts social activities; victims may withdraw from social interactions to avoid reminders of trauma.

# Study Notes on Victimisation

## 1.2.3 Factors Influencing Victimisation

### 1.2.3.5 Income
- Low-income groups at higher risk of violent/non-violent crime (Van der Hoven & Maree 2005:59; Siegel 2011:71).
- Poor victims more often experience violent crimes; wealthy victims typically face personal theft.
- Wealthy individuals attract thieves due to visible signs of affluence (Siegel 2011:71–72).

### 1.2.3.6 Neighbourhood
- Residents near squatter camps face higher risks of burglary and theft compared to those in affluent suburbs (Van der Hoven & Maree 2005:58).
- Informal camps often exhibit increased criminal activity due to anonymity and social problems.
- Urban areas more crime-oriented due to anonymity and weaker social bonds compared to rural areas.
- Communal living and interdependence in rural societies contribute to lower victimisation rates (principle of Ubuntu).
- Poor urban planning and inadequate security heighten susceptibility to crime (Van der Hoven & Maree 2005:58).

### 1.2.3.7 Repeat Victimisation
- Individuals/groups can be more victim-prone due to personal traits, social situations, or relationships with offenders.
- Key assumptions about repeat victimisation (Van der Hoven & Maree 2005:65):
  - Past victimisation predicts future occurrences.
  - More prior incidents increase likelihood of subsequent victimisation.
  - Victimisation typically occurs shortly after previous incidents.
  - Risk escalates for additional victimisations.

- Reasons for repeat victimisation (Wiesel 2005:12):
  - **Flag Explanation**: Attractive/vulnerable targets remain constant over time.
    - Examples of attractive targets: corner houses, convenience stores, taxi drivers, high-performance vehicles.
  - **Boost Explanation**: Repeat offenders gain insights from initial offences, leading to reoffending.

---

## 1.2.4 Conclusion
- Discussed social and demographic factors increasing victimisation risk: young, unmarried individuals, particularly black males/females, and factors such as income, neighbourhood, and repeat victimisation.

# STUDY UNIT 2.1

## 2.1.1 Introduction
- Vulnerability refers to the susceptibility of certain populations to victimization (Spencer, 2015).
- External and internal factors contribute to a greater likelihood of victimization (Clark, 2005).

## 2.1.2 Key Concepts
- **Victim Risk**:
  - Low-risk: Individuals unlikely to experience danger (Van der Hoven & Maree, 2005).
  - Medium-risk: Individuals with potential exposure to danger.
  - High-risk: Individuals consistently exposed to danger.
  
- **Victim Precipitation**: 
  - Victim's behavior may provoke the offence, influencing crimes like murder and assault (Saponaro, 2013; Van der Hoven & Maree, 2005).

- **Routine Activities Approach**: 
  - Crime occurs when a motivated offender, suitable target, and absence of capable guardian co-exist (Saponaro, 2013).

## 2.1.3 Definition of ‘Victim Vulnerability’
- Victim vulnerability is the likelihood of becoming a victim of crime, influenced by behavior, lifestyle, and interaction with offenders (Bruce, 2013; Van der Hoven & Maree, 2005).

## 2.1.4 Vulnerability to Victimisation
- Focus on five concepts: victim precipitation, facilitation, attractiveness of the target, lifestyle, and victim/offender interaction.

### 2.1.4.1 Precipitating Factors
- **Victim Precipitation**: The victim's responsibility in their victimization, which can be passive or active (Doerner & Lab, 2002).
  - Active: Victim initiates confrontation (Siegel, 2011).
  - Passive: Circumstances provoke conflict (e.g., reputation attacks).
- Victim-blaming shifts responsibility from offenders and overlooks social conditions contributing to crime (Van der Hoven, 2007).
- Precipitation may not explain criminal behavior comprehensively; many offenders plan crimes (Doerner & Lab, 2002).

### 2.1.4.2 Facilitating Factors
- Facilitation occurs due to negligence by the victim that aids the offender (e.g., leaving doors unlocked) (Sheley, 2000; Van der Hoven & Maree, 2005).
- Targets who are intoxicated or distracted become easier victims (Karmen, 2009).

### 2.1.4.3 Attractiveness of the Target
- Target appeal to offenders; some targets deemed easier (Karmen, 2009).
- Example: Individuals on phones in isolated areas are attractive targets (Saponaro, 2013).
- Tourist vulnerability: Lack of awareness and conspicuousness increases risk (Geldenhuys, 2016; Van der Hoven & Maree, 2005).

### 2.1.4.4 Lifestyle Factors
- **Lifestyle**: Daily activities and social roles that influence victimization risk (Hindelang et al., 1978).
  - Social roles expose individuals to different risks (e.g., younger individuals more likely to be victimized at night).
- Social position impacts risk; higher positions generally correlate with lower risks due to safer activities and living environments (Saponaro, 2013).
- Rational decision-making based on lifestyle can increase or decrease exposure to risk, with reckless behaviors leading to higher risk of victimization (Van der Hoven & Maree, 2005).

# STUDY UNIT 2.2: SPECIFIC CATEGORIES OF VICTIMS WHO ARE PARTICULARLY VULNERABLE TO CRIME

## 2.2.1 Introduction
- Focus on children, youth, women, and the elderly as vulnerable victims.
- Contributing factors to victimization include:
  - Abuse
  - Poverty
  - Political violence
  - Discrimination
  - Social isolation

## 2.2.2 Key concepts
- **Child**: 
  - Defined by the South African Constitution (1996) §28(3) and Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act 32 of 2007 as under 18 years (Da Costa 2014:4).
- **Child sexual abuse**: 
  - Involves activities that a child cannot comprehend or consent to; may involve coercion (Omar et al 2012:6; Barbaree & Marshall 2006:9).
- **Bullying**: 
  - Repeated negative actions by individuals, both direct (physical) and indirect (social exclusion) (Olweus 1994).
  - School bullying involves repeated negative actions by peers (De Wet 2005:707).

## 2.2.3 Children as vulnerable victims
- Victimization occurs in various circumstances, including family dynamics, political unrest, and socio-economic issues.
- **Role of Parents**:
  - Key caregivers and protectors; risk escalates when neglect or abuse occurs (Siegel 2009:302).
  - Lack of basic necessities leads to neglect (Benokraitis 2011:393).
  
- **Types of Abuse**: 
  - Various forms: physical, sexual, emotional neglect (Conklin 2004:190).
  - Sexual abuse, especially within families, is prevalent (Reid 2003:215).
  
- **Impact of Sexual Abuse**: 
  - Leads to disrupted development, mental health disorders, and higher risk of future victimization (Siegel et al 2003:206; Fergusson et al 2008:614).

- **Community and Family Factors**:
  - Violence in the home and community contributes significantly to children’s vulnerability (Clark 2012:83).
  - Antisocial behavior correlates with parenting styles and family background (Becroft 2009:2, 6).

- **Statistics & Reporting**:
  - Challenges in sex offense statistics—many go unreported (Da Costa 2014:16).
  - Increase in calls to Childline South Africa indicating rising child abuse cases (Van Niekerk 2009:1).

- **Street Children**: 
  - Often from dysfunctional families; face extreme vulnerabilities (Peacock & Rosenblatt 2013:201).
  - Engage in survival strategies, risking further victimization (Dastile 2015:32).

- **Factors Contributing to Vulnerabilities**: 
  - Economic disparities, community violence, lack of education, inadequate parental support (Da Costa 2014:55).

#### 2.2.1 Child Prostitution and Child Sex Tourism  
- Commercial sexual exploitation of children (Geldenhuys 2016:18).
- Common among homeless children or those estranged from families (Mbali 2005:263–264). 
- Approximately 40,000 child prostitutes in South Africa; many more at risk (Geldenhuys 2016:20).

---

#### 2.2.4 Youth as Victims of Crime  
- Adolescents/young adults face highest rates of victimisation; homicide peaks at ages 18-21 (Conklin 2004:91).
- Rates of rape, sexual assault, robbery, etc., peak between 16-19, then decline.
- Active lifestyle increases exposure to crime; gang involvement prevalent among ages 12-24 (Bartollas 2003:294).
- Youth crimes less likely to be reported; youth injuries result in lower likelihood of seeking medical help.
- Youth using weapons more likely to be victimised (Da Costa et al., 2014:46).
- Characteristics of female youth sex offenders:
  - Family instability and dysfunction.
  - High prevalence of prior victimisation.
  - Victimise familiar children.
  - Target mainly females, sometimes males.
- Apartheid's legacy contributed to a culture of violence (Da Costa & Victor-Zietsman 2013:28).

---

#### 2.2.5 Women as Victims of Crime  
- Women prone to marital violence, rape, and harassment (Singh 2005:190).
- Vulnerability varies: minority, poor, and disabled women more at risk (Dastile 2014:6).
- Rural women often face greater sexual victimisation; less access to help (Singh 2005:190).
- Abuse leads to a cycle of violence with phases: tension-building, explosion, honeymoon (Singh 2005:191–192).
- Myths in the justice system impede assistance (e.g., domestic violence viewed less seriously).

**Key Legislation:**
- **Domestic Violence Act 116 of 1998**
- **Protection from Harassment Act 17 of 2011**

**Key Case Points:**
- Violence against women often perpetrated by known individuals.
- Increased victimisation—reporting and assistance issues compared to attacks from strangers.
- Issues of shame and guilt prevent women from leaving abusive situations.

2. Right to offer information regarding the case.
3. Right to receive information about the case.
4. Right to protection from further harm.
5. Right to assistance during the process.
6. Right to compensation for losses incurred.
7. Right to restitution from the offender.

# STUDY UNIT 3.1

### 3.1.1 Historical Context of Crime Prevention
- **1066 England**: Crime prevention responsibility shifted to male citizens forming groups to police themselves.
  - **"Watch and ward" concept**: Citizens observed the city at night, identifying threats and raising alarms.
- **Mid 1700s**: Introduction of English Magistrates as "thief-takers" responsible for public protection.
  - Selected from reformed offenders, allowed to recover stolen property for compensation.
- **1829**: Establishment of the Metropolitan Police by Sir Robert Peel; focus on formal roles in crime prevention.
- **1960s Resurgence**: Shift back to community involvement; community regarded as cornerstone in crime prevention.

### 3.1.2 International Developments in Crime Prevention
- **USA (1971)**: Creation of the National Crime Prevention Institute (NCPI) to enhance community involvement.
- **England (1966)**: Local crime prevention panels; Home Office Crime Prevention Centre established in 1983.
- **France, Sweden, Denmark**: Formalized community crime prevention; e.g., France initiated prevention councils in 1983.
- **Canada**: Advocated "proactive social development approach" focusing on grassroots issues (poverty, unemployment).

### 3.1.3 Theoretical Framework of Crime Prevention
- **Dixon (2006)**: Crime as a primary social problem; calls for careful integration of crime prevention within social policy.
  - Warning against "criminalisation of social policy" and "socialisation of crime prevention."
  
### 3.1.4 Factors for Successful Crime Prevention
1. **State and community involvement**: Integral in comprehensive policy implementation.
2. **Multidimensional approach**: Incorporates various crime prevention models and programs.
3. **Causation theories**: Programs rooted in factors contributing to crime and victimization.
4. **Uniqueness of crime patterns**: Attention to specific causes and conditions of crime per locality.
5. **Planning and evaluation**: Necessity for cost-effective program assessments.

### 3.1.5 Crime Prevention Models
- **3.1.5.1 Social Crime Prevention Model**: 
  - Comprehensive, developmental approach; integrates social control and learning theory.
  - Focus on critical socialization units (family, schools, peers); encourages positive social bonds.
  
- **3.1.5.2 Limiting Opportunities for Crime**: 
  - Aimed at restricting criminal opportunities.
  - Programs include urban planning, publicity for security consciousness, and physical deterrents (CPTED).
  
- **3.1.5.3 Community-Based Crime Prevention Model**: 
  - Awareness of high risks leads to crime prevention; activities include neighborhood watches and community policing.
  
- **3.1.5.4 Biophysical Prevention Model**: 
  - Focuses on intrinsic human conditions; advocates early identification/intervention in at-risk youth.
  - Combination of medical and psychological treatments tailored to individuals.

### Conclusion
- Crime prevention has evolved significantly, transitioning from community-based efforts to structured government initiatives. The importance of integrated approaches combining social, economic, and environmental strategies is essential for effective crime prevention.

## 3.3.1 Introduction
- Importance of understanding public attitudes toward punishment and crime's societal impact.
- Justification for punishment based on deterrence, incapacitation, rehabilitation, restoration, and retribution.

## 3.3.2 Key Concepts
- **Deterrence**: Discouraging actions by instilling fear, defined as the inhibition of criminal activity via state penalties (*Conklin 2013:323*).
- **Incapacitation**: Custodial control of offenders to prevent future crimes (*Conklin 2013:334*).
- **Rehabilitation**: Restoration of offenders through planned interventions aimed at character change (*Conklin 2013:203*).
- **Restitution**: Offenders making monetary payments or rendering services to victims/community (*Conklin 2013:318*).
- **Retribution**: Justly deserved punishment; an eye for an eye concept.

## 3.3.3 Objectives of Punishment
- Understanding the goals of punishment within the criminal justice process to determine guilt or innocence and appropriate sentencing.

### 3.3.3.1 Deterrence
- **Individual Deterrence**: Aims to prevent repeat offenses by instilling fear of punishment in specific individuals.
- **General Deterrence**: Discourages crime in the general population through fear of punishment.
- Factors influencing behavioral change include rehabilitation, socialization, and maturation (*Brown et al 1996:375*).

### 3.3.3.2 Incapacitation
- Purpose: Decrease offenders' physical capacity to commit crimes; focused on eliminating criminal opportunities.
- Methods: Imprisonment, house arrest, detention (*Conklin 2013:334*).
- Types:
  - **Collective Incapacitation**: Same sentences for same crimes, regardless of individual characteristics (*Lab 2014:305*).
  - **Selective Incapacitation**: Focuses on high-risk offenders using different sentences based on individual risk.

### 3.3.3.3 Rehabilitation
- Objective: Restore offenders as constructive citizens via treatment, education, and training.
- Current terminology includes 'correctional facilities' and 'therapeutic communities', emphasizing reformation over punishment.

## 3.3.4 Conclusion
- Summary of objectives should emphasize the multifaceted role of punishment in the criminal justice system.

### 3.3 Rehabilitation

#### Introduction
- Shift in South African Department of Correctional Services (DCS) towards rehabilitation.
- Central focus on reintegration and personal development of offenders to reduce recidivism (Ndebele, 2013).
- Rehabilitation aims to change attitudes and behaviors for societal acceptance post-release (Welch, 1996).

#### Types of Treatment

- **Individual Therapy, Casework, and Cognitive-Behavioural Programmes**
  - Facilitated by trained professionals (psychiatrists, psychologists, social workers).
  - Focus on changing psychological issues that contribute to criminal behavior (Conklin, 2013).
  - Criticisms:
    - Ineffectiveness with criminals due to lack of resource availability.
    - Therapy may not address real-world issues faced upon release.

- **Group Counselling**
  - Inmates treated together, often guided by trained correctional staff.
  - Encourages mutual understanding and emotional support (Conklin, 2013).

- **Family Intervention**
  - Focuses on the entire family dynamic, improving communication and behavior patterns (Conklin, 2013).

- **Education and Work Programmes**
  - Aim for inmates to achieve educational qualifications (including Grade 12) and acquire work skills for reintegration (Conklin, 2013).

- **Behaviour Modification**
  - Changes conduct through reinforcement strategies.
  - Mixed views on long-term effectiveness post-release (Conklin, 2013).

- **Milieu Therapy or Therapeutic Community**
  - Integrates rehabilitation throughout the institution.
  - Focus on responsibility and supportive environments (Conklin, 2013).

#### Rehabilitation Programmes in South Africa
- Approximately 28 rehabilitation programmes available in correctional centres (ISS, 2012).
- Compulsory for inmates serving 24 months or longer; access based on individual needs (DCS).

#### Effectiveness of Rehabilitation
- Factors affecting effectiveness:
  - Poor funding and inadequate staff availability.
  - Resource limitations and relevance of skills post-release (Conklin, 2013).
- South African context: 
  - Overcrowding and cultural dynamics complicate rehabilitation efforts (CSVR, 2004).

### 3.3.3.4 Restitution/Restoration

#### Restorative Justice
- Shift from retributive justice to a restorative framework post-apartheid.
- Restitution aimed at compensating victims and restoring relationships (DoJ & CD).
- Definition: Restitution as compensation for loss or injury caused by crime (Conklin, 2013).

#### Examples of Restorative Justice Processes
- **Victim-Offender Mediation (VOM)**
  - Facilitated meeting for discussion of crime's impact (DoJ & CD).
  
- **Family Group Conferencing (FGC)**
  - Involvement of victims, offenders, and community in response discussions (DoJ & CD).

- **Circles for Dialogue, Peace, and Sentencing**
  - Consensus-building among all parties impacted by the crime (DoJ & CD).

#### Benefits of Restorative Justice
- Reduces re-offending.
- Empowers victims and involves community support (DoJ & CD).
- May encourage offender responsibility and rehabilitation (Conklin, 2013).

# STUDY UNIT 3.3

## 3.3.3.5 Retribution
- **Definition**: Emphasizes punishment of offenders as deserved for harm caused. 
- **Key Principles**:
  - Punishment proportional to harm and blameworthiness (Conklin 2013:337).
  - Offenders act out of free will; focus on past actions rather than future prevention.
  - Restores balance among citizens; aligned with **lex talionis** ("an eye for an eye").
- **Punishment Goals**:
  - Primary focus on punishment; secondary on deterrence, incapacitation, rehabilitation, restitution.
  - Distinct from revenge—defined as personal and emotional, whereas retribution is state-imposed.
- **Retribution Elements**:
  - **Proportionality Principle**: Punishment must match crime severity.
  - **Just Deserts**: Offenders deserve punishment.
  - **Atonement**: Compensates community for harm.
  - **Moral Disapproval**: Reflects community values against crime.

## 3.3.4 Conclusion
- Shift from retributive to rehabilitation/restoration models of justice.
- Restorative justice emphasizes victim’s harm repair over offender punishment.
- Each punishment objective (deterrence, incapacitation, rehabilitation, restitution, retribution) is interconnected and should be considered collectively.
