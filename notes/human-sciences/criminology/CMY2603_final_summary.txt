- **Child and Youth**: Defined under the South African Constitution as individuals under 18.

- Youths (12-21 age group) are victimised significantly compared to older demographics (**Pelser, 2008**).

## CMY2603/1 Study Notes on Youth Misbehaviour

### 1. Youth Misbehaviour Overview
- **Statistics (YRBS, 2008)**
  - 10% female learners report forced sex and higher spousal violence rates than males.
  - 45,000 female learners became pregnant, linking to school dropouts post-financial issues.
  - UNFPA: Youth unemployment at 70%, leading to idling and potential criminal influences.

### 1.1 Culture of Violence
- **Pelser (2008)**
  - High crime rates, with a significant portion of crimes committed by individuals under 25.
  - Normalization of violence among poorly socialized youth stems from past socio-political events (e.g., Soweto rebellions).

### 1.2 Youth Protests and Violence
- **#FeesMustFall Campaign (2015-2018)**
  - Protests arose due to unmet expectations, primarily over unaffordable education fees.
  - Historical context: Long-standing tradition of violent student protests in post-apartheid South Africa.
  - Mergers of tertiary institutions aimed at transformation but led to heightened frustrations, sparking protests.

### 1.3 Government Response
- Resulting actions include no fee increases for the 2016 academic year and increased funding for the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS).

### 1.4 Summary of Youth Misbehaviour Context
- Explored terminology and Eurocentric influences on youth misbehaviour.
- Next learning unit will explore the nature and extent of misbehaviour in South Africa.

**10. YOUTH BEHAVIORAL ISSUES**

- Increase in school drop-out percentage.
- Increase in STDs and HIV/AIDS.
- Escalation in social violence (interpersonal violence among drinking youths).
- Increase in substance abuse (Rataemane, nd:4).

**Effects of Underage Drinking:**
- More youth deaths.
- Potential for future alcohol addiction (Rataemane, nd:5).
  
**Discourses on Youth Issues:**
- Alcohol is an integral part of South African culture.
- Readily available and associated with festivities.
- Legal consumption age is below 18 in several countries, including South Africa.
- 21.5% to 62% of adolescents use alcohol; university usage is between 22% and 80% (Rataemane, nd:6, 11).

---

**2.4 SEXUAL OFFENCES**

- Sexual offences, e.g., rape, often involve known perpetrators (family, friends, partners).
- Societal attitudes can shift blame onto the victim due to ambiguous notions of consent.
- Common factors discouraging reporting: fear of perceptions, negative associations, intimidating reporting process (Louw, 2013).

Refer to **Prescribed Book Section 3.2.3** for detailed information on child and youth sex offenders.

---

**2.5 VIOLENT OFFENCES**

- Violent offences involve violence or threats against victims.
- Youth violence is a prominent issue in South Africa, contributing to premature death and harm.
- Research indicates youthful victims are six times more likely to commit crimes later (CJCP: 2017:1).

**Risk Factors for Youth Violence:**
- Individual factors.
- Peer influence.
- Social, political, and cultural factors.

**WHO’s Violence Sub-Types:**
- Self-directed violence.
- Interpersonal violence.
- Collective violence.

**Categories of Violence:**
- **Psychological:** Harassment aimed at degrading or controlling victims.
- **Physical:** Actions causing physical harm.
- **Sexual:** Non-consensual sexual activity.

Statistics indicate one in three young people in South Africa face sexual violence (Daily News, 2019:1–2).

**Violent Offences Discussion:**
- **Family Violence:** Majority exposed in household settings; CJCP (2017:3) notes 53% witnessed family violence.
- **School Violence:** CJCP (2012) found 22.2% of learners faced violence; 6.3% common assaults; 4.7% sexual attacks; 61% reported peers with weapons.

---

**2.6 DRUG USE AMONG YOUTHS**

- Increasing drug abuse; commonly used drugs include tobacco and alcohol.
- Dagga (marijuana) is frequently used despite prohibition (Peltzer, 2003).

**2.9 OFFICIAL AND UNOFFICIAL CRIME INFORMATION SOURCES**

- **Official Sources:** SAPS crime statistics are a primary data source.

Child and youth misbehaviour in South Africa is significant, manifesting in various sexual and violent offences. Statistics may not wholly reflect crime states.

## LEARNING UNIT 3: Risk Factors Pertaining to Youth Offenders  

### 3.1 Introduction  
- **High-profile youth crimes** (e.g., Griekwastad murders) raise societal concerns.  
- Youth criminal behavior reflects broader social issues (e.g., poverty, racial prejudice).  
- Comprehensive risk factors affecting youth misbehavior exist, varying by individual experiences (Hillege et al., 2017).  

### 3.2 Key Concepts  
- **Risk Factor**: Variable predicting likelihood to offend; classified by levels (minimal, remote, high, imminent).  
- **Static Risk Factors**: Unchangeable (e.g., age, history of conduct disorder) (Maree, 2018:83).  
- **Dynamic Risk Factors**: Changeable (e.g., aggression, antisocial peers).  
- **Protective Factors**: Conditions preventing antisocial behavior (e.g., strong parental relationships) (Maree, 2018:83).  

### 3.3 Risk Factors on a Social Level  
- Includes **community, family, school, and extra-familial variables**.  

#### Community Risk Factors  
- **Economic Inequality**: Contributes to crime; clients experiencing deprivation are more likely to offend (Maree, 2018:94).  
- **Unemployment**: Affects both youth directly/indirectly; exacerbated by economic conditions (Maree, 2018:85).  
- **Community Disorganisation**: Loss of social control leads to increased crime (Maree, 2018:85).  
- **Substance Availability**: Links to crime; drug use leads to violence and theft (Maree, 2018:86).  
- **Climate of Violence**: Normalization of violence affects youth behavior (Maree, 2018:87).  

### 3.4 Family Variables  
- Influential factors include **socio-economic status, family structure, parental supervision, inter-generational offending, and family violence**.  

#### Socio-Economic Status  
- Youth from lower socio-economic backgrounds are at greater risk of crime (Maree, 2018:88).  
- Overcrowding can lead to exposure to criminal influences.  

#### Family Structure  
- Broken homes linked to behavioral issues; higher rates of offending in blended families (Siegel & Welsh, 2014:186).  
- Quality parent-child relationships mitigate risk of offending (Simmons et al., 2018:9).  

#### Lack of Parental Supervision  
- Correlates with youth criminality; poor support leads to association with problematic peers (Maree, 2018:90).  

#### Inter-Generational Continuity in Offending  
- Parental criminality increases likelihood of youth offending (Siegel & Welsh, 2014:191).  
- Sibling interactions can reinforce deviancy.

### 3.5 RISK FACTORS IN THE SCHOOL

- **Main Causes Related to Crime in Schools (Maree 2018:92)**  
  - Involvement in gang activities  
  - Negative perceptions among learners  
  - Presence of gangs and weapons  
  - Substance use (e.g., dagga)  
  - Lack of counselling services  
  - Parental apathy  
  - Hero worship of criminals  

#### Disorganised Conditions in and Around the School (4.4.3.1)
- Physical and emotional safety crucial for learners and teachers.  
- Smaller classes/schools manage better than overcrowded ones.  
- Overcrowding leads to vandalism affecting learning environments.  
- Need for facility upgrades and minimisation of vandalism (Maree, 2018:93).  
- School violence examples:  
  - **Columbine School Shooting (1999)**: 13 fatalities (CNN Library, 2018:1).  
  - Incident in South Africa (2008): Learner killed with sword (Geldenhuys, 2016:12).

#### The Learner (4.4.3.2)
- Poor academic performance linked to expulsion and community release.  
- Dropouts lack opportunities for social skills development (Maree, 2018:94).  
- Low education correlates with increased crime (Maree, 2018:94).

#### The Educator or Teacher (4.4.3.3)
- Disorder in schools demotivates teachers.  
- Teachers face large classes, absence issues, and insufficient training (Maree 2018:95).  
- Lack of parental support and fear for safety affect teacher-student interactions.

### 3.6 EXTRAFAMILIAL RELATIONSHIP FACTORS
- Importance of extrafamilial relationships in adolescence.  
- Peer influences can lead to delinquency (4.4.4).

#### Anti-Social Peer Associations (4.4.4.1)
- Group association is critical for adolescents; misbehaviour often occurs in groups (Maree, 2018:96).

#### Gang Membership (4.4.4.2)
- Gangs often represent a form of belonging and are rooted in weak family structures (Maree, 2018:97).

### 3.7 RISK FACTORS ON AN INDIVIDUAL LEVEL
- Includes biographical, biosocial, and psychosocial factors.

#### Biographical Variables
- **Age (4.5.1.1)**: Age is not purely a criminogenic factor but an indicator of developmental stage (Maree, 2018:99).  
- **Gender (4.5.1.2)**: Boys exposed to more criminogenic factors; impulsiveness and rebelliousness are noted.

#### Biosocial Factors
- Include environmental and biological factors.

- **Biochemical Factors (4.5.2.1)**: Diet and hormonal influences can affect behavior (Maree, 2018:100).  
- **Neurophysiological Factors (4.5.2.2)**: Brain activity linked to behavioral disorders; trauma or dysfunction may lead to deviant behaviour (Maree 2018:101).  
- **Genetic Factors (4.5.2.3)**: Inherited predisposition can influence antisocial behavior and familial patterns of crime (Maree, 2018:101; Siegel & Welsh, 2014:78).

#### Psychosocial Factors
- **Intelligence as a Factor (4.5.3.1)**: Low IQ correlates with increased misbehaviour; cultural intelligence aids peer relations (Maree, 2018:102–103).  
- **Personality Traits (4.5.3.2)**: Traits such as impulsiveness, low self-control linked to criminal behaviour (Maree, 2018:103).
  
### 3.8 HISTORY OF CONDUCT AMONG YOUNG OFFENDERS (4.6)
- First crime committed by youth can indicate future offending patterns (Maree, 2018:103).

### 3.9 IDENTIFIED COMBINATIONS OF RISK FACTORS (4.7)
- Combinations of risk factors heighten the probability of antisocial behaviour; multiple factors are key, not a single risk (Maree, 2018:104).

### 3.10 SUMMARY
- No single factor determines criminal behaviour; risk factors emerge from family, community, and individual contexts. Recognize issues as warnings for societal intervention.

### 3.11 SELF-ASSESSMENT QUESTIONS
- Focus on identifying key risk factors from family, community, and individual perspectives in relation to crime and delinquency factors.

### 4.1 Introduction  
- Youth identified as key population for future investment.  
- Importance of early crime prevention to reduce adult crime likelihood.  
- Youth misbehaviour arises from various contexts, exacerbated in environments of violence (Maree, 2013:69).  
- Development hindered by interrelated risk factors without protective elements (Coetzee, 2018:199).  

---

### 4.2 Key Concepts  
- **Primary Prevention**  
  - Aims to modify/eliminate criminogenic aspects in individuals and environments (Coetzee, 2018:201).  
- **Secondary Prevention**  
  - Focus on early identification of at-risk youths and intervention before criminal behaviour occurs (Lab, 2018:29).  
- **Tertiary Prevention**  
  - Targets youths with criminal records for treatment/punishment to prevent re-offending (Lab, 2010:271).  
- **Diversion**  
  - Seeks to redirect youths from traditional court systems, offering alternative sentences (Seroczynski et al., 2016:663).  
- **Recidivism**  
  - Refers to relapsing into crime after release for prior convictions (Quan-Baffour & Zawada, 2012:73).  
- **Risk Factors**  
  - Emotional poverty, incomplete families, weak support systems, socioeconomic factors, marginalisation, deviant peers, individual characteristics, school experiences.

---

### 4.3 International Backdrop  
- South Africa's commitment to international declarations holds government accountable (Coetzee, 2018:201).  
- Key instruments:  
  - **UN Guidelines for the Prevention of Juvenile Delinquency** (Riyadh Guidelines).  
  - **UN Standard Minimum Rules for the Administration of Juvenile Justice** (Beijing Rules).  

---

### 4.4 Primary Prevention Level  
- Proactive measures to modify individual and environment to mitigate misbehaviour risks (Coetzee, 2018:201).  
- **Family Role**  
  - Primary prevention seen as caregiver responsibility; family unit influences children's future behaviour (Maree, 2013:75).  
- **Mentorship**  
  - Positive role models critical; absence limits healthy relationships (Bartol & Bartol, 2017:189).  
- **School Role**  
  - Teachers can predict potential problem behaviours; school environment impacts socialisation (Lab, 2010:250).  
- **Media's Educational Role**  
  - Media campaigns raise awareness on crime-related issues; examples include SHOUT Trust and Soul City (Jewkes, 2015:6).  
- **Police Role**  
  - Essential to build trust and partnership with youth; examples include the Adopt-A-Cop programme (Wisler & Onwudiwe, 2009:38).  
- **Government and Society Role**  
  - Address structural issues, such as housing, education, employment; programs include NCPS, GEAR, and NYDA (Coetzee, 2018:205).  

---

### Summary  
- Youth misbehaviour requires an integrated approach at various societal levels, considering family, mentors, schools, media, and government roles.  
- Emphasis on proactive strategies to reduce risks associated with youth crime.

# Study Notes on Youth Misbehaviour and Prevention Measures

## 4.5 SECONDARY PREVENTION LEVEL
- **Objective**: Early identification and prevention of potential juvenile offenders.
- **Target Groups**: High-risk children displaying risky behaviors (abandonment, truancy, disobedience).
  
- **Programmes**:
  - Ons Plek shelter for female street children.
  - Streetwise: Educational programme for youth.
  - The Homestead Project for street youth.
  - The Launch Pad shelter for drug-free youths.

- **Role of School**:
  - Critical in socialization and youth development.
  - Importance of preventing school violence.
  - Implementation of policies and clear communication of rules.
  - Authority should be applied consistently; possible misbehavior indicators need addressing.

## 4.6 TERTIARY PREVENTION LEVEL
- **Definition**: Reacting to juvenile misbehaviour post-offense to prevent recidivism.
- **Methods**: 
  - Diversion from formal court proceedings.
  - Referral to Child Justice Court.

- **Legislation**:
  - **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (1996)**: 
    - Section 28 outlines rights for child offenders.
    - Focus on best interests of youth and incarceration as last resort.

- **Child Justice Act 75 of 2008**:
  - Establishes youth criminal justice system.
  - Aims to divert minors from formal processes.
  - Guidelines for trial and sentencing.

## Diversion (Section 10.4.3.1)
- **Child Justice Act 75 of 2008 key features**:
  - Establishes minimum age of criminal capacity.
  - Mechanism for addressing children lacking criminal capacity.
  - Procedures for releasing or detaining children.
  - Introduces child justice courts and restorative justice principles.

## 4.7 SUMMARY
- Youth misbehaviour has historically been overlooked; growing focus in recent decades due to advocacy.
- Child Justice Act 75 of 2008 aims to redirect youth from formal legal proceedings and enhance diversion opportunities.
- Significant roles played by family, school, police, and government in prevention strategies.
