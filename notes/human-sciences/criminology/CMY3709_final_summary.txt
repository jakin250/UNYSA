#### 1.1.1 Definitions
- **Natural Sciences**
  - **Term**: Natural sciences
  - **Class**: Type of science/system of scientific knowledge
  - **Distinguishing Features**: Focuses on inorganic or organic nature.

- **Human Sciences**
  - **Term**: Human sciences
  - **Class**: Type of science/system of scientific knowledge
  - **Distinguishing Features**: Studies the human mind.

#### 1.1.2 Disciplines
- **Definition**: Sciences that focus on specific segments of reality.
- **Examples**:
  - **Natural Sciences**: Chemistry, Astronomy, Physics.
  - **Human Sciences**: Psychology, Sociology, English.
  - **Criminology**: Studies human behavior in crime.

#### 1.1.3 Intersection of Natural and Human Sciences
- **Example**: Geography integrates both natural phenomena and human interactions with environments.

### 1.2 The Nature of Research

#### 1.2.1 Definition of Research
- **Term**: Research
- **Class**: Activity/process
- **Distinguishing Features**:
  - Deliberate search for new knowledge.
  - Goals encompass collection, processing, and expansion of scientific knowledge.

#### 1.2.2 Research Process
- Steps:
  1. **Awareness of Research Problem**: Recognition of knowledge gaps.
  2. **Formulation of Research Problem**: Define scope and particulars of issues being researched.
  3. **Data Gathering**: Systematic collection of relevant information.
  4. **Data Processing**: Analyzing and interpreting data collected.
  5. **Writing Report**: Documenting findings and methodologies.
  6. **Utilisation of Findings**: Applying research outcomes to enhance knowledge or practical applications.
  
#### 1.2.3 Types of Research
- **Basic Research**: 
  - Aims: Acquisition of new insights.
  - Characteristics: Theoretical or empirical; expanded knowledge.
- **Applied Research**: 
  - Aims: Solve specific problems.
  - Nature: May also be theoretical or empirical.

# Lesson 1.2. Philosophy of Science 

### Outcomes
- Define "philosophy of science"
- Overview of important perspectives in philosophy of science
- Distinguish between naturalistic and anti-naturalistic schools
- Set out differences between positivism and humanism 

### Key Concepts
- **Practice of Science**: Exploration of reality through research.
- **Objectivity**: Observing phenomena without personal biases; value judgments avoided.
- **Value-free**: Neutral insights, uninfluenced by personal beliefs.
- **Paradigm**: Accepted assumptions in a discipline.
- **Deterministic System**: Nonexistence of free will; behavior determined by external forces.
- **Cause-Effect Pattern**: Human behavior follows probability patterns rather than fixed laws.
- **Empirical Observations**: Based on sensory experience.
- **Empiricism**: Knowledge acquisition through verifiable sensory observation.
- **Generalised**: Claims about larger groups based on smaller studies.
- **Positivists**: Advocates for sensory observation and experimentation for knowledge acquisition.
- **Humanists**: Focus on the meaning of experiences for individuals; value the interaction between researcher and subjects.
- **Continuum**: A spectrum of beliefs or theories varying in degree.
- **Scientific Ideal**: Desired goals of scientific inquiry.
- **Objectivity Ideal**: Pursuit of neutral and unbiased knowledge.
- **Survey Method**: Data collection method typically using random sampling.
- **Participative Observation**: Researcher involvement in natural settings of subjects.
- **Interview**: Information gathering through conversation with respondents.

### Sub Lesson 1.2.1 Definition and Perspectives
- **Definition**: Examination of scientific assumptions, foundations, methods, and implications.
- **Functional Perspective**: Relation of science to society; queries on science's societal function.
- **Structural Perspective**: Nature and dynamics of science; assesses differences between natural and human sciences.
- **Critical Perspective**: Advocates for scientific analysis and evaluation.
- **Knowledge Perspective**: Challenges about authenticity and reliability of scientific knowledge relative to reality and observer bias.

### Perspectives in Philosophy of Science
- **Naturalistic**: Focuses on observable phenomena and scientific methods.
- **Anti-naturalistic**: Emphasizes subjective meanings and interactions.

### Schools of Thought
- **Naturalistic School**
  - **Positivism**: Supported by sensory observation, experimentation, and generalization.
  - **Conceptions**: Logical, critical, historical, and hermeneutic positivism.
  
- **Anti-naturalistic School**
  - **Humanism**: Emphasizes subjective human experiences and meaning; dynamic interaction between observer and subject.
  - **Conceptions**: Phenomenology, critical rationalism.

# Study Notes

## Sub lesson 1.2.2 Naturalistic and Anti-Naturalistic Schools of Thought

### Naturalistic School of Thought
- **Unitary View**: Human beings and nature viewed as a natural unit; no essential difference from natural phenomena.
- **Determinism**: Human behavior follows a cause-effect pattern.
- **Scientific Knowledge**: Only knowledge arising from empirical observations is valid; metaphysical concepts and common knowledge are rejected.
- **Empirical Method**: Described as the only valid scientific method; reliant on sensory observation and verification.
- **Generalisability**: Preference for knowledge that can be generalized into valid laws regarding human behavior.

### Implications for Criminology
- **Crime as Natural Reality**: Crime studied as part of natural reality.
- **Validity of Knowledge**: Only scientific knowledge is valid for understanding criminal behavior.
- **Methodology**: Empirical method is the sole acceptable approach to crime study.

### Anti-Naturalistic School of Thought
- **Uniqueness of Human Situations**: Acknowledges complexity and distress in human situations and actions.
- **Study Methods**: Recommended use of distinctive methods for understanding human behavior in context (situatedness).
- **Value of Non-Scientific Knowledge**: Everyday knowledge recognized as valuable in study.
- **Contextual Restraint**: Understanding behavior requires acknowledgment of time and place; knowledge is context-bound.
- **Pluralistic Methodology**: Acceptance of mixed-method approaches specific to different sciences.

### Implications for Criminology
- **Authenticity of Study Object**: Crime viewed as a true human phenomenon.
- **Study Methods**: Different methodologies required to study human situations.
- **Context-Bound Knowledge**: Recognizes the importance of context in understanding behavior.

---

## Sub lesson 1.2.3 Positivistic and Humanistic (Anti-Positivistic) Conceptions of Science

### Operational Continuum
- **Scientific Method**: Question of whether natural and human sciences employ the same scientific methods.
  - **Positivism**: Affirmative
  - **Humanism**: Negative

### Differences in Scientific Ideal
- **Positivism**: Focus on generally valid explanations; research seeks universal applicability.
  - Example: Study of gang phenomena in prisons aims to generalize findings to the entire population of South African gang members.
- **Humanism**: Focus on understanding specific human situations; context-bound interest.
  - Example: In-depth interpretation of individual gang member experiences within a singular prison context; findings not generalizable.

### Objectivity Ideal
- **Positivism**: Researchers maintain a neutral stance, separate from their subjects, to achieve value-free knowledge.
  - Method: Employs standardized techniques for maximum control over data observation and collection.
- **Humanism**: Challenges the possibility of total objectivity; emphasizes the influence of personal values and experiences on knowledge. 

---

### Summary of Key Points
- **Naturalistic View**: Focus on empiricism, determinism, generalizability.
- **Anti-Naturalistic View**: Focus on context, uniqueness, pluralistic methods.
- **Positivism vs Humanism**: Differing approaches to knowledge generation and researcher subject-object relationships.

### Key Concepts
- **Research Objective**: Main aim of research; types include exploratory, descriptive, explanatory.
- **Conceptualisation**: Demarcation of terms and concepts for organization in research.
- **Operationalisation**: Converting abstract concepts into measurable terms.
- **Hypothesis**: Presumptive statement for empirical research.
- **Theory**: Cohesive statements categorizing or explaining phenomena.
- **Observational Data**: Systematically gathered data for analysis, with raw data being unprocessed.
- **Nomothetic Research Strategy**: Aims for universally valid knowledge underlying phenomena; applicable broadly.
- **Data Printed Descriptively**: Graphic summarization of numerical/statistical data.
- **Ideographic Research Strategy**: Focus on unique events or phenomena; emphasizes contextual significance.
- **Field Notes**: Recorded observations/data.
- **Inferential Statistical Techniques**: Statistics for hypothesis testing and sample drawing.

- (c) Valid knowledge.

### Sub Lesson 1.3.2 Quantitative and Qualitative Research Methodologies
- **Quantitative Research**: Structured, controlled; seeks universal knowledge; assumes observable reality.
  - Decisions on conceptualisation and operationalisation are similar across disciplines.
  - Importance of hypotheses; data often numeric.
  
- **Qualitative Research**: Different methodologies in human vs. natural sciences; emphasizes studying humans in unique situations.
  - Theories emerge from observational data; flexible procedures.
  - Strategies exist for data organization and interpretation.

### Lesson 3.3: Measures of Central Tendency

- **Purpose**: Describes scores of a data set; represents a frequency distribution of objects with varying dimensions and characteristics.

#### Outcomes
- Identify the three measures of central tendency.

#### Sub Lesson 3.3.1: The Mode
- **Definition**: Score occurring most frequently in a distribution.
- **Example**: In a distribution of executed prisoners (1977–1995), mode is 0 (24 states executed none).
- **Bimodal/Multimodal**: A distribution may have two modes (bimodal) or more (multimodal).
- **Determination**: Construct frequency table; create polygon or histogram to visualize.

#### Sub Lesson 3.3.2: The Median
- **Definition**: Middlemost score in a ranked data set.
- **Calculation**: Median found by ranking scores and locating the central value.
- **Example**: Median in 50 states’ execution data is 1 (about half executed 1 or 0).
- **Stability**: Less affected by extreme scores, making it suitable in certain data distributions.

#### Sub Lesson 3.3.3: The Mean
- **Definition**: Sum of values divided by the number of values (average).
- **Application**: Takes all values into account; preferred for interval data but used for ordinal and nominal data.
- **Sensitivity**: Affected by extreme values (e.g., Texas’s 104 executions skewing average).
- **Interpretation**: Mean of 6.26 (average executions per state) reflects distribution sensitivity.

- **Variance Calculation**:
  1. Subtract mean from each value.
  2. Square the results.

2. ~95% values within 2 standard deviations.
3. ~99% values within 3 standard deviations.

## Hypotheses and levels of statistical significance
### Outcomes
- Explain hypothesis formulation and significance.
- Distinguish between null and alternative hypotheses.

### Introduction
- **Definition**: Hypothesis is an assumption about population differences or variable relationships; originates from Greek "hypo-thesis" (sub-statement).
- **Testing Hypotheses**: Researchers gather and analyze data to support or reject hypotheses.

### Induction & Falsification
- **Induction**: Example hypothesis relating divorced parents to crime.
- **Falsification**: If one counterexample exists (child of divorced parents not committing crime), the hypothesis is falsified.

### Sub lesson 3.5.1 Types of hypotheses
- **Types**:
  - **Null Hypothesis (H0)**: No differences or relationships (e.g., “No relationship between background and death penalty opinion”).
  - **Alternative Hypothesis (H1)**: There are differences or relationships (e.g., “There is a relationship between background and death penalty opinion”).

### Statistical Evaluation
- Rejection of H0 based on low probability of occurrence under H0 implies support for H1, but carries risk of false conclusion if chance events occur.

# Chapter 3: Statistical Significance and Hypothesis Testing

## 3.5: Hypotheses and Statistical Significance
### 3.5.1 Importance of Multiple Hypotheses
- Researchers often include several null and alternative hypotheses.
- Number of hypotheses affects participant numbers: more hypotheses require more participants.
  
### 3.5.2 Levels of Statistical Significance
- **Significance Level**: Determines whether to reject or retain the null hypothesis.
  - Common levels: 
    - **0.05**: 5% type I error risk.
    - **0.01**: 1% type I error risk.
    - **0.001**: 0.1% type I error risk.
- **Type I Error**: Rejecting a true null hypothesis.
- **Rejecting/Hypothesis Testing**:
  - p > 0.05: Retain null hypothesis (assume no significant difference).
  - p ≤ 0.05: Reject null hypothesis (likely significant difference).
  - Data with p < 0.10 is valuable but less trusted.

# Lesson 5.1: Experiments as a Quantitative Method
## (5 hours to complete Lesson 5.1)

### Outcomes
- Distinguish between laboratory and field experiments.
- Identify advantages and drawbacks of experimental studies.

### Sub lesson 5.1.1: Introduction
- Definition of an experiment (Schaefer 1989:41): artificially created situation for variable manipulation and control.

### Sub lesson 5.1.2: Characteristics
#### Types of Experiments
- **Classical Experimental Design / Laboratory Experiment**
  - Key elements:
    - Equivalence
    - Pretesting and post-testing
    - Experimental and control groups

  **Example 1: VIEWS ON ABORTION**
  - Female students: experimental group (watched film), control group (did not watch film).
  - Result: Change in opinions following exposure to independent variable (film).

  **Example 2: MOCK PRISON**
  - Haney, Banks, and Zimbardo (1973): pseudo-prison environment with "warders" and "prisoners."
  - Observations of extreme behavior led to experiment termination.

- **Quasi-Experimental Design / Field Experiment**
  - More realistic situations, greater external validity.
  - Lower internal validity; less control over experimental conditions (Neuman 1994:187).

  **Example 4: FAMILY VIOLENCE**
  - Sherman and Berk (1984): three police response methods to family violence. 
    - Findings: Arrest is most effective in deterring future violence.

  **Example 5: PROJECT RAPE**
  - Harari and White (1985): male passer-by reactions to simulated attack.
  - Findings: 85% group assistance; 65% solitary assistance.

### Sub lesson 5.1.3: Advantages of Experimental Studies
- Quick and economical to conduct.
- Increase validity via controlled exposure and duration.
- Repeatability (Haralambos and Holborn 1991:704).

### Sub lesson 5.1.4: Drawbacks of Experimental Studies
- Artificial situations limit generalization; reactions may differ in natural settings (Hagan 1990:45).
- Difficulty in obtaining representative subjects.
- Ethical concerns regarding manipulation of human subjects.
- Long-term social change studies not feasible.

### Sub lesson 5.1.5: Conclusion
- Experiments face validity issues in criminology; assume dichotomous populations (criminal vs. non-criminal).

## Ethical Conduct

### Sub Lesson 7.1. Ethical principles
- Basic ethical guidelines established by various international bodies.

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.2. Non-maleficence
- **Definition**: "Do not harm" - addresses awareness of potential harm or distress to participants (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 119).
- Types of harm: physical, psychological, social, legal.
- Requires ongoing evaluation of situational risks (e.g., during interviews).

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.3. Beneficence
- **Definition**: Maximize benefits, minimize harm (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 121).
- Risk-benefit analysis essential; balance benefits against risks and costs (Leary, 2012: 213).
- Levels of beneficence:
  1. Basic knowledge
  2. Improved research techniques
  3. Enhanced practical outcomes
  4. Benefit researchers
  5. Benefit research participants

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.4. Voluntary participants
- Participation must be voluntary; disruption of participants' lives is a factor (Babbie 2001:470).
- Informed consent is crucial; no use of participants’ information without agreement (Neuman 2000:283).

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.5. Informed consent
- Consideration of the information necessary for participant decision-making:
  1. Goal of investigation
  2. Expected behavior
  3. Duration of involvement
  4. Procedures
  5. Advantages/disadvantages
  6. Possible dangers
  7. Researcher's credibility (Leary, 2012: 314; Barbie, 2016: 64).
- Parental/guardian consent for minors; there are specific processes for different age groups (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 122).

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.6. Transparency
- Transparency prevents misleading participants through deception like withholding or misrepresenting information (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 123).
- Ethical complaints require immediate attention and rectification.

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.7. Privacy, anonymity, and confidentiality
- **Privacy**: Setting for data collection; ensures participant comfort.
- **Confidentiality**: Agreements limiting access to participant information.
- **Anonymity**: Participants are not required to disclose information that identifies them (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 124).

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.8. Access to service
- Should not be compromised based on participation.

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.9. Compensation
- Fair compensation for participant involvement is crucial.

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.10. Debriefing
- Essential to explain study details post-participation and address any participant concerns.

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.11. Actions and competence of the researcher
- Researchers must maintain ethical standards and personal competence.

---

### Sub Lesson 7.1.12. Cooperation with contributors and sponsors
- Ensure ethical considerations are upheld in partnerships.

# Study Notes

## Chapter 7: Ethical Considerations in Research

### Sub Lesson 7.1.8: Access to Service
- Importance of providing access to services for control groups in experimental research.
- Ethical obligation to avoid denying beneficial services for research purposes (Marlow, 2005: 105).
- Possible solutions:
  - Comparison group from the community.
  - Waiting-list approach allowing one group to access services while another group waits.
- Necessity to ensure support is provided during delays (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 125).

### Sub Lesson 7.1.9: Compensation
- Compensation in research is a debated topic; reimbursement of costs is acceptable.
- Key principles:
  1. Timing of incentive (reasonable to reimburse prior costs).
  2. Amount of incentive should be appropriate and not excessive.
- Normally given post-participation as appreciation to avoid impacting informed consent (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 125).

### Sub Lesson 7.1.10: Debriefing
- Immediate debriefing post-study is recommended for addressing participant experiences and minimizing harm.
- Must be voluntary; opportunities for counseling post-research may be offered (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 126).

### Sub Lesson 7.1.11: Actions and Competence of the Researcher
- Ethical obligation for researchers to possess competence and honesty (Barbie, 2016: 70).
- Importance of properly planned research to minimize failures.
- Ethical practices extend from project proposal to data storage—data should be securely stored for 5-10 years (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 127).

### Sub Lesson 7.1.12: Cooperation with Contributors and Sponsors
- Contributors can include colleagues and stakeholders; sponsors provide financial support.
- Confidentiality and collaboration agreements should be signed, clearly outlining roles.
- A research proposal should detail funding applications (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 128).

### Sub Lesson 7.1.13: Dissemination and Publication of Findings
- Publication must align with agreements with contributors and sponsors.
- All role players should review preliminary reports for potential changes or improvements.
- Published research findings must be accessible to ensure scientific credibility (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 128).

### Sub Lesson 7.1.14: Social and Cultural Sensitivity
- Researchers must respect participants' dignity and cultural contexts.
- Information should be provided in participants' primary language; necessary permissions should be sought when researching specific cultural groups (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 129).

### Sub Lesson 7.1.15: Conflict of Interest
- Conflict arises from dual roles in research (e.g., researcher/practitioner).
- Recognizing various examples, such as being both recruiter and researcher (Fouche, Strydom & Roestenburg, 2021: 129).

### Sub Lesson 7.1.16: Summary
- Ethical principles include: avoiding harm, ensuring informed consent, and maintaining competence.
- Importance of ethics committees in reviewing research proposals for ethical compliance.

# Study Notes on Research Methodology in Social Sciences

## Chapter 1: Introduction to Research Methodology
- **Definition**: Research methodology refers to the systematic approach to designing, conducting, and analyzing research.
- **Importance**: Essential for validating findings and establishing credibility in social science.

## Chapter 2: Research Paradigms  
- **Quantitative vs Qualitative Methods**  
  - Quantitative: Numerical data; emphasizes statistical analysis (Neuman & Neuman 1994).
  - Qualitative: Non-numerical data; focuses on understanding phenomena (Mouton 1985).
  
### Learning Unit 2.1: Qualitative Research
- **Definition**: Exploration of social phenomena using non-numerical data sources.
- **Key Authors**: 
  - Taylor & Bogdan (1984): Introduction and applications.
  - Mouton (1983): Methodology discussions.

### Learning Unit 2.2: Quantitative Research
- **Characteristics**: Utilizes structured tools like surveys for data collection (Vito et al. 2008).
- **Statistical Tools**: Application of descriptive and inferential statistics (Siegel 1954).

## Chapter 3: Research Design  
- **Types of Research Designs**  
  - Descriptive, Correlational, Experimental, and Quasi-experimental designs.
  
### Learning Unit 3.1: Descriptive Design
- **Purpose**: To provide an accurate representation of characteristics (Phillips 1976).

### Learning Unit 3.2: Experimental Design
- **Elements**: Includes control groups; random assignment is critical (Walker & Maddan 2005).

## Chapter 4: Data Collection Methods
- **Surveys**: Common method, especially in quantitative research (Schnetler 1989).
- **Interviews**: Structured and unstructured interviews for qualitative insights (Schurink 1986). 

### Learning Unit 4.1: Observation Method
- **Definition**: Data collected through watchful observation of subjects in a natural or controlled environment (Oberholzer 1983).

## Chapter 5: Sampling Techniques  
- **Types of Sampling**  
  - Probability Sampling: Random selection (Neuman 2000).
  - Non-Probability Sampling: Non-random selection, e.g., convenience sampling.

## Chapter 6: Data Analysis  
- **Qualitative Analysis**: Thematic analysis, coding, interpretation of meanings (Neuman 1997).
- **Quantitative Analysis**: Statistical testing and interpretation of numerical data (Vito et al. 2008).

## Chapter 7: Reporting and Evaluating Research  
- **Structure of Research Reports**: Abstract, Introduction, Methodology, Results, Discussion, Conclusion.
- **Criteria for Evaluation**: Validity, reliability, and generalizability of findings (Tashakkori & Teddlie 2003).

## Chapter 8: Ethical Considerations in Research  
- **Key Ethical Principles**: Informed consent, confidentiality, and responsible reporting (Nel et al. 1990).

## Chapter 9: Relevant Legislation and Cases  
- **Importance of Legislation**: Guides ethical research practices (Mouton & Joubert 1990).
- **Key Cases**: Influence of legal structures on social research.
