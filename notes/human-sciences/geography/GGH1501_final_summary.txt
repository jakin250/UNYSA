### 1.1 INTRODUCTION
- Definition of Geography:
  - Diverse subject with varying interpretations.
  - Modern geography as a science: exploration, description, and explanation of interactions between natural and human environments.
  - Focus on studying relationships between people, places, and environments.
  - Concept of ‘interconnectedness’ in geographical inquiries.
- Aim: Ask geographical questions to critically assess world problems rather than memorize facts.

### 1.2 AIM AND LEARNING OUTCOMES
- **Learning Outcomes**:
  - Appreciate geography’s dimensions and applications.
  - Provide geographic descriptions using a geographical perspective.
  - Explain uniqueness and interrelationships of locations.
  - Analyze phenomena by spatial location and distribution.
  - Showcase understanding of human-environment interactions.
  - Differentiate contemporary geographical methods.

#### 1.4.1 Making meaning of Geography
- **Geographic Concepts**:
  - Importance of spatial context in geographical studies.
  - Distinction between geography's descriptive and scientific approaches.
  - Fundamental geographic questions:
    - **Where?** - Basic inquiry into location.
    - **What is it like?** - Attributes and characteristics of places.
    - **Why?** - Explanations for phenomena's locations and characteristics.

  - Place, location, region.
  - Themes: Physical geography, Human geography, Biological geography.
  - Methodologies: Remote sensing, GIS, Geo-statistics, Qualitative methods, Cartography.

## LEARNING UNIT 1: THE NATURE OF GEOGRAPHY AND GEOGRAPHICAL THINKING

### 1.4.2 Geography through the ages
- Geography as one of the oldest sciences, essential for exploring "Where?" and "What is it like?". 
- Evolution from navigation to solving global problems (e.g. urban planning, disaster management).
- **Legislation/Case Reference**: None cited.

### 1.4.3 Geographic descriptions of location
- **Maps**: Key medium for spatial descriptions; identifies phenomenon locations and spatial patterns.
- **Scale**: Maps drawn to scale; expresses detail correlation; 
  - Scale types and their relationship to detail.
- **Map Projections**: Essential for accurate location description; note four distortion types.
- **Geographic Grid**: Functions via latitude and longitude; links latitude with time.
- **Analytical Tools**: 
  - Remote Sensing
  - GPS
  - GIS
- **Spatial Concepts**:
  - **Spatial Location**: Absolute (coordinates) vs. Relative (descriptive location).
  - **Spatial Variation**: Different characteristics across locations (e.g. climate).
  - **Spatial Distribution**: Patterns observed (e.g. South African harbors).
  - **Spatial Association**: Interrelation with other phenomena to explain distributions.

### 1.4.4 Places and regions: unique geographic locations
- Each place has a unique name and character shaped by:
  - Unique location (situation).
  - Human-environmental factors (site characteristics).
- **Region Demarcation**: Simplifies reality for specific purposes; regions not uniformly homogenous.
- Types of analyses:
  - Area analysis
  - Spatial analysis
  - Systems analysis
- **Cultural Landscape**: Impact of human activity on physical landscape.
- **Regions**: 
  - Functional (nodal)
  - Formal (uniform)
  - Vernacular (perceptual)

### 1.4.5 Scale: from global to local
- Geographical perspectives vary by scale; 
  - Global focus on broad patterns.
  - Local scale requires detail.
- **Globalisation**: 
  - Concept of a 'shrinking world'; increased interaction.
  - Role of technology.
  - Space impacts and cultural globalisation effects.
- **Examples**: Increased foreign investments in South African mining; global influences on culture.

### 1.4.6 Time and space: the distribution of features
- Arrangement of phenomena (spatial distribution) key in analysis; focus on density, concentration, and pattern.
- **Key Properties**:
  - **Density**: Size and numbers relation (e.g. population density calculations).
  - **Concentration**: Comparison of similar areas needed for meaningful analysis.
  - **Pattern**: Differences between natural and man-made distributions.
  
### 1.4.7 Interaction between places
- No location isolated; all interconnected.
- **Types of Diffusion**:
  - Relocation
  - Expansion
- **Spatial Interaction**: 
  - Triggered by demand/supply across distances; mutual shaping of locations.
- **Distance Decay**: Interaction diminishes with distance; examples provided on Durban and Johannesburg trade.

### Summary Table of Analytical Methods (Example)
| Analytical Method   | Main Characteristics                                      | Theoretical Concepts                               |
|---------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Area Analysis       | Focus on geographic characteristics of specific areas    | Cultural landscapes, regionalism                   |
| Spatial Analysis     | Examines patterns and distributions                      | Density, concentration, spatial distribution       |
| Systems Analysis    | Understands interconnections among systems               | System dynamics, resilience, and feedback loops    |

- Note: 
  - Vernacular regions are perceptual and vary by perspective; cultural identity important in regional definitions.

#### Measurement Scale

- **Ordinal** 
  - Facilitates comparison of observations.

  - Quantitative data with arbitrary zero (0).

- **Ratio**
  - Highest level of measurement, quantitative with a fixed zero.
  - Allows mathematical/statistical operations.
  - Example: Elevation measurements (e.g., 602.2 m for trigonometric station) – enables direct comparisons.

### 7.4.3.2 Determining Relative Location
- **Relative Location Definition:**
  - Describes a place in relation to another place.
  
- **Direction:**
  - Cardinal compass directions:
    - Place B: North-east of A
    - Place C: South-east of A
    - Place D: West of A

- **Bearing:**
  - Expressed as angle measured clockwise:
    - Place B: 45° from position A (true bearing)
    - Place C: 135° from position A (true bearing)
    - Place D: 270° from position A (true bearing)

#### Practical Implications of Magnetic vs. True North
- **Magnetic Compass:**
  - Points to magnetic north pole, not geographic.
- **Navigational Access:**
  - Confusion may arise due to declination (e.g., magnetic north may be 25° west of true north).
  
- Example:
  - If a friend indicates “2.5 km straight east” using a compass, you may fail to locate if using true north on a map due to declination differences.

#### Magnetic Declination Information (Figure 7.7)
- Mean magnetic declination: 25°10' west of true north
- Annual magnetic variation: 6' westward, leading to adjustment over time.

#### Important Terms Related to Bearing (Figure 7.8)
- Essential components: base line, point of observation, point of bearing, line of bearing, angle of bearing.

### October 2017 Magnetic Bearing Calculation Example
1. **Strategy 1: True bearing with magnetic declination adjustment**
   - Draw true north base line; measure angle (True bearing = 63°).
   - Magnetic declination: Calculate from 2015 to 2017 (2 years).
   - Total change: 2 × 6' = 12'.
   - Adjust declination: 25°10' + 12' = 25°22'.
   - Magnetic bearing: True bearing + declination → 63° + 25°22' = 88°22'.

#### 7.4.4 Map Projections
- **Definition**: Transformation of Earth's spherical surface to a flat map.
- **Distinctions**: Distortion of properties (distance, shape, area, direction) based on chosen projection.
- **Classification**:
  - **Geometric Principles**: Separated according to their foundation (Section A, p. 57, Fig. 1-25).
  - **Preserved Properties**:
    - **Size**: Equal area or equivalent projections.
    - **Distance**: Equidistant projections.
    - **Shape**: Conformal projections.
    - **Direction**: Azimuthal or zenithal projections.
- **Distortion Factors**: Increases with larger surface area representations. 
- **Measuring Challenges**:
  - Accurate along predetermined central points or standard lines.
  - Equidistant projections maintain true scale only along specific lines.
- **Graticule Usage**:
  - Meridian convergence at poles.
  - Constant distances along parallels.
  - Consistent area measurements between grid cells.
  - Straight equator and meridians.
  - Right angles between meridians and parallels.

#### 7.4.5 The Language of Maps
- **Definition**: Communication method of maps through symbols and data.
- **Locational Context**:
  - Derived from map title and depicted geographic data.
  - Importance of map orientation, indicated by cardinal directions or grid context.
- **Symbol Classification**:
  - **Point Symbols**: Fixed-location phenomena (dots, icons).
  - **Line Symbols**: Linear features (roads, rivers).
  - **Area Symbols**: Two-dimensional objects (dams, parks), differentiated by shading and patterns.
- **Symbol Properties**: Defined by color, shape, size, and orientation in the map legend.
  
#### 7.4.6 Map Scale
- **Function**: Relates map representation to real-world distances.
- **Types of Scales** (Section A, p. 56, Fig. 1-23):
  - **Ratio/Scale**: Numerical ratio of map distance to ground distance.
  - **Verbal Scale**: Descriptive scale (e.g., "1 inch = 1 mile").
  - **Graphic Scale**: Visual line representing scale on the map.

#### 7.4.8.1 Mapping Relief Through Contour Lines
- **Contour Line Definition**: Line joining points of equal elevation.
- **Contour Map Representation**: Orthogonal and equidistant spacing.

**Contour Line Features**:
1. Lines do not intersect.
2. Closed loop or edge-connected.
3. Must have height values or index contours every 5th line.
4. Height values placed for user readability.
5. Spacing indicates steepness; closer means steeper.

**Steepness Inference with Contours**:
- **Gradient Calculation**: Height difference over distance to quantify steepness.
- Formula:
