## THE CONCEPT OF STATE

- **State**: vital yet complex concept in political science.
- Issues with definition stem from neglect in Anglo-American political science compared to European scholarship.
- Common terms (state, nation, government) are often used interchangeably, causing confusion.
- **Key Elements of a State**:
  - Authorities (government) exercising power.
  - A permanent population.
  - Defined geographic territory.
  - Sovereignty (highest authority recognized by other states).
- The state represents **values** that guide structures and functions.

## OTHER RELATED CONCEPTS

- **Political System**:
  - System comprising identifiable parts with specific functions and interactions.
  - Not synonymous with the state; often misconceived as such.
  - Should not be conflated with David Easton’s systems theory.

- **Society**:
  - Refers to people in a group context, characterized by interpersonal relations and shared values.
  - Complex relationship with the state; terms should not be used interchangeably.

- **Community**:
  - Vaguely defined; a closely knit group sharing common interests, typically within a geographic area.

## 2.1 NORMATIVE AND EMPIRICAL APPROACHES IN THE STUDY OF REGIMES
- **Normative Approach**: Value-driven; prescribes how regimes should be.
- **Empirical Approach**: Observational; describes how regimes are.
- Findings from empirical studies often influenced by normative factors and vice versa.

### Key Concepts
- **Values**: Qualities that guide beliefs and judgments about regimes (e.g., democracy, individual freedom).
- Distinction between normative statements (value judgments) and empirical facts (observable realities).
- **Examples**:
  - Normative: "All states should adhere to democratic principles."
  - Empirical: "In the U.S., the president is elected by the people."

### Underlying Assumptions
- Influence interpretations of democracy based on specific value frameworks.
- Empirical facts interpreted through normative lenses; importance of recognizing implicit values.

## 2.2 THE IMPORTANCE OF CLASSIFICATIONS, TYPOLOGIES, AND COMPARISONS
- Classifications identify patterns in governance structures.
- **Typologies**: Define categories based on shared characteristics, mark theoretical developments.
- **Comparisons**: Help validate classifications and theories about governance relations.

### Summary of Importance
- **Description**: Analyze and discuss regime characteristics.
- **Identification**: Recognize similarities and differences (e.g., path to political leadership).
- **Classification**: Determine if a regime is democratic based on traits.
- **Theory Building**: Establish relationships and theories (e.g., poverty and authoritarianism).
- **Hypothesis Development**: Formulate hypotheses about regime characteristics and relationships.
- **Testing and Prediction**: Compare cases to test hypotheses and predict future political transitions.

## REGIMES: CLOSED HEGEMONY

### I N T R O D U C T I O N
- Closed hegemony = authoritarian regimes
- Alternative term: "nondemocratic"
- **Outcome**: Define, describe, and discuss closed hegemony; identify closed hegemonic regimes.

### F O C U S Q U E S T I O N S
- Definition of closed hegemony
- Characteristics of types of closed hegemonies
- Related concepts
- Political life characteristics in closed hegemonies

### 3.1 THE CONCEPT OF CLOSED HEGEMONY
- **Closed hegemony**: Regimes not democratic (Dahl, 1984)
- Characteristics:
  - Sovereign authorities
  - Accountability to themselves, not the citizens
  - No effective participation of the ruled in decisions
  - Required obedience from citizens
  - Top-down flow of information

### 3.2 CHARACTERISTICS OF OTHER VARIETIES OF CLOSED HEGEMONIES
- Two subclasses: authoritarianism and totalitarianism

#### 3.2.1 Authoritarianism
- Defined by Jackson and Jackson (1997): Historically linked with tyrants, requiring complete loyalty/obedience.
- Characteristics:
  - Power exercised from above, regardless of consent
  - Emphasis on authority over individual liberty
  - Possible tolerance of some freedoms (economics, religion)

#### 3.2.2 Totalitarianism
- Encompasses characteristics of authoritarianism but lacks any private domain.
- Totalitarianism characterized by:
  - No distinction between state and private life
  - The aim to politicize all aspects of existence, contrasting with the more limited monopoly of power in authoritarian regimes.

### Important Authors & References
- **Dahl (1984)**: Closed hegemony definition
- **Jaggers and Gurr (1995)**: Autocracy operational definition
- **Jackson and Jackson (1997)**: Views on authoritarianism
- **Heywood (1997)**: Characteristics of authoritarianism and totalitarianism
- **Sartori (1976)**: Hegemonic party concept
- **Friedrich and Brzezinski (1963)**: Six indicators of totalitarianism

### Key Concepts
- **Closed hegemony**: A regime with restricted political participation and sovereignty held by the ruling class, demanding obedience from citizens.
- **Authoritarianism**: Regimes emphasizing authority, varied tolerances of private life, and limited political participation.
- **Totalitarianism**: Regimes with the state infiltrating all aspects of life, abolishing private spheres, and aiming for total control.

### Differentiating Factors
- **Legitimacy**: Authoritarian regimes may enjoy varying levels of legitimacy; totalitarian regimes typically do not.
- **Public vs. Private Domain**: Authoritarianism allows for a private domain, while totalitarianism does not.

This structured overview encapsulates the core principles of closed hegemonic regimes, highlighting key differences between authoritarianism and totalitarianism.

# Chapter 3: Closed Hegemony

## 3.1 Definition of Closed Hegemony
- Ideological control of human life.
- Characteristics:
  - **Totalitarianism** features:
    - One-party state led by an all-powerful leader.
    - Secret police for population control.
    - Media control.
    - Military and police monopoly.
    - State-controlled economy.
- Examples of totalitarian regimes:
  - Ancient Sparta, USSR, Mao's China, Hitler's Germany, Mussolini's Italy, Iran, Afghanistan (Taliban), apartheid South Africa.
  - Current examples with partial relaxations: Cuba, China, Iran.
- Legitimization often through controlled elections.

## 3.2 Types of Authority
### Hereditary Rulers
- Concentrated political power in monarchs (e.g., Saudi Arabia, Jordan, Swaziland).
- Distinction from **constitutional monarchies** with limited powers (e.g., UK, Sweden).

### Personal Rule
- Elected rulers with monopolistic, often lifelong power (common in post-colonial states).
- Personality cults influence obedience (e.g., Hitler in Nazi Germany).

### Military Rule
- Junta rule prevalent in developing countries; emerged via coups.
- Often involves western or eastern bloc support.
- Suspending normal political structures, repressing dissent.

### Religious Authority
- Religious leaders can embody authoritarian traits, imposing strict adherence to ideology.

## 3.3 Characteristics of Closed Hegemony
- Spectrum of authoritarianism to totalitarianism.
- **Figure 3.1**: Visual representation of varying degrees of authoritarianism vs. totalitarianism.

## 3.4 Related Concepts
- Key terms associated with closed hegemonies:
  - **Absolutism**: Absolute governance belief (e.g., divine right of kings).
  - **Autocracy**: Rule by a single individual; power may be shared by a few.
  - **Despotism**: Arbitrary, tyrannical political power.
  - **Dictatorship**: Absolute rule during crises, often tyrannical (e.g., Hitler, Stalin).
  - **Totalitarian-Democracy**: Absolute rule masquerading as democracy (e.g., Nazism).
  - **Tyranny**: Power exercised for personal interests, exploiting authority.
  - **Communist Regimes**: Claim of "people's democracy" but exhibit extreme totalitarianism.
  - **Religious Fundamentalism**: Often displays totalitarian characteristics; limited private domain.

## 3.5 Political Dynamics in Closed Hegemonies
- Lack of free citizen participation; obedience is mandated.
- Political activities differ from democracies; compliance enforced via surveillance.
- Propagation of specific value systems to develop national pride.
- Constitutions often resemble ideological manifestoes; rarely enforce rights like free speech.
- Executive power dominates state institutions; legislatures are often controlled and manipulated.

# Study Notes on Democracy

## 4.2 Characteristics of Democracy
- **Historical Context**  
  - Democracy as a dominant, culturally embedded regime in the West.
  - Variations exist in democracies across the US, UK, Europe, Scandinavia, affecting institutional evolution.

- **Essential Elements (Jaggers & Gurr, 1995)**  
  - **Effective Preferences**: Institutions for citizens to express political preferences; requires meaningful competition and participation.
  - **Constraining Executive Power**: Institutionalized limitations on executive authority.
  - **Civil Liberties**: Guarantees similar to human rights for political participation and daily lives.

- **European vs. American Models**  
  - Emphasis on social rights in Europe (state responsibility vs. market).
  - Economic rights, labor unions, and political parties are vital in 20th-century Europe.

- **Effective Involvement**  
  - Sovereignty of the people; political rule on behalf of the people requires accountability.
  - Rights for effective involvement include organization, voting, expression, and press freedom.

## 4.3 Indicators of Democracy
- **Purpose**: Criteria to identify democratic regimes.
- **Criteria/Indicators**  
  - **Competition**: Free and fair elections; candidate choice; requires freedom of speech and organization.
  - **Political Participation**: Regulations should be minimal; citizens must actively engage.
  - **Constraints**: Limits on governmental power.

- **Measuring Democracy**  
  - Use of quantitative measures (e.g. voter percentages) to assess competition and participation.
  - Jaggers and Gurr provide a framework for classifying democracy on a spectrum.

## 4.4 Variations of Democracy
- **Semi-Democracies**: Terms indicating regimes displaying some democratic traits but not fully democratic.
  - Examples: Authoritarian democracy, electoral democracy, guided democracy.
  
- **Manifestations and Focus**  
  - Varying properties of democracy identified (e.g., who rules, ideological content, decision-making processes).
  - Variations affect practical manifestations (e.g., polyarchy, procedural democracy).

## 4.5 The Nature of Political Life in a Democracy
- **Political Processes**: Focus on effective involvement and group participation, shaped by procedural conditions.
- **Preconditions**: Rights like speech, assembly, press, and constitutional limitations on power.
- **Cultural Emphasis**  
  - Individualism and autonomy in political culture; reliance on persuasion over coercion.
  
- **Features of Democratic Culture (US Example)**  
  - Suspicion of political power
  - Government accountability to the people
  - Emphasis on individual rights and equality
  - Support for elected governance and state loyalty
  - Trust beyond family circles

## Conclusion
- **Contested Concept**: Democracy embodies people's involvement in state affairs; historical legitimacy often used to justify authoritarianism.

# Study Notes on Direct and Indirect Democracy

## Page 51 
### 5.2 Direct Democracy
- **Definition**: Direct democracy involves citizens making decisions directly, without third-party mediation.
- **Key Principle**: Equality of participation for eligible citizens; obliteration of distinction between government and governed.
- **Historical Context**: Athenian democracy (5th-4th century BCE) exemplified direct democracy; key institutions included the ekklesia (assembly).
  
#### 5.2.1 Athenian Democracy
- **Characteristics**:
  - Citizen participation in military, festivals, and judiciary.
  - Monthly assemblies for legislative decisions passed by majority vote.
  - Measures like ostracism to prevent factionalism.
  - Legal obligations for amending and repealing laws; noncompliance led to charges of unconstitutionality (Lipson 1976).
- **Accountability**: Officials served one year, accountable to auditors ensuring checks on bureaucracy’s power (Lipson 1976).
- **Key Characteristics**: Citizen participation, accountability of officials, rule of law; effective due to institutional reinforcement (Lipson 1976).
- **Limitations**: Restricted to male citizens over 20 years; actual assembly attendance often below 15% of eligible citizens.

#### 5.2.2 Theoretical Limitations
- **Critique**: Logistics of large groups render direct democracy impractical (Schattschnneider [1969]).

### 5.2.3 Other Forms of Direct Democracy
- **Jean-Jacques Rousseau**:
  - Advocated that freedom comes from obeying laws developed by the general will, distinct from individual interests (will of all).
  - General will: Constant, pure, serves collective interest (Rousseau in Sartori 1987).
- **Modern Examples**: Elements in Swiss cantons and Libya under Mu'ammar al Qadhafi, though lacking full democratic characteristics.

### 5.2.4 Advantages and Disadvantages of Direct Democracy
- **Advantages**:
  - Increased citizen control over decisions.
  - Better-informed citizenry.
  - Direct expression of public interests; legitimacy from self-made decisions (Heywood 1997).
- **Disadvantages**:
  - Impractical in large societies; excessive time and resources needed.
  - Complexity in decision-making by citizens lacking technical expertise.

### 5.3 Characteristics of Indirect Democracy
#### 5.3.1 Concept of Indirect Democracy
- **Definition**: Involvement of representatives for decision-making.
- **Nature**: Limited democracy where sovereignty resides with people but exercised through elected representatives. Participation occurs primarily during elections.

#### 5.3.2 Representatives and Electoral Systems
- **Challenges**:
  - Ensuring representatives reflect voter interests.
  - Maintaining strong voter-representative relations.
- **Role of Political Parties**: Integral to organizing and facilitating representation.
  
#### 5.3.3 Electoral Systems
- **Definition**: Frameworks for selecting representatives; stipulations for franchise based on citizenship and age.
- **Systems**:
  - **Traditional System**: One representative per constituency, typically through plurality or absolute majority vote.
  - **Proportional Representation**: Allocates seats based on the proportion of votes received.
  - **Mixed System**: Combines elements of both traditional and proportional systems.

# Chapter 5: Democracy

## 5.3 Representation Systems
### 5.3.2 Types of Representation
- **5.3.2.1 Traditional System**
  - Disproportionate representation across political parties
  - Increased contact between voters and representatives

- **5.3.2.2 Proportional Representation**
  - Representatives per party correspond to voter support
  - Fairer representation, though less direct contact with representatives

- **5.3.2.3 Mixed System**
  - Combination of traditional and proportional systems
  - Traditional elections supplemented by proportional adjustments
  - Example: Local government in South Africa

### 5.3.3 Advantages and Disadvantages of Indirect Democracy

#### Advantages
- Practicable form of democracy (Heywood 1997:68)
- Reduces decision-making burden on citizens
- Generally places power in the hands of educated and experienced officials
- Promotes stability by keeping citizens distanced from political conflicts

#### Disadvantages
- Elections alone do not guarantee democracy; can be used by authoritarian regimes
- Lack of reliable links between government and governed decreases effectiveness (Heywood 1997:68)

## 5.4 Characteristics of Semi-Direct Democracy
- **Definition**: Indirect democracies that incorporate direct participatory mechanisms
- **Not to be confused with**: Semi-democracy (incomplete democracy)
- **Prominent Example**: Switzerland

### Mechanisms of Participation
- **People's Assemblies**: Rare in some Swiss cantons; facilitates local participation.
- **Plebiscite**: Non-binding electorate preferences.
- **Referendum**: Binding vote on specific issues; prevalent in Switzerland.
- **Popular Initiative**: Allows citizens to propose legislation; another Swiss mechanism.
- **Recall**: Initiation to remove public officials; predominantly successful with judges in the USA.
- **Deliberative Democracy**: Engages citizens in decision-making; emphasizes reciprocity (Gutmann & Thomson 2000:3).

### Advantages of Semi-Direct Democracy
- Enhances political participation and can lead to more democratic governance.
- Reduces marginalization in multi-ethnic societies, potentially stabilizing the system.

### Disadvantages of Semi-Direct Democracy
- Costly and time-consuming direct mechanisms.
- Low voter turnout seen in referenda (e.g., 2002 Swiss referenda turnout ranging from 41.2% to 47.2%).
- Potential voter fatigue from overuse of direct mechanisms; may disrupt necessary political change.

### Conclusion
- Power emphasis lies on popular involvement in decision-making processes.
- Critical for addressing government and state affairs.

## 2. Characteristics of Majoritarianism
- Defines a practice prioritizing the majority's will in decision-making.  
- Can apply in direct and indirect democracies; historical context in classical Athens noted.  

### 2.1 Majority Government
- Majority government enforces decisions on all; political party representation is key.  
- The government must align with majority support in the legislature, affected by electoral systems.  

### 2.2 Coalition
- Majority party may lack actual majority; requires coalition to govern.  
- Minority governments must gain majority support for proposals, risking marginalization of minority voices.  

### 2.3 Minority Government
- Political dynamics can lead to minority parties governing without actual majority control.  
- The majority's interests may override minority concerns unless political will dictates otherwise.  

## 3. Advantages and Disadvantages of Majoritarianism
### 3.1 Advantages
- Simplicity in decision-making; majority typically reflected in governance.  

### 3.2 Disadvantages
- Risks ignoring minority interests, leading to instability.  
- Concerns raised by Michael Hechter on ethnic domination and grievances.  

### 3.3 Tyranny of the Majority
- Majority insensitivity towards minorities enacts a "tyranny of the majority."  
- Cited by Alexis de Tocqueville regarding societal alienation of dissenters.

### 7.4.3.1 Grand Coalition
- Majoritarianism vs. Consociational Democracy
  - Majoritarianism: Competitive leadership; opposing parties.
  - Consociationalism: Cooperative governance; grand coalition of elites.
  
- Examples of Consociational Democracy
  - **Switzerland**: Federal Council with seven members representing major political parties and language/religious groups; rotating chairmanship.
  - **Austria**: Coalition government with legislative cooperation through a representative committee for draft legislation.

  - Executive councils may include diverse advisors; president elected from one group, vice-president from another.
  - **South Africa:** Interim Constitution (Act 200 of 1993) supported a coalition; current Constitution lacks formal provisions but includes ministers from other parties.

### 7.4.3.2 Mutual Veto
- Grand coalition relies on consensus decision-making.
- Consensus requires a mutual veto, protecting group interests.
- Risks: Potential tyranny of the minority; however, advocates argue groups will avoid excessive veto use to prevent deadlocks.

### 7.4.3.3 Principle of Proportionality
- Each group should influence decisions proportionately to their strength.
- All appointments and resource allocations must adhere to this principle.
- Deviations can occur: over or equal representation of groups, deviating from numerical proportionality.

### 7.4.3.4 Segmental Autonomy
- Allows segments to handle domestic matters per their cultural needs.
- Objective: Recognize segmental cleavages as constructive for stable democracy (Lijphart, 1977).

- **Corporative Federalism**: Allows non-territorial segments autonomy in governance.
- Cultural affairs and education often display extensive segmental autonomy in Europe (e.g., Netherlands, Austria, Belgium).

### 7.4.4 Factors Promoting Consociational Democracy
- **Willingness to Cooperate**: Leaders must ensure group interests are negotiated fairly.

# Study Notes on Procedural and Substantive Democracy

## 8.1 THE CONCEPT OF PROCEDURAL DEMOCRACY
- **Definition**: Procedural democracy necessitates that citizens (demos) adhere to specific criteria in decision-making processes.
- **Key Assumptions**:
  - **Membership**: More than one member required for democracy; members are bound by rules.
  - **Binding Decisions**: Need for a process to generate binding decisions on critical issues.
  - **Decision-Making Phases**:
    - Setting the **agenda**
    - Determining the **final outcome**
  - **Member Participation**: Only members make binding decisions; qualifications may apply.
  - **Validity of Demands**: All members' (demos') demands hold equal validity; no single demand is superior.

## 8.2 THE CRITERIA FOR PROCEDURAL DEMOCRACY
- **Dahl's Criteria (1989)**:
  - **Effective Participation**:
    - Equal opportunity for expressing preferences in decision-making.
  - **Voting Equality**:
    - Equal weight of choices at decisive stages; ensures political equality and autonomy.
  - **Enlightened Understanding**:
    - Requirement for citizens to have access to information to make informed decisions.
  - **Control Over Agenda**:
    - The demos must decide the agenda; sovereignty of the people is key.
  - **Inclusiveness**:
    - All adult members included, with exceptions for transients and mentally impaired individuals.
- **Conclusion**: Criteria delineate democratic processes, ensuring fairness and equal opportunity in decision-making.

## 8.3 PROBLEMS WITH THE THEORY OF PROCEDURAL DEMOCRACY
- **Problem of Inclusiveness**:
  - **Inclusion Problem**: Who qualifies for the demos?
  - **Scope of Authority**: Limits on the demos’ control; concerns about moral alienation.
- **Problem of Decision Rule**:
  - Issues in establishing valid decision-making criteria; potential defects in numerical rules.
- **Problem of the Public Good**:
  - No process guarantees achievement of the public good; leads to support for substantive democracy.
- **Problem of "A People"**:
  - Definition challenges in multi-ethnic and separatist societies.
- **Problem of Institutions**:
  - Query whether specific political institutions are required for democracy.
- **Problem of the Proper Threshold**:
  - Assessment of how closely real-world democracies approximate ideals; determining acceptable levels of democracy.
- **Gap between Polyarchy and Democracy**:
  - Exploration of factors that widen or narrow the gap; elitism vs. democracy.
- **Problem of the Common Good**:
  - Competitive struggle among conflicting groups in modern politics.
- **Limits and Possibilities of Democracy**:
  - Evaluation of non-democratic governments and the limits of democratization.

## 8.4 THE CONCEPT OF SUBSTANTIVE DEMOCRACY
- **Definition**: Substantive democracy extends beyond procedural aspects to consider outcomes and public welfare.

### Chapter: 12 Definitions in Academic Studies
#### Learning Unit: Types of Definitions

- **Definiens vs. Definiendum**
  - Definiens: Explanation of a term (e.g., "a concept with two or more values" for "variable").
  
- **Types of Definitions**:
  - **Lexical Definitions**:
    - Found in dictionaries (reportive).
    - Reflect language conventions.
    - Can be true/false based on accuracy.
    - Example: Political party - "group organized to further a common political aim."
  
  - **Stipulative Definitions**:
    - Personal definitions stating how a concept is used.
    - Cannot be proven true/false; arbitrary nature.
    - Must align with scientific community standards.
    - Example: Political party defined as "any organisation that tries to obtain and exercise political power."
  
  - **Essentialistic Definitions**:
    - Define concepts using essential properties.
    - Properties are permanent and defining characteristics.
    - Determining essences is complex and can hinder research.
    - Example: Essence of political party may differ from other organizations.
  
  - **Operational Definitions**:
    - Practical definitions specifying how concepts are measured/used in research.
    - Indicators for the concept and subcategories are included.
    - Example: Political party for research includes registered parties that did not participate in last election.

## Chapter 13: Political Typologies
### 13.1 Typologies of Government
- **Definition**: Typologies classify phenomena using criteria.
- **Two-way mapping**: Involves two criteria for classification, forming a two-dimensional system.

### 13.2 Aristotle’s Classification of Constitutions
- **Nature of Government**:
  - **One person**:
    - **Good**: Monarchy
    - **Bad**: Tyranny
  - **The few**:
    - **Good**: Aristocracy
    - **Bad**: Oligarchy
  - **The many**:
    - **Good**: Polity
    - **Bad**: Democracy
- **Criteria Used**:
  - **Count of rulers**: "Who rules" (empirical).
  - **Interest served**: Rulers' motivations (in their own vs. the people’s interest).
- **Value Judgment**: Governments ruling for the people are classified as "good"; others as "bad".
- **Democracy**: Viewed negatively by Aristotle, yet considered the least unfavorable type of government.

### 13.3 Importance of Classification
- **Contemporary Relevance**: Political reality evolves, necessitating the use of modern classifications alongside Aristotle's concepts.

### 2.1 Source Referencing
- Clearly indicate sources of information in the text.
  
#### 2.1.1 Direct Quotations
- Format: Author Surname (Year: Page Number).
  - Example: “...” (Andrews 1968:9).

### 2.2 Quoting Sources

# Chapter 14: Referencing and Bibliography

## 14.2 Referencing in Legal Writing

### 14.2.1 Quotation Formatting
- Full stop follows source indication, not quotation.
  - Example: “Smuts was a leader” (Marais 1980:100).

### 14.2.2 Compiling a Bibliography
- **Definition**: List of all sources used in the essay.
- **Criteria**: 
  - Do not include unused sources.
  - Avoid listing quotations from secondary sources as if from originals.

### 14.2.3 Bibliography Structure
- **Alphabetical Order**:
  - Organized under author's surname or decided headword.
  - All sources listed together; Acts of Parliament under a separate heading.

- **Notation of Books**:
  - Format: Author(s), initial(s), year, title (italicized/underlined), edition, publication place, publisher.
  - Example: Russell, B. 1975. *History of Western Philosophy*. 4th edition. London: Allen & Unwin.

- **Notation of Articles**:
  - Format: Author(s), year. Title of the article. Periodical volume(issue).
  - Example: Lodge, T. 1985. The politics of refusal. *Leadership* 5(1).

- **Quotations Between Authors**:
  - Must locate original author's bibliography.
  - Example: Truman, D. 1951. *The Government Process*, in Edelman, M. 1967. *The Symbolic Uses of Politics*. Chicago: University of Illinois Press.

- **Multiple Works by Same Author**:
  - Use first letter of title to differentiate; add letters (a, b).
  - Example: Poulantzas, N. 1974a. *Classes in Contemporary Capitalism*. London: Verso.

- **Headword Usage**:
  - Begin notation with headword from text reference.
  - Example: Tomlinson Report. 1955. Summary of the report... Pretoria: Government Printer (UG 61±1955).

- **Interviews**:
  - Include details for identification:
  - Example: Lombard, P. Chief Director, Department of Foreign Affairs.

### 14.2.4 Bibliography Capitalization
- Capitalize only the first letter of the first word in a book/article title.
  - Example: “The purpose of political science” (article).
- Capitalize all main words in the journal name.
  - Example: *Journal of Political Science*.

### 14.2.5 Missing Information
- Indicate missing data with square brackets in notation.
  - Example: [sa] or [sl].

### 14.2.6 Circa
- Use “circa” (ca) for approximate dates.
  - Example: Communist Party of South Africa, ca 1948.

### 14.2.7 Study Guides
- Noted as books, include full citation.
  - Example: Van der Merwe, J.A. 1988. *African Politics: study guide for APL303-U*. Pretoria: University of South Africa.

### 14.2.8 Readers and Compilers
- Cite articles in readers or whole readers as follows:
  - Example for article: Mueller, S.D. 1984. Government and opposition in Kenya... in *African Politics 3*.
  - Example for whole reader: Labuschagne, G.S. & Muller, M.E. 1988. *South African Foreign Policy and Relations*.

### 14.2.9 Newspapers
- Note format: Newspaper Title. Date.
  - Example: Sunday Times. 10 August 1986.

### 14.2.10 Historical Works
- Indicate the original publication date for re-issued historical works.
  - Example: Rousseau, J-J. 1770. *The Social Contract*...

### 14.2.11 Unpublished Works
- Format for dissertations/theses:
  - Example: Jacobs, D. 1985. *The Theory of Political Science...*. Unpublished DPhil thesis.

### 14.2.12 Official Documents
- Acts are listed separately with notation starting with country.
  - Specific examples not provided in the text.

# Law Study Notes

## Chapter 1: Election Mechanisms

### The US Presidential Elections
- **Plurality Technique**: Win by being first past the post; majority not required.
- **Example**: Wilson elected president but not Condorcet winner.
- **Approval Voting**: Voters can cast two votes (first and second choices).
    - Applied to 1912 election, outcome potential: **Taft** as Condorcet winner.
    - **Theoretical Example (G1 & G2)**: 
        - G1: 99% prefer A, B, C. 
        - G2: 1% prefer B, C, A.
        - Winner B is absurd, A is majority and Condorcet winner.
  
### French Electoral System
- **Two-Ballot System**: Majority in first round; plurality in second round.
- May fail to produce Condorcet winner, leading to paradoxical outcomes.

## Chapter 2: Parliamentary Elections

### Proportional Representation
- **Valuation**: Degree of proportionality between vote distribution and seat allocation.
- **Methods Aiming for Proportionality**:
    - **D'Hondt**
    - **Sainte-Lague**
    - **Hare's Method**
- **Example from 1968 French Election**: 
    - **Table I**: Notable overrepresentation of Gaullist party despite lack of majority support.

### UK Electoral System
- **Cube Law**: Exaggerates vote distribution into seat distribution.
- **Historical Examples** (various elections):
    - Conservative party with less vote percentage gaining more seats (e.g., 1950, 1951).

## Chapter 3: Majority Election and Gerrymandering
- Majority election in single-member districts encourages **gerrymandering**.
- Countries vary in achieving proportionality through nationwide constituencies or regional mandates.

### Hare Single Transferable Vote
- **Method**: Eliminate candidate with least first-place votes; redistribute until majority found.
- **Condorcet Winner Determination**: Not always adhered to (Example: Candidates X, Y, W, Z).

## Chapter 4: Parliamentary Voting Methods
- **Three Basic Methods**:
    - Successive Method (e.g., Norway)
    - Elimination Method (e.g., Sweden)
    - Amendment Method (e.g., UK, USA)
- **Strategic Voting**: Players may not vote sincerely based on agenda influence.
    - Example: 1956 American Congress decision on school funding illustrates tactical voting influence in outcomes.

### Arrow's Impossibility Theorem
- **Summary**: Majority rule cannot guarantee transitive group preferences.
- **Conflict**: Rationality vs. democracy; leads to potential dictatorship scenarios.

### Condorcet Paradox
- Solutions involve:
    - Social consensus on preferences.
    - Use of methods like the Borda count.

### Path Dependency in Voting
- Example of airport location decision in Norway demonstrates how agenda order influences outcome.

## Chapter 5: Supranational Bodies
- **Decision-Making in International Bodies**: Challenges in applying one person, one vote due to nation size disparities.
- **Quantitative Voting**: Allocating different vote counts to participants (e.g., UN Security Council).

### Power Index Concept
- Utilized to analyze influence implications of quantitative voting in supranational contexts.

## 6.1 The Security Council
- **Power Index Calculation**: Measures decisiveness in collective choices, influenced by decision rules and votes cast.
- **Banzhaf Numbers**: Ranges from 0 to 1, indicating the probability of decisiveness by one group.
- **Vote Requirement**: Positive decisions need 5 permanent and 4 non-permanent member assents (total of 9 votes).
- **Power Index Comparison**: 
  - Permanent Members: 0.196 each
  - Non-Permanent Members: 0.0019 each 
  - Permanent members' voting power is approximately 90 times greater than non-permanent members.

## 6.2 The European Community
- **Decision-Making Structure**: Considers population and territory size differences.
- **Current Main Body**: Council of Ministers operates with:
  - Unanimity, qualitative voting
  - Binding decisions and recommendations.
- **Voting Techniques**: Utilizes qualified majority (54/76 votes required) and at least 8 states for proposal approval.
- **Allocation of Votes**: Based on square roots of population, not directly proportional to population size.
