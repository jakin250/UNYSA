## 3
### LEARNING UNIT 1: What is theory, meta-theory and methodology and why should we study it?

- **Knowledge Objectives:**
  - Explain, prescribe, and predict.
  - Theories interrelate empirical and normative knowledge via methodology.
  
- **Methodology:**
  - Approach for gathering, explaining, and interpreting knowledge.
  - Controversies arose during the behavioral revolution in the 1950s and 60s.
  
- **Approaches to International Relations:**
  - **Classical/Traditional Approach:**
    - Derived from philosophy, history, law; no strict methodology; critical of empirical standards (Jackson & Sørensen, 2007:285-286).
  
  - **Behaviouralism:**
    - Focuses on the explainable rather than normative; utilizes quantifiable data; rejects metaphysical interpretations (International Relations Theory, 2015).
  
  - **Positivism:**
    - Linked to behavioralism; emphasizes scientific methodologies and quantitative data; criteria for theory (Vasquez, 1995; Jackson & Sørensen, 2007:290):
      1. Accurate and limited in scope.
      2. Applicable to most cases.
      3. Verifiable or falsifiable.
      4. Powerful in explanations.
      5. Open to improvement.
      6. Consistent with existing knowledge.
      7. Clear parameters.
  
  - **Post-positivism:**
    - Reaction to positivism; allows for "objective truth" in social phenomena; includes phenomenology, Marxism, critical theory, and postmodernism (Jackson & Sørensen, 2007:291; USC Libraries, 2013).

### Study Objectives

### Summary of Theories and Analytical Concepts
- **Realism**
  - Core Concerns: War and security; states survive in uncertain environments.
  - Key Actors: States.
  - Central Concepts: Anarchy, self-help, national interest, relative gains, balance of power.
  - Approach to Peace/Conflict: Protect autonomy via military preparedness; alliances.
  - Change Beliefs: Gradual change through cooperation.

- **Liberalism**
  - Core Concerns: Cooperation among diverse actors to achieve peace.
  - Key Actors: States, IGOs, NGOs, international/transnational organizations.
  - Central Concepts: Freedom, reciprocity, complex interdependence, international rules.
  - Approach to Peace/Conflict: Prefer peaceful solutions and cooperation.
  - Change Beliefs: Change through cooperation, not radical.

- **Constructivism**
  - Core Concerns: Role of ideas and social identities in shaping politics.
  - Key Actors: Individuals, NGOs, transnational networks.
  - Central Concepts: Ideas, identities, discourses that lead to normative change.
  - Approach to Peace/Conflict: Advocates for normative adherence and progressive ideas.
  - Change Beliefs: Change through redefined understandings.

- **Decoloniality**
  - Core Concerns: Coloniality of power, being and knowledge.
  - Key Actors: Individuals, states, MNCs, Bretton Woods institutions.
  - Central Concepts: Influence of colonial legacies on current power dynamics.
  - Approach to Peace/Conflict: Disrupt existing colonial systems and advocate new structures.
  - Change Beliefs: Liberation and overthrow of oppressive systems.

### LEARNING UNIT 3: Realism  
#### 3.3.1 Classical realism  
- Mainstream approach to international relations prior to the behavioural revolution.  
- Key philosophers: **Thucydides**, **Machiavelli**, **Hobbes**.  
  - **Thucydides**: Explored Greek city-states' power struggles.  
  - **Machiavelli**: Emphasized leadership's role in state influence; viewed rulers' pragmatism over moral considerations.  
  - **Hobbes**: Argued humans act on selfish desires; need for authoritative government (Leviathan) to control chaos.  
- Power dynamics and self-interest dominate human interactions.  
- Conflict is inevitable due to human nature; states exist in a constant state of competition.  

#### 3.3.2 Neoclassical realism  
- Humans driven by selfish desires for survival; interactions are unpredictable and chaotic.  
- Concerns power dynamics and conflict arising from state interests; ethics crucial for stability despite anarchic structure.  
- States are the main actors; absolute authority does not exist, only balance of power among states.  

#### 3.3.3 Neorealism and structural realism  
- Balance-of-power theory: states must enhance military capabilities to ensure security.  
- **Hard power**: Coercive influence through military/economic means; often viewed negatively.  
- **Soft power**: Influencing through culture and ideas.  
- Example: **Pearl Harbor Bombing** illustrates hard power dynamics in state interactions.  

### 3.4 THREE LEVELS OF ANALYSIS ACCORDING TO REALISTS  
#### 3.4.1 Individual level of analysis  
- Emphasizes human nature: self-centered, aggressive, power-seeking traits lead to conflict.  
- Anarchy is intrinsic; without restraints, aggressive behavior is expected (Hobbes’ view).  
- Power pursuit defines politics; the tragic presence of evil complicates state relations.  

#### 3.4.2 State level of analysis  
- States viewed as primary actors shaped by power dynamics; sovereignty began with **Treaty of Westphalia**.  
- Realist scholars stress the authoritative exercise of state power to regulate societal behavior.  
- States must protect sovereignty through military and economic strength; conflict persists due to human unpredictability.  
- Hobbes’ Leviathan metaphor illustrates the state's role in maintaining order and justice, while legitimizing power.  

#### 3.4.3 International level of analysis  
- States are the critical actors; their interactions reflect and influence global power dynamics.  
- Conflict results from states pursuing national interests amid potential anarchy.  
- Predictive capacity of a state's behavior is limited due to sovereign autonomy.

- **National Interests**
  - States seek to maximize power and achieve national objectives.  
  - Changes in power dynamics can prevent monopolization of power.  
  - Consideration of economic power as a component of military and political leverage.

#### LEARNING UNIT 5: Constructivism

- **5.1 Overview**
  - Constructivism posits that states form rules and agreements, impacting legitimacy and reducing anarchy (Lawson, 2015:156).
  - Onuf (1989) argues that states moderate behavior based on common norms.

- **5.2 Key Definitions of Power**
  - Power defined as the ability to influence others against their will.
  - Realists and liberals emphasize material resources, while constructivists focus on ideas and social influence.

#### 5.3 MAIN ASSUMPTIONS OF CONSTRUCTIVISM IN INTERNATIONAL RELATIONS THEORY
1. **Role of Individuals**
   - Individuals influence community and governmental decisions and international politics based on background and culture.

2. **Interpretation**
   - No absolute right or wrong in understanding; perspectives are influenced by differences in culture and knowledge.

3. **Multiple Influences**
   - International politics shaped by quantifiable (material) and unquantifiable (experiential, values) factors.

- **Historical Context**
  - Emerged in response to inadequacies of realism and liberalism around the late Cold War period (Baylis, Smith & Owens, 2014:134).

#### 5.4 THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
- **5.4.1 Individual Level of Analysis**
  - Ideas and norms shape international relations, stressing shared experiences and identities (D’Anieri, 2014:97–98).
  - **Noteworthy Case Study:** Mikhail Gorbachev's reforms during the Cold War enhanced his role in shaping perceptions.

- **5.4.2 State Level of Analysis**
  - State actions influenced by norms and identities that eventually become institutionalised (D’Anieri, 2014:101).
  - Constructivism is essential for understanding international relations beyond materialism.

  - **Key Concepts:**
    - Shared norms and identities can predict actions and behaviors.
    - Violations of social norms risk state legitimacy (Lawson, 2015:156).
  
  - **Case Study:** "Armageddon Letters" during the Cuban Missile Crisis illustrate varying perspectives of Cuba, USSR, and USA.

- **5.4.3 International Level of Analysis**
  - Interaction of norms, ideas and individual actions shapes the international system.
  - Identity can oscillate between national and international spheres, facilitating a complex consensus based on social interactions.

### LEARNING UNIT 5: Constructivism  

- **Constructivism Overview**
  - Focus on identity formation by non-governmental actors and institutions (Blanton & Kegley, 2015:37).
  - Norms and principles influence behavior, ideas, and beliefs, creating mutual legitimacy for actions.
  - Violations of collective norms lead to consequences, such as ICC or ICJ indictments or sanctions.

- **Subjectivity of Norms**
  - Norms are socially constructed; their validity is debated due to lack of a single objective reality (D’Anieri, 2014:102; Fierke, 2010:189, 191; Lawson, 2015:156).

- **Concept of Power**
  - Constructivism expands the definition of power to include the influence of ideas, contrasting with realism and liberalism's focus on material resources (D’Anieri, 2014:99).
  - It critiques the simplistic categorization of international relations by acknowledging the complexity of ideas’ influence.

- **Anarchy and Cooperation**
  - Anarchy is deemed complex, not just a feature of international relations; cooperation is facilitated by agreed rules (Onuf, 2013; Lawson, 2015:156–157).
  - States navigate anarchy through constructed identities and relationships, implying that these structures support cooperation.

- **Comparative Analysis with Other Theories**
  - Constructivists argue against realists’ views on anarchy and cooperation, proposing a more nuanced understanding of international relations (D’Anieri, 2014:97).

- **Ideological Context: Capitalism vs. Communism**
  - The Cold War illustrated ideological competition where USA (capitalism) and USSR (communism) employed economic and technological strategies (Marshall Plan vs. Molotov Plan).

### 5.5 A CRITIQUE OF CONSTRUCTIVISM  
- **Criticism of Constructivist Approach**
  - Viewed as a lens rather than a cohesive theory; focuses on perceptions instead of categorical norms (Blanton & Kegley, 2015:35; D’Anieri, 2014:97).
  - Critics argue lack of empirical methodology hampers constructivism's legitimacy (Fierke, 2010:181).

- **Neorealism's Challenge**
  - Neorealists emphasize material wealth as more determinative than social norms in state behavior (Blanton & Kegley, 2014:37).
  - Norms are seen as superficial tools rather than deeply constructed social elements (Jackson & Sørensen, 2007:173).

- **Liberalism's Perspective**
  - Similar critiques focus on material capacity's role in determining states' capabilities within structures (Blanton & Kegley, 2014:37).

- **Questions on Norms and Methodology**
  - Ambiguity in how norms and identities are constructed and disseminated is noted (Blanton & Kegley, 2014:39).

- **Decolonialist Critique**
  - Constructivism is challenged for accepting prevailing power dynamics and relationships, questioning the creation of identities in frameworks of oppression.

### 6.4 COLONIALITY OF POWER  
- **Definition:** Modern power dynamics rooted in racial classifications as posited by **Anibal Quijano** (2000, 2007).
- **Key Points:**  
  - **Economic Exploitation:** Dispossession and control of resources in the Global South.  
  - **Military Control:** Global North's dominance in institutions like the UN.  
  - **Education as Control:** European-centered education erases indigenous knowledge of gender and identity.  
  - **Knowledge Colonization:** Indigenous knowledge deemed inferior, affecting legitimacy in international relations.  
- Traditional approaches to international relations often dismiss decolonial perspectives, worsening power imbalances.
- Rising economies (e.g., **BRICS**) misleadingly perceived as decolonisation examples; reality reflects persistent dominance and lack of structural change.

- **Language's Role:** Language imposed by colonialism alienates individuals from their identity and culture.
- Comparisons of individualism in Western vs. indigenous collectivist contexts highlight differing worldviews.
- The Global North's narrative perpetuates the idea of Global South peoples as inferior, impacts their agency in international relations, and denies their historical depth.  
- **Example Reference:** **Bernard M. Magubane**’s work on racial constructs illustrating the negative implications of coloniality of being on agency and identity.

- **Criticisms**:

     - Focus on thinkers like Mignolo, Grosfoguel, Quijano, and Torres.

     - Question assumptions of indigenous languages as intrinsically liberating.

  3. **Progress of Eurocentric Theories**:  
     - Claims theories too developed for alternatives to “catch up.”  
     - Acceptance of status quo by world leaders; African leaders cling to colonial state structures.

### 6.10 CONCLUSION

- **Need for Decolonization**:  
  - Current theories reinforce global coloniality and unjust international relations.  
  - Emphasizes epistemic and ontological decolonization for legitimacy in international relations.

  - Theories such as realism, liberalism, structuralism, and constructivism are relevant primarily to Euro-American contexts.
  - Inadequacy in addressing African conflicts signals need for epistemic break from mainstream theories.

  - International relations theory must embrace multiple understandings beyond Euro-American epistemologies.
  - Emphasizes self-definition of relations and struggles by marginalized voices.

- **Principle of Engagement**
  - Mutual self-interest relies on ethical commitments; defines "benevolent cooperation."
  - States in the Global North should recognize and respect interests of Global South states.

  - Acknowledges Global South’s emerging political and economic powers creating sustainable development through cooperation.
