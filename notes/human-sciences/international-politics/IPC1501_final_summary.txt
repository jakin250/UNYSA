### STUDY UNIT 2: Actors in international politics  

#### 2.3.4 The State and Its Sovereignty  
- **Sovereignty**: Legal authority without supreme authority over state.  
- Originated post **Peace of Westphalia (1648)**, establishing sovereign state ideal.  
- Rulers granted absolute control, superior to religious authorities.  
- **Internal Sovereignty**: Supreme government authority within state borders, binding laws on inhabitants.  
- **External Sovereignty**: Independence from other states' involvement; sovereignty claims often reflect relative power inequalities.  
- **Sovereignty and Interdependence**: States are interdependent despite claims of independence.

#### 2.4 States and Their Interests  
- **National Interests**: Reflects state's pursuit, complex determination process.  
- Questions regarding **national interest**:  
  - Representation of majority interests?  
  - Are interests subjective, or influenced by governance?  
  - Can interests change with government changes?  
  - Is there a universal definition of national interest?  
- Economic, ideological, moral, judicial, and military criteria influence national interests.  
  - **Economic Criteria**: Policies enhancing economic standing are prioritized.  
  - **Ideological Criteria**: Alignments based on belief systems (e.g., Cold War alliances).  
  - **Moral and Judicial Criteria**: Right and wrong perceptions affecting policies.  
  - **Military Criteria**: Military strength often prioritized for state security.  
- Norms of international community influence values and governance standards.

#### 2.5 The State and the System of States (International Political System)  
- **International Political System**: Interaction of states; terms used include “international community” and “society of states”.  
- **System Definition**: Pattern of repeated interactions shaping state behavior; includes both national and international systems.  
- Key Differences:  
  - Absence of government’s monopoly on coercive power in international politics.  
  - Lack of consensus and cohesion compared to national political systems.  
  - No supreme authority or legal enforcement mechanism across states.  
- States operate independently, resolving conflicts without centralized legal authority.  
- Distinction between national and international politics essential, though overlaps exist during internal chaos (e.g., civil wars).  
- Order can prevail in international engagements despite conflicts.

**2.6 INTERNATIONAL AND SUPRANATIONAL ORGANISATIONS AND INTERNATIONAL POLITICS**

- Definitions: Inclusive approach to international organisations; distinction between global (universal) and regional organisations.
- Global organisations: No true global organisations exist; UN as a prominent example. 
- Regional organisations: Often overlooked; successful examples in international politics.
- Political powers: Member states cede certain powers to international organisations (Stratton 2008:4).
- Influence: Roles of international organisations in policymaking are increasing (Andreev 2007:3).
- Functions: Enabled by states yet tend to act independently; possess internal norms and rules (Andreev 2007:6).
- Leading powers: Define norms in IGOs; influence balanced by values from smaller powers and non-state actors (Gardner 2011:12).
- Importance: International organisations help governments cooperate for mutual interests; reflect realities of international politics rather than effect radical change.
- Supranational organisations: Decisions binding on all members, risking state sovereignty; development mainly in Western Europe.

**2.7 TRANSNATIONAL GROUPS AND INTERNATIONAL POLITICS**

- Characteristics: Operate across state borders without direct government control.
- Nature: Primarily non-political but increasingly influential in international politics.
- Actions: Involvement in information flow, movement of goods, money, and people.
- Decision-making: Transgovernmentalism transcends national interests; majority principle in international decisions (Andreev 2007:2).
- Key examples: Humanitarian agencies, international law and human rights organisations (e.g., UNDP, WFP) (Andreev 2007:3).
- Economic influence: Multinational megacorporations control key industries; importance of international banking groups.
- Migration: Labor movement impacts state interdependence; refugee movements affect state and international organisation interactions.
- Non-state actors: Significant role post-9/11; influence on global governance, particularly climate change policies (UNEP 2013).

**2.8 THE ACTORS IN INTERNATIONAL POLITICS**

- Individual role: Individuals, not states or other entities, represent actors in international politics. 
- Public opinion: Influences governmental foreign policy in democratic and totalitarian states; strengthens the government’s position.
- Representation: Individuals act as representatives of states; only notable through group context.
- Limitations: Individual actions limited; importance lies in their roles as representatives.
- Subnational groups: Minorities can lead to separatist movements; impact territorial integrity and sovereignty.
- Geographic regions and political groups: Last noted actors influencing international politics.

**STUDY UNIT 3: Power and International Politics**

**THE NATURE OF POWER**  
- Power has traditionally been linked to the "realist" approach in international politics (Morgenthau, 1948).  
- Power relates closely to the state as a primary actor.  
- Significance of power in international relations includes:  
  - **Influence:** Determines settlements or circumstances between nations.  
  - **Political Geography:** Shapes the likelihood of conflicts.  
  - **State Preferences:** Affects cooperation or opposition among states (Gartzke, 2009).

**3.1 DEFINITIONS OF POWER**  
- **D’Anieri (2015):** Power is the ability to achieve goals.  
- Definitions highlight:  
  - Benn (1967): Power as potential to influence change.  
  - Dahl (1957): Power to get others to act contrary to their preferences.  
  - Deutsch (1967): Power to prevail in conflict.  
  - Goldhamer & Shils (1939): Power to influence behavior per one’s wishes.  
  - Isaak (1975): Control over others' actions.  
  - Khandwalla (1977): Securing goals through force.  
  - Reitz (1979): Capacity to compel action.  
  - Weber (Dahl, 1968): Probability of one actor carrying out will despite resistance.  
  - Couloumbis & Wolfe (1990): State power is a function of capabilities and willingness to use them.  
- Common elements: Power involves relationships between states. Context matters in power dynamics (Papp, 1988):  
  - Different contexts (C, B, A) can influence power exercise and resistance.  
- Related concepts:  
  - Power as influence, ability, coercion, capacity, and an instrument in politics.  
- Power consists of quantifiable and subjective elements.  
- No single accepted definition exists; descriptions of aspects (economic, military, sociopolitical) are preferred.

**3.2 REALISM AND FURTHER DEVELOPMENTS**  
- Approaches:  
  1. **Realism** (Morgenthau, etc.)  
  2. **Neorealism** (Waltz)  
  3. **Neoliberal Institutionalism** (Keohane)  

**3.2.1 Realism**  
- Emerged post-WWII, opposing utopian approaches, shaping US foreign policy during the Cold War.  
- Framework for understanding global conflicts and justifying power use (Holsti, 2004).  
- **Morgenthau’s Central Concepts:**
  - **Power:** International politics is a struggle for power, viewed as an end (Morgenthau, 1948).  
  - **Rationality:** States act rationally to maximize interests.  
  - **Balance of Power:**
    - Techniques to maintain system stability; various forms exist.  
    - Consensus on balance maintains peace among states.  
    - Historical context of balance shifted with the Cold War dynamics (Gartzke, 2009).

**3.2.2 Neorealism**  
- Developed in the late 60s, responding to Cold War dynamics.
- Integrated systems elements into realism; focused on state behavior within a system.  
- Waltz emphasized an anarchistic international framework where states pursue security.  
- Power is viewed as necessary for survival, not merely a goal.  
- States are unitary actors aiming for preservation or domination (Keohane, 1986).

### 3.2.3 Neoliberal institutionalism
- Closely linked to Robert Keohane's later works, notably *International Institutions and State Power* (1989).
- Combines realism/neorealism with liberal views, emphasizing the role of institutions in international politics.
- Institutions are critical for structuring joint decisions by human actors, highlighting autonomous human action.

**Implications:**
- Institutions change as a result of human actions.
- Institutions influence state behavior, expanding focus beyond state actions to include other groups.
- Economic factors are equally significant as security in determining state goals.

**Key Aspects:**
- Influenced by regional integration studies of the 1950s and 1960s (e.g., Karl Deutsch).
- Increased interaction alters state attitudes and promotes transnational coalitions.
- Institutions facilitate cooperation amid previous neorealist conflict assumptions.
  
**Premise:**
- Variations in institutionalization affect government behavior.
- Cooperation/conflict patterns depend on institutional context.

**Example:**
- Southern African Development Coordinating Conference (SADCC) and Frontline States influenced regional politics towards South Africa.
- Institutions like OAU and NAM shaped international responses to apartheid, not merely state power dynamics.

**Influence of Institutions:**
- Determine information flow and negotiation possibilities.
- Enable compliance monitoring and implementation of agreements.
- Shape expectations regarding international agreement implementation (e.g., World Bank, IMF).

**Nature of the International System:**
- Decentralised, with all states on an equal footing (anarchistic).
- Politics institutionalized, with actions interpreted through established norms.

**Forms of Institution:**
1. Formal intergovernmental and transnational non-governmental organizations.
2. International regimes with specific issue-related rules.
3. Conventions shaping actor expectations without explicit rules, crucial for “complex interdependence.”

### 3.2.4 Power and non-state actors
- Multiple non-state actors (e.g., WTO, NATO, OPEC) impact political boundaries and dynamics.
  
**Theoretical Approaches Overview:**

| Approach   | Analytic Unit(s)                            | View of Actor(s)                | Behavioural Dynamic                             | Issues                          |

| Realism    | State as the principal actor                | Unitary actor                   | Rational actor seeking national interest       | National security is paramount   |

### 3.3 POWER AND RELATED CONCEPTS

#### 3.3.1 Power and ability or capacity
- Power relates to both objective (measurable resources) and subjective elements.
- Capacity signifies total resources available (e.g., military, economic, geographic).
  
**Importance:**
- Foundation for influencing relations and determining state interactions.

**Misconceptions:**
- Power should not be equated solely with capacity; subjective factors (perceptions) also play a role.

#### 3.3.2 Power and authority
- Authority is a specific form of power; compliance results from voluntary acceptance rather than coercion.
- Authority entails the right to enforce compliance and obedience.

**Key Differences:**
- Authority involves recognition and legitimacy, while power can exist independently.
  
**Example in International Politics:**
- UN Security Council possesses authority to pass binding resolutions, with compliance expected due to its recognized role.

### STUDY UNIT 3: Power and International Politics

- **Legislation**: UN Charter provides authority for military action.
- **Case Example**: Saddam Hussein’s invasion of Kuwait violated international law; Allies’ action against Iraq was authorized by Security Council.

#### 3.3.3 Power, Coercion and Force
- **Brute Force vs. Coercion**:
  - Brute force: Direct use of power without regard for opposition (Berridge, 1992).
  - Coercion: Threat of harm to change an opponent's behavior.
- **Gartzke (2009)**: Power may lead to conflict or influence; manifests as force or its threat.
- **Holsti (1964)**: Coercion involves high external involvement due to conflicting foreign policy objectives; actions perceived as threats by opponents.
  - Influencing strategies:
    - Threats of punishment, non-violent punishment, limited force.
- **Examples**:
  - Relations during Cold War (Soviet Union vs. Western Coalition, etc.).
  - Syrian Civil War: External intervention influenced outcomes.

#### 3.3.4 Power and Influence
- **Influence**: Subjective aspect of power, cannot be objectively measured.
- **Morgenthau**: Distinction between power and influence; influence required to exercise power.
- **Types of Influence** (Holsti, 1992):
  - Convincing, compensation, punitive measures, coercion.
- **Deterrence**: Nuclear weapons as a means of influence, e.g., Cuba missile crisis (1962).

### 3.4 ELEMENTS OF POWER
- View power through three elements: 
  1. **Subjectivity**: Power influenced by perception and willingness to act (Sullivan, 1990).
  2. **Relationship**: Power relative to that of other states (Hocking & Smith, 1990).
  3. **Composite Entity**: Power includes economic, military, and sociopolitical facets; no singular measure of power.
  
#### 3.4.1 Subjective Element
- **Holsti (1964)**: Influence depends on perception, morale, and willingness to exercise power.
- **Nuclear Deterrence**: Perception of military capability shapes power dynamics (Gartzke, 2009).

#### 3.4.2 Power as a Relationship
- Power derived from interactions; evaluates comparative strengths and responses of states (Hocking & Smith, 1990).

#### 3.4.3 Composite Entity
- Power encompasses military, economic, and sociopolitical aspects.

### INTRODUCTION  
- International law acts as a constraint on the power of states in the absence of a central governing authority.  
- The focus is on state compliance and the protection of interests within the international system.  

### DEFINITION OF INTERNATIONAL LAW  
- Various definitions highlight the evolving nature of international law:  
  - **Brierly (1980):** Body of rules binding civilized states.  
  - **Akehurst (1982):** Governs relations between states.  
  - **Von Glahn (1986):** Rules binding on states comparable to domestic law.  
  - **Heunis (1986):** Governed by states' free will in conventions and customs.  
  - **Grieco, Ikenberry & Mastanduno (2015):** Collection of crafted rules providing rights and obligations.  
- Sovereign states remain primary subjects though individuals and organizations are increasingly recognized as subjects of international law.  
- International law provides a framework requiring voluntary compliance without enforcement mechanisms, differing fundamentally from domestic law.  

### 5.1 THE LAW OF NATURE AND POSITIVISM AS THE BASIS OF INTERNATIONAL LAW  
#### 5.1.1 The law of nature (jus naturale)  
- Influences international law through moral principles suggesting states should interact orderly.  
- Seen as subjective, lacking in practicality for state behavior.  

#### 5.1.2 Positivism (jus gentium)  
- Asserts law results from state consent, which is manifest in treaties or customary law.  
- Authority determines legality rather than moral principles.  
- Recognizes international law is fluid, shaped by consent and practices of states.  

### 5.2 SOURCES OF INTERNATIONAL LAW  
- International law lacks formal legal codes; hence rules derive from varied sources.  

#### **Section 38 of ICJ Constitution:**  
1. International conventions (treaties)  
2. International customs  
3. General principles of law  
4. Judicial decisions and scholarly writings (secondary source)  

### 5.2.1 International conventions (treaties)  
- Defined as instruments through which states formalize legal commitments (Von Glahn 1986).  
- Treaties are fundamental, binding only participating states per **Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, section 34.**  

### 5.2.2 International custom  
- Reflects binding practices believed obligatory among states.  
- Changes in state behavior often indicate evolving customs.  
- Example: Law of the Sea treaty governs maritime rights.

### 5.2.3 General principles of law  
- As per section 38, ICJ may reference principles recognized by civilized nations.  
- Encompasses principles from private law applicable to international relations, serving as a fallback in disputes.  

### **Conclusion**  
- The interplay of natural law and positivism shapes international legal frameworks, ensuring coherence and predictability in state relations.  
- Understanding these foundations is crucial for grasping the efficacy and scope of international law in contemporary politics.

- International law draws on principles familiar to civil legal systems for justice maintenance. Notably, it does not merely replicate private law institutions but utilizes them as policy guidelines (Brierly 1980:62–63).

**5.3 THE LEGAL CHARACTER OF INTERNATIONAL LAW**  
- The legal status of international law is often debated; viewed by some as a moral influence rather than genuine law (Brierly 1980:41).  
- Valid laws require a sovereign community, which international law lacks due to the absence of a central authority with legislative, executive, and judicial power.  
- Unlike municipal law, international law functions without a specific legislative body or compulsory enforcement mechanisms.  
- Membership in institutions like the **International Criminal Court (ICC)** implies acceptance of its authority (Grieco et al. 2015:186).  
- Decisions from the **International Court of Justice** (ICJ) only apply to involved parties and are binding if states consent (Section 59, ICJ Constitution).  

**5.4 WHY STATES SUBMIT TO INTERNATIONAL LAW**  
- Reciprocity is crucial in encouraging states to observe international law; breaches justify counteractions (e.g., a state acting against a serious violation by another state).  
- States are motivated to adhere to international law due to reputation concerns and potential consequences for breaches.  
- While states may break international law, it often leads to more significant repercussions; compliance is generally viewed as beneficial (Grieco et al. 2015:186).  
- International law deters illegal behavior through the expectation of reciprocation among states.  
- Observance of international law promotes predictability in state relations and helps manage conflicting interests (Henkin 1979:337).  

**5.5 THE ROLE OF THE UN IN RESTRAINING POWER**  
- Example of international law's restraint: **UN Charter**, which mandates peaceful resolutions of differences among member states.  
- The UN has a significant role in maintaining international peace and security (Grieco et al. 2016:192).  
- **Chapter 7** of the UN Charter includes binding resolutions, e.g., arms embargo against South Africa and military actions against Iraq during the Gulf War.

### 9.2.1 Cases and Limitations of Human Rights  
- Human rights discussions often centered on humanitarian intervention.  
- The universality of human rights is shaped by definitions of "human."  
- Evolution of human rights includes key instruments like the **Genocide Convention**.  
- The **African Charter on Human Rights** serves as a legal framework beyond mere legislation.  
- Instances of human rights violations, e.g., **Rwanda**, **Herero and Nama genocide (1904-1908)**.  
- Conflicts in **Yugoslavia** led to significant refugee crises in Europe post-World War II.  

---

### 9.3 Humanitarianism and Sovereignty  
- **Humanitarianism**: Actions promoting welfare and human rights through non-state actors.  
- **Humanitarian intervention** conflicts with principles like non-intervention and state sovereignty (*Kardas 2001*).  
- UN Charter (1945): Members must refrain from force against territorial integrity or political independence.  
- Humanitarian efforts challenge state sovereignty during crises, necessitating governance improvements.  

---

### 9.3.1 Humanitarian Intervention, State Actors and the Limits of Moral Actions  
- **Humanitarian intervention** defined: International actions to assist suffering populations.  
- **Kardas (2001)**: Forcible action by states to prevent human rights abuses without target state consent.  
- Advocates argue states lose sovereignty when rights are violated (*Shimko 2016*).  
- Choice between individual rights protection and state sovereignty forms a core dilemma.  
- **Adam Roberts (1993)**: Military action without state approval to prevent suffering.  
- Historical roots trace back to 16th-17th century just war discussions (*Kardas 2001*).  

---

### 9.3.2 International Security and Responsibility to Protect (R2P)  
- **Responsibility to Protect (R2P)**: Global commitment to prevent atrocities like war and genocide.  
- **R2P's three pillars**:
  1. State obligation to prevent grave crimes.  
  2. International community's obligation to assist states.  
  3. Responsibility to intervene when states fail (controversial).  
- Emergence from failures in early 1990s humanitarian crises, notably **Rwanda**.  
- R2P prioritizes human rights, potentially altering traditional views on state sovereignty (*Francioni & Bakker 2013*).  
- Case studies (e.g., **Libya**, **Syria**) raise questions about R2P as a norm of customary international law.  

---

### 9.4 Conclusion  
- Humanitarian intervention's role in conflict prevention highlighted.  
- Shifting moral obligations around sovereignty and humanitarianism discussed.  
- Issues remain regarding global community responsiveness to human rights violations.

# STUDY UNIT 10: State System and the Future of International Relations  

### 10.1 INTRODUCTION
- **State Importance**: States play a critical role in IR influencing future developments.
- **Key Changes**:
  - Spread of Weapons of Mass Destruction (WMD)
  - Emergence of new superpower in Asia
  - Rise in demands for global governance (Frieden, Lake & Schultz 2010:487).
- **Theoretical Implications**: Calls for refined concepts regarding state, sovereignty, and international order (Brown 2006:122).
- **Resource Allocation**: States continue to formalize national interests and resource distribution.

### 10.2 MODERN ERA AND STATE SYSTEM
- **Origin**: Modern state system established post-Thirty Years War in 1648 (Shimko 2016:2).
- **Westphalian System**: Characterized by sovereignty without higher authority (Grieco, Ikenberry & Mastanduno 2015:37).
- **Evolution**: Historical context shows that Western state systems faced near total collapse (Brown 2006:128).
- **Principle of Sovereign Equality**: Inviolability and territorial integrity are foundational (Aspinall 2002:2).
- **Concept of Modern State**: Complex, requiring clear definition and historical context (Petho 2010:7).
- **Nation Building**: Post-WWII, newly independent states formed modern apparatuses (Brinkerhoff 2008:986).

### 10.3 POST-MODERN WORLD
- **Global Challenges**: Issues like nuclear proliferation, terrorism, pandemics require cooperative solutions (Blanton & Kegley 2017:294).
- **State Sovereignty**: Post-modern states may relinquish sovereignty to civilian-led unions (Grieco, Ikenberry & Mastanduno 2015:449).
- **Historical Calm**: Pre-French Revolution, relative calm contrasted later turbulence (Shimko 2016:30).
- **State Authority**: Modern state sovereignty model inadequately addresses contemporary political realities (Agnew 2005:437).

### 10.4 CHANGES IN THE 20TH CENTURY
- **Globalization and Technology**: Extreme forms emerged contributing to state development (Blanton & Kegley 2017:500).
- **Sovereignty**: Defined post-Thirty Years War as rulers answerable to no higher authority (Shimko 2016:6).
- **Evolving Threats**: IR theorists express concerns over new actors in development and national security without adequate knowledge (Brinkerhoff 2008:995).
- **Historical Context**: 500 years post-modern state emergence, statehood's relevance remains unchallenged (Grieco, Ikenberry & Mastanduno 2015:385).

### 10.5 CONFLICTS AND THE FUTURE OF WEAPONS OF MASS DESTRUCTION (WMD)
- **Conflict Prevention**: Conflicts deter escalating through mutual losses (Shimko 2016:280).
- **WMD Concerns**: Financially dominant states develop nuclear capabilities; terrorist threats amplify global insecurity (Blanton & Kegley 2017:257).
- **Prevention Strategies**:
  - **Actor Incentives**: Altering incentives for WMD abstention.
  - **Material Control**: Preventing access to technology required for WMD (Frieden, Lake & Schultz 2010:494).
- **Nuclear War**: Unwinnable war scenario necessitates preventing nuclear armament (Shimko 2015:206).

### 10.6 CONCLUSION
- **Economic Growth Impact**: Influences technological and resource interactions among states (Blanton & Kegley 2017:417-420).
- **Dialogue Emphasis**: Operating states require robust international communication (Petho 2010:9).
- **Future Challenges**: States must address inter-state conflicts and evolving international dynamics (Frieden, Lake & Schultz 2010).
- **Community Role**: Building and strengthening states is crucial for future stability (Grieco, Ikenberry & Mastanduno 2015:385).
