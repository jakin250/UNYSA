### 1.1 INTRODUCTION
- Modern nation-state within a world-system framework; link with world order.
- The goal is to analyze the world-system, emergence of the nation-state, and foreign policy dynamics.

### 1.1.1 The origins and the nature of the modern world system
- Origin: Colonialism, beginning in 1492, shaping the capitalist world economy (Wallerstein, 2004).
- Emphasis on capital accumulation and the role of nation-states and hegemons.
- Wallerstein's world-system analysis critiques modernity and builds on earlier critiques by Braudel and Marx.
- Theories attempt to explain world-system functions; "world-system analysis" is prominent.

### 1.1.1.1 The modern world-system and the characterisation of the contemporary world
- Wallerstein (1974): modern world system defined by societal structures, tension, power dynamics.
- Power hierarchy: Core (North) vs. Periphery (South); dominance and exploitation.
- Colonialism's impact: destroyed local structures, created economic dependency.
- Global coloniality enables ongoing domination and exploitation; propped by power hierarchies.

### 1.1.2 Critique of Wallerstein's model
- Criticism for Eurocentrism; overlooks coloniality (Quijano, 2007).
- Coloniality explains enduring power patterns originating from colonialism (Maldonado-Torres, 2007).
- Grosfoguel (2011): critique focuses on interrelated hierarchies (racial, capitalist, imperialist) beyond just economic factors.
- Suggests modern world-system as complex "entangled package" affecting all social dimensions.
- Wallerstein's model serves foundational role for varied analytical perspectives.

## 1.2 THE EMERGENCE OF THE MODERN NATION-STATE
- Modern nation-state emerged from inter-state wars and Westphalian Treaty (1648).
- Treaty established territorial sovereignty and mutual recognition of borders (Grovogui, 2002).
- Political authority centralized under sovereign rules within defined territories.
- Evolution of state structures and sovereignty over subsequent centuries.

## 1.3 THE WORLD ORDER

- **World-system vs. World order**
  - World-order is transient; world-system remains consistent.
  - Historical shifts in world order (Mignolo 2011) linked to global powers: Spain, Portugal, Holland, France, England, USA.
  - World-system characterized by static capitalist economy (Wallerstein 2004).

- **World order categories (Nimako 2011)**:
  - **Westphalian sovereignty**: Colonization of Africa, placing it on the world's periphery.
  - **United Nations sovereignty**: Facilitated decolonization; Africans became subjects of neo-colonial states.
  
- **Hegemony’s role**:
  - Stability managed by a dominant power; military and political superiority essential for hegemony (Wallerstein 2004).
  - Military force used as a strategy against emerging threats (e.g., USA and Iraq, Libya, North Korea).

- **Africa’s position**:
  - Enmeshed in global coloniality; reflects ongoing North-South asymmetry.
  - World order shifts do not easily change Africa's status.

### 2.2 THE PURPOSE OF INTERNATIONAL RELATIONS THEORIES
- **Framework necessity**: Theories provide a structure for understanding countless international facts.
- **Connecting facts**: Theories illustrate interdependence among phenomena (Waltz 1986).
- **Establishing hypotheses**: Essential for meaningful analysis; informs about data relevance (Waltz 1986).
- **Descriptive power**: Theories construct plausible world images (Burchill 1996).
  
- **Theoretical relevance (Halliday 1994)**:
  1. Identifies significant facts.
  2. Offers diverse interpretations.
  3. Engages moral questions beyond mere facts.
  
- **Guidance**: Theory directs observations, highlighting interventions and facilitating context in chaotic phenomena (Rosenau 2003).

### 2.3 WHY ARE THERE MANY THEORIES OF INTERNATIONAL RELATIONS?
- **Diversity in theories**: Varied frameworks necessary for comprehensively understanding international politics.
- **Cultural and contextual factors**: Theories arise from different academic and geographical perspectives (Dunne et al. 2016).
- **Conceptual frameworks**: Provide numerous perspectives and interpretations of world politics (Burchill 1986).
- **Clarity through variety**: Multiple theories aid in making sense of global events and processes (Burchill 1986).
- **Interpretational flexibility**: Same facts can yield different narratives and conclusions (Woods 1996).

- **Role of theories**: Provides structure; enables conceptualization of both historical and contemporary events.
- **Evaluation criteria for theories (Burchill 1996)**:
  - Understanding and explanatory power.
  - Predictive success.
  - Intellectual consistency.
  - Scope and self-reflection capacity.

- **Complexity of international politics**: Even the best theories cannot predict changes; uncertainty remains a feature of foreign policy.

## Chapter 2.4.4: Realism: Linking Theory to Evidence

- **Objective Strategy**: 
  - Describes external environment objectively.
  - States perceive accurate power distribution.
  - Aims to predict foreign policy actions based on environment.

- **Phenomenological Strategy**:
  - Actor’s actions stem from perceptions and world views.
  - Focuses on cognitive understandings of decision-makers.
  - Realist statements reflect belief in an anarchic international system.
  
## Realism in Summary
- **Basic Premise**: Humans are in a constant state of war.
- **Actors**: States as primary entities.
- **Nature of Society**: Anarchic system.
- **Main Concern**: Survival/self-preservation through dominance.
- **Foreign Policy**: Driven by military considerations.
- **Universal Concept**: Equilibrium.
- **Intellectual Sources**: 
  - Thomas Hobbes (Leviathan, 1651)
  - Hans Morgenthau (Power Among Nations, 1948)
  - Kenneth Waltz (Man, the State and War, 1959)
- **Historical Evidence**: 17th-century wars, WWI, WWII, US War on Terror.

## Chapter 2.5: Liberalism
- **Overview**: Emergence post-Cold War, focusing on individual freedom and welfare.
- **Goals**: Harmony through liberal democracy, characterized by human rights and separation of powers.
- **Key Theorists**:
  - Immanuel Kant (Perpetual Peace, 1795), advocating against despotism.
  - Francis Fukuyama (The End of History, 1992), critiques realism and emphasizes the importance of recognition as the source of war.

### 2.5.1: Foreign Policy Implications
- **Realist Principles**: Historical states operate under realism; democracies describe different foreign policy approaches.
- **Democratic Legitimacy**: Potential for peace arises from the mutual recognition sought by liberal democracies.

### 2.5.2: Versions of Liberal Internationalism
- **Liberal Internationalism 1.0**: Woodrow Wilson’s post-WWI vision of collective security and League of Nations.
- **Liberal Internationalism 2.0**: Post-WWII UN and Bretton Woods systems; US-led international order.
- **Liberal Internationalism 3.0**: Prospective reworking of global institutions to reflect a broader membership.

### 2.5.3: Linking Theory to Evidence
- **Evidence of Liberal Claims**:

## LIBERALISM IN SUMMARY

- **Basic Premise**: Desire for recognition drives mankind.
- **Actors in the International System**: States and non-state actors.
- **Nature of Society and International System**: Emphasis on a pacific federation.
- **Main Concern**: Prosperity through peaceful cooperation.
- **Foreign Policy Strategy**: Republican constitutionalism in international relations.
- **Universal Concepts**: Recognition and universal history.
- **Intellectual Source**: Immanuel Kant, *Perpetual Peace*, 1795.
- **Contemporary Theorists**: Francis Fukuyama, *The End of History and the Last Man*, 1992.
- **Linking Theory to Evidence**: Establishment of post-war liberal regimes.

## DELL THEORY OF CONFLICT PREVENTION

- **Core Argument**: Intersection of traditional state threats (e.g., Taiwan-China) and commerce as new paradigms of international relations (Friedman, 2006:516).
- **Critiques of Realism and Liberalism**: 
  - Realism sees constant state rivalry; 
  - Liberalism views only historical states as prone to war.
- **Commerce's Primacy**: Economic interdependence deters war.
- **Golden Arches Theory of Conflict Prevention**: Economic development (e.g., McDonald's) correlates with decreased likelihood of warfare.
- **Dell Theory**: No two countries in a major global supply chain (e.g., Dell) will engage in conflict as they prioritize economic benefits over warfare (Friedman, 2006:522).
- **Examples and Observations**:
  - Taiwanese elections influenced by fear of economic repercussions from potential conflict with China (Friedman, 2006:527).
  - Indian-Pakistani tensions moderated by economic considerations due to global supply chains.

## INTELLECTUAL ROOTS AND CONTEMPORARY RELEVANCE

- **Kant’s Economic Argument**: Commerce promotes peace, as outlined in *Perpetual Peace* (Kant, 1970).
- **Shift in Diplomacy**: States must engage with both firms and other nations; control over economic events has diminished, focusing on negotiation rather than direct control.
- **Scholarly Contributions**: 
  - Stopford, Strange, and Henley (1991) emphasize the changing dynamics between states and firms, where international production and trade dictate diplomatic relations.

## 2.7 COMPLEX THEORY AND COMPLEX INTERDEPENDENCE THEORY
- **Context**: James Rosenau questions relevance of theories post-Cold War, regarding microelectronics and globalization.
- **Complexity Theory Core Elements**:
  - **Adaptive System**: Interrelated, autonomous agents impacting one another; patterns of behavior established but adaptable to challenges.
  - **Agent Organization**: Each agent organizes into a coherent whole capable of systemic impact.
  - **Vulnerability**: Agents face extinction if they fail to adapt.
  - **Sensitivity to Events**: Minor changes can have significant effects.

- **Non-linearity**: Human systems learn and adapt continually; guideline approach is emphasized.

- **Complex Interdependence (Keohane & Nye)**:
  - **Multiple Channels**: Connections through governmental, non-governmental, and transnational ties; includes interstate, trans-governmental, and transnational interactions.
  - **Agenda Characteristics**: Issues exist without a clear hierarchy; military concerns may not dominate; domestic policy issues may blur lines with foreign policy.
  - **Military Force Use**: Uncommon among interdependent states for regional conflicts.

## 2.8 DEBATE (Mearsheimer's Views)
- **Argument Against Interdependence**: John Mearsheimer critiques optimism, especially concerning China's rise and its implications for security.
  - **Economic Relations**: States are interdependent; conflict has high economic costs.
  - **Rational Leadership**: Economic prosperity deters war; leaders strive for wealth.
  - **Historical Precedent**: Economic ties did not prevent WWI despite substantial interdependence.

### 3.9 FP INSTRUMENTS
- **Policy Formulation Stages** (Heywood 2013:358):
  - **Stage 1**: Decisions about how to decide; interests and sympathies considered.
  - **Stage 2**: Issue definition and forecasting; problem views can differ among stakeholders.
  - **Stage 3**: Objectives and priorities set; influenced by media, political parties, interest groups.
  - **Stage 4**: Analysis and review of policy options; preferred option selected.

- **Power and Foreign Policy** (Kegley 2009:77):
  - States vary in power; influences access to foreign policy instruments.
  - Powerful states have broader access (Brighi & Hill 2016:162), less powerful states rely on diplomacy (Brighi & Hill 2016:162).

- **Resources vs. Capabilities** (Brighi & Hill 2016:162):
  - **Resources**: Existing advantages (geography, population, etc.).
  - **Capabilities**: Resources not yet translated into instruments.

- **Example of Capability to Instrument**:
  - **Military**: Capability becomes an instrument through actions (e.g., Iraq War) (Kegley 2009:78).
  - **Diplomacy**: Derived from a country’s reputation (e.g., South Africa as peacemaker) (Nathan 2017).

- **Instruments of Foreign Policy** (Brighi and Hill, in Smith et al. 2016:164):
  - Military action
  - Political intervention
  - Negative sanctions
  - Positive sanctions
  - Diplomacy

### 3.10 MILITARY ACTION
- **Definition**: Use or threat of armed force as a means of coercion (Petric 2013:163).
- **Types**: 
  - Punitive military action.
  - Invasive military actions (e.g., US Navy SEAL operation against Osama Bin Laden).
  - Occupation of foreign territory.

- **Military Capability** (Kegley 2009:77):
  - Varies among states; changes rapidly (Wyllie 2008:82).
  
### 3.11 POLITICAL INTERVENTION
- **Example**: Murder of Jamal Khashoggi (October 2, 2018).
  - Khashoggi: Saudi journalist criticizing royal policies.
  - His murder illustrates extreme political intervention by the Saudi government.

### 3.12 NEGATIVE SANCTION
- **Definition**: Economic measures to achieve foreign policy goals (Petric 2013:173).
  - **Example**: US sanctions against Iran regarding nuclear development.
  - Consequences: Significant economic impact on Iran; sanctions reimposed by Trump in 2018.

### 3.13 FOREIGN POLICY DECISION MAKING
- **Overview**: Choices affecting a nation's actions (Mintz & DeRouen 2010:3).
- **Approaches**: Internal vs. external factors (Petric 2013:75).
  
- **Internal Factors** (Rosenau):
  - Decision-maker qualities, experience, social values, economic development, government stability (Petric 2013:77).
  
- **External Factors**:
  - Opposition, cooperation changes, economic and social group activities (Petric 2013:76-77).
  
- **Components** (Mintz & DeRouen 2010:4):
  - Identify decision problem.
  - Search for alternatives.
  - Choose an alternative.
  - Execute the alternative.

**4.2 TYPES OF NON-STATE ACTORS IN THE MODERN INTERNATIONAL SYSTEM**  

**4.2.1 International Organisations (IOs)**  
- IOs have significant influence on foreign policy; they include national governments’ memberships.
- Examples of IOs:  
  - **United Nations (UN)**: Virtually universal membership.  
  - **European Union (EU)**: Geographical membership.  
  - **NATO**: Security-focused organisation.  
  - **International Monetary Fund (IMF)**: Economic stability-focused organisation.  
- **Intergovernmental Organisations (IGOs)** are multilateral organisations made of national governments (e.g., UN, NATO, IMF).  

**4.2.1.1 The World Bank**  
- Group of five organisations (IBRD, IDA, IFC, MIGA, ICSID).  
- Objective: Reduce poverty and support development; goals for 2030 target poverty reduction and income growth.  
  - Reference: *World Bank (2019)*.  

**4.2.1.2 Joint Vienna Institute (JVI)**  
- Focuses on education and training; established by Austrian authorities and IMF.  
  - Reference: *Joint Vienna Institute (2019)*.  

**4.2.1.3 International Labour Organisation (ILO)**  
- UN agency focused on setting labour standards and promoting decent work.  
  - Reference: *International Labour Organisation (2019)*.  

**4.2.1.4 The Berne Union**  
- Non-profit trade association for export credit and investment insurance.  
- Supports cross-border trade principles and mitigates political risks associated with international trade.  
  - References: *Barone (2019)*; *Chen (2018)*.  

**4.2.1.5 World Intellectual Property Organisation (WIPO)**  
- Promotes innovation and creativity for economic and cultural development.  
  - Reference: *World Intellectual Property Organisation (2019)*.  

**4.2.1.6 Multinational Corporations (MNCs)**  
- Defined by *Gilpin (1987:231)* as firms owning and managing economic units in multiple countries.  
- Controversial role; a shift in economic control from governments to a few large entities (*Smith et al 2011*).  
- Key trends include:  
  - Dominance of large MNCs in the marketplace.  
  - Increasing wealth concentration.  
  - Growing divide between rich and poor.  
- The rise of MNCs in emerging economies (BRICS) poses challenges to established powers like the USA (*Kaya 2015:1*).  
- Example of historical context: Dutch East India Company; contemporary examples include Adidas and its impacts on global consumer markets.

- **China's Role**: Aggressive infrastructure diplomacy without political conditions, but leads to complications for local economies (Economy & Levi 2014).
- US response: New Africa Strategy under President Trump to counter China's influence, highlighting predatory practices (Bolton 2018).

- Contemporary relevance during Thabo Mbeki's presidency, advocating the **African Renaissance**.
- **NEPAD (2001)**: A commitment by African leaders to eradicate poverty and achieve sustainable development.
- **NEPAD Vision**: Active participation in the global economy while addressing exclusion in a globalizing world.
