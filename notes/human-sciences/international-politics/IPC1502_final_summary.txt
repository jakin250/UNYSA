## 1. Introduction to Law
- Definition of Law: System of rules created and enforced through social or governmental institutions.
- Purpose of Law: Maintain order, resolve disputes, and protect liberties and rights.

## 2. Sources of Law
- **Legislation**: Statutes enacted by a legislative body.
- **Case Law**: Judicial interpretations of statutes and legal principles.
- **Common Law**: Derived from judicial decisions and precedents.
- **Customary Law**: Established practices that have legal significance.

## 3. Legal Principles and Theories
- **Legal Positivism**: Law is a command of the state and must be followed regardless of morality.
- **Natural Law**: Law that is derived from universal moral principles.
- **Legal Realism**: Law as it is applied in practice, considering realities of social and political contexts.
- **Critical Legal Studies**: Challenges traditional legal doctrines as reflecting power dynamics and social injustices.

## 4. Case Law and Precedent
- **Stare Decisis**: Legal principle of determining points in litigation according to precedent.
- Hierarchy of courts affects the weight of precedent; higher court decisions bind lower courts.
- Case examples illustrate application of legal principles and test the robustness of existing laws.
- **Key Cases**: 
  - **Case Name v. Case Name**: Summary of legal issue and outcome.
  - Importance in shaping legal understanding or precedent.

## 5. Conclusion
- Understanding the foundations of law, sources, principles, and case law is crucial for legal study and practice. The interplay between legislation and judicial interpretation shapes the development of legal systems globally.

# Law Study Notes

## Chapter 1 - Introduction to Legal Principles

### 1.1 Overview of Legal Systems
- Legal systems classify into various types (common law, civil law, etc.).
- Functions of law: dispute resolution, social control, and the distribution of power.

### 1.2 Sources of Law
- **Statutes**: Written laws passed by legislative bodies.
- **Case Law**: Judicial interpretations of statutes.
- **Constitutional Law**: Fundamental legal principles outlined in the constitution.
- **Regulatory Law**: Laws created by administrative agencies.

### 1.3 Key Legal Concepts
- **Legal Precedent**: The principle of adhering to previous court decisions.
- **Jurisdiction**: The authority of a court to hear a case.
- **Legal Reasoning**: The process of applying legal rules to specific facts.

## Chapter 2 - Criminal Law

### 2.1 Definition and Scope
- Criminal law addresses behavior that is an offense against the state or public.

### 2.2 Classifications of Crimes
- **Felonies**: Serious crimes usually punishable by imprisonment for more than one year.
- **Misdemeanors**: Lesser offenses punishable by less than one year of imprisonment.

### 2.3 Key Elements of a Crime
- **Actus Reus**: The physical act of committing a crime.
- **Mens Rea**: The mental state or intent behind the crime.
- **Causation**: The link between the actus reus and the effect.

### 2.4 Defenses to Criminal Charges
- **Insanity Defense**: Defendant could not understand the nature of their act.
- **Duress**: Act was committed under threat.
- **Self-Defense**: Use of reasonable force to protect oneself.

## Chapter 3 - Contract Law

### 3.1 Elements of a Contract
- **Offer**: A proposal to enter into a contract.
- **Acceptance**: An agreement to the terms of the offer.
- **Consideration**: Something of value exchanged by both parties.
- **Legality**: The contract's purpose must be lawful.

### 3.2 Types of Contracts
- **Bilateral Contracts**: Mutual exchange of promises.
- **Unilateral Contracts**: A promise in exchange for an act.
- **Express Contracts**: Clearly stated terms.

### 3.3 Breach of Contract
- Material vs. minor breaches and consequences.
- Legal remedies: Damages, specific performance, rescission.

### 3.4 Defenses to Enforcement
- **Capacity**: Parties must have the legal ability to contract (minors, mentally incapacitated).
- **Unconscionability**: Contracts must not be excessively unfair to one party.

## Chapter 4 - Tort Law

### 4.1 Definition and Purpose
- Torts are civil wrongs that cause harm or loss to individuals.

### 4.2 Types of Torts
- **Intentional Torts**: Deliberate actions causing harm (assault, battery, defamation).
- **Negligence**: Failure to take reasonable care results in damage or injury.
- **Strict Liability**: Liability without fault in certain cases (product liability).

### 4.3 Defenses to Tort Claims
- **Contributory Negligence**: Plaintiff’s own negligence contributes to the harm.
- **Assumption of Risk**: Plaintiff knew and accepted the risks involved.

## Chapter 5 - Property Law

### 5.1 Types of Property
- **Real Property**: Land and anything permanently attached to it.
- **Personal Property**: Movable items that are not land.

### 5.2 Ownership Rights
- **Possession**: Physical control and the right to exclude others.
- **Use**: The right to utilize property in certain ways.
- **Disposition**: The right to sell or transfer ownership.

### 5.3 Transfer of Property
- Methods of transferring ownership (deeds, wills, leases).

### 5.4 Land Use Regulations
- Zoning laws and their impact on land use and development.

## Chapter 6 - Constitutional Law

### 6.1 Overview
- Studies the interpretation and application of the Constitution.

### 6.2 Constitutional Principles
- **Separation of Powers**: Division of government into branches.
- **Checks and Balances**: Each branch has measures to limit the others.
- **Federalism**: Division of powers between national and state governments.

### 6.3 Landmark Supreme Court Cases
- **Marbury v. Madison**: Established the principle of judicial review.
- **Brown v. Board of Education**: Declared racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional.
- **Roe v. Wade**: Recognized a woman's right to choose an abortion. 

## Chapter 7 - Administrative Law

### 7.1 Definition
- Regulation and creation of policy by governmental agencies.

### 7.2 Functions of Administrative Agencies
- Rulemaking, enforcement, and adjudication.

### 7.3 Judicial Review of Agency Actions
- Standards for reviewing whether agency actions exceed their authority.

### 7.4 Due Process in Agency Procedures
- Right to a fair procedure in administrative actions.

- **Civil Rights Act**: Addresses discrimination.
- **Clean Air Act**: Regulates air emissions.
- **Title IX**: Prohibits gender discrimination in education.

- **Marbury v. Madison**: Judicial review.
- **Miranda v. Arizona**: Rights of the accused.
