# Study Notes on International Organisations

## Chapter Two: Introduction to Global Organisations

**Study Outcomes**  
- Define international organisation  
- Describe key features of international organisations  
- Distinguish between intergovernmental, international non-governmental, and interstate organisations with examples  

### 2.1 Introduction
- Evolution of international organisations and institutions is crucial for international politics.  
- International politics emerged from international law and diplomatic history.  
- **Ellis (2008)**: International organisations as corporations with agency and autonomy.  
- Scholars debate the autonomy of international organisations (Andreev 2007:3).  
- Current view: International organisations lack ontological independence (Barnett & Finnemore 1999:705).  
- **Realism**: Dominant post-WWII approach focusing on state power and interests, viewing international institutions as tools for dominant states.  
- **Liberal Institutionalism**: Recognises cooperation among intergovernmental actors with shared interests (Andreev 2007:2).  
- Shift from state-centric frameworks post-1960s due to global issues (Groom & Taylor 1990:9).

### 2.2 International Organisations
- "International" often misused; better described as "interstate" or "intergovernmental".  
- Transnational relations refer to non-governmental interactions.  
- **International organisation**: Structured, formal relationships governed by rules, capable of influencing states.  
- Key characteristics (Archer 1992:36–37):  
  - **Membership**: Composed of two or more sovereign states; includes both governmental and non-governmental organisations.  
  - **Aim**: Established to serve common interests of members; should not exclusively favor one member.  
  - **Structure**: Continuous formal structure established by agreement (treaty or constitution) not dominated by one member.  
- **Definition**: A formal arrangement transcending national boundaries to facilitate cooperation (Plano & Olton 1988:416; Archer 1992:37).  

### 2.3 Classification of International Organisations
- Classifications based on membership type:  
  - **Intergovernmental Organisations (IGOs)**: Composed of state representatives (e.g., UN, NATO).  
  - **International Non-Governmental Organisations (INGOs)**: Composed of like-minded representatives (e.g., Amnesty International, Greenpeace).  
- IGOs are not created via intergovernmental agreements are classified as NGOs (Economic and Social Council Resolution 288 [x], 1950).  
- Business organisations (multinational enterprises) are not classified as international organisations.  
  - Examples include Exxon, General Motors, Nestlé (Archer 1992:38–45).  
- Emphasis on IGOs as states remain primary actors in international politics (Bennett 1991:2).  
- **Scope of Membership**:  
  - **Regional organisations**: Limited to nearby states with similar characteristics (e.g., Benelux).  
  - **Universal organisations**: Comprise nearly all sovereign states (e.g., UN and its agencies).

## Chapter Three: The Nature and Development of Global Organisations

### 3.1 Introduction
- Importance of studying global organisations in international politics.
- Historical context: International organisation field near collapse pre-1980s (Rochester in Jonsson 1993:463).
- Post-Cold War: Renewed focus on international cooperation.
- Increased attention on global issues (human rights, security, humanitarian challenges) post-Cold War (Adler-Nissen 2014:170).

### 3.2 Definition of a Global or Universal Organisation
- **Global/Universal Organisation**: Membership ideally includes all states; no universal organisation exists in practice.
- The United Nations (UN) exemplifies this category.
- Definitions:
  - **Pearson and Rochester (1984)**: IGOs include members from all regions.
  - **Hughes (1994)**: Universal IOs serve the interests of all states.
  - **Ray (1992)**: Wider purpose with the goal of serving all states.

### 3.3 Different Types of Global Organisations
1. **Multipurpose Global Organisations**:
   - Broad scope, various areas (security, human rights, etc.) (Bennett 1991; Couloumbis & Wolfe 1990; Rourke 1986).
   - Examples: League of Nations, United Nations.

2. **Single-Purpose or Functional Global Organisations**:
   - Specific functions; examples include:
     - Labour: International Labour Organisation (ILO)
     - Health: World Health Organisation (WHO)
     - Communication: Universal Postal Union, International Telecommunications Union (ITU)
     - Monetary: International Monetary Fund (IMF), World Bank
     - Education/Culture: UNESCO
     - Transport: International Civil Aviation Organisation, Intergovernmental Maritime Consultative Organisation
     - Food: Food and Agriculture Organisation (FAO)
     - Weather: World Meteorological Organisation (WMO)

### 3.4 The Nature and Role of Global Organisations
- Dual views on international organisations:
  - **Cynical view**: Insignificant in addressing global issues.
  - **Idealistic view**: Envisioning global solutions ignoring state sovereignty.

- **Distinction**:
  - Global organisations facilitate state interactions without diminishing sovereignty (Bennett 1991; Archer 1992).
  - International organisations as instruments of state policy, not superstate authorities.

- Functions:
  - Provide diplomatic forums, promoting cooperation and addressing grievances (Bennett 1991).
  - Possessing no independent means for enforcing actions but serve as venues for negotiation.

- **Supranationalism**: Transfers decision-making authority from member states to a central body, as seen in the European Union.

### 1. Supranational Organizations
- **Characteristics**
  - Authority exceeds constituent member states.
  - National sovereignty subject to supranational body decisions.
  
- **Member State Sovereignty**
  - Few states willing to surrender sovereignty (especially over political/military issues).
  - Resistance observed (e.g. Norway, Britain on Maastricht Treaty).
  - Concerns over reduced parliamentary power and economic sovereignty.

- **Example: European Union**
  - Authority derived from member state consent (Papp 1988:70).
  - Bodies like ECSC, Euratom exemplify supranational IGOs.
  - Membership conveys voluntary acceptance of imposed regulations (Archer 1992:40–41).

---

### 2. Development of Global Organizations
- **Overview of Historical Development**
  - Emergence in 19th century; 20th century organizations (League of Nations, UN) stem from multilateral conferences, Hague system, and international public unions (Archer 1992:3).

- **Concert of Europe**
  - Shift from bilateral to multilateral diplomacy initiated by the Congress of Vienna (Sept 1814 – June 1815).
  - Conceptually, a system for regular consultations among great powers.
  - Actuality - informal meetings addressing mutual interests (Claude 1971:24–25).
  - Awareness of interdependence among European states increased; however:
    - Lacked permanent organizations; sporadic meetings predominate.
    - Limited membership led to dominance by great powers.
  
- **Hague System**
  - Comprised of two conferences (1899, 1907) initiated by Czar Nicolas II.
  - Focused on universality and involvement of small states alongside great powers.
  - Marked a shift towards collective problem-solving and institutionalization (Claude 1971:30).
  - Established the Permanent Court of Arbitration and promoted peaceful resolution of disputes.
  
- **Public International Unions**
  - Emerged post-1850 to facilitate functional cooperation (e.g., International Telegraph Union, Universal Postal Union).
  - Addressed non-political issues (health, agriculture, etc.) and created permanent mechanisms for ongoing cooperation (Claude 1971:34–38).
  - Prelude to modern functional cooperation structures.

## 4.1 Introduction
- The League of Nations marked the formal phase of global organizations.
- Established post-World War I as a collective security initiative (Olson, 1994:244).
- Focuses on origins, nature, composition, dissolution, and significance as a precursor to the UN.

## 4.2 Origins
- Founded after World War I (1914–1919) as part of President Wilson's strategy for internationalism (Muravchick, 2005:11).
- Formulated during the Paris Peace Conference, initiated January 1919 by major powers and smaller regions. 

### 4.2.1 Factors Influencing Establishment
#### 4.2.1.1 Official and Nonofficial Proposals
- Peace maintenance proposals were explored even during WWI.
- Notable groups: the League to Enforce Peace and Wilson's advocacy for a League for Peace.
- Wilson's Fourteen Points incorporated call for a general association of nations.

#### 4.2.1.2 Earlier Developments in International Organizations
- League reflected the Concert of Europe, Hague system, and public international unions.
- Established innovations: Permanent Commission on Armaments and Mandates Commission.

#### 4.2.1.3 The First World War
- Stressed necessity of preventing future wars amidst the realities of WWI.
- Established cooperation among states to promote peace.

#### 4.2.1.4 Postwar International Political Scene
- Military coalition of victors influenced the League's establishment.
- Allies' dominance shaped decision-making in the League.
- Rise of small states with a need for equal participation in the Assembly.
- Presence of dissent among great powers affected the League's efficacy.

## 4.3 General Nature
- The League's Covenant included a preamble and 26 articles governing membership and constitutional framework.
- Membership categorized into original members, later invitations, and dominions/colonies.
- Primary aim: Promote peace, with focus on international economic and social cooperation.

## 4.4 Structural-Functional Features of the League of Nations
### 4.4.1 The Assembly
#### 4.4.1.1 Membership
- All League members partook in the Assembly.
- Each member could appoint three representatives.

#### 4.4.1.2 Functions
- The Assembly addressed world peace matters.
- Exclusive powers included decisions on membership admissions and treaty reviews.
- Joint functions with the Council involved membership nominations and amendments to the Covenant.

#### 4.4.1.3 Voting Procedure
- Four voting methods based on decision type:
  - Unanimity required for most decisions (Article 5(1)).
  - Majority vote necessary for procedural decisions (Article 5(2)).

### 4.4 The Council
- Consists of smaller, mobile body with defined powers
- **Membership:**
  - Great powers: Britain, France, Italy, Japan (USA did not join, left with four permanent members)
  - Nonpermanent members: Initially four, increased to six in 1926; elected on a three-year rotation.
  
- **Meetings:**
  - At least once a year; special sessions convened at the request of any member or the Secretary-General in case of threats to peace [Article 4(3)].

- **Voting Procedure:**
  - Unanimous voting required, except for procedural matters and commission appointments (majority vote) [Article 5].
  - Disputing parties excluded from voting.

- **Functions:**
  - Address matters within League's competence that threaten peace [Article 4(4)]:
    1. Approve Secretary-General's appointments [Article 6(3)].
    2. Formulate plans for disarmament and control arms production [Article 8].
    3. Advise on attacks against members or threats to their independence [Article 10].
    4. Implement Permanent Court of International Justice verdicts [Article 13(4)].
    5. Establish Permanent Court of International Justice [Article 14].
    6. Recommend military contributions for Covenant protection [Article 16(2)].
    7. Suspend members [Article 16(4)].
    8. Approve agreements with mandatories and review annual reports on mandates [Article 22].
    9. Execute decisions regarding Saar Territory/Danzig minorities.

### 4.4.3 The Secretariat
- Permanent body led by a Secretary-General, appointed by the Council with Assembly approval; officials serve League, not respective governments.
- Departments include legal, central, political, information, etc., tasked with gathering information and ensuring execution of decisions [Articles 6, 7, 11(1), 15(1-2), 18-24].

### 4.4.4 Other Bodies
- **4.4.4.1 The Permanent Court of International Justice:**
  - Established under Article 14; not integral but autonomous.
  - Consists of 15 judges, serves as an advisory body and adjudicates disputes.
  - Limited jurisdiction as states required consent for disputes and access [Article details].

- **4.4.4.2 The International Labour Organisation (ILO):**
  - Originated from 1919 Peace Treaty; ensures fair working conditions of labor [Article 23].

- **4.4.4.3 The Permanent Mandates Commission:**
  - Oversees mandates of territories administered by member states post-WWI; annual reports submitted to Council [Article 22(7)].
  - Territories classified into A, B, and C mandates based on development readiness.

- **4.4.4.4 The Permanent Disarmament Commission:**
  - Advises on disarmament and military regulation [Article 9].

- **4.4.4.5 Nonpolitical and Technical Bodies:**
  - Includes economic, health organizations, and various committees addressing social issues.

### 4.5 Evaluation of the League of Nations
- Success measured by ability to prevent war; early effectiveness diminished with major power conflicts.
- Key failures:
  - Ineffective in maintaining peace during conflicts involving major powers (1931, Japan).
  - Memberships excluded influential states like the USA and highlighted European-focused governance.
- Achievements: 
  - Established mechanisms and frameworks for future organizations (e.g., UN).
  - Contributions through agencies like the ILO and Permanent Court of International Justice led to lasting legacies despite overall failure in dispute resolution. 
- Mixed legacy: Prepared groundwork for international cooperation beyond political efforts.

### 5.1 Introduction
- The UN emerged from WWII, aiming to create a universal state organization (Muravchik 2005:7).
- It aimed to address League of Nations failures in preventing war while balancing conservatism and change.
- Challenges included nationalism and existing international relations.

### 5.2 Origin of the United Nations
- Origins trace back to multilateral conferences during WWII.
- Key milestone: Atlantic Charter (August 14, 1941) by Churchill and Roosevelt outlining post-war world principles:
  - No territorial aggrandizement.
  - Territorial changes only with involved states' consent.
  - Recognition of states' self-governance rights.
  - Fair distribution of raw materials for economic progress.
  - Economic collaboration for improved social conditions.
  - Peaceful cohabitation post-Nazism.
  - Freedom of movement and disarmament.

- Declaration of the United Nations (January 1, 1942) signed by USA, UK, USSR, China and other states.
- Key Conferences:
  - Casablanca (January 1943) for war and post-war policy.
  - Moscow (October 1943) emphasized great powers' commitment to peace.
  - Tehran (November 1943) acknowledged the invitation of all states to join the future UN.
  - Bretton Woods (July 1944) led to the establishment of the IBRD and IMF.
  - Dumbarton Oaks (September–October 1944) advanced UN Charter proposals.
  - Yalta (February 1945) refined Security Council voting procedure.

- The UN Charter signed on June 26, 1945, took effect in October 1945 (Chapter XIX).

### 5.2.1 Factors Influencing the UN Charter
- **Previous International Organization Experience**:
  - UN learned from League of Nations, incorporating its elements while trying to rectify its failures.

- **Influence of Great Powers**:
  - Major powers, especially the USA, had significant influence on the Charter's drafting.

- **Role of Small States**:
  - Smaller nations actively involved in the San Francisco Conference contributing to the drafting process.

- **Public Opinion**:
  - The process was publicized heavily, affecting decisions made by delegates.

- **Principal Considerations**:
  1. Linkage of organization goals to Allied wartime objectives.
  2. Importance of great powers' participation for the UN’s effectiveness.
  3. Recognition of economic/social issues in maintaining peace.
  4. Responsibility linked to power in decision-making on military actions.

### 5.3 The General Nature of the United Nations
- The UN Charter establishes the organization, relations, and functions.
- The Charter serves as a multilateral treaty.

## 5.3 The UN Charter and International Law

### 5.3.1 The Objectives and Basic Principles of the United Nations
- **UN Charter Structure**: Comprises a preamble and 111 articles.
- **Main Objectives (Article 1)**:
  1. Maintain international peace and security.
  2. Develop friendly relations among nations.
  3. Achieve international cooperation and promote respect for human rights.
- **Principles (Article 2)**: 
  - Seven guiding principles for member actions:
    - Sovereign equality of all members.
    - Good faith fulfillment of Charter obligations.
    - Peaceful settlement of disputes.
    - Refrain from threats or use of force.
    - Assistance to UN actions.
    - Influence on non-member state behavior.
    - Non-intervention in domestic matters (with Chapter VII exceptions).
  - Categories of principles: 
    - **Limiting UN Power**: Sovereign equality, domestic jurisdiction (Articles 2(1) and 2(7)).
    - **Positive Actions**: Peaceful dispute settlement, refraining from force, compliance with UN actions (Articles 2(2) to 2(6)).

### 5.3.2 The United Nations and State Sovereignty
- **Sovereignty Post-1945**: Basis of the international system.
- **Sovereign Equality**: Legal status vs. actual power disparities (e.g., permanent Security Council members).
- **Limitations on Sovereignty**: 
  - Article 2(2) duties impose restrictions.
  - Article 2(7) protects domestic jurisdiction but allows for Chapter VII enforcement measures.
- **Chapter VII**: 
  - Article 41 (nonviolent sanctions).
  - Article 42 (military action authorization).
- The UN's authority is contingent on member cooperation, affirming that sovereignty isn't absolute but encompasses rights and obligations.

### 5.3.3 The Charter as an Instrument of International Law
- **Definition of International Law**: Principles ensuring orderly relations among states, without a central enforcement body.
- **UN Charter's Role**: 
  - Functions as a multilateral agreement influencing state conduct.
  - Key principle: States must resolve differences peacefully (Article 2(4)).
  - Reduces the incidence of wars through established norms.
  
- **Dynamic Nature of the Charter**: 
  - Develops via interpretation and application by member states (e.g., abstention practices, General Assembly competencies).
- **Sources of International Law**: Responsibility lies with the General Assembly:
  - Article 13: Initiates studies and recommendations for law development and codification.
  - Engages further through various legal committees and international conferences.
- **Status of Declarations**: Confirmation by member votes can elevate declarations to established international law, e.g., Declaration on the Granting of Independence to Colonial Countries and Peoples (1960). 

**Note**: References to articles, legislation, and cases should be considered for a deeper understanding of the nuances in the applications of these principles. The evolving nature of the Charter outlines the significance of state practice in forming customary international law.

## 5.3 The Development Potential of the UN

- **Importance of Development**: UN must adapt to changing environments. Charter provisions serve as a flexible framework subject to interpretation and amendment (Goodrich 1974:30-31).

### 5.3.4 Formal and Informal Charter Amendment
- **Amendment Procedures**: 
  - Chapter XVIII allows amendments by:
    - Two-thirds majority in the General Assembly.
    - Two-thirds in a convened conference with Security Council consent.
  - Ratification requires approval by two-thirds of members including permanent Security Council members.
  
- **Usage**: Formal amendments infrequently employed; changes are often achieved through interpretation or informal practices (Bennett 1991:88-89).

### 5.3.4.1 Examples of Informal Amendment
- **Customary Practices**:
  - Non-consideration of abstentions as vetoes.
  - "Uniting for Peace" resolution shifted General Assembly/Security Council roles.
  - Bloc voting emergence impacts decision-making (Bennett 1991:88-89).
  
- **Veto Power**: Common use has shifted its nature from an exception to a standard, affecting resolutions significantly (Akça Ataç 2014:6).

### 5.3.4.2 Interpretation
- **Interpretative Authority**: Not explicitly defined in the Charter; organs and member states self-determine interpretation methods (Goodrich 1974:32-33).
  
- **Member State Interpretation**: States interpret Charter to advance national interests leading to conflicting understandings, complicating consensus (Claude 1971:174-175).

## 5.4 Membership
- **Membership Provisions**: Articles of the UN Charter outline the conditions for membership, emphasizing "peace-loving" states as eligible (Article 4).
  
- **Cold War Influence**: Membership decisions driven by geopolitical considerations; only neutral states managed to gain admission without veto (Muravchik 2005:1).

- **Post-Cold War Expansion**: Admission process revamped in 1955 leading to significant increases in membership, with implications for voting dynamics in the General Assembly.

- **Voting Shares**: New members, albeit small, hold equal voting rights, shifting focus to development and anti-colonialism (Bennett 1991:75-76).

- **Suspension and Expulsion**: Articles 5 and 6 detail the process for suspending or expelling members via General Assembly votes upon Security Council recommendations (Bennett 1991:76-77).

- **Impact on States**: Conflicts in UN are rooted in self-determination, economic cooperation standards, and human rights (Alger 1998:5).

# Study Notes on the United Nations General Assembly (UNGA)

**6.3 Methods of operation**  
- GA operates through:  
  - Plenary meetings  
  - Seven main committees  
  - Two organizational committees  
  - Ad hoc committees and subsidiary bodies  

**6.3.1 Plenary meetings**  
- Attended by all UN member representatives (max. five per state).  
- Observers from non-member states may attend.  
- Regular session at UN HQ, New York; meets annually.  
- Rules of procedure accepted in 1947; regularly revised.  
- Special sessions convened at request of Security Council or majority vote.  
- Election of a president occurs by secret ballot.  
- Office of vice-presidents predominately from member states; total increased from 17 to 21 (1978 resolution).

**6.3.2 The seven main committees**  
- Bulk of GA work. Each committee prepares recommendations for plenary approval:  
  1. First Committee (political, security, membership issues)  
  2. Second Committee (economic, financial)  
  3. Third Committee (social, humanitarian, cultural)  
  4. Fourth Committee (trusteeship, non-self-governing territories)  
  5. Fifth Committee (administrative, budgetary)  
  6. Sixth Committee (legal)  
  7. Special Political Committee (permanent since 1956).

**6.3.3 The two organizational committees**  
- **Credentials Committee**: Examines representative credentials, reports to plenary.  
- **General Committee**: Organizes GA's work, includes president, vice-president, committee chairmen.

**6.3.4 The ad hoc committees and subsidiary bodies**  
- GA creates subsidiary bodies to consider proposals or implement resolutions.  
- Membership can include all states or individual members.  

**6.4 Decision-making in the General Assembly: Bloc formation**  
- Each state has one vote; decisions = resolutions adopted by majority.  
- Important matters require a two-thirds majority (Art. 18).  
- Voting often occurs by show of hands; resolutions initiated by regional blocs (e.g., African and Asian, Latin American).  
- Group formations influence voting patterns; diverse groups based on geographic and mutual interests.

**6.4.1 Voting in the GA**  
- States can vote yes, no, abstain, or be absent.  
- Regional organizations help in coordinating member positions and negotiating.  
- Conflicts of loyalties may arise due to memberships in multiple blocs.  
- Voting often involves behind-the-scenes trading for votes.

**6.5 Functions of the United Nations General Assembly**  
- **6.5.1 Internal functions**  
  - **Constituent**: Votes on admission, suspension, expulsion of members.  
  - **Elective**: Elects Security Council members, Economic and Social Council members, Secretary-General.  
  - **Financial/Admin**: Approves budget, distributes expenses, supervises financial affairs.  
  - **Supervisory**: Discusses activities of other UN organs based on reports.  

- **6.5.2 External functions**  
  - Defined in Art. 10-14 of the Charter.  
  - GA may recommend any question within Charter scope; cannot recommend on matters pending in Security Council (Art. 12).  
  - GA can engage in crisis decisions through the “Uniting for peace” resolution (1950).

**6.6 The effect of resolutions**  
- **6.6.1 Resolutions on internal matters**  
  - Binding unless stated otherwise; applies to agenda items, constituent functions.  
  - Non-compliance results in boycotts or withdrawal from UN.

- **6.6.2 Resolutions on external matters**  
  - Primarily recommendations; exert moral pressure but are non-binding.  
  - Legal implications of some resolutions debated within international law; effectiveness depends on state compliance.

# Chapter Seven: The United Nations Security Council

## 7.1 Introduction
- **Creation**: Established to enhance UN effectiveness in preventing wars, tasked with maintaining peace and security.
- **Differentiation**: Clearer functions than the League of Nations' Council.

## 7.2 Composition
- **Original Structure**: 5 permanent members (P5) & 10 nonpermanent members (Article 23).
  - Permanent Members: Republic of China, France, USSR, UK, USA.
- **Changes**:
  - Replacement of the Republic of China by Communist China in 1971 (Bennett 1991:80).
  - USSR's seat transferred to Russia post-dissolution in 1991 (The Europa World Year Book 1994:20).
  - Nonpermanent membership increased from 6 to 10 in 1965, focusing on geographic representation (Barros 1972:31–32).

## 7.3 Methods of Operation
- **Continuous Function**: Members represented at UN headquarters (Article 28).
- **Participation**: Non-Security Council member states may be invited to discussions (Articles 31 & 32).
- **Procedural Rules**: Council adopts its rules (Article 30); meetings held regularly, presidency rotates monthly (Article 29).
- **Subsidiary Bodies**: Allows for creation of committees (ad hoc or permanent) to assist in operations.

## 7.4 Decision-Making in the Security Council: The Veto and Double Veto
### 7.4.1 The Veto
- **Definition**: Allows any permanent member to block decisions on substantive matters (Article 27).
- **Use of Veto**: Requires consensus from permanent members for nonprocedural decisions; flexible application of the veto tradition (Barros 1972:37–38).
- **Influence**: Reflects great power dynamics in international relations (Levine 2011:339).

### 7.4.2 The Double Veto
- **Concept**: Required when distinguishing between procedural and nonprocedural matters; provisions agreed upon at the San Francisco Conference, but not formally adopted (Barros 1972:24).
- **Modern Application**: A technique has evolved to bypass conventional double veto applications, with the president of the Council deciding on voting weight (1950 implementation). 

## 7.5 Functions of the Security Council
### 7.5.1 Constituent Functions
- **Membership Admission**: Security Council evaluates membership applications before General Assembly approval.
- **Secretary-General Appointment**: Joint responsibility with the General Assembly; reflects political significance in maintaining peace and security.

- Ghanaian; focused on reforming UN roles.

#### 3.2 Coordinator of UN Autonomous Bodies
- Coordinates policies among various UN agencies.

#### 3.3 Chief Executive

#### 4.3 Special Rights per Article 99

#### 4.4 Implementing Charter Objectives
- As head of the main organ, the Secretary-General initiates actions for UN goals.

### 5. Secretariat Personnel

#### 5.2 Status as International Officials
- Oath of allegiance ensures independence from national considerations.

### 6. Role of the Secretariat

### 9.2 The Trusteeship System: Chapter XII  
- **Article 77**: Applies to territories in trust agreements.  
   - Categories:  
       1. Mandated territories  
       2. Territories taken from enemy states post-WWII  
       3. Territories voluntarily placed under trusteeship  
- **Article 80**: Status quo maintained until agreements are formalized; negotiations shouldn't be delayed.  
- **Article 76**: Objectives include:  
   1. International peace and security  
   2. Development toward self-government and independence  
   3. Respect for human rights and interdependence of nations  
   4. Equal treatment in various matters  
- Functions in relation to the Trusteeship System supported by the General Assembly and Trusteeship Council. Exceptions for strategic territories (Articles 82, 83, 85).  
- Few territories voluntarily placed; most were mandated (notably South West Africa debate).  

### 9.3 The Trusteeship Council: Chapter XIII  
#### 9.3.1 Composition  
- **Article 86**: Comprised of UN members that administer trust territories and permanent Security Council members. Election by the General Assembly.  
- By 1974, mostly non-administering members.  
- Each member appoints a representative (Article 86(2)).  

#### 9.3.2 Methods of operation and decision-making  
- Assembles annually or upon request (Article 90).  
- Adopts own rules; decisions by ordinary majority (Article 89).  

#### 9.3.3 Functions  
- **Articles 87 & 88**: 
   - Consider reports from administering states.  
   - Receive and investigate petitions collaboratively.  
   - Conduct periodic visits to trust territories.  
   - Use formatted questionnaire for annual reports.  
- Improved petition process compared to the League of Nations. Potential for misuse due to high petition volume.  

### 9.4 Declaration Regarding Non-Self-Governing Territories: Chapter XI  
- Forms basis for UN duties towards colonies outside the Trusteeship System.  
- Administering states prioritize the welfare of inhabitants and must promote:  
    1. Political, economic, social development  
    2. Self-government and political institutions  
    3. International peace and security  
    4. Development cooperation  
    5. Information reporting to the Secretary-General  
- Limited or no UN oversight on non-self-governing territories.  

### 9.5 The General Effect of Charter Provisions  
- **Trusteeship System** has largely succeeded in promoting independence.  
- By 1993, only Palau remained under trusteeship; delays due to local interests conflict with the USA.  
- Future dissolution of the Trusteeship Council anticipated as further independent territories emerge.

## Chapter 11: The International Court of Justice

### 11.1 Election of Judges
- **Separate Elections**: General Assembly & Security Council conduct independent elections.  
- **Majority Requirement**: Absolute majority needed in both organs for election; no consensus from permanent Security Council members required.  
- **Candidate Criteria**: Suitably qualified candidates, representation of major civilizations and legal systems emphasized.  
- **Special Joint Conference**: If majorities not met after three attempts, a joint conference of General Assembly and Security Council convenes.  
- **Nominations**: Non-UN member states can nominate candidates for elections in the General Assembly.  
- **Term**: Judges serve nine-year terms, with five judges elected every three years.  
- **Ad Hoc Judges**: A party without a judge of its nationality may elect an ad hoc judge for the case duration.  
- **Decision-Making**: Majority of judges present decides; quorum is nine judges; president acts as tiebreaker (Bennett 1991:70).  

### 11.2 Access to the Court
- **Eligibility**: Accessible to parties subscribing to the statute, includes all UN members.  
- **Non-UN Members**: May join statute based on terms set by the General Assembly with Security Council's recommendations.  
- **Restrictions**: Private individuals cannot access the court (Bennett 1991:70).  

### 11.3 Functions of the Court
- **Dispute Resolution**: Final settlement of legal disputes between parties.  
- **Advisory Function**: Provides legal advice to the General Assembly, Security Council, and other UN organs, with authorization from the General Assembly (Bennett 1991:70–71).  

### 11.4 Jurisdiction of the Court
- **Types of Cases**: Jurisdiction for cases referred by parties and those specified in the UN Charter, treaties, or conventions (Bennett 1991:171).  
- **Voluntary Basis**: States not compelled to bring cases to court; disputes can be taken to other tribunals.  
- **Opinion Binding**: Advisory opinions not binding; judgments from voluntary cases are binding.  
- **Enforcement of Judgments**: Article 94(2) enables Security Council to enforce judgments, though rarely utilized (Bennett 1991:171).  

### 11.5 Role of the Court
- **Limited Caseload**: Few cases and requests for opinions due to states' reluctance to bring disputes to court.  
- **Judgment Risks**: States prefer political solutions due to unpredictable court outcomes (Akehurst 1984:210–215).  
- **Contribution to Law**: Although limited in dispute resolution, the Court contributes to the evolution of international law (Bennett 1991:169).

- Proposal for strict enforcement of membership rights related to contributions due (changes from two years to one year arrears) (Shafei 1994:2).

## 13.1 Human Rights in the International Arena

### 13.2.1 Economic Questions

## 13.3 The Refugee Question
- Instability leads to the refugee crisis; causes include wars and disasters.
- Refugee influxes create dilemmas for host nations; issues scale impacts.

- Shift from **disarmament** to **arms control**: maintaining balanced arms arsenals.
- Cold War focus on **Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT)**; post-Cold War emphasis on non-signatory states' nuclear potentials.
- Examples: US-North Korea crisis resolution; UN peacekeeping roles in regional conflicts.
- Arms control agreements related to post-WWII Japanese defense capabilities mentioned.

## 13.5 Conclusion
- UN's role in enhancing state services and promoting multilateral engagement.
- Importance of effective communication and collaboration among states highlighted (Muravchik 2005:113).

### 14.2 A Brief Overview of the Historical Development of the International Organisation

- **UN Structure**:
  - **Central System**: Security Council (15 members), Economic and Social Council (45 members), General Assembly, Secretariat (Secretary-General).
  - **Funds and Programmes**: UN Development Programme (UNDP), UN Children's Emergency Fund (UNICEF), UN Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD), World Food Programme (WFP).
  - **Specialised Agencies**: World Health Organisation (WHO), Food and Agriculture Organisation (FAO), International Labour Organisation (ILO), UN Educational Scientific and Cultural Organisation (UNESCO).

- **Key Developments**:
  - WHO influences member state compliance with International Health Regulations.
  - UNESCO promotes global cooperation in education, culture, and science.

- **UN's Evolution**:
  - Established to maintain international peace and security, in contrast to the League of Nations.
  - Clear distinction in roles between the Security Council (active anti-aggression) and General Assembly (recommendations).
  - Security Council lacks practical implementations of defense force agreements (see Chapter VII).
  - Traditional reliance on member states and allies for enforcement actions (i.e., Korean and Gulf Wars).

- **Power Distribution**:
  - General Assembly lacks power when Security Council is engaged.
  - Exception: “Uniting for Peace” resolution of 1950.

- **Secretariat's Role**:
  - Primarily administrative and bureaucratic functions. 
  - Secretary-General's authoritative role in alerting Security Council about threats to peace (see Section 99 of the Charter).

- **Economic and Social Council**:
  - Smaller body (54 members) taking over League of Nations functions for economic and social coordination.

### 14.3 The UN at the Intersection Between and Within States

- **Shift in Focus**:
  - Recognition of individual rights as foundational for international order.
  - Justice must prevail within states to ensure global stability.

- **UN Involvement**:
  - Concerns for order within states.
  - Preventing inter-state aggression.
  - Settling disputes within states.

  - New peacekeeping operations include humanitarian aid, weapon inspections, and international weapons movement registration.
  - Promotion of human rights as a cornerstone of UN operations increasingly recognized.

**Chapter 15: Introduction to Regional Organisations: The Nature of Regional Organisations**

**15.1 Introduction**
- International law & organisations seek to restrict conflict and promote state cooperation (Hastedt & Knickrehm 1994).
- Origins trace back to the 19th and 20th centuries, with significant post-WWII development.
- International organisations enhance the effectiveness of the international system (Bennett 1991).
- Increasing interdependence among states blurs national vs. international politics (Smith 1990).
- Notable rise in regional organisations post-1945; example: formation of the European Union, NAFTA, and African blocs like ECOWAS.

**15.2 Definition of Terms**
- Terminology surrounding regional politics is often ambiguous: regionalism, regional organisation, regional integration, etc.
- **Regionalism**: Integrationist tendencies within specific areas (Adeniji 1993; Asante 1985).
- **Integration**: Political unification process among states; can be complete (e.g., USA) or incomplete (economic/military focus).
- Emphasis on federalism and functionalism within regional organisations, which may arise from existing integration.

- Regional organisations labelled as limited-purpose or particularistic international organisations (Padelford et al 1976; Deutsch 1988).
- Definitions vary based on territorial proximity or functional consolidation—EU vs. NATO.
- Regional organisations may not always align geographically (e.g., USA in various global organisations).

# Study Notes on Regional Organizations

## Chapter 15: Regional Organizations

### 15.1 Introduction to Regional Organizations
- Inquiry into oceans as unifying/divisive factors in regional determination.
- Questions of membership relevance (NATO with Greece and Turkey).
- **SEATO**: Intended for Southeast Asian protection, dominated by non-Asian members.
- **OAS**: Inclusive of the Americas, yet lacks geographic definition.
- **ANZUS**: Members include Australia, New Zealand, and the USA, not correspondingly regional.
- **Commonwealth of Nations**: Global membership primarily from former British colonies. 

### 15.2 Defining Regional Organizations vs. Regional Systems
- **Regional Organization**: Defined by shared objectives (territorial, social, cultural, economic, political) with a formal structure via intergovernmental agreements (Bennett 1991:216).
- **Regional System**: Broader concept reflecting interaction patterns among states in a region (Nye 1987:5).
- Membership is a political choice (e.g., South Africa and SADC).
- Political act vs. geographical inclusion.

### 15.3 Basis of Cooperation in Regional Organizations
- Rise in regional organizations post-1945; twice as many as universal organizations (Bennett 1991:215).
- Cooperation based on common interests and shared values.
- **Territorial proximity** not a prerequisite for cooperation.
- Cultural/political affinities, trust, and consensus drive motivation for cooperation.
- Hostilities can spur coalitions (e.g., Arab League).
- External threats may catalyze organization formation (NATO, SEATO).
- Interdependence increases attraction to regional organizations; however, national interests dominate (Padelford et al 1976:468–491).

### 15.4 Sovereignty and Supranationalism
- Regional organizations do not typically lead to significant political integration.
- Charters uphold member sovereignty (OAS, AU, Arab League).
- **NATO example**: Member interests dominate over integration (French withdrawal).
- Sovereignty defined as ultimate legal authority (Labushagne et al 1993:iii).
- Degree of sovereignty surrender relies on consent; EU is an exception with supranational authority (Papp 1988:52).

### 15.5 Institutional Machinery of Regional Cooperation
- Requires political motivation and institutional frameworks.
- Cooperation ranges from consultation to operational integration (e.g., NATO actions in 1993).
- Central authority dependent on member consensus and trust (Padelford et al 1976:471).
- Authority follows "salience law": political importance inversely affects organizational authority.
- Intergovernmental organizations have secretariats and executive councils for operational governance (Papp 1988:51).

### 15.6 Classification of Types of Regional Organizations
- No standardized method for classification; overlaps can occur.
- **Nye’s Criteria (1987)**:
  - Formal functions (military, political, economic)
  - Number of functions
  - Controversial functions (technical vs. political)
  - Size of membership
  - Degree of territorial proximity
- **Couloumbis & Wolfe (1990)**:
  - Type 1: Regional defense organizations (NATO, Warsaw Pact)
  - Type 2: Economic organizations (EU, OPEC)
  - Mixed organizations (OAS, AU, Commonwealth) with multifaceted cooperation.
- **International Relations Dictionary Classification**:
  - Military alliances (NATO, Warsaw Pact)
  - Economic groupings (EU, EFTA, LAFTA, COMECON)
  - Political organizations (OAS, AU, Arab League).

### 16.1 NATO and the Warsaw Pact
- Coalition between the **Soviet Union** and Eastern European neighbours raised NATO's future.
- **Supporters**: NATO maintained peace in Europe post-1945; disbanding would be a mistake.
- **Opponents**: USSR posed no real threat; NATO's deterrent effect is illusory; call for NATO disbandment.
- Analysts propose inviting former Warsaw Pact members to join NATO.

### 16.2 Economic or Functional Regional Organisations
- Largest category of regional organisations, focusing on economic, social, or political cooperation.
- Objective: Achieve mutual economic goals unattainable through individual policies.
- Examples include:
  - **Central American Common Market (CACM)**
  - **Latin American Free Trade Association (LAFTA)**
  - **ASEAN**
  - **COMECON**
  - **OEEC**
  - **OPEC**
  
- **European Union**: 
  - Developed from various organisations; represents advanced supranationalism.
  - Member states ceded decision-making powers for broader economic integration.
  - Political integration remains less advanced than economic.

- **Key Questions**:
  - Is integration mutually beneficial?
  - Does it reduce conflict incidence within regions?
  - Does participation hinge on equality among members?

### 16.3 Multifunctional Regional Organisations
- Characterized by diverse functions and objectives.
- **Examples**: **OAS**, **AU**.

#### 16.4.1 Case Studies
**16.4.1.1 The Organisation of American States (OAS)**
- Most significant multifunctional regional organisation; notable for executive structure and history.
- **History**: Established in 1890; restructured in 1948 as OAS; underwent changes in 1967.
  
- **Structure**: 
  - Comprises 33 members; *Cuba* and *Nicaragua’s* statuses are contentious.
  - Institutional framework includes a **General Assembly** and **Permanent Council** for consultations in conflicts.

- **Objectives**: 
  - Maintain peace and security through political methods (mediation, arbitration).
  - Involved in economic and social cooperation among member states.
  - Has acted in collective security, primarily as a deterrence.

**16.4.1.2 The Organisation of African Unity (OAU) / African Union (AU)**
- Established in 1963; largest membership of 50 African states; underwent transformation in 2002 to AU.

- **Objectives**:
  - Promote unity, best living standards, protection of sovereignty, eliminate colonialism, foster international cooperation.
  
- **Structure**:
  - Headed by the Meeting of Heads of States; directed by a Council of Ministers with specialized commissions.
  
- **Evaluation**:
  - Mixed success in mediating disputes (notable historical tensions include Algeria-Morocco and Somalia conflicts).
  - Challenges persist due to sovereignty concerns and varied member interests.
