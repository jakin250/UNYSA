**1.1 Why do we need an understanding of the history of precolonial African societies?**
- Understanding precolonial history is essential for contextualizing post-1652 developments.
- **Cultural Roots**: Many South Africans trace political and cultural ties to precolonial Khoekhoe and San.
- **Critique of Eurocentrism**: Knowledge aids critique of early European narratives depicting the land as uninhabited.
- **Global Context**: Knowledge frameworks prevent viewing Southern Africa's history in isolation; recognizes transitions from hunting to farming.
- **Interaction Dynamics**: Understanding traditional lifestyles informs on indigenous-European interactions during colonization.
- **Mixed Farming Emergence**: Acknowledges historical contributions of Bantu-speaking communities and their interactions with other societies.

---

**1.2 How did the natural environment of southern Africa shape precolonial economies and settlement patterns?**
- Natural environment influences economic, social, political, and technological developments.
- Communities both adapted to and altered their environments.

**Topography**
- Refers to physical characteristics influencing human and animal migration and settlement.
- Most of southern Africa consists of the Great African Plateau, enhancing agriculture due to favorable conditions.
- The escarpment's fertile river valleys supported mixed farming and determined community migration routes.

**Climate**
- Climate characterized by rainfall, wind, and temperature, direct effects on agriculture and habitation.
- Low and erratic rainfall restricts viable agricultural areas, with significant portions receiving <625mm/year.
- Reliance on hunting and herding in arid regions; migratory patterns formed by resource scarcity.
- Wind patterns influence seasonal rainfall, affecting agricultural viability in regions like the south-western Cape.

### 1. Vegetation (pg. 13)
- **Karoo Shrubs & Succulents**: 
  - Drought-resistant, thick fibrous leaves. 
  - Found in western Cape, Little & Great Karoo, Northern Cape. 
  - Suitable for small livestock; prompted nomadic lifestyles.
  
- **Northeastern Vegetation**: 
  - Higher rainfall; suitable for crops. 
  - Types: Savannah (grassy plains) & Thornbush (thorn trees). 
  - Key for pastoralism; required seasonal migration for grazing.
  
- **Grassveld Vegetation**: 
  - Lush grasslands, few trees; found in highveld region (Eastern Cape, Lesotho, Free State, Mpumalanga, Limpopo).
  - Seasonal migration essential for grazing.

### 2. Fauna (pg. 14)
- Rich animal & insect life; vital for food (meat) and trade (ivory, hides).
- Migration patterns of game affected hunting practices.
- Insects: 
  - Food source (e.g., locusts) and pests (destroying crops).
  - Disease carriers (e.g., mosquitoes - malaria; tsetse flies - sleeping sickness).

### 3. Minerals (pg. 14)
- Variety: iron, tin, copper, gold, diamonds.
- Used by precolonial communities for tools and trade items.
- Environmental factors influenced lifestyles and settlement patterns.

### 4. The Economy, Social Organisation and Political Structure of the Precolonial San (pg. 15)
#### Who were the San hunter-gatherers?
- Earliest indigenous people of southern Africa; descendants of Late Stone Age hunters/gatherers.
- Not isolated; common globally among early humans.
- Lived in diverse environments; knowledge from archaeological studies and modern descendants informs understanding.

#### The economy and technology of the San
- Economy linked to the dry environment; focused on hunting and gathering.
  
##### Hunting
- Primary source of protein; various game hunted, including antelope.
- Techniques: bow & arrow, snares, traps.

##### Gathering
- Key activity; used digging sticks for roots and bulbs.
- Diverse plant foods: fruits, nuts, roots, etc.
  
##### Division of Labour
- Men hunted; women gathered; collaboration in small groups.
  
##### Concept of Wealth
- Self-sufficiency; did not accumulate possessions or trade for wealth.
- Lifestyle suited environmental conditions; pressures from herding and farming economies.

#### The social organisation of the San
##### Nomadism
- Systematic seasonal migrations based on resource availability.
- Possible territorial claims linked to water sources.

##### Egalitarianism
- No significant wealth disparity; equal access to resources.
- Division of labour exists, but critical roles of women recognized.

##### Flexible Social Structures
- Small, adaptable groups; size fluctuated with resource availability and social dynamics.

##### Sharing Ethic
- Food sharing among community members; no surplus food storage; viewed environment as communal resource.

#### Ritual participation among the San
- Ritual ceremonies crucial; involved shamanism and trance dances.
- Shamans acted as intermediaries between physical and supernatural realms.

##### Rock Paintings
- Interpretations connect to social, economic, and religious aspects of San life; symbols depict religious beliefs and cultural values.

## Political Organisation and System of Justice
- **San Society:**
  - Small bands, absence of wealth-based political power.
  - Decisions made collectively by all adults (men & women).
  - Role of shamans for guidance.
  - **Consensus-based decision-making**.
  - Conflict resolution on a one-to-one or family basis.
  - Limited capacity to resolve disputes.

## Khoekhoe Lifestyle Comparison
### Who were the Khoekhoe?
- Early pastoralists in southern Africa, possibly linked to hunter-gatherers.
- Introduced livestock leading to the "pastoral revolution."
- Theories on livestock dispersal: migration vs. trade.

### The Khoekhoe Economy
- Similar hunting & gathering practices to San, but primarily pastoralism.
- **Economic Activities:**
  - Mainly herding sheep, later cattle.
  - Pottery for milk storage; role of livestock as material wealth.
  - Cattle valued for milk, transport, and trade.

### Social Organisation
- **Wealth Influence:**
  - Ownership of livestock created social hierarchy.
  - Contrasts with egalitarian San structure.

### Group Size & Mobility
- Khoekhoe communities larger (approx. 500 people) vs. smaller San clans (<50 people).
- Flexibility necessary during environmental stress.

### Transhumance
- Seasonal migration to ensure livestock diets.
- Preserved natural resources, similar to San hunting practices.

### Settlement Patterns
- Khoekhoe settled in grazing areas with water; contrasted with San's wide geographic presence.

### Social Relationships
- Livestock important in marriage, inheritance, justice administration.
- Slaughtered for significant events; absence in San communities.

### Gender in Khoekhoe Society
- Women had lower social status than men, limited political power.
- Significant economic roles but restricted from formal decision-making.

### Political Structure
- Wealth-based political power led to chiefs managing herding economy.
- Chiefs responsible for resource allocation and conflict resolution.
- Clientship relationships for those with diminished wealth.

## Interaction between Hunter-Gatherers and Hunter-Herders
- **Competition for Resources:**
  - Khoekhoe pastoralism vs. San hunting grounds led to friction.
  - Differing views on animals: Khoekhoe - wealth; San - food.
- **Conflict and Coexistence:**
  - Incidents of stock theft and territorial disputes.
  - Evidence of peaceful coexistence with intermarriage and trade.

## Chapter 1: Early Societies in Southern Africa

### 1.5 Lifestyle of Mixed Farming Communities (200 AD Onwards)
- **Transition to Mixed Farming**  
  - Emergence around 200 AD.
  - Bantu-speakers migrated from East/Central Africa.
  - Introduction of crop cultivation; labeled as Mixed Farming or Iron Age due to iron processing.

- **Economic Diversity**  
  - Mixed economy: crop cultivation, livestock herding, hunting, mining, trade.
  - Key crops: millet, sorghum, legumes, squashes, pumpkins.
  - Livestock: sheep, goats, cattle.
  - Mining: iron and copper introduced from outside southern Africa.

- **Shift in Economic Focus**  
  - Shift from crop cultivation to livestock herding by 1000 AD.
  - Livestock herding became predominant; crop cultivation secondary.
  - Hunting's significance increased; ivory trading developed in late periods.
  
- **Trade Evolution**  
  - Early trade was localized among neighbors for essential goods.
  - By 800-900 AD, established regional trading networks, including with Arabian traders.
  - Late mixed farmers engaged in long-distance trade; luxury goods traded.

- **Mining Practices**  
  - Early miners focused on iron, copper, limited gold.
  - Late period saw expansive mining for greater demands.

### Social Organization
- **Settlement Patterns**  
  - Early farmers settled in fertile areas for crop cultivation; adopted slash-and-burn agriculture.
  - Late farmers preferred high grasslands for livestock herding; more mobile due to cattle raising.

- **Population Density**  
  - Higher density among early mixed farmers due to balanced diets, food surpluses, stable lifestyles.
  - Population density increased further in late period, more hierarchical.
  
- **Division of Labour**  
  - Early period: Women cultivated crops, cared for children; men involved in hunting.
  - Late period: Women continued with crops but also engaged in pottery; men focused on herding, trading, mining.

- **Status of Women and Men**  
  - Early communities: Women had limited status; excluded from decision-making and livestock enclosures.
  - Late communities: Women’s status diminished; men dominated wealth from cattle and trade.

### Cultural Practices
- **Social Practices**  
  - Bridewealth (lobolo) became common; cattle used in marriage exchanges.
  - Introduction of mafisa: lending cattle for support, enhancing political influence.
  - Initiation ceremonies for boys and girls continued across both periods.

- **Architecture and Art**  
  - Houses retained circular forms; transition to stone as a building material in late period.
  - Pottery served practical and symbolic purposes; artistic expressions included carvings and rock engravings.

- **Religious Beliefs**  
  - Traditional African religion practiced throughout both periods; belief in a Superior Being communicated through ancestors.
  - Religious symbols found, such as soapstone images associated with ancestors.

### Political Organization
- **Evolution of Political Structures**  
  - Early mixed farmers had small-scale, communal governance; lacked stratified chiefdoms.
  - Villages remained mostly autonomous: no significant accumulation of wealth among them.
  - Emergence of some wealthier individuals suggesting nascent chiefdoms for dispute resolution.

### 1.6 Interaction of Mixed Farming Communities with Khoekhoe and San 
- Early mixed farming communities entered territories of San and Khoekhoe.

- Initial sporadic relations evolved into complex interactions, alliances formed.
- Mixed farmers considered San inferior but valued their environmental knowledge and spirituality.
- Coexistence led to trade, intermarriage, and clientship over centuries.
- Khoekhoe and San contributed as hunters, rain-makers, and tool makers, receiving agricultural products and iron in return.
- Some Khoekhoe and San adopted new farming techniques, while others preserved traditional lifestyles.
- Influence on Bantu-speaking farmers evident in linguistic borrowings (e.g., 'click' sounds).

### 2.2 Europe and the Non-European World
- European expansion perceived as the initiation of modern history; often regarded as superior.
- Dominance not inevitable; pre-15th century regions (Asia, South America) were technologically advanced.
- *Key Developments*:
  - **Agricultural Development**: Established centuries prior; significant food production evolved.
  - **Non-Agricultural Activities**: Growth of trade, infrastructure (e.g., China’s roads, canals) before Europe.
  - **Knowledge of Societies**: Europeans ignorant of major world regions; advanced knowledge in Arab and Chinese civilizations.
  - **State-Building**: Strong empires (e.g., Aztecs, Incas) contrasted with weaker European states; notable stability before European contacts.
- Decline of non-European powers set stage for European assertion post-1500, catalyzed by crises leading to resource expansion.

### 2.3 Emergence of Portugal as a World Power
- Portugal initiated overseas expansion in the 1400s despite its agricultural poverty.
- Influences on expansion:
  - **Prince Henry the Navigator's Role**: Supported maritime exploration, but was not sole cause.
  - **Search for Resources**: Nobility sought profit opportunities, knowledge of gold fields in West Africa attracted explorers.
  - **Geographical Advantage**: Proximity to Africa facilitated maritime ventures; knowledge of wind systems became crucial.
- Phases of expansion included significant conquests, starting with capturing Ceuta in 1415, learning trade routes for wealth generation.

### 1. Early Portuguese Exploration

#### 1.1 Settlement of North Atlantic Islands
- Madeira, Azores, and Cape Verde settled (1419-1460).
- Bases established for Atlantic exploration.

#### 1.2 Expansion along African Coast
- Portuguese reached Sierra Leone (1460) and Gold Coast (1471).
- Entry into Congo (1483).
- Bartholomeus Dias rounded Cape of Good Hope (1488).
- Vasco da Gama navigated to India (1497), initiating spice trade.

#### 1.3 Control of the Spice Trade
- Established forts along African coast.
- Key bases in Cape Verde, Sao Tomé, Luanda, and Mozambique.
- Dominated half Europe's spice supply by 1560s.

#### 1.4 Wealth through Trade and the Slave Trade
- Gold from Gold Coast boosted wealth.
- Slave trade significant from 1450s to 1600.
- Lisbon became third-largest European city by 17th century.

#### 1.5 Challenges of Southern Africa
- Limited settlement attempts in southern Africa due to:
  - Difficult coastline navigation.
  - Lack of fresh water and navigable rivers.
  - Hostility from local Khoekhoe.

### 2. Rise of the Netherlands

#### 2.1 Competition with Portugal and Spain
- Spanish discovery of Americas (1492) diverted focus from spices.
- Portugal maintained East trade until Dutch rivalry.

#### 2.2 Dutch Maritime Industry
- Dutch agriculture improvements supported maritime expansion.
- Dominated shipping trade by carrying goods cheaply.
- Amsterdam became Europe's financial centre.

### 3. Dutch East India Company (VOC)

#### 3.1 Foundation and Objectives
- VOC founded in 1602, granted charter by States General.
- Exclusive trade monopoly in the East established.
- Involvement in violent conquests (e.g., Banda islands).

#### 3.2 Monopolistic Structure of VOC
- Three levels of monopoly:
  - Sole right to trade between East and Netherlands.
  - Authority to negotiate, occupy lands, declare wars.
  - Control over internal trading activities in the region.
- Principle of maximum profit and direct returns emphasized.

### 4. Establishment of a Refreshment Station at the Cape

#### 4.1 Exploration of the Cape's Potential
- 1640s: Dutch interest in Cape for fresh water and provisions.
- 1647: VOC ship Nieuwe Haerlem ran aground; crew's successful scouting.

#### 4.2 Establishment of the Refreshment Post
- 1651: VOC decided to establish a refreshment station based on positive reports (Remonstrantie).
- Jan van Riebeeck appointed as commander.
- The post aimed to remain small to minimize costs.

### 5. Conclusion
- Jan van Riebeeck settled at Table Bay (1652) marking permanent European presence.
- Initial limitation of settlement by VOC led to extensive colonial development.

## 3 LEARNING UNIT 3: THE DUTCH SETTLEMENT AT THE CAPE

### 3.1 Introduction
- Establishment of the refreshment station by the VOC was intended as a small settlement for resupplying ships.
- Over time, the settlement evolved into a large colony.
- Focus of this unit includes:
  - Cape as a refreshment station.
  - Agricultural practices in the south-western Cape.
  - Challenges faced by farmers.
  - Transition to pastoral farming in the interior.

### 3.2 The Cape as a Refreshment Station
- VOC charter prioritized securing trade routes over colonization.
- Cape was meant to supply necessities for ships, not as a major trading post.
  
#### Early Goals
- Jan van Riebeeck's arrival in 1652 aimed to create a small outpost to support VOC interests.
- Responsibilities included:
  - Building a fort.
  - Starting a vegetable garden.
  - Supplying fresh provisions.
  - Acquiring livestock from Khoekhoe.
- Van Riebeeck struggled to fulfill these tasks during his 10-year tenure.

#### Early Experiences
- Initial years faced potential disaster; supplies often insufficient.
- Poor support from VOC limited capacity to sustain settlement.
- Farming efforts hampered by factors such as:
  - Environmental conditions (droughts, floods).
  - Inability to farm independently.
- Van Riebeeck proposed freeburgher farming to allow officials to farm independently for profit.

#### Emergence of Freeburgher Farmers
- VOC’s reluctance gave way when benefits for VOC were presented.
- First nine freeburgher farmers established in February 1657.
- Each granted 11.3 hectares intending to replicate Dutch intensive agriculture techniques.

### 3.3 Intensive Agriculture at the South-Western Cape
- Early freeburgher farmers inadequately met agricultural needs until around 1680.
  
#### Reasons for Failure
- **Inexperience**: Lack of adaptation to local conditions and agricultural skills.
- **Financial challenges**: Ingrained poverty among farmers; limited financial support from VOC.
- **VOC Control**: Interference in farming decisions and pricing caused production difficulties.
- **Continuing VOC Farming**: Competition from VOC's own agricultural pursuits limited freeburgher profitability.
- **Climate and Soil Conditions**: Adverse weather impacted crop yield; suboptimal soil conditions hindered productivity.
- **Labour Issues**: Reliance on costly, unskilled VOC soldiers; initial failure to employ local Khoekhoe.
- **Importation of Slaves**: Initiated in 1658, eventually easing labour shortages.

- Farmers became further indebted, and many abandoned farming. 
- 1664 emergency aid by VOC highlighted agricultural difficulties.
- Governor Simon van der Stel's arrival in 1679 reformed land allocation, transitioning to extensive agriculture and influencing population distribution.

#### The Arrival of Governor Simon van der Stel
- Promoted land grants on freehold basis leading to larger farms.
- Shift toward extensive farming methods; increased livestock farming.
- Wheat prices increased, resulting in overproduction and subsequent market problems.
- Constraints on selling excess wheat forced farmers to diversify crops or seek alternative income.

### Conclusion
- The evolution from a small refreshment station to a considerable agricultural society indicates complexities and challenges of colonial farming practices led by the VOC. 
- Interaction between local conditions and VOC policies significantly shaped agricultural outcomes.

## 3.4 The South-Western Cape During the VOC Period

- **Settler Policy**
  - VOC's intention: Limited territorial expansion; maintained contained settlements.
  - Grazing rights granted to unused lands; compromised agricultural productivity.

- **Population Trends**
  - Limited Dutch immigration due to relative prosperity and lack of persecution in the Netherlands.
  - Significant numbers of German settlers by late 17th century (20% of population).
  - Influx of French Huguenots in 1688, fleeing religious persecution.

- **Demographic Changes**
  - By 1717, VOC aimed to halt white immigration; economy shifted to reliance on slave labor.
  - White population grew to approximately 27,000 by 1795, mostly in Cape Town and the agricultural districts.

- **Cape Town Development**
  - Initial population of about 1,000 (1710); grew to 16,500 by late 18th century.
  - Cape Town viewed as a refreshment station by the VOC; key trading hub for settlers.

- **Economic Activity**
  - Economy fluctuated with trade and smuggling; largely reliant on market dynamics.
  - VoC officials wielded considerable power, often disregarding farmers' needs.
  - Lower-ranked officials supplemented income through private trading.

- **Social Structure**
  - Predominantly white citizenry engaged in various trades; significant population of free blacks and Khoekhoe.
  - Diverse labor force: Free blacks occupied trades, while slaves and Khoekhoe workers supported agricultural activities.
  - Cultural influences from Asian (Indonesian & Malay) traders introduced through VOC connections.

- **Agricultural Districts**
  - Farmers faced socio-economic challenges; divided into free (whites) and laboring classes (blacks).
  - Long-standing economic fluctuations; varying degrees of wealth among farmers.
  - Stable occupation mainly in grain and wine farming; reliance on slave labor and inadequate infrastructure (e.g., roads).

## 3.5 Pastoral Farming in the Cape Interior

- **Trekboer Economy**
  - Emergence of "trekboers" — hunter-herders abandoning traditional agriculture.
  - Trekboers constituted two-thirds of the white farming community by 1795; expanded frontiers into northern and eastern territories.

- **Shifts in Settlement**
  - Beginning of 18th century marked a move away from south-western Cape; formed a connection with the interior of Africa.
  - Transition from refreshment station to an established colony observed.

- **Economic Push and Pull Factors**
  - **Push Factors**: Struggles of freeburghers in the south-western Cape drove migration.
  - **Pull Factors**: Desire for economic independence and the availability of land in the interior, favored by stock-farming as a viable alternative to traditional agricultural pursuits.

- **Stock-Farming Opportunities**
  - Stock-farming emerged as a key livelihood for struggling farmers; less restrictive than established agricultural pathways.
  - Bywoners (landless squatters) contributed to the herding community dynamics; viewed positively due to land availability.

- **Cultural Impacts and Adaptation**
  - Dispersal of trekboers altered the socio-economic landscape, fostering new community norms and identity distinct from European ties.

## Chapter 3: Trekboers and White Expansion into the Cape Interior

### Unit 3: Trekboer Lifestyle and Factors Influencing Expansion

#### Characteristics of Trekboers

- **Economic Structure**:
  - Predominantly pastoralism with hunting, trading, and raiding.
  - Some trekboers (often "eenlopendes") lived more nomadically, forging ties with indigenous communities.
  - Economic independence possible through local natural resources.

- **Environmental Adaptation**:
  - Problems like drought addressed through semi-nomadic lifestyles.
  - Pastoralists could migrate, overcoming issues faced by agriculturalists tied to land and markets.

- **Capital Requirements**:
  - Minimal capital compared to agriculturalists; basic needs fulfilled through natural herding opportunities.
  
- **Livestock Growth**:
  - Initial herds acquired via trading with Khoekhoe; rapid increase in stock from 350 in 1658 to 76,000 by end of 17th century.
  
- **Land Access**:
  - Free grazing permits and loan farm schemes allowed trekboers access to vast unregulated lands.
  - Freedom to migrate avoided high costs associated with freehold land ownership.
  
- **Labour**:
  - Lesser labor intensity of pastoral farming; reliance on Khoekhoe as efficient herdsmen.

- **Market Dynamics**:
  - Transport issues hindered agriculturalists; trekboers benefited from relatively low market dependency through subsistence practices.

#### White Expansion into the Cape Interior

- **Lack of VOC Policy on Expansion**:
  - VOC opposed territorial expansion; increasing settlement was unplanned.

- **Population Growth**:
  - Post-1707 self-generated white population growth; strong family structures supported expansion.

- **Stock Farming Influence**:
  - Early agriculturalist expansion followed by stock farming; seasonal grazing concessions led to permanent cattle posts inland.

- **Land Allocation**:
  - Original land grants inadequate; subsequent grazing permits and loan farms spurred rapid settlement into interior regions.

- **Hunting Influence**:
  - Trekboers utilized advanced hunting technology, depleting game and facilitating movement into new areas.

- **Societal Changes**:
  - Breakdown of Khoekhoe society led to vacant lands; many retreated, inadvertently guiding white farmers.

- **Environmental Factors**:
  - Natural geographical barriers limited, conducive climate fostered movement.

- **Psychological Factors**:
  - Desire for independence from VOC and resistance to manual labor; individualism prevalent among trekboers.

- **Resistance from Indigenous Groups**:
  - Khoekhoe resistance through raiding; San groups posed challenges in certain regions.

- **Limits to Expansion**:
  - Increasing pressure on land due to population growth; necessity for market structures and resources limited trekboers' long-term viability.

#### Trekboer Lifestyle Characteristics

- **Economic Conditions**:
  - Reliance on hunting and pastoralism with minimal trade.
  - Self-sufficiency in food and resources; little incentive for market engagement.

- **Social Dynamics**:
  - Isolation resulted in cultural limitations; lack of communal institutions like schools and churches.
  - Material culture simplistic due to mobility; no establishment of skilled trades such as carpentry.

- **Market Engagement**:
  - Varied levels of market integration; those distant from Cape Town less integrated into the economic system.

## Chapter 3: Social and Political Structures in the 18th Century

### 3.1 Domestic Arrangements
- Simple dwellings: single room for multiple purposes (sleeping, living, cooking, livestock enclosure).
- Trekboers (specifically eenlopendes) resided in wagons or reed shelters.

### 3.2 Political Life
- Isolation led to individualism, rebellion against authority.
- Intolerance among trekboers towards control over relations with indigenous peoples and laborers.
- Increasing distance from Cape Town diminished direct governmental control.
  
### 3.3 Government Influence
- Local government agents (landdrosts and heemraden) emerged post-1740s; limited presence.
- Insufficient military power diminished VOC's (Dutch East India Company) authority.
  
### 3.4 Economic Decline
- 1780s-1790s: Decreased VOC authority and economic influence in the southwestern Cape.
- Increased conflict between trekboers and indigenous peoples over land competition.
  
### 3.5 Law and Order
- Trekboers took law into their own hands due to weak controls.
- Dependency on Cape administration for arms and supplies.
- VOC attempts to control trekboer independence generally resisted.
- Discomfort with trekboer actions against Khoekhoe, such as livestock plunder.

### 3.6 Rebellions against VOC Authority
- Notable rebellions: Estienne Barbier (1738-1739) and Adriaen van Jaarsveld (1793). 
- Trekboers often operated independently during the 18th century.

### 3.7 Conclusion
- Late 18th century: Trekboer expansion impeded by environmental challenges and competition (e.g., Xhosa in the east).
- Further exploration of trekboers' fortunes and interactions in Learning Units 6 and 7.
