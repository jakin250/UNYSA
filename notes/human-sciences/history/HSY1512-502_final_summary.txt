### LEARNING UNIT 4: KHOESAN COMMUNITIES AND THE IMPACT OF COLONIALISM

- **Khoesan Overview:**
  - **Definition:** Khoesan includes Khoekhoe (herding societies) and San (hunter-gatherers).
  - **Revival Movement:** Contemporary Khoesan groups use the term to identify themselves.
  - **Historical Groupings:** Five main groups; San and Cape Khoekhoe (early), Nama, Koranna, Griqua (colonial period).

#### 4.1 The Khoesan before the advent of Europeans
- **Economic Life:**
  - **Nomadic Lifestyle:** Hunter-gatherers and herders; practised transhumant pastoralism.
  - **Cattle:** Main source of wealth; valued for milk, used in trade and warfare.
  - **Hunting:** Utilised bows and traps; seafood important for coastal groups.
  - **Trade:** Engaged in trade with Bantu-speaking groups and Europeans; sought valuable iron and copper.

- **Social and Cultural Life:**
  - **Community Structure:** Groups ranged from 20 to several families; overseen by a headman.
  - **Fluid Membership:** Members would shift between groups.
  - **Conflict & Cooperation:** Common livestock raiding; myth and ritual important for cultural expression.
  - **Religion:** Worshipped Tsui-//Goab; rites of passage crucial (birth, marriage, death).

#### 4.2 Early encounters between Khoesan and Europeans, 1488–1652
- **Initial Contact:**
  - **1488:** Bartholomeu Dias' crew encounters Khoesan; limited interaction.
  - **1497:** Vasco da Gama’s visit ends with skirmishes.
  - **Impression of Violence:** Khoesan perceived as violent; were defending land and delayed colonization.

- **Increasing Interaction:**
  - **16th Century:** Limited contact; strategic geographic location led to increased European interest.
  - **1590s Onward:** Trading intensified, Khoesan traded cattle for tobacco, iron.
  - **Economics of Trade:** Relaxed interactions as Khoesan believed sailors were trading rather than settling.

## 4.2 Early Khoesan Contact with European Traders

- **Trading with Khoekhoe Communities**:
  - 1590s: Dutch sailors bartered iron for livestock from Khoekhoe.
  - Trading boom ended in 1610 due to hyperinflation in the iron market.
  - Khoesan evolved into savvy traders, controlling trading terms.
  
- **Key Figures**:
  - **Coree**: Captured by English in an attempt to influence Khoesan.
    - Refused to cooperate; ended up opposing the English upon return.

  - **Autshumato (Harry)**: Collaborated with Dutch.
    - A link between Khoesan and European traders; proficient in Dutch and English.

- **Significant Event - Shipwreck of Nieuwe Haerlem (1647)**:
  - Stranded sailors lived off bartering; led to positive initial contact with Khoesan.
  - **Remonstrantie Report (1649)**: Advocated for a VOC refreshment station.
  - Resulted in permanent Dutch settlement in 1652, viewed as invasion by Khoesan.

---

## 4.3 Expansion of Dutch Settlement (1652–1720)

- **Jan van Riebeeck's Leadership (1652–1662)**:
  - Established a refreshment station; aimed at maintaining friendly relations with Khoekhoe.

- **Land Allocation (1657)**:
  - Surprised Khoesan; marked a shift to permanent settlement.
  - Encroachment led to Khoesan withdrawal from livestock trade and increased theft.

- **Khoe-Dutch Conflict**:
  - **First Khoe-Dutch War (1659)**: Khoesan guerilla tactics initiated armed conflict.
  - 1660: Khoesan sought peace; claimed land rights denied by Dutch.

- **Second Khoe-Dutch War (1673–1677)**:
  - Gonnema's leadership led to Khoesan resistance being suppressed.
  - Resulted in greater Dutch land acquisition with minimal resistance.

- **Consequences of Warfare**:
  - Political disempowerment of Khoesan leaders; decreased independence.
  - Cultural breakdown due to loss of livestock and land.

---

## 4.4 Khoesan Experience in the 18th Century

- **Dispossession and Labor**:
  - By 1720, Khoesan communities became laborers after land loss.
  - Labor relationships underscored economic and social inequality.

- **Conditions on Farms**:
  - Poor living conditions; often treated worse than slaves.
  - Frequent violence and exploitation; legal rights existed but were ineffectual.

- **Indentured Labor System**:
  - **Inboekstelsel**: Instituted to compel offspring of Khoesan-slaves to work until 25.
  - Similar to slavery; laborers faced exploitation without escape to freedom.

- **Resistance Against Conditions**:
  - Strategies included desertion, sabotage, and armed resistance.
  - Example: Jan Paerl's rebellion in 1788 against dispossession.

- **Cultural Alienation and Alcoholism**:
  - Loss of traditional practices led to increased alcoholism.
  - Alcohol used as payment exacerbated dependence.

- **Continued Resistance and Struggles**:
  - Khoesan resistance persisted into the late 18th century.
  - Notable uprisings (e.g., 1793 and 1799) against mistreatment and economic grievances.

## 4.5 The Khoesan in Contemporary South Africa 
- **Cultural Integration**: Difficulty in identifying 'pure' Khoesan due to intermarriages; descendants advocate for recognition.
- **Cultural Preservation**: Museums, cultural organizations, and universities are actively preserving Khoesan heritage.
- **Legacy of Colonialism**: Acknowledgment of dispossession of land and destruction of culture during colonial and apartheid eras.
- **Constitutional Recognition**: Constitution of 1996 refers to 'Khoe' and 'San,' but does not explicitly recognize them as indigenous peoples.
- **Cultural Visibility**: 
  - Rock paintings and matjieshuise signify Khoesan identity.
  - Khoesan languages influenced Xhosa, but risk of extinction persists.
- **Modern Groups**: Emerging groups like Nama and Griqua seek land rights and cultural recognition in South Africa, Namibia, and Botswana.
- **Self-Identification**: Preference for identifiers such as Griqua or Nama over 'Coloured'; ongoing struggles for rights and services.

## 4.6 Conclusion
- **Impact of Colonial Expansion**: Traditional Khoesan communities lost livestock and land, leading to the loss of autonomy and influence.
- **Assimilation into Labour Force**: Khoesan assimilated as laborers, facing challenging conditions; they exhibited political will against colonial forces, underpinning future African nationalism.

### 5.1 Slavery and Bonded Labour
- **Global Context**: Slavery is a historical phenomenon, integral to various economies; most societies have experienced forms of bonded labor.
- **African Context**: African bonded labor (clientship, serfdom) differed from European slavery, offering some rights and access to kinship.
- **Modern Slavery**: Introduction of slavery marked extensive transfers of people due to European expansion; key features include:
  - Slaves' role in economic production.
  - Legal definitions of slaves as property, devoid of rights.
  - Importation of outsiders as slaves, severing community ties.

### 5.2 The Origins and Importation of Cape Slaves
- **Dutch East India Company (VOC) Policy**: Labor shortages at Cape led to reliance on slavery; reasons include:
  - Need for amicable relations with Khoesan for trade.
  - Khoesan's refusal to adopt colonial labor practices.
  - VOC's cost-saving measures opposed to wage labor.
- **Economic Logic**: Slavery was aligned with Dutch colonial practices; offered white landowners opportunity for economic advantage and social stability.
- **Supply Challenges**: Demand for slaves exceeded supply; VOC's reliance on imports was marked by challenges, including competition from other traders and high mortality rates during voyages.

### Methods of Importation
- VOC imports using their ships for projects.
- Freeburghers obtaining slaves from foreign traders.
- Individual transactions from travelers selling personal slaves.
- Internal sales from deceased/ bankrupt farmers’ estates.

### 5.3 The Role of Slaves in the Cape Economy

- **Slave Demography**
  - Distribution of slaves varied across regions and sectors:
    - Cape Town: One-third owned by **VOC**, balance privately owned.
    - Southwestern Cape: Dominant in agricultural roles (wine, wheat).
    - Interior: Limited number on pastoral farms.
  - Slave ownership concentrated in agricultural areas; wealthy citizens owned multiple (e.g., Martin Melck - 204 slaves across 11 properties).
  - Early 19th-century shift due to **British occupation of 1806** leading to:
    - Expansion of agriculture and demand for slaves.
    - Decline in urban slave density, more evenly distributed inland.
    - Increased growth of slave numbers in the Eastern Cape post-1820.

- **Economic Function of Slaves**
  - VOC Company slaves involved in various administrative roles (warehouses, workshops).
  - Privately owned slaves: domestic roles, artisans, hawkers.
  - Agricultural tasks included seasonal work, maintenance, and general farm duties.
  - Trekboers relied on fewer slaves; more suited to using Khoesan labor.

- **Economic Impact of Slavery**
  - Key in maintaining viability of VOC and agricultural expansion.
  - Conditions could lead to economic depression due to labor shortages.
  - Dependency on slavery hindered technological advancement and investment in diversification.
  - Slaves served as an economic commodity, security for loans, and protection against inflation.
  - Slavery created leisure for some whites but increased unemployment amongst them due to population growth.

### 5.4 Slave Life at the Cape

- **Legal Status**
  - Slaves viewed as property; subject to harsh treatment.
  - Prices influenced by labor potential, age, health, etc.
  - Legal rights obscure; charges for murder rare.
  - Difficulties in accessing courts and legal systems; owners rarely penalized compared to slaves.

- **Lack of Human Rights**
  - No property ownership, marriage rights, or legal contracts for slaves.
  - Family structures existed but stability increased only after 1808 due to cessation of external slave trade.
  
- **Control of Slaves**
  - Two layers of control: master-slave dynamics and VOC enforcement.
  - Common use of corporal punishment; threats of sale used as a psychological control.
  - Paternalistic perceptions (Robert Shell), although physical coercion remained prevalent.
  
- **Methods of Control**
  - Corporal punishment common; severe discipline led to maximizing profits.
  - VOC courts imposed harsh penalties for slave offenses.
  - Various laws (curfews, passes) restricted slave freedoms.
  - Hierarchies within slave communities (e.g., interpreters, mandoors) managed control and compliance.

## 5.4 Slave Life in Cape Town

- **Hierarchical Structure**:
  - Slave women supervised by matron (Cape-born, mixed origin).
  - Distinction between domestic and field workers; domestic slaves had closer bonds with masters.
  - Larger farms had knechts/slave mandoors supervising.

- **Living Conditions**:
  - Urban slaves (Cape Town) enjoyed better living conditions, living within owner households.
  - Rural slaves faced harsher conditions; work hours could reach up to 15 hours/day.
  - Direct supervision by owners or knechts; sexual exploitation common despite legal prohibitions.

- **Food & Resources**:
  - Rations and provisions varied significantly; some supplementation possible through farm produce.

## 5.5 Education and Religion

- **Access to Education**:
  - Limited, with a school in the VOC lodge established in 1685 for loyalty and Dutch fluency.
  - Majority of rural slave children received no education; literacy rates low.

- **Religious Influence**:
  - Christianity spread slowly; average of 20 slaves baptized/year until mission efforts in the 19th century.
  - Islam gained traction in the 18th century; offered support networks and cultural identity post-1800.

## 5.6 Family Life

- **Challenges in Family Development**:
  - Legal prohibitions on marriage; demographic imbalances hindered partnerships.
  - Lack of privacy and economic stability; children born into slavery, with potential separation from mothers before 1782.

- **Social Consequences**:
  - Familial instability led to violence, tension, and various forms of sexual outlet.

## 5.7 Manumission, Miscegenation, and Free Blacks

- **Manumission**:
  - Low rates noted; 1,075 slaves manumitted (1715-1791).
  - Restrictions imposed by VOC; required approvals, financial responsibility post-manumission.

- **Miscegenation**:
  - Relationships predominantly outside marriage; offspring absorbed into slave class.
  - Lack of substantial free black community due to poverty and social restrictions.

## 5.8 Afrikaan Language Development

- **Role of Slaves**:
  - Significant influence on the emergence of Afrikaans; initially a creole for communication among slaves.
  - Evolved with inputs from Malay, Arabic, Khoesan, and European languages.

## 5.9 Slave Resistance

- **Nature of Resistance**:
  - Localized, uncoordinated manifestations; included escape, sabotage, and violence.
  - Varied by region; rural slaves often faced harsher conditions than urban counterparts.

- **Absence of Organized Revolt**:
  - Attempts in 1808 and 1825 were suppressed.
  - Factors impeding unity: diverse origins, language barriers, absence of common cultural tradition.

## 5.10 Impact of Slavery on Social Order

- **Racial Divisions**:
  - Created a sense of white superiority and distaste for manual labor.
  - Impact on indigenous communities through indentured systems akin to slavery.

- **Class Distinctions**:
  - Blurred boundaries; even poor whites viewed as superior to slaves.
  - Slavery reinforced racial hierarchies and stereotypes within society. 

This summary condenses the key points of slave life at the Cape, emphasizing the social structure, living conditions, and resistance movements while referencing essential legislation and contextualizing the environment surrounding slavery in South Africa's historical setting.

### 6.1 The Concept of a Frontier

- **Frontier/Frontier zone**: Area of contact between different peoples, not merely a boundary line. 
- Interaction determined by environmental limits: climate, terrain, vegetation.
- Societies in a frontier zone initially distinct; fluid relationships and diverse experiences.
- **Open phase**: Initial contact, trade cooperation or conflict, power vacuums.
- **Closing phase**: One group establishes undisputed control, relationship dynamics regulated but not static. 
- Historical ‘frontier’ usage in South African context traditionally applies to outer VOC colony areas.
- Example: Characteristics of the southwestern Cape's frontier development over time, ending with Dutch supremacy post-1713 smallpox epidemic.

---

### 6.2 Environmental Features of the Northern Frontier

- Region: North of white settlement in the south-western Cape, from Stellenbosch to Gordonia, including Hantam, Roggeveld, Little Namaqualand, Bushmanland, and Transorangia.
- Characterized by harsh, arid conditions; low rainfall, high evaporation; no permanent rivers except Gariep.
- Underdeveloped boundaries; weak control by colonial authorities, shaped by indigenous inhabitants adopting adaptive survival strategies.
- Environmental aridity resulted in limited vegetation; reliance on small herds and hunting.

---

### 6.3 The Inhabitants of the Northern Frontier

- Weak colonial authority presence from Cape Town; ineffective law enforcement.
- Four changes of colonial powers (VOC, British, Batavians) disrupted consistent policy.
- Social dynamics driven by resident capabilities, resource availability, and trade control (especially firearms).
  
#### **Khoesan Peoples**
- **San**: Hunter-gatherers; resisted settlers fiercely.
- **Nama**: Pastoralists along Gariep, hunting and gathering, mobile for resource exploitation.
- **Korana**: Herders along middle Gariep; trade with Cape influenced interactions with other groups.
- **Einiqua**: Occupied middle Gariep between Nama and Korana.
- Fluid ethnic boundaries; inter-group trade, alliances, and intermarriage blurred distinctions.

## 6.4 Interaction between the Inhabitants of the Northern Frontier

- **Fluid Relations**: 
  - Struggle for survival affected relationships; cooperation and conflict coexisted.
  - Access to scarce resources and control of trade led to alliances and attacks.

- **Role of Firearms**: 
  - Firearms essential for hunting, raiding; created a cycle of impoverishment and power shifts.
  - Resource acquisition conferred power, with stronger groups gaining followers and influence.

### Older Communities: Contestation and Consolidation

- **San Resistance**:
  - Resisted intrusions from pastoralists and trekboers since 1700.
  - White settlers’ access to firearms led to conflicts and decline in wildlife population.
  - Commandos formed to attack San, resulting in significant casualties and displacement.

- **Cooperation Between Groups**: 
  - San sometimes cooperated with pastoralists (Nama, BaTlhaping) to share resources.
  - Many San joined or allied with Baster and Oorlam groups due to violence.

- **Impact on Nama and Korana**:
  - Nama retreated north, aligning with Baster and Oorlam groups for protection.
  - Korana increased cooperation with BaTlhaping to resist hostile groups, merging through intermarriage.

### New Communities: Oorlam Groups

- **Afrikaner Oorlams**:
  - Known for raiding; aligned with Petrus Pienaar until conflict prompted migration.
  - Established strong base along the Gariep; integrated impoverished groups.
  - Relationships with missionaries evolved from tension to alliance, aiding in trade integration.
  - Established influence shifted to Namibia, forming critical economic and military links.

- **Witbooi Group**:
  - Led by Kido Witbooi; established ties with LMS missionaries for trade security.
  - Became significant power in Namibia during the 19th century.

### New Communities: The Griqua

- **Formation and Identity**:
  - Griqua ancestry traced to Khoekhoe and trekboers; emerged from displacement.
  - Adam Kok and family became influential by attracting followers and exploiting resources.
  
- **Economic Development**:
  - Wealth through elephant hunting and trade with BaTlhaping.
  - Partnerships with missionaries bolstered community structures and independence.

- **Cultural Integration**:
  - Christianity fundamental to Griqua identity; aided in consolidation against colonial pressures.
  - Community's name change from “Baster” to “Griqua” symbolized transformation and heritage recognition.

## 7.3 Interaction on the frontier: the late VOC period (1770s to 1795)
- **European Presence Pre-1770s**: Explorers, traders, and hunters entered eastern territories; significant migration began post-1700.
- **Increase in Settlements**: Land allocation systems of the VOC facilitated rapid European settlement, increasing land occupied tenfold.
- **Frontiersmen Characteristics**: Isolated, self-sufficient, individualistic farmers focused on cattle ranching; vulnerability led to aggression influenced by racial and religious prejudices.
- **Impact on Khoesan & Xhosa**:
  - **Khoesan**: Decline due to hunting pressures and firearms; forced relocation due to conflict.
  - **Khoekhoe**: Land dispossession and social structure destruction; led to employment with settlers for survival but often exploitative.
- **Xhosa Encounters**: 
  - Economic competition for land and resources; trade occasionally fostered cooperation.
  - Conflicts resulted from competition over limited grazing lands, notably in Zuurveld.
  - **First Frontier War (1779-1781)**: Sparked by cattle theft; resulted in severe losses for both sides and no significant territorial gains.
- **VOC Response**: Ineffective governance and temporary boundary adjustments; increasing tensions due to VOC's financial struggles and inability to enforce policies.

## 7.4 Interaction on the frontier: the period of the first British occupation (1795 to 1803)
- **Occupation Context**: Britain occupied the Cape due to European wars; priority on external interests, not local development.
- **Tensions Resurface (1797)**: Xhosa re-established chiefdoms; conflicts led to the Third Frontier War (1799-1802).
- **Khoekhoe Rebellion**: Alliance with the Xhosa, driven by dissatisfaction under white frontiersmen, disrupted frontier economic activities.

## 7.5 Interaction on the frontier: the Batavian period (1803 to 1806)
- **Governance Shift**: Batavian administration focused on establishing stable boundaries at the Fish River while limiting Xhosa rights.
- **Policy of Conciliation**: Aimed to de-escalate tensions; removal of Khoekhoe from Xhosa influence and settlement programs initiated.
- **Resource Limitations**: Limited military presence hindered efforts to control border movements adequately.

## 7.6 Interaction on the frontier: the second British occupation (1806 onwards)
- **British Colonial Policy Post-1806**: Centralized administration focused on enforcing law and order; reduction in conciliatory efforts.
- **Conflict Resolution**: 
  - **Fourth Frontier War (1811-1812)**: Military strategy to expel Xhosa led to significant violence and land devastation.
  - British dominance temporarily stabilized relationships along the frontier, closing it on their terms.

## 7.7 Conclusion
- **Significance of Eastern Frontier Interactions**: Many conflicts shaped Southern Africa's historical landscape; Xhosa resistance to encroachment and the resulting military commitments affected colonial governance.
- **Long-term Consequences**: Frontier conflicts precipitated the Great Trek into the interior, further transforming regional dynamics and colonial relations.
