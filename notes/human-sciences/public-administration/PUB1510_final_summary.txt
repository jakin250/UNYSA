### 1.1 Introduction
- Challenges in the workplace influenced by South Africa's rapid social and political changes.
- Importance of self-management for effective management of others.
- Need for understanding personality, behaviour, and self-concept.

### 1.2 Self-management Defined
- **Timm (1987)**: Self-management maximizes time/talents to achieve goals based on values.
- **Sues (2015)**: Behavioral attributes related to managing personal/work lives.
- Keywords:
  - **Process**: Continuous management.
  - **Time and Talents**: Unique personal resources.
  - **Worthwhile Goals**: Rooted in a sound value system.
  - **Value System**: Critical for effective self-management.
  - **Behaviour**: Personal traits influencing conduct.

### 1.3 Understanding Your Self-concept
- Definition of **personality**: Formed by internal/external factors; impacts management capability.
- Definition of **self-concept** (Neill, 2005): Total understanding of self (spiritual, emotional, social, physical) based on knowledge of oneself and surroundings.

#### 1.3.1 Self-acceptance
- Acknowledgment of strengths/weaknesses vital for growth (Rainbow 2001/2002).
  - **Uniqueness**: Embrace individual expression for opportunities.
  - **Right to Achieve**: Pursuit of a valued, meaningful life.
  - **Appreciate Who You Are**: Honesty promotes realistic goal-setting.
  - **Improve Self-image**: Positive reinforcement enhances life approach (Rainbow; Seleme, 2015).

#### 1.3.2 Self-awareness
- **Values**: Personal beliefs/goals (status, money, knowledge).
- **Abilities**: Natural talents (verbal, numerical, artistic).
- **Skills**: Developed abilities (writing, public speaking).
- **Interests**: Enjoyable activities (sports, reading).
- **Personality**: Character traits (friendly, sensitive).
- **Self-esteem**: Self-perception shaped by experiences.
- **Needs**: Important aspects (security, recognition, achievement).
- **Behaviour**: Self-discipline and responsibility.

### 1.4 What Determines Your Self-concept?
- Influenced by **environment** (societal reactions) and **inner self** (intellectual, emotional capabilities).
- Positive reinforcement from others strengthens self-image.
- Importance of a positive self-view on personal behaviour and workplace contributions.

### 2.1 Introduction
- Importance of self-management includes time management.
- Time allocation for various activities (work, study, social life, sleep) emphasizes its value as a scarce resource.
- Necessity of efficient and effective time management to do well in personal and professional realms.

### 2.2 Key Concepts of Time Management
- Time is a limited resource: 24 hours = 86,400 seconds per day.
- Importance of personal time management to improve professional conduct and life efficiency.
  
#### Activities Breakdown
- Personal Work Contributions (Weekly Total: 89 hours)
  - Sleep: 49 hours
  - Eat: 14 hours
  - Dress: 7 hours
  - Commute: 5 hours
  - Chores: 14 hours
- Working Hours: 40-50
- Family/Leisure: 30-40
- **Total Weekly Hours**: 168 

(Figure 2.1 illustrates activities spent time on; source: Ferner 1995:5)

### 2.2.1 Common Timewasters 
**(Mathews, 2018)** lists 10 timewasters and avoidance strategies:
1. **Smartphones**: Minimize distractions by keeping devices away.
2. **Multitasking**: Focus on one task at a time for efficiency.
3. **Noisy Environments**: Utilize cubicles or headphones for concentration.
4. **Workplace Clutter**: Keep a tidy and organized workspace (digital and physical).
5. **Nutritional Habits**: Healthy eating and hydration to maintain energy levels.
6. **Procrastination**: Start with small tasks to build momentum.
7. **Social Networks**: Block distracting sites during focus time.
8. **Excessive Meetings**: Prioritize attendance, avoid unnecessary meetings.
9. **Decision Fatigue**: Limit decisions by reducing daily choices.
10. **Email Overload**: Set dedicated times to manage emails.

### 2.2.2 What Does Time Management Mean?
- **Definition**: Organizing, planning, and allocating time effectively (Mind Tools, 2019).
- Links to self-management and involves skills like planning, organizing, implementing, and controlling (Ferner, 1995:11).
  
#### Differences between Efficiency and Effectiveness
- **Efficiency**: Doing things right.
- **Effectiveness**: Doing the right things.

#### Important Issues in Time Management
- **Commitment**: Overcoming poor habits.
- **Analysis**: Understanding time usage and identifying problem areas through tools like time logs.
- **Planning**: Essential for structured time management; ultimately saves more time and resources.

### 4.6 Meetings

#### 4.6.1 Notification and Validity of a Meeting
- **Notification Timing**: Must occur a reasonable time before a meeting, as per constitution.
- **Invalidation**: A meeting can be invalidated if notice is not given in accordance with rules.
- **Content of Notification**:
  - Nature of the meeting
  - Venue, date, and time
  - Purpose of the meeting if no agenda is provided
  - Include:
    - Institution name
    - Signatory details
    - Contact information
    - Date sent
- Notice can be informal or formal. The secretary is responsible for sending notices.
  
#### 4.6.1.2 Quorum
- **Definition**: Minimum number of voting members required for valid decisions.
- **Constitutional Requirement**: Typically one more than half the total members.
- **Quorum Principles**:
  - Valid decisions need a quorum.
  - If absent, decisions are invalid.
  - Secretary must record the presence of a quorum.
  - Chair cannot unilaterally declare a quorum present.

#### 4.6.1.3 The Agenda
- **Purpose**:
  - Ensures all matters are discussed.
  - Maintains discussion order.
  - Prepares members for the meeting.
- **Management**:
  - Secretary and chair prepare the agenda.
  - Members can propose additional items before the meeting.

#### 4.6.2 The Course of the Meeting
- **Procedural Principles**:
  - Orderliness: One subject at a time.
  - Equality: Equal consideration for all members.
  - Fairness: Courtesy and impartiality.
  - Rights of the minority and majority.

#### 4.6.2.1 The Opening
- **Quorum Check**: Secretary confirms quorum.
- **Agenda Reading**: Chair presents agenda, with permission required for changes.

#### 4.6.2.2 The Attendance Register
- **Registration**: Members sign to confirm attendance; helps with minutes preparation.

#### 4.6.2.3 The Minutes
- **Reading**: Minutes are read or considered read.
- **Acceptance**: Chair seeks a proposal to accept minutes; can include amendments.

#### 4.6.2.4 Approval of the Agenda
- **Modification**: Can only be adapted with permission from the meeting.

#### 4.6.2.5 Correspondence
- **Reading**: Secretary reads any relevant correspondence.

#### 4.6.2.6 Motions and Resolutions
- **Definition**: Proposals presented for consideration; accepted proposals become resolutions.
- **Structural Guidelines**:
  - Must be positively stated, e.g., "That the fundraising campaign be abolished."
  - Begin with “that”; seconding is mandatory.
  - Only one motion discussed at a time.
  - Ownership of motions lies with the meeting.
  - Withdrawals require meeting approval.

##### Amendments
- Purpose: Alter original motion while preserving intent.
- If an amendment is rejected, the original remains. If accepted, it becomes a substantive motion and should be in writing.

## 4.6.2.5 Voting Procedures
- **Amendments**: Only one amendment discussed at a time; chair summarizes and words amendment before voting.
  
### (b) Reply to the Motion
- Proposer responds to opposition with no new arguments.
- Right to reply extends to the seconder upon request.

### (c) Voting
- Chair determines if motion supported—if not, call proper vote.
  - **Show of Hands**: Default method.
  - **Secret Vote**: Used when public vote may influence personal convictions.
- Votes counted by chair or nominated member.
- **Tie**: Results in rejection unless chair exercises deciding vote.
- Results announced with vote counts.
- Motion’s results cannot be revisited post-vote.
- Members must vote; abstention rare; exceptional circumstances only.
- Voting verification for complex/multiple motions, e.g.:
  - Motion tabled for donation amounts with subsequent amendments.
  
### (d) Procuration
- Legal document granting proxy authority; permitted if constitutionally allowed.

## 4.6.2.7 Reports
- Members and committee report compilation addressed in section 6.3.

## 4.6.2.8 Dismissal

## 4.6.3 Reporting
- **Minutes**: Confidential record detailing meeting decisions, written by the secretary.

### (a) Content of Minutes
- Include date, time, place, attendees, motions, decisions, votes, reports, and important arrangements.

### (b) Purpose of Minutes
- Refresh members' memory, authorize matters, compile organizational history.
- Minutes focus on subjects discussed, not verbatim speaker statements.

### (c) Characteristics of Good Minutes
- Exact reflection of proceedings and decisions.
- Clear, concise, objective.
- Complete enough for absent members to understand the session's outcome.
- Written in present tense (Afrikaans) or past tense (English).

### (d) Minute Information
- Exact wording of decisions, main arguments, contract details, appointments, and instructions recorded.

### (e) Minute Writing Suggestions
- Utilize scribblers, key words, and gather explanations when needed.
- Compile promptly post-meeting; assess if minutes provide a full picture for absentees.

### (f) Pre-Circulation Advantages
- Increased accuracy, quicker action on instructions, improved meeting prep.

## 4.7 Conclusion
- Meetings involve discussion and decision-making among multiple participants; different types require specific conditions (location, agenda, reporting).
- Essential for information sharing and collaboration in organizations.

### 5.2 Everyone Needs Communication Skills

### 5.3 Definition of Communication
- **Definition**: Sharing information, ideas, feelings through spoken/written words and body language.
- **Frost, Vos and Dreyer (1993)**: Communication as generating meaning within a cultural context.
- **Key Principle**: Extend acceptance, admiration, and respect to effectively communicate.
- Necessary attitude shifts include:
  - Seeing people as perfect.
  - Stopping attempts to change others.
  - Avoiding judgment.
  - Enjoying individuals as they are.

### 5.4 The Elements of Communication
- **Sender-Receivers**: Both parties communicate simultaneously, sharing meaning and understanding.
- **Messages**: Ideas/feelings represented by symbols; includes verbal (words) and nonverbal (expressions, gestures).
- **Channels**: Routes through which messages travel; includes sound and sight in face-to-face and media channels.
- **Feedback**: Responses indicating whether messages were accurately understood.
- **Noise**: Interference affecting message interpretation; includes:
  - External noise (environmental factors).
  - Internal noise (personal distractions).
  - Semantic noise (language barriers).
- **Setting**: The environment which impacts communication effectiveness; influences formality and intimacy.

### 5.5 Types of Communication
- **5.5.1 Intrapersonal Communication**
  - Occurs within an individual; involves personal thought and feelings.
  
- **5.5.2 Interpersonal Communication**
  - One-on-one basis; informal setting; involves all elements of communication.

- **5.5.3 Small Group Communication**
  - Involves small numbers to solve problems; structured messages; effective feedback opportunities.

- **5.5.4 Computer-Mediated Communication**
  - Includes technologies like emails and chats facilitating communication and information sharing.

- **5.5.5 Public Communication**
  - Speaker sends a structured message to an audience; utilizes exaggerated communication methods; limited feedback.

### 5.6 Verbal and Nonverbal Communication

#### 5.6.1 Verbal Communication
- Communication through language, learning identity and surroundings.
- Types: Spoken (interviews, broadcasts) and written (letters, books).
- Code used: Language; words lack physical resemblance to what they denote (e.g., traffic signs).
- Language manipulates surroundings, influences others, creates realities.
- Effective language use is crucial in professional communication.

#### 5.6.2 Nonverbal Communication
- Encompasses all non-language communicative means, divided into:
  - Body-related (body language, facial expressions, eye contact, touch).
  - Environmental cues (time, space, silence).
- Can convey richer messages than verbal communication.
- Often occurs alongside verbal cues to enhance meaning.

#### 5.6.3 Comparison of Verbal and Nonverbal Communication
- Verbal and nonverbal communication are interactive and essential.
- Verbal communication relays information in language forms but can be supplemented by nonverbal cues (e.g., tone, expressions).
- Nonverbal cues often reflect true emotions when verbal messages conflict, showing their power.

#### 5.6.4 Direct vs. Indirect Communication
- Verbal communication conveys direct messages; nonverbal delivers indirect cues.
- Cultural variations exist in interpreting nonverbal messages, yet basic emotions (e.g., happiness, anger) have common significances.
- Indirect information through nonverbal communication can be more impactful than verbal language.

### 5.7 Listening Skills
- "Our first responsibility as effective listeners is to understand ourselves." – Carolyn Coakley.
- Listening is crucial for effective communication, often a neglected skill.
- Distinction: Hearing (passive) vs. Listening (active comprehension).
- Many adults listen with only 25% efficiency; poor listening hampers communication.

#### 5.7.1 Misconceptions about Listening
- **Listening is not my problem!** – People often see others as poorer listeners.
- **Listening and hearing are the same** – Hearing is passive; listening involves interpreting meaning.
- **Good readers are good listeners** – Good reading doesn’t guarantee good listening skills.
- **Smarter people are better listeners** – Intelligence aids listening but is not a definitive factor.
- **Listening improves with age** – Performance may decline despite increased ability due to learned habits.

#### 5.7.2 Types of Listening
- **Informative Listening**: Focus on understanding the message; vital in learning environments and workplaces.
- **Relationship Listening**: Emphasizes understanding others; therapeutic forms include counseling.
- **Appreciative Listening**: Enjoyment-oriented (e.g., music, speaker style).
- **Critical Listening**: Necessary for informed decision-making; involves evaluation and judgment.
- **Discriminative Listening**: Recognizes subtle differences in communication, enhancing understanding.

#### Sources of Difficulty
- **By the Speaker**:
  - Volume too low.
  - Message complexity.
  - Disorganized delivery.
  - Conflicting body language.
  - Unconventional message delivery.

- **By the Listener**:
  - Distraction or preoccupation.
  - Listening only for openings to respond.
  - Formulating rebuttals instead of engaging.
  - Personal biases affecting perception.
  - Lack of clarification when confused.

### 5.8 Barriers to Communication
- **Definition**: Factors hampering message flow from sender to audience.
- **Categories of Barriers**: 
  - **Perceptual Barriers**: 
    - Unique nature of perception; influenced by cultural background, past experiences, and education.
  - **Language Barriers**: 
    - Use of jargon can confuse; jargon is acceptable only among same-field communicators.
  - **Physical Barriers**: 
    - Environmental factors affecting communication include noise, uncomfortable settings, and untidy documents.
  - **Physiological Barriers**: 
    - Internal issues like headaches, hunger, or fatigue impair concentration.
  - **Psychological Barriers**: 
    - Mental state impacts communication; boredom, embarrassment, and stress interfere with understanding.

### 5.9 Telephone Communication
- **Effective Communication**: 
  - Excellent telephone manners are crucial for administrative roles.
  - Calls can create good or poor impressions based on tone and demeanor.
  
- **Advantages of Telephone**:
  - Time-efficient and cost-effective.
  - Reduces need for meetings and physical visits.
  
- **Disadvantages of Telephone**:
  - Ambiguity between official and personal calls.
  - Difficulties in managing conversations.
  
- **Voicemail**:
  - Defined as a personal answering system; involves voice prompts and message storage mechanisms.

- **Guidelines for Taking Telephone Calls**:
  - **Receiving Calls**:
    - Improve voice qualities; display alertness and courtesy.
  - **Making Calls**:
    - Plan and state purpose clearly.
  - **Answering Calls for Others**:
    - Identify yourself and obtain necessary information.
  - **Dealing with Complaints**:
    - Listen actively, maintain composure, and follow through on actions.
