## 3.2 Definition of a state
1. **Political Community**: An organized entity under one government.
2. **Citizenship**: Inhabitants form citizens of a specific state.
3. **Organization for Law**: People organized to uphold laws within a territory.
4. **Juridical Requirements**: Must have territory, population, government, and sovereignty.
5. **Civic Order**: Represents the collective power for social order and development.

- **Characteristics**: Territory, population, government, sovereignty.
- **Citizens**: Permanent residents with specific rights; guests from other states have limited rights.

---

### Forms of State
- **Unitary**: Centralized government (e.g., Britain, France).
- **Federal**: Shared power between central and regional authorities (e.g., USA, Germany).
- **South Africa**: Federal with strong unitary elements.

    - Growth milestones:

- Causes of population growth: 
  - Advances in medicine → lower infant mortality.
  - Increased life expectancy (1900 vs. 2000): 
    - Men: from 46 to 72 
    - Women: from 48 to 79.

### 9.3 Yearly Population Growth Rate

- Urbanisation leads to:
  - Smaller families.
  - Transition from traditional to modern values.
  - Decreasing growth rate despite overall increase in population.
  - Greater needs for healthcare and welfare.
  
### 9.4 Relationships Among Concepts
- Urbanisation is correlated with:
  - Smaller family sizes,
  - Shift in societal values,
  - Changing growth rates,
  - Age composition trending older.

### 9.5 Implications for Public Services
- Increased urbanization and an ageing populace demand:
  - Enhanced healthcare systems.

## History of the Social Contract
- **Philosophers**: Thomas Hobbes, John Locke, Jean-Jacques Rousseau (16th-18th centuries).
   - Hobbes: *The Leviathan* (1651) – dark view of the natural state (war).
   - Locke: *Two Treatises of Government* (1690) – protective civil community.
   - Rousseau: *The Social Contract* (1762) – happiness in the natural state; issues arose later.
- **Core Needs for Social Contract**:
   - Freedom
   - Property
   - Lives
   - Hobbes: Need for release from the state of war.

## Essence of the Social Contract
- **Hobbes**: Surrender individual authority to a sovereign.
- **Locke**: Creation of civil community for protection.
- **Rousseau**: Association of individuals to solve collective issues.

## Parties to the Social Contract
- **Hobbes**: Individuals only; government not a party.
- **Locke**: Society and government both part of the contract.
- **Rousseau**: Emphasizes individual and community roles.

## Consequences of the Social Contract
- Agreement on surrendering individual authority for communal authority.
  
## Relationship of Authority
- **Hobbes**: Society subservient to sovereign.
- **Locke**: Equal level of sovereign and society.
- **Rousseau**: Sovereign subservient to society.

## Theoretical Perspectives
- No uniform theory; used to explain state formation and inter-relationships among societal roles.
- Metaphorical explanation: Social contract theory elucidates state origin and role-player relationships.

## Conclusion
- Governments provide public services as a result of the social contract dictating services beyond individual capabilities.

#### 11.2 Providers of Public Services
- **Service providers:**
  - Private sector (includes community institutions)
  - State
  - Joint action of both

- **Private Sector: Individual Services**
  - Recipient pays directly.
  - Services priced per unit.
  - Non-paying members may be excluded.
  - Services are exhaustible.

- **State: Collective Services**
  - Recipients do not necessarily pay.
  - Cannot be sold per unit; value is not quantifiable.
  - Non-paying members cannot be excluded.
  - Non-exhaustible; funded via taxes.
  - Monopolistic provider.

- **Joint Provision: Quasi-Collective Services**
  - Characteristics of both collective and individual services.

#### Public-Private Partnership (PPP)
- Inkosi Albert Luthuli Central Hospital: example of PPP.
  - Government's responsibility to provide affordable, quality healthcare.
  - Outsourcing of non-core services to the private sector.
  - Aims: efficiency, quality improvement, risk transfer.
  - Requires budgetary agreements for service provision, ensuring no costs for patients.

- Service provision can be categorized into private, public, and joint actions.

## 12.2.1 Can society itself provide the services?

## 12.2.2 What is the purpose of the services?
- **Purpose of the State**: Meet community's material and spiritual needs.
- **Private Sector’s Purpose**: Maximize profit for owners and shareholders.
- Fundamental purposes of public service provision:
  - Meet community needs equitably.
  - Deliver services irrespective of financial loss.
- **Criterion of Purpose**: Distinguishes between profit motive and equitable service provision.

## 12.2.3 Is a competitive free market necessary for the service?
- Private sector operates competitively to determine market prices.
- Public sector lacks self-regulation; government not in competition with itself.
- Maintain competitive market:
  - Assess necessity of free-market mechanism.
  - Determine threats to free-market from government service provision.
- Government's aim: Ensure equal access to services without competition with private sector.

## 12.2.4 How comparative is the service to be provided?
- **Criterion of Comprehensiveness**: Scope and expertise required for service provision.
- Government involvement in enterprises (e.g., **Sanral, Sasol, South African Airways**) due to market incapacity.
  - Establishing essential services may exceed private sector capabilities and funding.

## 12.2.5 To whom does the service or rule apply?
- Membership in voluntary organizations: rules applicable only to members.
- State rules: compulsory for all individuals within the jurisdiction.
- **Criterion of Membership**: Relevant when enforcing rules through public services.
- Example: Immunization laws enforced by the government to protect public health.
