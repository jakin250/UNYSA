# Law Study Notes

## Chapter 1: Forms of Government

### 1.1 Parliamentary vs. Presidential Democracy
- **Parliamentary Democracy**
  - Executive (Prime Minister + Cabinet) is part of the legislature (House of Commons and House of Lords).
  - Characterized by:
    - Overlapping of functionaries 
    - Formal sovereignty resides with Parliament
    - Example: Britain
    - Cabinet can be removed by a motion of no confidence (Ranney 1993:242).
  
- **Presidential Democracy**
  - Distinct separation of executive and legislative branches.
  - Characterized by:
    - Separation of functionaries
    - Checks and balances across legislature, executive, and judiciary.
    - Example: USA

### 1.2 South African Context
- South Africa's Constitution (1996) will clarify its form of democracy:
  - Chapter 4: Parliament (Legislature)
  - Chapter 5: President and National Executive
  - Chapter 8: Courts and Judiciary

### 1.3 Three Categories of Government Authority
- **Legislative Authority**
  - Established by the Constitution, divided into:
    - National sphere: Parliament
    - Provincial sphere: Provincial Legislatures
    - Local sphere: Municipal Councils

- **Composition and Legislative Power**
  - Parliament has legislative power over national affairs.
  - Provincial legislatures have authority within their provinces.
  - Municipal councils handle local affairs.
  - Overlap exists where Parliament and provincial legislatures can affect municipal matters.

### 1.4 Legislative Powers in South Africa
- **National Assembly**
  - Powers include:
    - Amending the Constitution
    - Legislation on matters in Schedule 4 (excluding Schedule 5)
    - Assigning legislative powers to other bodies (Section 44).
  
- **National Council of Provinces**
  - Participates in constitutional amendments.
  - Legislation on Schedule 4 matters and considers National Assembly legislation (Section 76).

- **Provincial Legislature**
  - Powers include:
    - Amending provincial constitutions
    - Legislation on Schedule 4 and 5 matters (as per Section 104).
  
- **Municipal Councils**
  - Authority to create by-laws for local governance (Section 156). 

### Legislative Authority Summary (Figure 1.1)
- National: Parliament
- Provincial: Provincial Legislatures
- Local: Municipal Councils
- Each sphere has jurisdiction limits, ensuring no overlap in legislative authority among provinces and municipalities. 

### Conclusion
- Understanding government authority in South Africa requires a focus on the Constitution and its framework of governance, highlighting the specific powers and limitations of each legislative entity.

  - **Executive Authority**: Vested in the Premier and Executive Council.

  - **Executive Authority**: Vested in the Municipal Council.

- **Jurisdictions**: 
  1. Execute national legislation

- **Jurisdictions**: 
  - Execute provincial legislation
  - Implement relevant national legislation
  - Administer national statutes assigned to provincial executives
  - Coordinate provincial administration
  - Draft/initiate provincial legislation

# Study Unit: Three Different Spheres of Government

## Introduction
- Provision of public services organized through tripartite division of government authority:
  - **National**
  - **Provincial**
  - **Local**

## Public Service Provision at the Different Spheres of Government
### National Sphere of Government
- Executive powers to provide public services depend on legislative powers.
- **Constitutional Reference**:
  - **Section 85**: President and Cabinet implement national legislation, develop policy, coordinate state functions, prepare legislation.
  - **Section 44**: Parliament holds legislative authority, can amend Constitution, pass legislation on various matters excluding exclusive provincial powers (Schedule 5).
- National executive implements policy and legislation, may intervene if provinces fail to fulfill obligations (**Section 100(1)**).

### Provincial Sphere of Government
- Provinces have jurisdiction as structured in **Schedules 4 and 5** of the Constitution.
- Powers listed in Schedules:
  - **Schedule 4**: Concurrent jurisdiction areas (e.g., health, education):
    - Part A: Housing, health services, agriculture, etc.
    - Part B: Local affairs (building regulations, fire services).
  - **Schedule 5**: Exclusive provincial jurisdiction areas (e.g., ambulance services, provincial roads).
- National executive may delegate responsibilities to provincial executive committees or municipalities (**Section 99 and 126**).

### Local Sphere of Government
- Local governance through municipalities as per constitutional provisions.
- Municipal council holds executive and legislative powers (**Section 151**).
- Local government categories:
  - **Category A**: Metropolitan municipalities (8)
  - **Category B**: Local municipalities (226)
  - **Category C**: District municipalities (44).
- Responsibilities include local services (e.g., tourism, health, public transport).

## Aims of Local Government
- **Constitutional Objectives** (**Section 152(1)**):
  - Democratic and accountable governance.
  - Service provision in a sustainable manner.
  - Promote social and economic development.
  - Ensure a safe and healthy environment.
  - Encourage community involvement in local matters.

# Study Notes on Local Government Services and Responsibility

## Chapter 2: Government Responsibilities and Public Services

### 2.3 Provision of Services by Government Spheres
- Local government is positioned to provide services like refuse removal more effectively than provincial or national governments.
- **Constitution Reference**: Section 152(1) outlines aims of local governments.
- Sphere of government responsibility linked to **shared powers** outlined in **Schedules 4 and 5** of the Constitution.

### 2.4 Rule of Subsidiarity
- **Definition**: National or provincial governments should not provide services that can be adequately handled by local institutions (Loxton 1993:80).
- Local institutions are preferred providers for services due to proximity to community needs.
- This decentralization ensures those closest to the need are responsible for solutions.

### Principles Underlying Assignment of Responsibilities
- **Nine Principles** (Robson 2006:327):
  - Principle of national unity
  - Principle of economic unity
  - Principle of equality
  - Principle of cooperation
  - Principle of provincial autonomy
  - Principle of self-determination
  - Principle of exclusivity
  - Principle of asymmetry
- **Research Findings**: Limited scientific methods applied in the assignment of responsibilities (Robson 2006:349).

### 2.5 Cooperative Government Concepts
- **Constitution Reference**: Section 41(1)(h) emphasizes mutual cooperation among government spheres.
- **German Basic Law Extract**: Articles 91a and 91b indicate joint responsibilities and cooperation between federal and state levels.
- **Essence of South Africa’s Constitution**: Highlights cooperation, mutual trust, and the need for consultation among governmental levels.
- **Comparison to German Law**: Both emphasize collaboration for improving living conditions.
- **Cooperative Government Defined**: Fundamental characteristic of governance promoting quality of life through teamwork across spheres.

### Summary
- Public services in South Africa fall under various government spheres, with responsibilities being exclusive or shared.
- Effective service provision is linked to the closest government sphere, adhering to the **subsidiarity principle**. 
- Interdependence among spheres established for adequate and effective service delivery, enhancing overall community wellbeing.

# Study Unit: Types of Public Institutions

## Introduction
- Provision of public services organized within tripartite division of government authority: national, provincial, local.
- Focus on institutions delivering services, e.g., traffic officers and departments.
  
## A Typology of Public Institutions
### 3.1 Public Service Organizations
- Institutions categorized into groups based on operational criteria.
- Two main groups:
  - National, provincial, and municipal departments.
  - Parastatal institutions (quasi-autonomous or public entities).

#### Parastatal Institutions
- Define: Institutions existing alongside state departments; “para” denotes alongside or parallel.
  
### 3.2 Departments (National/Provincial/Municipal)
- Includes all administrations and departments across government spheres.
- Political structure:
  - National: E.g., Department of Health led by Minister.
  - Provincial: E.g., Gauteng Department of Education led by MEC.
  - Local: E.g., Departments like Culture and Recreation with municipal manager overseeing.

#### Establishment/Abolishment of Departments
- Decided by the President with the Minister for Public Service and Administration or respective provincial Premier.
- Conditions for establishing a new department:
  - New portfolio necessitating a department.
  - Review requiring split of existing department.
  - Merging of multiple departments after review.

#### Common Operational Characteristics
- Operate within a political milieu under direct supervision of political office bearers.
- Services focus on public interest; accountable for actions.

### 3.3 Parastatal Institutions
- Defined as institutions not falling under government department strict supervision.
- Commonly function autonomously with more management freedom.
  
#### Purpose of Parastatal Institutions
- Address strategic, social, or economic intervention needs.
- Employ commercial principles in service delivery.
- Ensure operational autonomy while retaining accountability.

### 3.4 Categorization of Parastatal Institutions
- Government Business Enterprises and other entities.
- Loxton’s nine categories of public entities:
  - Advisory
  - Regulatory
  - Judiciary
  - Tertiary education
  - Research and development
  - Cultural/environmental management
  - Commercial/industrial enterprises
  - Social services
  - Constitutional support

#### 3.4.1 Advisory Institutions
- Established due to complex government tasks; provide specialized advice for policy implementation.
- Examples:
  - Advisory Council for Universities and Technikons
  - National Planning Commission

#### 3.4.2 Regulatory Institutions
- Significant role in ensuring compliance with regulations.
- Examples:
  - South African Council for the Architectural Profession (SACAP)
  - National Energy Regulator of South Africa

# Study Notes

## 3.4 Regulatory Institutions
- **Examples**:
  - South African Council for the Architectural Profession (SACAP)
  - National Energy Regulator of South Africa
  - South African Bureau of Standards
  - South African National Accreditation System
  - Mine Health and Safety Council

- **Government Regulation Characteristics**:
  - Specialized fields (e.g., South African Reserve Bank).
  - Restricts actions (e.g., Independent Communications Authority).
  - Acts in the interest of public welfare.
  - Aligns with accepted principles (e.g., professional standards).

- **Rationale for Parastatal Institutions**:
  - Perform better than state departments due to specialized focus.
  - Closer to regulated entities, leading to self-regulation.
  - Representation of various interests (e.g., SACAP includes architects).

## 3.4.3 Judiciary Institutions
- **Key Institutions**:
  - **Special Income Tax Court**: Adjudicates taxpayer appeals against SARS decisions.
  - **Industrial Courts and Labour Appeal Courts**: Resolve employer-employee disputes.
  - **Medicines Control Council**: Evaluates safety and effectiveness of medicines.

- **Classification**:
  - Legislative
  - Executive
  - **Judiciary**: All institutions classified under judicial authority.

- **Relation to Executive Authority**:
  - Special Income Tax Court links to enabling function.
  - Industrial Courts focus on industrial peace.
  - Medicines Control Council aligns with health protection.

- **Separation Necessity**:
  - Parastatal institutions better in specialized fields than general courts.
  - Impartiality in justice administration requires separation from executive departments.

## 3.4.4 Institutions for Tertiary Education
- **Context**:
  - Higher education institutions (e.g., Unisa) function independently of direct state provision.
  
- **Aims of Higher Education**:
  - Knowledge promotion via teaching and research.
  - Community involvement.
  
- **Rationale Against State Provision**:
  - Need for autonomy and academic freedom in education (Lovejoy, 1953).
  - Specialized governance structures (Loxton, 1992).
  - Institutions are governed by councils and responsible for their staffing.

## 3.4.5 Research and Development Institutions
- **Examples**:
  - Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC)
  - Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR)
  - National Research Foundation (NRF)

- **Common Features**:
  - Focus on private goods and services.
  - Operate within a free market.
  - Need for flexible structures versus state departments.
  
- **Operational Characteristics**:
  - Established via acts of parliament.
  - Governed by boards comprising experts.
  - Funded by governmental grants and commercial activities.

## 3.4.6 Cultural and Environmental Management Institutions
- **Cultural Institutional Framework**:
  - Established per the Cultural Institutions Bill of 1998.
  
- **Examples**:
  - South African National Parks Board (SANPARK)
  - National Botanical Institute of South Africa
  - South African Cultural History Museum

- **Characteristics**:
  - Must accommodate South Africa's multi-ethnic and cultural diversity.

    - **Quadrant I**: Important and urgent activities (Crises, pressing problems).
    - **Quadrant II**: Important but not urgent activities (Prevention, relationship building).
    - **Quadrant III**: Not important but urgent activities (Interruptions).
    - **Quadrant IV**: Not important and not urgent (Time wasters).

- Quadrant II activities are ideal for purposeful management.
- Continuous engagement in urgent matters can lead to loss of initiative and purpose.

- Definition: Achievement of desired aims (completion of right tasks).
- Factors for Right Task:
  - Type of Service
  - Timeframe (e.g., 10 years)
  - Scope of Service (all South Africans)
- Thornhill (1995): Measurement criteria depend on the nature of activities and demands from those requiring measurements.
- Importance of Annual Reporting on Effectiveness.
- Example: Canada emphasizes public reporting on institutional effectiveness (Van Rooyen 1991).

#### 9.3.2 Efficiency  
- Definition: Use of right methods to achieve tasks (Van Niekerk 1978).
- Interaction: Efficiency combines effectiveness and economy (Botes 1991).
- Key Points:
  - Institutions can be effective but inefficient.
  - Efficiency considers the means to achieve goals.
- Measuring Efficiency: Reporting on public entities as per **Reporting by Public Entities Act 93 of 1992**.

#### 9.3.3 Economy  
- Relationship: While efficiency and economy are distinct, one expects efficient services to be economical.
- Criterion of Public Affordability: Evaluates if the government can afford specific services.
- Example: High-technology medicine is efficient but may not be economical when compared to basic health care.

### 9.4 Public Accountability  
- Importance of accountability for public service effectiveness, efficiency, and economy.
- Principle from Magna Carta: No taxes levied without Parliamentary approval tied to public service funding.
- Conclusions:
  - Taxes fund public services.
  - Population representatives must approve funded services.
  - Representatives need sufficient information for informed decision-making and accountability.
  - Public officials are accountable for use of public money.

### 9.4 Public Accountability

- **Definition and Importance**
  - Public accountability is defined as the obligation of public officials to publicly answer for the responsibilities conferred upon them (Cloete, 1996; Loots, 1991).
  - Essential for preventing misuse of public institutions for non-public welfare purposes.
  - Requires external checks to identify improper actions (Briloff, 1967).

- **Roles and Responsibilities**
  - Public officials handle public funds on behalf of society, necessitating a position of trust and accountability.
  - In South Africa, directors-general of state departments are accountable to Parliament, with the Auditor-General tasked with auditing public institutions and reporting to Parliament.

- **Accountability Framework**  
  - **Legislation and Oversight**
    - **Constitutional provisions:** Define the accountability mechanisms in public service.
    - **Auditor-General:** Responsible for audit reports submitted to Parliament, facilitating accountability.

### 9.5 Assessing Public Service Effectiveness

- **Evaluation Criteria**
  - Public service effectiveness measured through: 
    - **Economy:** Use of resources.
    - **Efficiency:** Output versus input ratio.
    - **Effectiveness:** Achieving intended outcomes.

# Study Notes on Enforceable State Authority

## Chapter 11: Authority of the State

### 11.1 Introduction to Enforceable Authority
- **Enforceable Authority**: The state's legitimate power to regulate behavior and enforce laws.
- **Legitimacy**: General acceptance by society that government power must be obeyed (p. 171).
  
#### Key Concepts:
- State vs. Institutional Authority: 
  - Unisa's rules apply only to its students; state laws apply to all citizens.
  - Government can impose penalties for non-compliance, such as imprisonment for tax evasion.
- **Authority of State**: Consists of enforceable rules from various government departments.

### 11.2 Government Enforcement of Rules
- **Examples of Regulation**:
  - **Tax Legislation**: Regulates income.
  - **Financial Control**: Regulated by the Department of Finance.
  - **Education Laws**: Enforces school attendance.
  - **Traffic Regulations**: Governs road usage.

### 11.3 Legitimacy of State Authority
#### 11.3.1 The Constitution
- The **1996 Constitution**: Considered the supreme law, providing legitimacy to government actions.
- **Quotations from the Constitution**:
  - Supremacy of the Constitution (p. 174).
  - Legislative authority bound by the Constitution.

#### Implications:
- All government branches must act within constitutional limits.
- Exercise of power by institutions must comply with constitutional provisions.

### 11.3.2 Political Legitimacy
- The government's power is only legitimate if it meets public needs and maintains national survival.
- Governments must balance political demands and enforce public policies to maintain legitimacy.
  
### 11.4 Ensuring Legitimacy in a Democracy
- **Legitimacy Mechanisms**:
  - Regular elections for legislature ensuring accountability.
  - Direct elections of executives in some democracies reinforce legitimacy.
- **Social Contract**: Power conferred upon the government by society to be utilized responsibly (p. 175).

### 11.5 Conclusion on Government Powers
- Essential for the government to possess authority to perform functions and enforce laws [yes, government needs powers].

  - Government has a lawful monopoly on violence.

## Protection against the abuse of power

### Introduction
- Governments possess legitimate power.
- Distinction between government and the private sector includes the monopoly on the death penalty.
- South Africa's instruments of power include forces that may be abused.

### Can the government abuse its power?
- Government poses threats to freedoms.
- A balance must be struck between maintaining order and preventing tyranny.
- **Legislation/Case Reference**: South African Constitution prohibits arbitrary exercise of power (Sections related to rights of life and freedom).

### Can the government be prevented from abusing its power?
- Social contract theory: People surrender some rights for protection of property and rights.
- John Locke's philosophy advocates the right to overthrow governments failing to protect rights.
- Mechanisms include:
  - Democratic processes (elections).
  - Awareness of historical abuses to safeguard current rights.
- **Legislation Reference**: Chapter 9 of the South African Constitution establishes institutions to support constitutional democracy, which help restrain government power.
- Required elements for a tolerable state:
  - Good Constitution (with Bill of Rights).
  - Democratic institutions with separation of powers.
  - Effective laws and governance.
  - Informed public participation and media access.
