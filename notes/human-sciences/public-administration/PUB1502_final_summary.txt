### 1.2 Local Government Pre-1994
- Local government is essential for effective service delivery.
- **Apartheid's legacy** significantly affected communities, warranting urgent reform.
- **Local Government Negotiating Forum** established in 1990 leading to:
  - **Agreement on Finance and Services.**
  - **Local Government Transition Act, 1993** outlined phases for reform:
    - Pre-interim: Local forums for temporary councils.
    - Interim: Post-democratic elections.
    - Final: New local government system establishment.
- The **1996 Constitution** established local government as a distinct sphere of government with primary service delivery responsibilities.
- **White Paper on Local Government, 1998** defined a developmental local government system for sustainable human settlements.

### 1.3 Reconstruction and Development Programme (RDP)
1. Issued by the ANC in 1994; not a blueprint, rather a policy framework.
2. **14 policy statements** crucial for local government restructuring.
3. Emphasizes local government as the nearest form of democracy, requiring a democratic, non-racial, non-sexist structure.
4. Existing local government laws to be amended or replaced with uniform national legislation.
5. Amalgamation of **800 segregated municipalities** into approximately **300 non-racial municipalities**.
6. Demarcation must include informal settlements within municipal boundaries.
7. Elected governments with service delivery responsibilities to extend into rural areas.
8. Calls for a strong metropolitan government for urban integration.
9. Municipalities to operate under a unified tax base.
10. New municipalities need service network restoration while addressing affordability through tariff structures.
11. Municipalities to receive intergovernmental transfers for service delivery backlog resolution.
12. Existing administrations should be reorganized in consultation with stakeholders.

- **Spheres of Government**: National, provincial, local; distinct yet interdependent.

- **Section 151**: Municipalities established for entire territory; councils hold executive and legislative authority.

- **Section 153**: Developmental duties prioritize community needs and participation in development programs.
- **Section 154**: Cooperative government emphasizing support for municipalities.
- **Section 155**: Categorization of municipalities and fiscal responsibilities; provincial roles in establishing and supporting municipalities.
- **Section 156**: Rights of municipalities to administer local matters; by-laws made on local governance must comply with national/provincial laws.

- **Section 160**: Councils establish rules for their proceedings; must be open unless justified.
- **Section 162**: By-laws enforced post-publication in provincial gazette; must be publicly accessible.

# Study Notes on Local Government Framework

## Chapter 1: Principles of Financial Management

### Principle 4: Cooperative Government
- Promotes cooperative fiscal management across government sectors.
- Encourages links with other government spheres and state organs.
- Aims to improve service delivery through mutual support and communication.

### Principle 5: Promoting Sustainability
- Establishes basis for effective financial management for service delivery.
- Municipal councils must adopt realistic budgets and account for operational costs.
- Strong internal control systems required for efficiency.
- Regular financial reporting to council and public for accountability.
- Emphasis on oversight, transparency, and community engagement by councillors.

---

## Chapter 7 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa

### Status of Municipalities (Section 151)
- Local government consists of municipalities serving the whole Republic.
- Authority rests with the Municipal Council.
- Municipal self-governance is subject to national/provincial law.
- National/provincial governments cannot hinder municipal powers.

### Objects of Local Government (Section 152)
1. Democratic and accountable governance.
2. Sustainable service provision.
3. Promotion of social and economic development.
4. Safe and healthy environment.
5. Community involvement in local governance.

### Developmental Duties of Municipalities (Section 153)
- Prioritize basic community needs and social/economic development.
- Participation in development programs at national/provincial levels.

### Municipalities in Cooperative Government (Section 154)
- National/provincial governments must support municipal capacity.
- Draft legislations affecting local government must allow for public comment.

### Establishment of Municipalities (Section 155)
1. Categories:
   - A: Exclusive authority.
   - B: Shared authority with category C.
   - C: Authority over multiple municipalities.
2. Criteria for categories and boundaries defined by national legislation.
3. Provincial legislation specifies municipality types within provinces.

### Powers and Functions of Municipalities (Section 156)
- Executive authority over local matters in Schedules 4 and 5.
- Right to create by-laws for administration.
- Conflicts between by-laws and legislation resolved under validity rules.

### Composition and Election of Municipal Councils (Section 157)
1. Municipal councils consist of:
   - Elected members.
   - Appointed members as provided by national legislation.
2. Election process must adhere to proportional representation.

### Membership of Municipal Councils (Section 158)
- Voting citizens are eligible, except those receiving remuneration from government service or disqualified by legislation.

### Terms of Municipal Councils (Section 159)
- Maximum term of four years as set by national law.

### Internal Procedures (Section 160)
1. Councils decisively elect chairpersons and may form committees.
2. Certain powers (by-laws, budgets, taxes) cannot be delegated.
3. Majority presence and vote rules for decision-making.
4. Public participation in by-law processes required.

### Privilege (Section 161)
- Provincial legislation to establish privileges for councils and members.

### Publication of Municipal By-Laws (Section 162)
1. By-laws enforceable only post-publication in official gazette.
2. Public access to by-laws mandated.

### Organised Local Government (Section 163)
- National/provincial legislation to recognize local government organizations and facilitate communication with governmental bodies.

### Other Matters (Section 164)
- Unspecified local government matters governed by national or provincial legislation within its framework.

## 1.7 WHAT IS INTEGRATED DEVELOPMENT PLANNING?

- **Definition**: Integrated Development Planning (IDP) – method mandated for local municipalities in South Africa to plan future development.
  
- **Context**: 
  - Legacy of **Apartheid planning** led to:
    - Racially divided areas.
    - Poor service access for the marginalized.
    - Underdeveloped rural areas.
  
- **Approach**: Involves municipalities and citizens collaboratively to find sustainable development solutions.

- **Purpose of IDP**:
  - Serves as a comprehensive plan for coordinated local development.
  - Aims to improve quality of life through:
    - Evaluation of existing conditions.
    - Economic and social development.
    - Land use, infrastructure, and environmental protection.

- **Legislative Requirement**: All municipalities must create an IDP, which guides planning and must align with annual budgets.

- **Process Duration**: IDP development takes 6-9 months, with annual reviews and a 5-year lifespan linked to council terms.

- **Management**: 
  - Executive mayors or committees oversee the IDP, often appointing an IDP coordinator.

### Legal and Constitutional Framework
- The IDP necessitates stakeholder consultation and final approval by the municipal council.

### Importance of IDP
- **Six Reasons for IDP**:
  - **Resource Allocation**: Ensures effective use of limited resources.
  - **Delivery Acceleration**: Identifies areas needing urgent attention.
  - **Funding Attraction**: Clear development plans attract investment.
  - **Democratic Engagement**: Facilitates stakeholder participation in decision-making.
  - **Addressing Apartheid Legacy**: Aims to integrate communities and extend services.
  - **Co-ordination Across Government Levels**: Ensures synergy in addressing local needs.

### Stakeholders in the IDP Process
- **Municipality**: Guides local development.
- **Councillors**: Make decisions reflecting constituency needs.
- **Communities**: Participate in prioritizing local needs.
- **National/Provincial Departments**: Align resources with local government plans.

## What is the IDP process?
- **IDP Process Plan**: Outlines management structures, public participation, timelines, responsibilities, and monitoring.
  
### IDP Production Phases
1. **Phase 1: Analysis**
   - Collect existing condition data and prioritize issues.
   - Outcomes: Development assessments and resource availability.

2. **Phase 2: Strategies**
   - Develop long-term vision and medium-term objectives.
   - Identify projects and strategies for achieving objectives.

3. **Phase 3: Projects**
   - Design project specifics: beneficiaries, costs, funding, duration, and management.
   - Establish performance targets and indicators.

4. **Phase 4: Integration**
   - Ensure alignment of projects with objectives.
   - Integrate broader development strategies (e.g., poverty alleviation).

5. **Phase 5: Approval**
   - Present IDP to council for adoption, may include public commentary.

## 1.2 District Municipality Role
- Coordinates planning and consolidation of IDPs and budgets for local municipalities.

### 2.3 Service Delivery Options

#### 2.3.1.1 Corporatisation
- **Definition**: Establishment of a corporatised entity for service provision.
- **Applications**: Public utilities (electricity, water); metropolitan councils for expansive jurisdictions.

- **Forms**: Specific service contracts, competitive tendering.
- **Quality Assurance**: Importance of comprehensive tender evaluations, contract specifications, and performance monitoring.

#### 2.3.1.5 Leases and Concessions
- **Comparison to Contracting**: Longer contract durations (20-30 years), contractor manages assets, takes on investment risks.

# 3.2 THE MUNICIPAL ACCOUNTING CYCLE

- **Financial Year**: 
  - Duration: 1 July to 30 June.
  - Potential change to align with public sector financial year (1 April to 31 March).

- **Accounting Cycle**:
  - Starts: 1 July.
  - Administers approved operating and capital budgets.
  - Monitored monthly for revenue and expenses alignment.
  
- **Budget Preparation**:
  - Timeline: November to February for the ensuing financial year.
  - Revision of current budgets for large municipalities.
  - Approval: Typically in May, effective from 1 July.
  
- **Annual Closure**:
  - Books closed on 30 June, followed by preparation of annual financial statements.
  - Statements signed by end of September (delayed if Auditor-General's approval pending).
  - Post-audit: Submitted to municipality council for debate from February onwards.

- **Key Publications**:
  - Approved capital and operating budgets.
  - Annual financial statements.

- **GAMAP**:
  - Framework for accurate financial transaction recording.
  - Goal: Build information for annual financial statements.
  - Most significant document: Annual financial statements.

- **Components of Financial Statements**:
  - Balance sheet, income statement, cash flow statement, notes, appendices, special reports.

### 1. Assets
- Assets may flow to a municipality from service use, either individually or collectively.
- Creation of assets does not always require incurring expenses (e.g., donations).

### 2. Liabilities
- Defined as a present obligation of the municipality due to past events.
- Obligation may arise from contracts, statutory requirements, practice, or customs.
- Must exist by 30 June and expected to result in resource outflow.
- Future commitments do not equate to present obligations; obligation arises with asset delivery or irrevocable agreements.
- Liabilities can be extinguished by creating another liability (e.g., refinancing loans).
- Common liabilities: trade payables for goods/services received, loans repayable.

### 3. Own Capital
- Residual interest in assets after deducting liabilities at balance sheet date.
- Comprises capital funds, reserves, unappropriated surpluses; can be negative (accumulated deficit).

### 4. Revenues (Income)
- Defined as ordinary income and gains from regular municipal activities (e.g., property rates, service charges).
- Revenue results in economic benefit increases, own capital surplus.
- Gains are increases from asset disposals, must be shown as revenue (contrasts previous IMFO approach).

### 5. Expenses
- Defined as decreases in economic benefits during the financial year.
- Ordinary expenses: interest on loans, costs for services, employee salaries, asset depreciation.
- Losses also meet expense definition: disposal of fixed assets or losses from extraordinary events.

### 6. The Double Account System
- Distinguishes between capital and operating accounts, separate expense/revenue classifications.
- Future GAMAP statements will clarify distinctions for financial practitioners and external users.
- Operating account transactions summarized in income statements.
- Example: asset funded by loan shows liabilities and capital expenditures distinctly.

### 7. Accounting for Fixed Assets
- Fixed assets recognized when probable future benefits arise from measurable value/cost.
- Capitalization at cost, depreciated over estimated useful lives (straight-line method).
- Depreciation reflects asset consumption during service provision.
- Must adhere to nationally prepared schedules for asset lifespans to ensure comparability and consistency.

### 8. The Financing of Capital Expenses
- Municipalities finance fixed asset creations via own resources (e.g., operational revenue) or repayable loans.
- External finance should be registered in the external finance fund (EFF), which requires consistent repayment.
- Lack of external funding may necessitate financing from operational revenues into an asset financing fund (AFF).
- Surplus revenues appropriated for capital may represent taxation in advance but should align with community development plans.

### 9. Summary
- GAMAP aligns municipal accounting with international practices.
- Accruals accounting reflects true financial states.
- Financial management is crucial for anticipating risks and maintaining growth in municipal jurisdictions.

# Study Notes

## 4.7 Risk Management in Municipalities

### 4.7.1 Identifying All Risks
- Managers must continuously identify risks as part of performance agreements.
- Individual managers accountable for their areas; CFO, internal auditor, and audit committee involved in broader risk identification.

### 4.7.2 Assessing Risks
- Assess risks by probability of occurrence and negative impact on business objectives.
- Material risks are those with high probability and substantial negative impacts.
- Non-compliance with legal requirements is considered unacceptable.

### 4.7.3 Managing and Mitigating Risks
- Develop policies to manage risks: segregation of duties, transaction authorization, monitoring, reporting.
- Different types of risks require tailored management strategies.
- Aim to eliminate risks by changing procedures; embed internal controls in daily activities. 
- Costs of controls should not exceed expected benefits.

### 4.7.4 Monitoring Risks and Risk Controls
- Managers must continuously monitor risk controls.
- Review controls if ineffective; risk management steering committee oversees implementation.
- Internal audit examines overall risk management effectiveness through occasional probes.

### 4.7.5 Reporting Risks
- Immediate reporting of identified risks to internal audit is essential.
- Internal audit assesses causes, probabilities, and consequences of risks.
- Compliance assessed via regular reports; documents management processes of key risks, including:
  - Physical and operational risks
  - Human resource risks
  - Technology risks
  - Business continuity and disaster recovery
  - Financial risks
  - Compliance risks

### 4.7.6 Risk Management Plans
- Risk management plans should detail:
  - Identified risks
  - Planned controls
  - Responsible persons
  - Monitoring methods
  - Control effectiveness
- Plans can be stored electronically.

### 4.7.7 Annual Risk Analysis
- Treasury Regulations mandate annual review of the risk environment by accounting officers.
- Annual assessments by Stats SA management before financial year-end to align goals with appropriated funds.

## 4.8 Internal Control

### 4.8.1 Definition
- Internal control is a management process providing reasonable assurance of:
  - Operational effectiveness and efficiency
  - Reliance on financial reporting
  - Compliance with laws and regulations.

### 4.8.2 Role of the Internal Audit Function
- Internal audit ensures control environment adequacy and assists in improving risk processes.
- Must be independent and report to the audit committee, uninhibited by restrictions.

### 4.8.3 Systems of Internal Control
- Necessary to safeguard assets and ensure compliance with duties.
- Internal controls aim to:
  - Safeguard income and assets
  - Maintain reliable accounting records
  - Ensure legal compliance
  - Promote operational efficiency.

### 4.8.4 Responsibility for Internal Controls
- Management holds responsibility for implementing internal controls.
- Internal audit assists with monitoring and recommending improvements.

### 4.8.5 Categories of Controls
- Types of internal controls:
  - Preventative: Before incidents
  - Detective: After incidents
  - Corrective: Address identified problems
  - Directive: Encourage acceptable behavior
  - Compensating: Mitigate control weaknesses.

#### Categories of Management, Administrative, and Accounting Controls
- **Management Controls**: Frameworks enabling objective achievement.
- **Administrative Controls**: Procedures guiding authorized activities.
- **Accounting Controls**: Safeguard assets and ensure reliable financial records.
