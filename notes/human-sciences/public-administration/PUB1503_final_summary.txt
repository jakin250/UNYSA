# PUB1503/1: Municipal Taxes: Property Rates

## 1.1 Introduction
- Property rates as a crucial municipal revenue source.
- Differentiation between property rates and consumption-based services (water/electricity).
- Essential dimensions of property rates revenue include fairness and equity.

## 1.2 Legislative Authority for Levying Rates
- **Section 229** of the Constitution: Municipalities can impose property rates, surcharges, and other taxes (excluding income tax, VAT, customs duty).
- **Section 11(3)(i)** of the Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000: Municipalities exercise authority by imposing rates and service fees.
- Taxation power limited to property rates for metropolitan and local municipalities; district municipalities lack taxation power.
- Historical authority derived from the Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993 and existing rating ordinances until Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004.

## 1.3 Background Perspective
- Terms: Property rates = Property tax = Assessment rates = Rates.
- Property rates represent the primary source of unencumbered revenue for municipalities.
- Potential comparison with gross revenue from electricity undertakings, which may exceed property rates but are encumbered.
- Assessed value of fixed property used as a tax base, often lower than market value, established by professional valuers.

## 1.4 Justification for a Municipal Tax
- Many municipal services are uneconomic and cannot be financed solely through trading profits.
- Increasing service charges can lead to reduced consumption, harming community quality of life and municipal revenue.
- Reliance on loans or government grants poses risks and could diminish local autonomy.
- Municipal property tax satisfies local communal needs effectively.

## 1.5 The Equity of Local Rates
- Two theoretical frameworks: Benefits Received Theory and Ability to Pay Theory.
  - **Benefits Received Theory**: Local ratepayers pay a rate proportional to the services received; challenges due to collective nature of services.
  - **Ability to Pay Theory**: Tax equity based on taxpayer wealth; modern realities complicate the relationship between property value and ability to pay.
- Practical considerations lead to blended application of both theories in tax equity discussions. 
- Notably, many benefitting from municipal services may not be ratepayers (e.g., non-residents, visitors).

## 1.6 The Canons of Taxation
### 1.6.1 The Generally Agreed Canons
- Canonical principles for effective taxation (fairness, efficiency, certainty, convenience, economy) must apply to property rates.

### Fixed Property and Wealth Distribution
- Poor individuals' fixed property represents a greater portion of unrealised wealth compared to affluent individuals.
- **Tax Burden on Low Income vs. High Income**: Low-income residents often taxed on a higher percentage of actual wealth (e.g., 90%) compared to affluent residents (e.g., 16-17%).
- **Concept of Putative Wealth**: Taxation based on market value, often subject to mortgages.

### Impact of Rising Property Values
- Increased property values can create financial stress for long-term residents, leading to “taxed out” situations.

### Business Property Taxation
- **Ability-to-Pay Principle**: Not strictly applied; small profitable businesses may pay lower rates compared to larger businesses with lower profit margins.
- **Sliding-Scale Rating**: Reduced rates for large residential properties conflict with ability-to-pay principles.

## Page 7
### Regressive Nature of Property Rates
- Regarded as a regressive tax, disproportionately affecting lower-income earners akin to personal income and VAT taxes.
- **Differential Rating & Rate Remissions**: Enhance tax fairness.

### Burden-to-Community Theory
- Properties should contribute to municipal costs incurred due to their presence, conflicting with benefits-received principles.
- Issues with dilapidated properties and vacant land as community burdens.

## 1.8 The Valuation of Fixed Property  

- Property value assessed by certified valuers.
- "Market value": price a willing seller and buyer agree upon in a free market.
- Difficult to determine market value without actual sale.
- Valuation considers both land and improvements, affecting municipal rates.
- Valuation methods differ between provinces (e.g., Transvaal vs. others).

### Valuation Methodology
- **Transvaal Approach**:  
  - Total property value assessed.
  - Market value of site & improvements evaluated separately.
  - Site value influences rated property value dynamics.

- **Other Provinces**:  
  - Separate valuation for land and improvements.
  - Improvements valued at replacement cost less depreciation.

### Importance of Valuation Machinery
- Essential for an effective rating system.
- Well-defined legislation for valuers' appointment and regular property revaluations needed.
- **Valuation Roll**:  
  - List of assessed site/improvement values with property owners’ details.
  - Required for municipalities to levy annual property rates.

### Contesting Valuations
- Property owners can contest valuations.
- Valuation court/board adjudicates; can adjust, increase or leave values unchanged.
- Municipalities can also contest valuations, ensuring impartiality.

### Revaluations and Their Effects
- Revaluations address fairness in property ratings and reflect current market conditions.
- **Challenges**:  
  - Politically undesirable shifts in rating impacts.
  - Rate rebates/remissions introduced to mitigate shifts.

### Interim Valuations
- Required for new properties, rezoning, or subdivisions.
- Adjusts property values based on material changes.

## 1.9 Summary
- Municipal property rates are primary revenue sources for municipalities.
- Legislative framework influences rate charges considering residents' economic conditions.
- Rural and semi-urban municipalities face low tax bases significantly affecting revenue.

# Study Notes on Rating Methods    

## 2.2 Methods of Rating    
- Three historically used rating systems in South Africa: **site rating**, **flat rating**, **composite rating**.  
- The **Municipal Property Rates Act (MPRA)** permits only flat rating.  

### 2.2.1 Site Rating    
- **Definition**: Rates imposed on the assessed value of land only, ignoring improvements.  
- **Advantages**:  
  - Easy administration; focus on site values reduces valuation disputes.  
  - Higher rates per rand discourage property speculation.  
  - Incentivizes optimal property use, especially in CBDs.  
  - No tax on improvements encourages maintenance and standards.  
  - Stimulates industrial activity and community growth.  
  - Maintains active land market as idle land incurs costs.    

- **Disadvantages**:  
  - Least productive due to no tax on improvements.  
  - Narrow rate base as improvements provide significant community value.  
  - Lack of revenue elasticity; requires regular property revaluations.  
  - Disconnection between demand for services and tax base since unimproved land does not necessitate service provision.  

### 2.2.2 Flat Rating    
- **Definition**: A single property rate on combined value of land and improvements.    
- **Advantages**:  
  - Most productive due to the broad tax base and regular valuation.  
  - Aligns municipal service financing with actual demand.  
  - Better reflection of owner's financial capacity regarding ability-to-pay principle.    

- **Disadvantages**:  
  - Lower rates may encourage holding unimproved properties.  
  - Tax on improvements can disincentivize maintenance and upgrades.  
  - Complex valuation of improvements increases administration costs.    

### 2.2.3 Composite Rating    
- **Definition**: Both land and improvement values are assessed at different rates (generally higher for land).    
- **Advantages**:  
  - More flexible than site or flat rating, with a balanced approach.  
  - Encourages property improvements while reducing speculation.    

### 2.2.4 A Unique System of Rating    
- A metropolitan municipality's unique system combines flat and site rating.  
- **Weighting**: Land valued at 12 times improvements, but applied as a flat rate for residential areas.  
- **Calculation**: Total rate liability calculated on a composite system basis but charged at a flat rate, aiming for equitable redistribution.

## 2.3 The Impact of Rating Systems on Local Government    
- **Research Conclusion**: Site rating correlates with marked economic progress more significantly than flat rating.  
- Historical data shows municipalities using site rating experienced higher growth in property values.  
- Investigating the impact of flat rating in the MPRA could provide insights into economic improvements over time.  

## 2.4 The Incidence of Rating    
- Traditionally, property owners are liable for rates, but burdens can shift.  
- Residential property lessors often recover rates through rental charges.  
- Commercial property owners include rates in pricing strategies, distributing the burden among consumers.  
- **Incidence Shifting**: Easier with improved properties compared to unimproved ones, which generate no revenue.  

## 2.5 Differential Rating (Rate Rebates)    
- **Definition**: Uniform rate levied on properties with rebates offered based on property class.  
- This system aims to address the different impacts of property types on the rate burden.

### 2.6 Rate Rebates, Reductions (Remissions), and Exemptions

- **Rate Rebates vs. Rate Reductions**
  - Rate rebates are integral to the differential rating policy.
  - Rate reductions (remissions) are often ad hoc.

- **Classes of Rate Reductions**
  - **Charitable/Welfare Activity Remissions**: 
    - Grant-in-aid for rate commitments related to community welfare.
    
  - **Direct Remissions**: 
    - For properties materially disadvantaged by new valuation rolls.
    - Example: Increases above average values receive remissions (e.g., 50%, 80%).
    
  - **Remissions for Pensioners**: 
    - For low-income pensioners who occupy their homes, capped at a maximum of 40%.
    - Sliding scale may apply based on income.

- **MPRA Options**: 
  - Expands on existing remissions and exemptions.

### 2.7 Erosion of the Rate Base

- **Productivity of Rates Base**: 
  - A broader base generates more revenue; site rating is less productive.
  
- **Impact of Rebates and Exemptions**: 
  - Rate rebates lead to voluntary erosion of the rates base.
  
- **Statutory Exemptions**: 
  - Significant exemptions for government properties (Rating of State Property Act, 1984).
  - Compulsory rebates: 10% for specific departments, 20% for others.

- **Justifications for Exemptions**:
  - Exemptions for charitable, religious, educational institutions, etc.
  - Exempt agricultural land outside urban areas.

- **Statutory Exemptions (s. 17 MPRA)**:
  - Includes properties used for worship, public infrastructure, nature reserves, mineral rights, and first R15,000 value of residential properties.

### 2.8 Collection of Rates

- **Legislative Requirements**:
  - Unpaid rates lead to legal proceedings after a demand has been served.
  
- **Equity in Rate Collection**: 
  - Effective collection machinery essential for fairness.
  
- **Rate Collection Controls**: 
  - Legislation enforces payment frequency, due dates, late fees.
  - Unpaid rates charge against property, enforceable by property sale to recover debts.

### Additional Notes
- **Legislation & Case Law**:
  - **MPRA**: Local municipalities given range for rate management.
  - Landmark cases support equal treatment of property valuations and rates.

- **Differential Taxation**: 
  - Similar to income tax structures.
  
- **Objectives of Differential Rating (George Marais)**:
  - Encourage residential investment and maintenance.
  - Relieve tax burden on low-income residents.
  - Shift tax burden to business properties that can pass on costs. 

- **Conclusion**:
  - Strategic handling of exemptions and remissions crucial for sustainable municipal revenue.

# 2.9 OTHER MUNICIPAL TAXES: LEVIES OF THE FORMER RSC & JSB
- Empowered by **Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993** (approval needed from national Minister of Finance/provincial MEC).
- Levies from now-defunct **RSC** (1985) and **JSB** (1990):
  - **Regional Levy**: Tax on payrolls imposed on employers in the municipality.
  - **Regional Establishment Levy**: Tax on business turnover affecting all business types (natural/legal persons).
  - Revenue from levies, although low, significant for urban municipalities; unclear application of revenues.
  - National Treasury's view: Revenues to finance **capital programmes** for reserves.
- **Abolished** effective **1 July 2006**; additional government grants were provided as compensation.

## 2.10.2 The Rates Policy
- According to **section 3 of the MPRA**, municipalities must adopt rates policies governing property taxation.
- Effective upon first valuation roll completion.
- Related to **Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003**; accompanies annual budget process.

### 2.10.2.1 Rates Policy as Risk Management Strategy
- Transparent tax basis for property owners.
- Clear specifications on:
  - Properties subject to rates/exemptions.
  - Annual rates increase limits (aligned with approved IDP).
  - Impacts on public benefit organizations.
  - Property rate payment frequency and error correction.
- Legal frameworks provided by **Municipal Property Rates Act** must govern policies.

### 2.10.2.2 Checklist Matters to Cover in a Rates Policy
1. **Objective**:
   - Fair, transparent, equitable levying practices.
   - Inform ratepayers rationale for rates.
   - Uniform regulatory framework.
2. **Imposition of Rates**:
   - Criteria on rateable properties and multiple-use assignments.
   - Limits on annual increases; address burdens on property owners.
3. **Exemptions/Reductions**:
   - Differential rating and categorizations.
   - Determine percentage rebates or reductions.
4. **Frequency of Payments**: Monthly, less frequent, or instalments.
5. **Correction of Errors**: Amendments period and penalties.
6. **Valuation Roll Frequency**: Compliance with the Act.
7. **Legal Requirements**: Formulation and implementation.
8. **Bylaws Creation**: For policy effectuation.

### 3.1 INTRODUCTION
- Focus on trading services (electricity and water) as key municipal revenue sources.
- Tariffs intended to yield a surplus, but not excessive.
- Charges include service provision and infrastructure maintenance.
  
### 3.2 BACKGROUND PERSPECTIVES
- Municipal trading surpluses primarily from electricity, also from water.
- Characteristics of tariffs:
  - Voluntary consumption control (e.g., RDP standards: 6 kl water; 400 kWh electricity).
  - Benefits-received principle: pay for consumed services.
  - Tariffs reflect the cost of service provision.
  - Consumption is measurable and attributable to consumers.
- Classification as trading services does not depend on actual surplus.

### 3.3 SETTING OF TARIFFS FOR PRINCIPAL TRADING SERVICES
- Establish tangible, measurable tariff objectives; regular monitoring for effectiveness.

#### Pricing Objectives
1. **Profit-Oriented**
   - Maximize profits, set prices to maximize revenue vs. total costs.
   - Acceptable profits target, or specific ROI goal.
2. **Sales-Oriented**
   - Attain specific sales targets, growth in sales assumed to increase profits.
   - Increase market share viewed as beneficial for profitability.
3. **Status Quo**
   - Maintain stable prices to limit competition.
   - May follow market leader pricing.

### 3.3.1 DETERMINANTS OF PRICE
- Considerations for price setting:
  - Demand for services
  - Production costs
  - Distribution and promotion costs
  - Quality of service
- **Administered Prices**: Set consciously rather than by market forces (e.g., electricity tariffs set by **Eskom**).
- Pricing policies: single price vs. flexible pricing approach.

### 3.3.2 MARGINAL COSTING
- Focus on total revenue and total cost changes when one unit sold.
- Marginal revenue: change in revenue from selling an additional unit.
  
### 3.3.3 PRICING OBJECTIVES OF MUNICIPAL TRADING UNDERTAKINGS
- Distinct from private sector due to virtual monopoly.
- Key objectives for tariffs:
  - No use as concealed taxes.
  - Consumer ability to pay considered (indigents excluded).
  - Uniform, fair tariff acceptance.
  - Cost recovery for all service-related expenses, unless justified.
  - Tariffs reflecting service standards and consumption levels.
  - Produce an operating surplus for future use.
  - Tariffs differentiated amongst consumer categories.
  - Transparency in tariff policy and cross-subsidization.
  - Clear, understandable tariffs.
  - Metered consumption with monthly readings.
  - Availability and fixed charges must be established, including for indigents.

### LEGISLATION
- **LGTA**: Municipal electricity tariffs approval by **National Electricity Regulator**.
- **Water Services Act (1997)**: Minister may prescribe water tariff norms/standards.

### Conclusion
- Municipal pricing reflects genuine service costs, differing from private sector profit maximization. High capital and fixed costs necessitate careful planning and demand management, often leaving municipalities under capacity. Marginal analysis less relevant due to service demand obligations.

## Chapter 3: Electricity and Water Tariff Structures

### 3.4 Electricity Tariff Structures
- **Two-part tariff**: Fixed cost charge + consumption charge.
  - High fixed costs for municipalities generating electricity vs. low for bulk purchasers.
  - Electricity: cannot be stored; complexity in fixed/variable cost determination.
- **Service Charges**: 
  - Design necessary to cover peak demand costs, primarily affecting large consumers.
  - Installation of demand meters for large consumers to assess maximum demand.

#### 3.4.1 One-part Single Energy Rate Tariff (Lifeline Tariff)
- Suitable for low-use residential consumers (<400 kWh/month).
- Comprises only an energy charge; not cost reflective.

#### 3.4.2 Two-part Tariff
- Fixed charge + energy charge.
- Appropriate for residential (>400 kWh/month) and small commercial consumers.

#### 3.4.3 Three-part Tariff
- Fixed charge + energy charge + capacity (demand) charge.
- More cost reflective; suitable for larger commercial and industrial consumers with demand meters.

#### 3.4.4 Time-of-use Tariffs
- Either a two-part or three-part structure.
- Charges vary according to time of use, promoting off-peak consumption.

### 3.5 Calculation of Electricity Tariffs
1. **Cost Categories to Identify**:
   - Bulk purchase from Eskom
   - Generation costs (if applicable)
   - Distribution costs
   - Depreciation and finance charges
   - Administrative costs, including provisions for bad debts
   - Surplus contribution to rates and general services
   
2. **Cost of Power**:
   - Main costs: bulk purchases and operational expenses of own power stations.

3. **Distribution Costs**:
   - Operational and maintenance expenses related to sub-stations, reticulation, meters, street lighting.

4. **Depreciation and Finance Costs**:
   - Costs associated with asset depreciation and loan finance charges for capital formation.

5. **Administration and Service Costs**:
   - Includes customer service, IT support, security, training, and provisions for bad debts and stock obsolescence.

6. **Surplus**:
   - Contribution to support municipal rates and general services.

7. **Other Revenues**:
   - Deductions for miscellaneous income from overdue accounts and service fees.

8. **Free Basic Electricity**:
   - Allocation of 50 kWh/month for low-income households.
   - Funding options: national revenue or cross-subsidies from higher consumers.

### 3.6 Tariff Structures for Water Consumption
- **Water Services Act 1997**: Aims for conservation and equitable access to basic water supplies.
- **Basic Water Supply**: Defined by quantity, availability, quality, and upgradability.
  
- **Water Services Authorities (WSAs)**: Legally obliged to provide water services; may outsource to Water Services Providers (WSPs).

#### Fundamental Principles for Water Tariffs
- Fairness with allowance for differential tariffs.
- Base tariffs on the cost of supply.
- Simplicity and understandability in implementation.

- **Lifeline Tariff**: Covers basic human needs, recovering only operational costs.

- **Block Rising Rate Tariff**: Sliding scale based on consumption above basic needs.

- Similar to block tariffs but applicable to all consumers.

### 4.1 Metering Issues in Water and Electricity Services
- Reliable metering requires accurate meters and consistent readings.
- Consumer understanding of meter readings is essential for transparency.
- Historical issues include missing or inadequate meters from former BLAs, leading to dissatisfaction and non-payment.
- Municipalities often bill based on assumed consumption due to lack of reliable metering.
  - Averages do not reflect actual consumption variations.
  - This method discourages water conservation; municipal councillors may set averages low to encourage payment.
- Result: High water loss rates (up to 50%) due to poor metering and infrastructure neglect.
- Wastage compounded by unmetered consumption and infrastructure deterioration.
- Pre-paid water meters offer cash flow benefits and promote conservation; community resistance poses challenges.

### 4.2 Effective Metering Systems
- Effective meter-reading begins with scheduling and coordination.
  - Requires data such as number of meters, readers available, and geographical distribution.
  - Timely and validated readings are essential for convincing consumers.
- Consistency in meter-reading periods is critical for consumer satisfaction and budget planning.
- Monthly reading cycles are preferred but not always cost-effective; some municipalities rely on estimates for interim periods.
- Communication with consumers regarding reading dates allows for self-reporting when access is not possible.
- Advanced technology, like handheld devices, improves accuracy and efficiency.
- Testing of meters available for consumers disputing readings; costs refunded if faults exceed specific thresholds.
- Proper billing administration is crucial to timely account processing post-meter readings.
  
### 4.3 Trading Surpluses in Relief of Rates
- Municipalities may transfer trading surpluses from electricity to subsidize rates, validated by the Croeser Working Group and IMFO guidelines.

- Surplus transfer impacts service pricing, effectively taxing consumers for ratepayer relief.

- Surpluses motivate municipalities to enhance trading services.
- Helps balance direct and indirect taxation, countering regressive nature of property tax.
- Municipalities leverage external loans based on taxation capabilities; surpluses facilitate service funding.

# Study Notes on Municipal Services

## Chapter 4: Municipal Electricity and Water Services

### 4.3.1 Arguments for the Appropriation of Trading Surpluses in Relief of Rates
- **Higher Interest Rates**: Municipalities face higher rates for external loans due to inadequate internal resources. 
- **Tax Levy Obligation**: If property owners can't cover loans, municipalities may need to raise property tax for servicing.
- **Redistribution of Surplus/Deficit**:
  - Surpluses and deficits of trading undertakings should transfer to the rate account.
  - Municipal services function as a cohesive operation, benefiting the entire community.
- **Fair Contribution from Residents**: 
  - All residents, including those in flats, should contribute to collective services.
  - Trading surpluses alleviate rate burden on property owners.
- **Compliance with Taxation Canons**: Trading surpluses serve as indirect taxation that is productive, elastic, and economically viable.

### 4.3.2 Arguments Against Appropriation of Trading Surpluses in Relief of Rates
- **Concealed Taxation**:
  - Trading surpluses can be viewed as hidden taxes, undermining the principle of equal tax burden.
  - Users of trading services may not be a homogeneous group, leading to regressive taxation effects.
- **Tariff Disparities**: 
  - Elderly and low-income households disproportionately impact by reliance on essential services.
  - Suggested differential tariffs could mitigate regressivity.
- **User Charge Principles**: Appropriating surpluses conflicts with user charging principles.
  - Profitable service consumers indirectly subsidize less profitable services.
- **Economic Implications**: Concerns arise that reliance on surpluses could hinder business development and national income.
- **Historical Context**: A 1976 memorandum asserts a mix of direct and indirect taxation for local authorities, justifying the appropriation of trading surpluses if it doesn’t abuse monopolies.

### 4.4 Recent Developments Regarding Municipal Water and Electricity Services

#### 4.4.1 Municipal Water Services
- **Water Services Act 1997**:
  - Assigns retail water services to local governments, including sanitation.
  - Aligns with constitutional provisions granting local authorities responsibility for water and sanitation services.
- **Water Services Authority (WSA)**: 
  - Any municipality responsible for ensuring access to water services.
  - Can appoint Water Services Providers (WSPs) but retains legal accountability.
- **Service Provision Options**:
  - WSA can provide services directly, engage WSPs via contracts, or form joint ventures.
  - Joint ventures enhancing regional capacity are permissible.
- **Regulatory Framework**: 
  - Water Services Development Plans (WSDPs) required, detailing service provisions and partnerships.
  - Annual audits assess compliance with national standards on service provision.

**Key Sections of Water Services Act**:
- **Section 19(1)**: WSA's procedures for selecting WSPs, including public sector options before private contracting.
- **Section 30(2)(d)**: Water boards may act as WSPs but must focus on bulk services.

**Regulations Overview**:
- Mandate regular audits and requirement of measures to reduce unaccounted water.
- WSAs must also ensure rapid repairs of leaks and measurement of water supply for all connections.

#### Conclusion:
Municipal trading undertakings' fiscal strategies depend on balancing between direct taxation principles and revenue generation via surpluses, as well as adapting to legislative requirements like the Water Services Act 1997.

#### 5.1 Industrial Effluent

- Refuse services include:
  - Household refuse removal
  - Garden refuse sites
  - Street cleaning
  - Refuse disposal/tip sites
  - Commercial and industrial refuse removal
- Cost recovery principles apply; costs are debited to cost centres; tariffs based on actual expenses.
- Refuse services can be traded based on measurable waste volume, unlike sewerage.
- Municipalities may charge uniform tariffs or tiered tariffs based on property stand sizes for refuse collection.

#### 5.3 Tariffs and Contract Arrangements 
- Specific contracts can be established based on customers' needs (e.g., container size, removal frequency).
- Municipalities may opt to include garden refuse disposal services as part of household refuse services or provide disposal sites.
- Cost centres for refuse services often distinguish between:
  - Residential users
  - Businesses using 85 l bins
  - Businesses using 1.1 m³ containers
- Tariffs cover medium expenses to ensure financial viability and may include cross-subsidization.

#### 5.4 Urban Passenger Transport Services
- Bus services usually operate on average costs; not feasible for classic two-part tariffs.
- Fare structures:
  - Fixed charge
  - Rate based on zones or stages travelled

- Municipalities increasingly handle national or regional functions; subsidies are provided to offset costs.
- Some provinces are more generous with subsidies than others.

## 5.8 EQUITABLE SHARE ALLOCATIONS
- Inter-governmental transfers (IGTs) from national government to municipalities.
- **Constitution**: Section 214(1)(a) mandates equitable revenue allocation among government spheres.
- Origin: Vertical subsidisation to cover operating costs for integrating former BLAs.
- IGT variability: Amounts differ by municipality and financial year; may be insignificant in large metros.
- Intended use: Unconditional grants aimed at improving service delivery to disadvantaged communities.
- Recommendation to separate IGTs from general revenue for accountability.
- Purpose: Cover lifeline tariffs for essential services (e.g., electricity, water) to support indigent households.

## 5.9 AMBULANCE SERVICES
- Provision varies by province; some municipalities contracted as agents for the province.
- Municipalities bear agency fees despite being not responsible for costs.
- Challenges: 
  - Unrecovered agency fees leading to unfunded mandates.
  - Reluctance of users to pay ambulance fees; collection inefficacy.
- Future: Uncertainty in service provision; potential for private sector involvement.

## 5.10 ASSIGNED REVENUES
- Primarily traffic fines assigned to local government per **National Roads Traffic Act Amendment Act of 2008**.
- Traffic fines historically significant for municipal revenue but have led to deficits in recent years.
- Issues: 
  - Decline in revenue due to insufficient fines imposed and challenges in fee collection.
  - Civil disobedience regarding payment of fines.

## 5.11 OTHER LICENCES
- Licensing intended as regulatory; modest revenue generation from dog and bicycle licences.
- Trade licences previously provided revenue but were abolished post-democracy, replaced with levies.

## 5.12 INTEREST ON INVESTMENTS
- Surplus funds invested to earn interest; legislation governs investment practices (e.g., **LGTA**).
- GAMAP requires accrued interest to be reported in income statements.
- Option to allocate interest to capital funds must be transparently accounted.

## 5.13 MISCELLANEOUS REVENUES
- Insignificant revenue items include:
  - Admission fees for municipal amenities; modest contributions to operational costs.
  - Fees for services like reconnection and meter readings, typically economic based.
  - Sale of by-products, which can be profitable or nuisance-dependent.
  - Various licensed items: rental income from municipal property should be market-related, with subsidized rates for non-profit entities.
  - Hire of municipal facilities typically at subsidized rates.
  - Jobbing services provided at economic rates.
  - Advertising on municipal property as a potential revenue source, but currently underexplored.
  - Inspection and clearance certificate fees generally economic in nature.

- Engage community stakeholders in creating institutional arrangements while retaining council decision-making authority.

- Financial policy should explicitly address:
  - Solvency and liquidity maintenance.
  - Indigent support.
  - Sustainable reward systems.
  - Service affordability and sustainability.

- Determine appropriate reserve levels for working capital and bad debts.

## 6.9 Summary
- Cost recovery systems are crucial for effective municipal functioning.
- Proper institutional foundation aids in minimizing debtors' turnover rates.
- Municipal bankruptcies attributed to poor cost recovery practices.

## Cost Recovery Mechanisms
- **Compliance Incentives**: Service disconnection politically contentious; non-payment led to illegal connections (e.g., Eskom cut-offs).
- **Types of Service Cut-offs**:
  - Removal of infrastructure (cables/pipes).
  - Severe reconnection fees.
  - Pay-off requirement before reconnection.
  - Self-disconnecting via prepaid meters.
- **Cross-subsidisation**: Charging rich households higher tariffs to support poorer households; effective in solid waste sector (Cointreau, 2005).
- **Voluntarism**: Consumers contribute labor when they can't afford services leading to unforeseen social health impacts (McDonald, 2002).
- **Prepaid Meters**: 
  - Prevent default on payments (MacDonald, 2002). 
  - May mask extent of service access issues for low-income users (Ruiters, 2002).

## Impacts of Cost Recovery
- **Access Limitations**: Wealthier households gain while low-income individuals face limited access, exacerbating inequality.
- **Responsibility Shift**: Increased social burdens on working-class women to find alternate services.
- **Health Risks**: Lack of clean water and reliance on harmful energy sources increase potential health issues.
- **Undermining Constitutional Protections**: Government's financial liability impacted by resource allocation issues (Pape, 2002).

## Recommendations for Improvement
- Expand prepaid systems to businesses for better income generation and manageable consumption recovery.
- Identify root causes of non-payment including billing and payment method suitability for low-income populations.
- Assurance of high-quality municipal service delivery and effective billing processes (Republic of South Africa 2016).
