## 1.1 INTRODUCTION
- Daily life closely intertwined with the environment; influences quality of life and welfare.
- Understanding environment components necessary for comprehending its impact.
- Environmental dilemma originates from historical human actions.
- Need for environmental services justified.

## 1.2 THE CONCEPT "ENVIRONMENT"
- **Definition**: Environment encompasses more than natural surroundings; includes objects, conditions, and influences impacting life.
- **Components**:
  - Natural environment/habitat.
  - Social, political, cultural, economic, and working conditions influencing environments.
  - Manmade environment, including infrastructure and technological resources.
- Interaction among environment components crucial.
  
## 1.3 HISTORICAL OVERVIEW

### 1.3.1 The Pre-Industrial Period
- Limited manipulation and exploitation of natural resources; lack of technology.
- Natural disasters and epidemics regulated population growth; minimal strain on resources.
- Harmony between people and natural environment predominated.

### 1.3.2 Industrial Period
- Industrial revolution led to exploitation of resources for commercial gain; rise of waste disposal issues.
- Increased life expectancy and population growth exerted pressure on natural ecosystems.
- Disturbance of human-nature harmony; alienation emphasized by Indira Gandhi’s remarks on human disconnect from nature.

### 1.3.3 Post-Industrial Period
- Awareness of environmental damage increased; advancements in technology revealed extent of destruction.
- Issues like ozone layer depletion and soil erosion become apparent.
- Recognition that conservation and management of the environment are crucial.
- Balance needed between development and environmental conservation.

- Certain services provided solely by government; determines service provision based on criteria learned in earlier studies.

## Chapter 2: Environmental Issues

### 2.1 Greenhouse Effect
- **Factors Contributing**:
  - Coal burning, vehicle exhaust, industrial waste.
  - Deforestation exacerbates CO2 accumulation (Gauteng Reports, 1997; Grant & Hawkins, 1995).
  
- **Global CO2 Responsibility**:
  - Developed countries: 50% of emissions.
  - Eastern Europe and developing nations (Brazil, Chile, India): 25% each (McMichael, 1993).

- **Consequences**:
  - Climate change; global temperature rise since 1900 by 0.6°C (Sunter, 1996).
  - Predictions: 1°C rise by 2025; 2.5 - 3°C by end of 21st century (McMichael, 1993).
  - Effects: Glacier melting, sea-level rise, flooding of low-lying areas (Sunter, 1996; Wilkinson, 1980).
  - Increased natural disasters; agricultural disruption; health impacts from warmer climates (Seager, 1990; McMichael, 1993).

- **Mitigation Strategies**:
  - Shift to lead-free fuel, battery-drawn vehicles, natural gas, renewable energy (solar, wind, hydro).
  
### 2.2 Kyoto Protocol
- **International Summit**: December 1997, Kyoto, Japan.
- **Treaty Objectives**:
  - Reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 5.2% from 1990 levels by 2012. 
  - Specific reductions: EU (8%), Japan (6%), USA (7%). No obligations for developing nations (Lemonick, 1997).
  
- **Implementation Challenges**:
  - 55% of major polluters needed for ratification.
  - USA's refusal cited industry concerns (Malan, 2002).
  - Status as of July 2010: 191 states ratified.

### 2.3 Ozone Layer Depletion
- **Location**: Atmospheric layer between 10-50 km.
- **Formation and Destruction**: Continuous process; CFCs as major destructors (Grant & Hawkins, 1990).
  
- **Consequences of Depletion**:
  - Increased UV radiation linked to skin cancers, cataracts, and immune suppression (McMichael, 1993).
  
- **UV Impact on Marine and Terrestrial Life**:
  - Negative effects on plant growth, agriculture; major crops sensitive to UV (McMichael, 1993).

- **International Response**:
  - Montreal Protocol (1987): Initiated CFC reduction (50% by 1999); revisions for complete phase-out by 2000 for developed nations.
  - Ongoing commitment from 196 states to recover ozone by 2050 (Speth, 2004).

### 2.4 Nuclear Power
- **Energy Source**: Makes up 13-14% of global electricity (World Nuclear News, 2010).
- **Advantages**: Fewer harmful emissions compared to fossil fuels (Seager, 1990).
  
- **Concerns**:
  - Radioactive waste management; no safe disposal solution exists (Seager, 1990).
  - Risks associated with reactor failure; historical context: Chernobyl disaster (1986) – extensive health and environmental consequences (Environmental Science Update, 1997).

- **Nuclear Weapons Link**:
  - Concerns over potential misuse or proliferation; threats from unstable regimes and terrorism (Wilkinson, 1980).
  - Nuclear testing impacts rendering areas uninhabitable (Seager, 1990).

## Conclusions
- **Environmental Protection**: Requires international cooperation and comprehensive legislation.
- **Energy Strategy**: Balances renewable energy sources with nuclear risks and waste management.

### 2.6 SOIL LOSS
- Annual soil loss in South Africa: 300-400 million tons; formation rate <0.1 ton per hectare/year (Huntley et al, 1989:38).
- Global issue; exacerbated by:
  - Intensive crop planting, inappropriate farming methods, chemical treatments.
  - Deforestation and overgrazing enhance erosion (Wilkinson 1989:205–206).
- Leads to desertification: irreversible crop production decline, biodiversity loss (McMichael 1990:206).

### 2.7 DEFORESTATION
- Global loss: from 6-7 billion hectares to ~4 billion (Schwella et al, 1996:313).
- Causes:
  - Commercial wood use, agricultural expansion, mining, firewood gathering, cattle grazing, veld fires (McMichael 1993:252; Schwella et al 1996:313).
- Tropical forests: crucial for biodiversity; 2/3 of plant and animal species (McMichael 1993:253).
- Need for education and protective measures against exploitation, fires, pests, and pollution (Sitar, 1993:96–97).

### 2.8 WATER SCARCITY
- Freshwater scarcity due to limited availability; heavy agricultural water use (~67%).
- Pollution from industrial waste and inappropriate disposal exacerbates scarcity (McMichael 1993:226).
- Water conflicts: e.g., Middle East waters shared among Turkey, Syria, Iraq (Euphrates & Tigris); Israel & Jordan (Jordan River); Egypt, Ethiopia, Sudan (Nile).
- Helsinki Rules: shared water rights among states without inconvenience (South Africa, Department of Water Affairs and Forestry 1997).
- South African policy: National Water Policy framework emphasizes equitable sharing, economic utilization, and environmental conservation (South Africa, Department of Water Affairs and Forestry 1997).

### 2.9 URBANISATION
- Definition: increased concentration of people in cities via rural-to-urban migration and natural city growth.
- Trends:
  - Developed countries: peaked 1955–60; stabilized at 0.8% in 1990.
  - Developing countries: rapid increase; expected 60% urbanization by 2030 (Population Reference Bureau).
- Impacts: urbanization strains natural resources, leads to habitat destruction and increased pollution (McMichael 1993:259; Seager 1990:107). 

### 2.10 POLLUTION
- Waste generation exceeds environmental absorption capacity, risking health and ecosystems (Fyfe 1990:21).
- Sources: households, industries, agriculture; non-biodegradable waste (e.g., batteries) contaminates groundwater (Fyfe 1990:21; Grant & Hawkins 1995:42).
- Disposal methods: improper waste management (landfills, burning), air pollution, and toxic residue production.
- Recycling: effective method for waste management, income generation in Third World (Seager 1990:108–109).
- Attitudes: littering leads to environmental degradation and psychological effects.

#### 1.2 Water Pollution
- Dumping of **untreated sewage**, **industrial waste**, and **chemical fertilizers** threatens water quality and ecosystems (Fyfe 1990; McMichael 1993).

#### 1.3 Air Pollution
- Main causes: gases (CO₂, SO₂, NO₂) and particles from burning waste, veld fires, and industrial emissions.
- Health impacts: respiratory illnesses (e.g., bronchitis, asthma, lung cancer) (Seager 1990).
- Ecological impacts:
  - Contributes to greenhouse effect.
  - Acid rain affects plant growth, soil quality, and aquatic life (McMichael 1993).

#### 1.4 Loss of Biodiversity
- Causes:
  - Urban expansion, forest destruction, wetland drainage.
  - Pollution and climate change effects (McMichael 1993).
- Illegal trade in wildlife: significant decline in species like elephants and rhinos (De Swardt 1997).

#### 1.5 Poverty
- Definition: absolute poverty impacts one in five globally; lack of resources for health and dignity (Miller 1995).
- Distinction: **North** (developed) vs **South** (developing countries) (Welsh & Butyrin 1990).
- Relation to environment:
  - **Poverty as impact**: Poor health linked to environmental conditions, leading to compromised livelihoods.
  - **Poverty as cause**: Financial constraints hinder use of eco-friendly technologies; overpopulation pressures sensitive land (Tham 1992).
- Addressing issues collectively is necessary for sustainable growth and ecological preservation (Sitarz 1993).

## 3.1 INTRODUCTION
- Global environmental issues (e.g., climate change, ozone depletion, population growth, poverty) require international cooperation.
- Environmental management is an international concern, not limited to national borders.

## 3.2 HISTORICAL DEVELOPMENT AND HIGHLIGHTS OF INTERNATIONAL ENVIRONMENTAL MANAGEMENT
- International environmental management encompasses activities by the global community, including treaties and conferences.
- Key developments:
  1. **Stockholm Conference (1972)**
  2. **Brundtland Report (1987)**
  3. **Rio Conference (1992)**
  4. **World Summit on Sustainable Development (2002)**

### 3.2.1 Stockholm Conference
- **Stockholm Declaration**: 26 principles addressing natural resources, pollution, urbanization, education, and socio-economic development.
- **Action Plan**: 109 recommendations for environmental actions.
- Established **UNEP** (United Nations Environmental Programme):
  - Promotes international cooperation.
  - Monitors global environmental conditions.
  - Guides policy formulation.

### 3.2.2 Brundtland Report
- Originated from the **World Commission on Environment and Development** (1983).
- Published as **"Our Common Future"** (1987).
- Introduced **sustainable development**: Meets present needs without compromising future generations.
- Influenced **UN Resolution 44/228** (1989) for the **Rio Conference**.

### 3.2.3 Rio Conference
- Largest UN conference held in **Rio de Janeiro** (1992).
- Key documents:
  - **Rio Declaration on Environment and Development**: Guidelines for environmental management emphasizing development.
  - **Agenda 21**: Comprehensive action plan covering socio-economic issues, biodiversity, and roles of various societal groups. 

### 3.2.4 World Summit on Sustainable Development
- Held in **Johannesburg** (2002) to review **Agenda 21**.
- Objectives:
  - Assess progress and identify new challenges.
  - Set targets and timelines for sustainable development.
- Key documents:
  - **Political Declaration**: Renewed commitment to sustainable development; calls for addressing threats like chronic diseases and natural disasters.
  - **Plan of Implementation**: Outlines activities and concrete targets for issues like sanitation, biodiversity, and renewable energy.

## 3.3 INTERNATIONAL TREATIES AS AN ENVIRONMENTAL MANAGEMENT MECHANISM

- Post-1972 treaties include:
  - International Convention on the Prevention of Pollution from Ships, 1974
  - Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES), 1973
  - Montreal Protocol on Substances that Deplete the Ozone Layer, 1987
  - Basel Convention on the Trans-boundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes, 1989
  - Framework Convention on Climate Change, 1992
  - Convention on Biological Diversity, 1992  
- Treaties impose rules/obligations; compliance is voluntary due to state sovereignty. No external enforcement can be applied if a state refuses to comply.

## 3.4 OBSTACLES TO INTERNATIONAL ENVIRONMENTAL MANAGEMENT

### 3.4.1 Sovereignty of states
- International cooperation is hindered by national interests prioritization.
- "Global commons" are resources outside national jurisdictions, leading to competition.
- States often disregard international treaty regulations.
- Examples of non-compliance: Iceland's whaling practices.

### 3.4.2 North-South polarisation
- Historical context from the 1972 Stockholm Conference highlighted discrepancies in environmental priorities between the North (development) and the South (conservation).
- South critiques Northern focus on environmental issues diverting attention from developmental needs (poverty, debt).
- North contributes significantly to environmental issues (e.g., 90% CO2 emissions).
  
#### Summary Table of Perspectives

**Causes of Environmental Dilemma**  
- South: Poverty, debt burden, trade imbalances.  
- North: Overpopulation, mismanagement, lack of technology.  

**Solutions**  
- South: Sustainable growth, debt relief, fair trade relations.  
- North: Birth control, environmental impact evaluations, debt relief for programs.  

- Sustainable development concept aims to balance growth and conservation, yet conflicts persist at international conferences.
- The South employs environmental concerns in negotiations (e.g., "debt-for-nature swaps"), leading to debt reduction incentive schemes.

## 3.5 REVIEW
- Effective international environmental management hinges on cooperation.
- Challenges include state sovereignty and North-South divides leading to prioritization of national interests over global environmental concerns.

### 4.1 INTRODUCTION  
- Regional environmental management addresses unique local problems, specifically in Southern Africa with the role of SADC.

### 4.2 ESTABLISHMENT OF THE SOUTHERN AFRICAN DEVELOPMENT COMMUNITY (SADC)  
- **SADCC established on 1 April 1980** under Lusaka Declaration to reduce member states' economic dependency on South Africa.  
- Initial members: Angola, Botswana, Lesotho, Malawi, Mozambique, Swaziland, Tanzania, Zambia, Zimbabwe, with Namibia joining in 1990.  
- **SADC founded on 17 August 1992** for greater economic integration; South Africa and Mauritius joined in 1994 and 1995, respectively.  
- Objectives of SADC:  
  - Development and economic growth  
  - Poverty relief  
  - Improvement of population quality of life  
  - Promotion of peace and security  
  - Sustainable resource utilisation and environmental conservation  
  - Development and transfer of technology (SADC ELMS, 1994; SADC ELM Sector, 1996)  

---

### 4.3 THE SOUTHERN AFRICAN DEVELOPMENT COMMUNITY AND THE ENVIRONMENT  
- **Institutional Framework**: Environmental issues managed since inception; initial scope expanded from Soil and Water Conservation to Environment and Land Management Sector.  
- **Long-term environmental policy and strategy enacted** to address:  
  - Population growth impacting natural resources  
  - Soil erosion  
  - Desertification  
  - Insufficient water  
  - Decrease in biodiversity  
  - Air and water pollution (SADC ELMS, 1994)  
- **Challenges** facing SADC:  
  - Limited financial and technological resources  
  - Lack of expertise and training facilities  
  - Insufficient research and environmental monitoring  
  - Low public participation (SADC ELMS, 1994)  

---

### 4.4 ENVIRONMENTAL STRATEGY AND OBJECTIVES  
- Objectives of Environmental and Land Management Sector:  
  - Improve health and environment for southern Africa’s people  
  - Support the majority poor  
  - Preserve natural heritage, biodiversity, ecosystems  
  - Promote sustainable economic development  
  - Enhance decision-making and technological capabilities  
  - Establish effective legal and institutional frameworks  
  - Launch information and educational programs for public participation  
  - Promote integration and global cooperation (SADC ELM Sector, 1996)  

---

### 4.5 PRIORITIES AND PROGRAMMES  
- **Key Priorities Identified**:  
  - Management of communal water resources  
  - Improvement of water quality  
  - Access to water and sanitation  
  - Economic growth  
  - Access to affordable housing  
  - Control over hazardous waste disposal  
  - Development of alternative energy sources (SADC ELMS, 1994)  
- **Programmes include:**  
  - Training in conservation, sustainable farming practices, and desertification management.  
  - Development of an Environmental Information System for regional coordination.  
  - Integrated water management planning (e.g., Zambezi River) to maximize benefits from communal water resources.  
  - Funded by member states and external donors (UNDP, Norwegian, Danish, German aid).

### 4.6 THE LESOTHO HIGHLANDS WATER SCHEME  
- Example of collaboration between Lesotho and South Africa.  
- **Treaty details**: Water from Senqu-Orange River pumped to Vaal River for South Africa, generating hydroelectric power for Lesotho.  
- **Economic Impacts**:  
  - South Africa benefits from access to water, vital for survival, sanitation, manufacturing, and mining; supports economic stability.  
  - Lesotho achieves energy self-sufficiency, receives royalties for water supplied, contributing to national income.  
- **Definition**: "Royalties" refers to payments made by South Africa to Lesotho for water, derived from users in South Africa.

## 4.5 TRANS-BORDER PARKS
- Definition: Conservation areas extending over national borders, managed collectively (Department of Environmental Affairs and Tourism 2002:88).
- Purpose: Restore ecosystems and historical migration patterns; eliminate barriers (fences) for wildlife.
- Key Contributors: 
  - Authorities from bordering areas
  - Private sector (funding support)
  - NGOs
  - Local communities (infrastructure development and job creation)
- Examples of Trans-Border Parks:
  - Ai-Ais/Richtersveld Park (South Africa and Namibia)
  - Kgalagadi Park (South Africa and Botswana)
  - Limpopo-Shashe Park (South Africa, Botswana, and Zimbabwe)

- Early focus: Conservation of species; later expanded to ecosystem management (Schwella & Müller 1992:72).
- Initial regulations (1652): Protected gardens, soil, trees, and wildlife (Rabie & Fuggle 1992:13).
- 1800-1900: 
  - Enhanced laws for tree protection and wildlife conservation.

- 1919-1969: 
  - Legislation on air/water pollution, land use, pesticides (Rabie & Fuggle 1992:16-18).
  - Creation of the Department of Planning (1964) for coordinated environmental legislation (Schwella & Müller 1992:73).
- 1970s: Global environmental awareness; establishment of various committees focused on environmental conservation (Rabie & Fuggle 1992:18-19).
- 1980-1990:
  - Emphasis on national environmental policy; introduction of Environment Conservation Act 100 of 1982.
  - Council for the Environment established as a statutory body (Schwella & Müller 1992:74).
  - 1989: Environment Conservation Act 73 of 1989 created a comprehensive policy for environmental protection.
- Need for integrated management recognized; the principle of sustainable development established (section 24 of Constitution, 1996).
- Significant policies include:
  - Reconstruction and Development Programme (RDP, 1994)
  - White Paper on Environmental Management Policy (1997).

## Chapter 5: The Legacy of Apartheid
- **Impact on Environment**
  - Political policies, notably apartheid, significantly influenced the environment.
  - Apartheid distorted relations with nature, causing overcrowding and unsafe practices for basic needs.

### Effects of Apartheid on the Environment
- **Manmade Environment**
  - Restriction of residence for “non-Whites” led to overcrowding.
  - Urban areas experienced inadequate infrastructure and sanitation.
  - Pollution from industrial activity and urban waste disposal.
  
- **Natural Environment**
  - Unsustainable land management in homelands.
  - Overgrazed pastures, deforested areas, reduced stream flow.
  - Soil erosion due to high population density.

### Quality of Life Concerns
- Quality of life closely linked to environmental conditions.
- Poor housing, inadequate sanitation, and pollution negatively impacted physical and mental health.
  
### Conservation and Environmental Awareness
- Apartheid hindered conservation efforts and awareness.
- Lack of access to natural areas and education on environmental management.

### Recommendations for Improvement
- Address environmental injustices through government initiatives.
- **Reconstruction and Development Programme (RDP)** aims to:
  - Ensure sustainable resource use.
  - Provide equal access to resources and healthy living conditions.
  - Promote community participation and empowerment.

## Chapter 6: Environmental Management: A Functional Perspective

## Chapter 6: Environmental Management

### 6.3 Provincial Environmental Governance

#### 6.3.1 Legislative Competence of Provincial Legislatures
- Exclusive legislative power in environmental matters as per South Africa 1996, section 44 (2).
- Environment-related matters (Part A, Schedule 5):
  - Provincial culture
  - Archives (non-national)
  - Libraries (non-national)
  - Museums (non-national)
  - Provincial recreation

- Local government matters (Part B, Schedule 5):
  - Noise pollution
  - Refuse removal and dumps
  - Solid waste disposal
  - Municipal parks and recreation
  - Beaches and amusement facilities

#### 6.3.2 Provincial Executive Authority
- All nine provinces have departments for environmental affairs executing related legislations; examples include:
  - **Gauteng Department of Agriculture and Rural Development** operates in three branches: Agriculture, Conservation, Rural Development. 

### 6.3.3 Local Government Environmental Management

#### 6.3.3.1 Role of Local Councils
- Local councils can promulgate by-laws under section 156 (1) and (2) of the Constitution for environmental matters in Part B of Schedules 4 and 5.

#### 6.3.3.2 Local Executive Authority
- Section 156(4): National and provincial governments assign administration of specific matters to local governments if:
  - Best managed locally
  - Local capacity exists

- Local government handles:
  - Noise pollution
  - Littering
  - Solid waste management
  - Local nature reserves

- Example: **City of Johannesburg** - Department of Environment and Infrastructure Services.

### 6.4 Implementation of Legislation by Executive Institutions

#### 6.4.1 Performance of Direct Environmental Functions
- Direct functions relate to government-owned land (e.g., parks).
- **National Parks Act 57 of 1976, section 12**: Authority to construct necessary infrastructure in parks.
- Former **Forest Act 122 of 1984** outlined responsibilities for habitat restoration and soil erosion prevention.

#### 6.4.2 Establishment of Control Measures
- Delegation of legislative powers to executive institutions (ministers/MECs) for regulation.
- **Environmental Conservation Act 73 of 1989**: Minister can issue regulations on waste management and noise control.

- **Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998, section 77**: Ministerial regulation-making powers, e.g., fisheries management.

#### 6.4.3 Authorisation of Actions
- Authority to grant permits/licenses for actions otherwise prohibited.
- **Environment Conservation Act**: Requires a permit for waste disposal site operations.
- **Nuclear Energy Act 131 of 1993, section 51**: Licenses needed for nuclear installations and radioactive material handling.

## 6.4    Executive Institutions and Environmental Rights
### 6.4.4    Granting of Rights
- **Legislation**: Environmental legislation empowers institutions to grant rights potentially harmful to the environment.
- **Example**: Section 18 of the **Marine Living Resources Act of 1998** allows the Minister to grant rights for fishing and processing.

### 6.4.5    Exemptions
- **Legislation**: Institutions can exempt individuals from compliance with certain provisions.
- **Examples**:
  - Section 25 of **Prevention and Combating of Pollution of the Sea by Oil Act 6 of 1981** allows exemptions for vessels.
  - Section 81 of the **Marine Living Resources Act** permits ministerial exemptions under specific conditions.

### 6.4.6    Quasi-judicial Functions
- **Definition**: Quasi-judicial actions affect rights, privileges, and liberties; encompass permit/license decisions.
- **Example**: 
  - **Council for Nuclear Safety** reviews licenses for nuclear installations (**South Africa 1993, section 51**).
  - **Appeal Rights**: Section 35 of the **Environment Conservation Act** allows appeal against permit denials.

## 6.5    Enforcement of Environmental Legislation
### 6.5.1    Notices
- **Authority**: Institutions can issue notices to cease pollution.
- **Enforcement**: Non-compliance constitutes a criminal offense.
- **Legislation**: **Atmospheric Pollution Prevention Act 45 of 1965** outlines procedures for remediation.

### 6.5.2    Suspension or Cancellation of Authorization
- **Conditions**: Permits/licenses may be suspended or revoked if conditions are breached.
- **Legislation**: Section 28 of the **Marine Living Resources Act** details contravention grounds.

### 6.5.3    Detention of Property
- **Authority**: Certain institutions may detain property until damage costs are settled.
- **Legislation**: Section 19 of the **Prevention and Combating of Pollution of the Sea by Oil Act** enables vessel detention.

### 6.5.4    Search and Seizure
- **Authority**: Officials may enter and seize property if pollution is suspected.
- **Legislation**: Section 51 of the **Marine Living Resources Act** allows confiscation of fisheries-related items.

### 6.5.5    Enforcement of Court Orders
- **Authority**: Institutions can enforce compliance with court orders regarding environmental damages.
- **Legislation**: Section 29(7) of the **Environment Conservation Act** allows institutions to repair damage if the transgressor fails.

## 6.6    Obstacles to Optimal Environmental Management
- **Fragmentation**: Numerous acts complicate implementation and enforcement.
- **Lack of Priority**: Environmental management is not prioritized, leading to insufficient funding and personnel.
- **Financial Solutions**:
  - Airport tax for tourists.
  - Allocation from gambling revenues.
  - Tax incentives for donations to environmental preservation.
  
- **Judicial Challenges**: Fixed fines limit judicial discretion; alternative penalties like community service should be considered.

## 6.7    Intergovernmental Relations and Environmental Management
### Cooperation Principles (Constitution, Section 41)
- Government levels must:
  - Foster friendly relations.
  - Assist and support each other.
  - Inform and consult on common interests.
  - Coordinate actions and legislation.
  - Adhere to procedures to avoid litigation.

### Legislative Conflicts
- **National Legislation**: Prevails over provincial legislation if:
  - It cannot be suitably regulated at the provincial level.
  - Uniform treatment across the Republic is required.
  - It is necessary for national security and environmental conservation (Section 146 of the Constitution).

## 6.8    Review
- Environmental management complexity arises from multi-tiered governance and various stakeholders, including NGOs. Further examination of non-governmental roles will be addressed in **Learning Unit 7**.

### 8.3 THE RECONSTRUCTION AND DEVELOPMENT PROGRAMME (RDP)

- **Overview**
  - RDP: socio-economic framework adopted in 1994
  - Objectives:
    - Basic needs satisfaction (housing, water)
    - Human resources development (education, training)
    - Economic development
    - Societal democratisation (ANC, 1994:1)

- **Environmental Considerations**
  - RDP objectives align with sustainable development principles.
  - Requires careful natural resource management.
  - Environmental degradation from apartheid has lasting effects.
  - Environmental considerations essential to prevent further damage.

- **Job Creation and Environmental Impact**
  - Natural resources critical for work (food, mining, manufacturing).
  - Careful management of water, land, renewable energy essential.
  - Wildlife and nature reserves contribute to job creation and tourism.
  - Recycling industry offers economic and job opportunities. 
  - Housing projects must avoid ecologically sensitive areas. 

- **Development Implications**
  - Economic development requires environmental impact studies.
  - Promotion of recycling and eco-friendly technologies.
  - Education in environmental matters enhances public awareness.
  
- **Policy Framework**
  - White Paper on Environmental Management emphasizes integrated, sustainable environmental management.
  - Environmental considerations must inform all decisions in project planning (ANC, 1994:39, 40).

#### 8.3.1 The Working for Water (WfW) Programme
- Launched in 1995 to address invasive alien plants and unemployment.
- Focus on:
  - Enhancing water security.
  - Improving ecological integrity.
  - Sustainable land use.
- Achievements:
  - Cleared over one million hectares of invasive plants.
  - Offered jobs/training to marginalized individuals (Müller, 2007:49).
- Multi-departmental initiative involving various government departments.

#### 8.3.2 The Working on Fire (WoF) Programme
- Initiated in 2003 to manage veld and forest fires.
- Basis: National Veld and Forest Fire Act 101 of 1998.
- Programme objectives:
  - Promote sound land management.
  - Job creation and skills development in firefighting.
  - Resource pooling for cost-effective fire management.
- Achievements:
  - Establishment of firefighting bases and training for over 1,000 firefighters (Müller, 2007:54).

### 8.4 REVIEW
- RDP's primary objectives align with sustainable development, serving as a strategy for achieving sustainability in South Africa.
- Implementation must prioritize environmental sensitivity to avoid resource depletion.

## Learning Unit 9: INTEGRATED ENVIRONMENTAL MANAGEMENT

### 9.1 INTRODUCTION
- Development in South Africa must consider environmental protection.
- Integrated Environmental Management (IEM) processes guide the inclusion of environmental considerations in development planning.

### 9.2 MEANING AND OBJECTIVES OF INTEGRATED ENVIRONMENTAL MANAGEMENT (IEM)
- Established by the Council for the Environment in 1984; a national environmental policy recommended in 1989.
- IEM aims to balance development and conservation by incorporating environmental considerations systematically during all development phases.

### 9.3 PHASES OF IEM
- **Phase 1:** Proposal development and environmental impact assessment.
- **Phase 2:** Consideration of the proposal and decision-making.
- **Phase 3:** Implementation of the development proposal.

**Key Parties Involved:**
- Developer: Individual/institution pursuing development.
- Government: Local, provincial, or national bodies approving proposals.
- Other stakeholders: Affected communities and interest groups.

#### 9.3.1 Phase 1: Proposal Development and Environmental Impact Assessment
- Proposal initiation for contemplated activities (e.g., construction).
- Steps include:
  - Notification and consultation with affected parties.
  - Consultation with government institutions for required permissions.
  - Identification of alternatives and environmental impact reduction strategies.
- **Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)**:
  - Required if proposal significantly impacts the environment.
  - Initial EA for uncertain impacts or activities listed in IEM documents.

**Activity & List References:**
- Activities with significant adverse effects (e.g., construction projects, hazardous waste transport).
- Environments requiring assessment (e.g., conservation areas, wetlands).

**Regulations (Gov Gazette, 5 Sept 1997):**
- Governs EIAs, identifying activities with potential significant effects.

**Approval Process:** 
- Developers must appoint independent consultants to follow regulations.

**Steps for Approval:**
1. **Submission of Application:**
   - Application to relevant provincial government or local authority.
   - Special referrals to Minister in specific cases (importance or policy implications).
   
2. **Submission of Scoping Report:**
   - Contains project description, environmental impact potential, issues, alternatives, and public consultation.

3. **Investigation and Environmental Impact Report:**
   - In-depth investigation into environmental impacts with a study plan submission before the EIR.
   - The report should cover alternative assessments, public participation, and issues raised.

#### 9.3.2 Phase 2: Consideration of Application and Decision-Making by Government
- After EIR receipt, government decides to approve or reject with record documentation.
- Appeals allowed within 30 days of decision issuance.

**Conditions for Approval:**
- Environmental management plans and contracts to enforce compliance during project implementation.

#### 9.3.3 Phase 3: Implementation of Development Proposal
- Approval leads to project implementation.
- Developer must monitor:
  - Compliance with condition approvals.
  - Mitigation measures and adherence to plans.
- Monitoring evaluates mitigation effectiveness and accuracy of environmental predictions.

#### 9.4 Review
- Auditing should occur after project completion to assess environmental impact.
- **Objectives of Auditing**:
  - Evaluate effectiveness of environmental planning.
  - Verify accuracy of investigation outcomes.
  - Reflect on decisions made during the process.
  - Assess effectiveness of approval conditions and monitoring.

- Information gleaned from audits enhances the IEM process (South Africa, Department of Environmental Affairs 1992; Council for the Environment 1989).
  
- **Importance of IEM**:
  - Essential for environmental consideration throughout development phases—from planning to implementation.
  - Achieves balance between environmental conservation and development.
