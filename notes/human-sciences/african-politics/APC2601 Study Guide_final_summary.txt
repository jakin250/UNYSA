## THEME 1
### Introduction to processes of political change

#### Learning unit 1
### 1. Processes of political change

- **Learning Outcomes**
  - Differentiate between external and internal political change.
  - Define methods of political change.

#### 1.1 Introduction
- 1994: South Africa transitioned from National Party apartheid to a democratically elected government.
- Political change affects citizens' lives and involves authoritative value allocation and power relations.
- **Political Change**: Loss of existing leadership or change in governance type (democracy, republic, monarchy, etc.).

#### 1.2 Political Change
- Political change is normal; leaders retire or are re-elected.
- Stable democracies experience smooth transitions; unstable democracies may face civil conflicts.
- Political change can lead to instability and violence during transitions.
  
##### Types of Political Change:
- **Internal Political Change**: Driven by citizens via constitutional means (elections) or unconstitutional means (coup d’état).
- **External Political Change**: Influenced by foreign powers through military threats or sanctions.

##### Methods of Political Change:
- Revolution
- Civil War
- Coup d’état
- Anticolonial Struggle
- Constitutional Means

- **Revolution**: Overthrow of established government by subjected people.
- **Civil War**: Conflict among citizens, including military and civilian factions.
- **Coup d’état**: Sudden illegal government overthrow, typically violent.
- **Anticolonial Struggle**: Peaceful protests, with exceptions (e.g., Kenya).
- **Constitutional Means**: Peaceful elections.

#### 1.3 Conclusion
- Post-1990s, African countries still struggle with democracy due to weak institutions, unconstitutional changes, and military threats.
- Ongoing challenges in democratization address institutionalization, opposition strength, and civil conflict.

## Democracy and Political Change  

### 2.2 DEMOCRACY
- Restoration of democracy requires understanding its meaning; various interpretations exist, especially in Africa.
- Democracy perceived as overused/abused, lacking a precise definition (Arblaster 1987:1).
  
### 2.3 WESTERN LIBERAL DEMOCRACY
- Liberal democracy linked to Western nations (e.g., USA, UK); characterized by:
  - Multi-party systems
  - Regular elections
  - Civil liberties
  - Rule of law and human rights
- Abraham Lincoln's definition: government "of the people, by the people, for the people" (Heywood 2007:72).
- Participation occurs directly or indirectly (Heywood 2007:74):
  - **Direct Democracy**: Public expresses views directly.
  - **Representative Democracy**: Public elects representatives to make decisions.
- Democratic organizations operate freely based on democratic principles.

#### Models of Democracy (Heywood 2007:76)
- **Classic Democracy**: Based on self-government (Ancient Greece).
- **Protective Democracy**: Citizens have freedom to live as they choose.
- **Developmental Democracy**: Broadens popular participation.
- **People’s Democracy**: Focuses on class power distribution.

### 2.4 ELECTIONS
- Elections vital for democracy; allow citizen involvement through representative selection.
  
#### Electoral Systems
- Varied systems across nations; multiparty systems versus one-party systems.
- Functions of elections (Heywood 2007:255-256):
  - Recruiting politicians
  - Making governments
  - Providing representation
  - Influencing policy
  - Educating voters
  - Building legitimacy
  - Strengthening elites
- Elections must be transparent, free, and fair.

#### Conditions for Free and Fair Elections
1. **Competitiveness**: Freedom of opposition party participation.
2. **Periodicity**: Elections held at regular intervals.
3. **Inclusivity**: Broad citizen participation; enfranchisement versus disenfranchisement.
4. **Definitiveness**: Elections yield real power to elected leaders.

### 2.5 INALIENABLE RIGHTS
- Civil liberties essential for democracy: freedom of speech, assembly, expression, and movement.
- Right to express views freely without reprisals; press unrestricted.
- Rule of Law: Applies equally to all citizens (Heywood 2007:326); equality before the law essential.

### 2.6 CONSTITUTION
- Constitution: Supreme law, limits governmental power; defines governance (Heywood 2007:321).
- Functions:
  - Empowers governments
  - Establishes unifying values
  - Provides stability
  - Legitimizes regimes
  - Protects freedoms
- "Constitutionalism": Governance accordance with established rules.

### 2.7 MAJOR REFERENTS OF A DEMOCRATIC REGIME (Osaghae 1998:134)
1. Pluralism and multi-partyism; competitive politics.
2. Popular participation; universal suffrage and accountability.
3. Rule of law; respect for human rights.
4. Constitutionalism; civil control of military and efficacy of institutions.

## 15. THEME 3: The Military and Political Change in Africa

### 16. Learning Unit 3: Political Military Coup and Political Change in Postcolonial Africa

#### LEARNING OUTCOMES
- Explain “military coups”
- Discuss political change complexities in postcolonial Africa
- Analyse military regime responses to dissent
- Explore the concept of a good coup and its legitimacy

#### KEY CONCEPTS
- Military coup, political change, dissent, good coup

### 3.1 INTRODUCTION
- Military coups viewed as significant agents of political change in postcolonial Africa.
- Analysis contrasts successful vs. failed coups; highlights differing impacts on political power acquisition.
- Military's political role is not unique to Africa; similar incidents in Latin America and Asia occur.
- Postcolonial Africa often experiences political change solely through coups, even post-election.
- Historical context: more than 200 coups recorded since 1960; first coup in Egypt (1958).
- Military's influence often overshadow civilian governmental power, undermining constitutional authority.

### 3.2 WHAT IS A MILITARY COUP?
- **Military coup (coup d'état):** Sudden overthrow of government by military forces, including state army and militia.
- High-ranking officers typically lead such actions with junior officer support.
- Normalization of coups indicates a perceived effective access to political power.
- Military coups often justified by claiming civilian government wrongdoings to gain public support.
- Mixed outcomes of coups; some civilian governments function effectively despite military takeovers.
- Authoritarian military governance often leads to suppressed dissent and oppressive rule.

### Political Change Dynamics
- Political change can be interpreted both positively and negatively: 
  - Democratic aspirations may be overthrown by military forces leading to non-democratic regimes.
- Complex relationship between military governance and democratic transformation; potential for military jurisdictions to claim democratic intentions while engaging in authoritarian practices.
- Rhetoric vs. action: military governments may invoke populist narratives to retain citizen support while failing to enact genuine political reforms.
- Importance of functional democratic institutions in mitigated military rule impacts.

## 3.3 MILITARY REGIME: CHANGE WITHOUT CHANGE?
- **Political Change**: Not inherently positive; must be legitimate (democratic, electoral).
- **Good Governance**: Expected in African states; military coups challenge this.
- **Military Legitimacy**: Military is not a democratic institution; involvement in politics remains authoritarian.
- **Coup Justification**: Military faces challenges of legitimacy when denouncing civilian government corruption.
- **Citizens' Response to Coups**: Often leads to situations reminiscent of previous regimes, potentially worse.
- **Accountability**: Military regimes lack citizen accountability mechanisms, operate on directives.
- **Nature of Change**: Military coups often merely cosmetic; serve self-justification for power.
- **Counter-Coups**: Often arise from discontent within the military, leading to political instability.
  - Tendencies for junior officers to challenge established military leaders.
  - Counter-coups lack citizen legitimacy; often result in failed coups.

---

## 3.4 MILITARY COUPS AND OPPOSITION TO DISSENT
- **Citizen Obedience**: Military regimes demand compliance, dismiss dissent as a threat.
- **Dissent Criminalization**: Military treats dissent as treason; activists face severe punishment.
- **Violation of Rights**: Right to dissent is often suppressed through censorship and other means.
- **Perception of Change**: Military frames its actions as for the public good, disregarding dissent.
- **Stability vs. Change**: Occurrence of coups leads to minimal change; citizens’ engagement suppressed.
- **Legitimacy and Accountability**: Military seeks legitimacy by presenting itself as acting for citizens but resists scrutiny.

---

## 3.5 WHY SO MANY MILITARY COUPS IN AFRICA?
- **Trend of Coups**: Military frequently ascends to power via coups rather than elections.
- **Power Dynamics**: The state has enabled military power exceeding that of civilian authority.
- **Recurring Phenomenon**: Coups followed by counter-coups; indicative of military disloyalty to political regimes.
- **Contagion Effect**: Successful coups inspire similar actions in neighboring regions.
- **Authoritarian Governance**: Military frequently feels marginalized, leading to discontent and possible coups.
- **Grievance-Based Coups**: Coping with discrimination can incite military to act against existing regimes.
- **Complexities of Military Coups**: Acknowledge diversity in Africa; not all nations experience coups uniformly.

---

## 3.6 A GOOD COUP: THE CASE STUDY OF GUINEA CONAKRY 2008 COUP
- **Definition of a "Good Coup"**: Military takeover happens in political vacuum; president dies, or there's no head of state.
- **Historical Context**: General Lansame Conte's tenure (1984-2008) characterized by authoritarianism post-coup.
- **Conte’s Regime**: Marked by corruption and repression; maintained power through manipulation.
- **Post-Conte Dynamics**: Colonel Moussa Dadis Camara's coup after Conte's death illustrated opportunistic takeover.
- **Legitimacy of Camara's "Good Coup"**: Supported by the formation of "National Council for Democracy and Development" (NCDD).
- **NCDD’s Function**: Provided a consultative platform; promised multi-party elections, enhancing legitimacy.
- **International Response**: Notably criticized by organizations despite local acceptance; elections held leading to Alpha Conde's presidency.

### 4.1 INTRODUCTION
- Focus on prevention strategies against military coups in Africa.  
- Emphasis on African Union (AU) and Organisation for African Unity (OAU) mechanisms.  
- Military coups normalized; necessary enforcement of preventive mechanisms to uphold constitutional governance.  

### 4.2 MILITARY COUPS AND NEW WAVE OF CONSTITUTIONALISM
- Emphasis on power acquisition through constitutional means, primarily elections.  
- Definition: Military coups as “constitutional coups” where unconstitutional acts are falsely legitimized.  
- AU condemns military coups to uphold constitutional principles; repeated military interventions are increasingly contested as unconstitutional.  
- Discussion of military's role as both protector and usurper of state power; calls for stringent enforcement of constitutional measures against military incursions into politics.   

### 4.3 THE IDEA OF DEMILITARISATION
- AU and regional communities mandate demilitarization, separating military from political command.  
- Demilitarization seen as a challenge; military often legitimizes its continued influence post-coup as necessary for stability.  
- Notable views by William Gutteridge on the oversimplification of demilitarization; discussions on military's historical entrenchment in African governance.  
- Emphasizes the need for absolute removal of military from politics to foster political stability and effective governance.  

### 4.4 STRATEGIES IN DEALING WITH DEMILITARISATION
#### 4.4.1 Non-interventionist Stance
- Sovereignty invoked to justify military takeovers; led to passive OAU/AU role in addressing contested governance.  
- Challenges arise when military rule gains public support; intervention viewed as a sovereignty breach.  

#### 4.4.2 Interventionist Stance
- AU's role aimed at restoring constitutional order; emphasizes inclusion and non-antagonistic approaches for stability.  
- Challenges include resistance from entrenched regimes manipulating constitutional structures; necessitates effective political will to support constitutional governance.  

### 4.5 THE LOMÉ DECLARATION
- Adopted in July 2000 as a framework to address unconstitutional government changes, including military coups.  
- Defines unconstitutional changes of government:  
  - **Military coup d'état** against democratically elected governments.  
  - **Mercenary intervention**.  
  - **Armed group replacement** of democratically elected governments.

### 5.1 Introduction to Opposition Politics
- Emergence due to weaknesses of one-party systems.
- Calls for multiparty democracy in late 1980s.

### 5.2 Factors Leading to Multiparty Politics in Africa
#### 5.2.1 The State’s Loss of Authority
- Loss of legitimacy by states (e.g., apartheid South Africa).
- Consequences for refusal to adopt multiparty systems (e.g., DRC, Liberia).

#### 5.2.2 The Changing International Political Arena
- Ideological conflict during the Cold War influenced support for one-party states.
- Post-Cold War dynamics led to abandoned support for oppressive regimes (e.g., Mobutu).
- End of Cold War aided the fall of apartheid, impacting international aid conditionality.

#### 5.2.3 A Rejuvenated Civil Society
- Role of civil society in anti-colonial and anti-one-party movements.
- Key leaders (Archbishop Tutu, James Chiona) promoted human rights and multiparty democracy.

#### 5.2.4 Precedent
- Inspiration from Eastern Europe’s uprisings against communist rule.

### 5.3 The Importance of Multi-Party Democracy
- Checks and balances crucial for preventing power abuse (Sir Acton’s principle).

### 5.4 The Need for/Purpose of Political Parties
- Definition by Heywood (2013:222):
  1. Aim to win political office.
  2. Organized with formal membership.
  3. Broad issue focus.
  4. United by shared preferences.
- Roles per Canton (2007:7):
  1. Training and recruiting candidates.
  2. Formulating policies.
  3. Gathering societal demands.
  4. Government supervision.

### 5.5 Other Roles Played by Political Parties
- Representation of interests; vital link between state and society.
- Example: Uganda’s no-party democracy exemplifies rarity of democracy without parties (Schattschneider 1943).

### 5.6 The Role of Opposition Political Parties
- Important for democracy consolidation in Africa.
- Roles as per Chiroro (2006), Kiiza (2005):
  1. Shaping public policy.
  2. Mobilising voters.
  3. Government criticism.
  4. Consolidating democracy.
  5. Representing minority interests.
  6. Nation-building contribution.
  7. Anti-corruption efforts.
  8. Training future leaders.
  9. Monitoring elections.

### 5.7 The Weakness of Opposition Political Parties in Africa
- Post-1990s opportunities not matched by opposition quality.
- Weakness factors include:
#### 5.7.1 Fragmentation
- Highly fragmented political landscape (e.g., Ivory Coast, Senegal, Liberia).
- Types of fragmentation (Rakner & Svasand, 2005):
  - Formal, competitive, electoral, parliamentary.
  
#### 5.7.2 Limited Resources
- Incumbents control resources; oppositional revenue strategies include membership fees, donations.

#### 5.7.3 Personalistic Parties
- Dependence on charismatic leaders; weak grassroots structure.

#### 5.7.4 Lack of Mass Base
- Absence of links to organizations (e.g., unions); hampers recruitment and campaigning.

### Conclusion
- Challenges faced by opposition parties hinder their effectiveness in addressing the political landscape in Africa.
- Strategic reforms and unified approaches are necessary for strengthening political opposition.

## 6. New Optimism About Africa’s Change

### 6.1 Death of Democratic Government in Africa

- **Van de Walle (2002:67)**: “Death of democratic government has been vastly exaggerated.”
  - **Progress**: From 1989-2000, 70 presidential elections with multiple candidates occurred, up from 9 competitive multiparty elections (1985-1989).
  - **Variability**: African states exhibit different forms of democracy, ranging from liberal to hybrid regimes.

- **Three dangers to democracy in Africa** (Van der Walle 2002:66-67):
  1. **Underestimating gains**: Imperfections in democratic practices shouldn't overshadow improvements in competition and participation since the 1990s.
  2. **Rigorous standards**: Pessimism applies unrealistic standards of liberal democracy that even mature democracies fail to meet consistently.
  3. **Oversimplification**: Disappointment leads to categorizing diverse African democracies as "imperfect democracies."

### 6.2 Progress and Challenges in Democratisation

- **Osaghae (1998)**: Offers a glimmer of hope for African democracies despite criticisms of leaders' intentions.
  - **Democratic faltering reasons**: Remnants of authoritarian rule hindered democratization.
  - **Positive signs**:
    - Varying consolidation in democracies (e.g., South Africa, Tanzania, Benin).
    - Transition of military leaders into civilian political roles.
    - Serious adherence to democracy due to Western financial ties.

- **External Influences**:
  - **UN, Commonwealth, EU**: Critical monitoring and potential sanctions (e.g., Zimbabwe).
  - **SADC**: Sub-regional enforcement of democratic standards.
  - **Civil Society's Role**: Engaging in protests against undemocratic governance.

### 6.3 Emergence of New Regimes After Multiparty Elections

- **Nature of regimes post-elections**: Prelude to questions regarding genuineness of democratic transitions.
- **Freedom House classification (2002)**:
  - **Botswana and Mauritius**: Models of democratic rule.
  - **Other countries (e.g., Cameroon, Gambia, Togo)**: Limited change with incumbents retaining power.

- **Continued authoritarianism**: Multiparty elections result in incumbents practicing grudging tolerance of political plurality.

### 6.4 Increasing Support for Democratic Rule

- **Priser (2009)**: Assessment from authoritarian rule to elections demonstrating a positive trend towards democracy.
  - Concerns: Quality of elections increasingly criticized as biased or non-competitive.

- **Democratic safeguards introduced**:
  - **Presidential term limits** and **Independent Electoral Commissions (IEC)** as mechanisms.
  
- **Results from Freedom House (2009)**:
  - More countries recognized as 'free' or 'partly free' than in earlier assessments.
  - Military coups: Decrease from 24 (1960s) to 5 (2000-2008).

### 6.5 Classification of African Regimes

- **Priser's Categorization**:
  1. Full democracies: **Mauritius** (only country qualifying).
  2. Flawed democracies: **Botswana, South Africa, Benin, Cape Verde**.
  3. Hybrid regimes: 13.
  4. Authoritarian regimes: 23.

- Qualification Criteria for “Free”:
  - Political rights and civil liberties benchmarks separating flawed democracies from full democracies.

### 6.6 Conclusion

- **Optimism in democratisation**: Continued multiparty elections promise enhanced political participation and governance.
- **Recognition of multifaceted progress**: Acknowledgment of challenges alongside positive strides in democracy across Africa.

## 7. Consequences of Failed Transition: The Case of the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) and Its Failed Democratic Transition  

### Learning Outcomes  
- Discuss reasons for Zaire’s descent into chaos  
- Evaluate Mobutu’s decision to transform the political system  
- Explain National Conference’s lack of progress  
- Analyse Zairian opposition’s performance during the National Conference  

### Key Concepts  
- Failed democracy, failed transition, weak state, kleptocracy, state collapse, warlord state, corruption  

### 7.1 Introduction  
- Sense of optimism about Africa’s democratization juxtaposed with civil unrest (Du Plessis, Jansen, Siebrits, 2014).  
- Failed transitions have severe consequences; focus on DRC’s transition failure leading to civil war.  
- DRC: never held genuine elections, under military/single-party rule for over three decades.  
- Name changes: Belgian Congo → Zaire → Democratic Republic of Congo (1996 after Mobutu’s overthrow).  
- Zaire faced chaos post-independence (1960) from leadership disputes, worsened by Mobutu’s mismanagement and corruption.  

### 7.2 Mobutu’s Non-transition to Democracy  
- 1990s pressures to restore multiparty democracy post-Cold War and Eastern Europe collapse.  
- Mobutu agreed to public meetings and allowed the formation of political parties in 1990, leading to political confusion.  
- National Conference initiated to discuss political transformation but undermined by Mobutu’s manipulation and violence (Schatzberg, 1997).  
- Mobutu’s tactics involved fomenting violence and co-opting opposition members to stymie transition efforts.  

### 7.3 The Failed National Conference  
- National Conference stalled due to internal disputes among delegates and Mobutu's interference.  
- Mobutu’s co-opting strategies, including financial incentives for opposition members.  
- Coalition Sacred Union formed but lacked unity; opposition was fractured, failing to capitalize on strategic opportunities.  
- Competing power dynamics with Mobutu controlling coercion and treasury, while opposition’s Tshisekedi managed the High Council.  
- Mobutu dissolved "governments," further complicating the situation.  

### 7.4 The Weak State  
- Zaire characterized as leaderless and chaotic (World Bank declared insolvency in February 1994).  
- Governance effectively limited to urban centers; rural areas largely ignored by the administration (Thompson, 2000).  
- Opposition’s internal struggles and inability to form a unified resistance contributed to continued Mobutu’s control.  
- Kabila filled leadership vacuum, supported by regional dynamics, ultimately overthrowing Mobutu in 1997.  

### 7.5 Conclusion  
- Case study of DRC illustrates consequences of failed democratic transition and the ineffectiveness of the National Conference.  
- DRC labeled as weak, warlord, or collapsed state, reflecting challenges in consolidating democracy.

### 8.2 PAN-AFRICANISM  
- Tim Murithi defines **Pan-Africanism** as solidarity and cooperation among African leaders and societies.  
- No single definition; core elements include social/political equality, freedom from exploitation.  
- Historical context includes European slavery, colonialism, neo-colonialism, and globalization.  
- Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade: African abductions, cultural alienation, inferiority narratives by European thinkers (e.g., Voltaire, Kant).

- Key figures advocating African liberation and image rebuilding include:
  - **Harriet Tubman, Frederick Douglass, Marcus Garvey, Haile Selassie, Jomo Kenyatta, Malcolm X, Robert Mugabe.**

- Significant thinkers and works:
  - **W. E. B. Du Bois:** The Souls of Black Folk, The Negro  
  - **Cheikh Anta Diop:** The African Origin of Civilization  
  - **Frantz Fanon:** Black Skin White Masks, The Wretched of the Earth  

- **Outcome of colonization:** Collective effort needed; three blocs arose.
  
**The Brazzaville Bloc**  
- First meeting: Abidjan, 1960, included 12 **French-speaking countries**.  
- Advocated economic integration, supported France during Algeria's independence, varying views on communism.  

**The Casablanca Bloc**  
- Led by Nkrumah, first met in Casablanca, 1961; included Libya, Egypt, Ghana.  
- Supported Algerian liberation; advocated political and economic integration.  

**The Monrovia Bloc**  
- Met 1961 in Liberia; included 20 countries.  
- Influential in the OAU's values focusing on unity, cooperation, and independence.  
- **OAU Charter Objectives:**
  - Promote unity and solidarity  
  - Defend sovereignty and integrity  
  - Eradicate colonialism  
  - Promote international cooperation  

- **OAU's Struggles:**
  - Primacy on sovereignty limited intervention in military coups (e.g., Ghana, Uganda).
  - OAU's inability to act contributed to tragedies like the **Rwandan Genocide**.

- Late 1990s, OAU shifted policies; notable intervention in **Sierra Leone** via **ECOMOG**.

### 8.3 AFRICAN UNION  
- Established in **2002** to address OAU’s ineffectiveness.  
- Governed by the **Constitutive Act**, with core objectives similar to OAU but allowing intervention in grave situations.  

**AU Objectives:**  
1. Unity and solidarity among African states.  
2. Defense of member states' sovereignty and integrity.  
3. Acceleration of political and socio-economic integration.  
4. Promotion of African positions on key issues.  
5. Encouragement of international cooperation.  
6. Promotion of peace, security, and stability.  

**AU Organ Structure:**  
1. Assembly of the Union  
2. Executive Council  
3. Pan-African Parliament  
4. Court of Justice  
5. Commission  
6. Permanent Representatives Committee  
7. Specialized Technical Committees  
8. Economic, Social and Cultural Council  
9. Financial Institutions  

- Contrast with OAU: AU can intervene in crises (e.g., **war crimes, genocide**).  
- Past actions include peacekeeping missions in various African nations, reflecting a commitment to uphold stability.  
- Emphasis on zero-tolerance for unconstitutional power changes; suspensions of member states (e.g., **Egypt, Mali**).

## 9. Regional Economic Communities

### 9.1 Introduction

- **Regional integration**: Elimination of discrimination among countries (Balassa 1961).
- **Stages**:
  - **De jure integration**: States sign treaties for cooperation.
  - **De facto integration**: Implementation of policies, may involve ceding state powers.

### 9.2 Regional Organisations in Africa

#### The Arab Maghreb Union (AMU)
- Established post-WW I; formalized in 1989.
- Conflicts (e.g., Western Sahara) hinder cooperation.
- Decision-making by **Summit of Heads of State**; headquartered in Rabat, Morocco.

#### Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS)
- Initiated in mid-1960s; officially established in 1975.
- Aims for a borderless region; works on free movement and peace.
- **ECOMOG**: Peacekeeping unit for civil conflict resolution in Liberia and Sierra Leone.
- Administrative change in 2007; now a **Commission**.

#### Economic Community of Central African States (ECCAS)
- Origins in the Central African Customs and Economic Union.
- Formed in 1983, addressing post-colonial economic relations.
- Struggles due to conflicts; members include Angola, Chad, Congo, etc.

#### East African Community (EAC)
- Continuation of earlier cooperation post-1977 dissolution.
- Treaty signed in 1999, operational by 2000.
- Includes Burundi, Kenya, Rwanda, etc.

#### Southern African Development Community (SADC)
- Established in 1990 to promote political liberation and equitable integration.
- Transitioned from SADCC to SADC treaty in 1992.
- Secretariat in Gaborone; involved in peacekeeping efforts and regional programs.

### 9.3 Conflict and Regional Integration

- Civil conflicts negatively impact economic performance and regional integration.
- War affects business cycles, infrastructure, and prioritization of policy resources.
- Disruption of trade relations complicates integration efforts (Bah 2013:523).

## Globalisation: A Threat to Africa’s Fragile Democracies

### Negative Effects of Economic Reform Policies
- Economic reforms often disempower populations.
- Governments resort to repressive measures to enforce compliance with austerity.

### General Dangers of Globalisation
- **Plattner (2002:60-61)**: Globalisation blurs national barriers and undermines decision-making at national levels.
  - Threatens both authoritarian and democratic systems.
  - May require limits on globalisation to preserve democracy.
  
### Merits and Demerits of Globalisation
- Globalisation has both positive and negative aspects.

### Iwara's Examination of Globalisation
- **Iwara (2007)**: Globalisation as a system of global governance uniting nations.
  - Focuses on African state sovereignty in global contexts.
  - Concerns of marginalisation and Western hegemony in global governance.

### Schools of Thought on Globalisation
- **Marxist School**: Critical perspective emphasizing loss of state autonomy to international actors.
- **Neoliberal School**: Positive outlook on globalisation as cooperative and beneficial for unity.

### Sovereignty Concerns
- **Sovereignty**: Originating in the 16th century, denoting supreme authority over a state.
- **Three Dimensions of Sovereignty (Iwara 2007:60)**:
  - **Legal Sovereignty**: Authority to govern without external interference.
  - **Political Sovereignty**: Control over law-making and governance processes.
  - **De Jure Sovereignty**: Legal recognition of a state’s power.

### Unequal Relations Between Nations
- Globalisation promotes disparities; powerful nations dictate terms.
- Poor representation of African states in international forums (Iwara 2007:62).

### Dependency on Globalisation
- African states' dependency on aid leads to submission to northern powers.
- Expectation to conform to dictates for development assistance.

### Exporting Western Liberal Democracy
- Seen as a major goal; pressure from Western nations influences African governance.
- **Iwara (2007:64)**: Proponents argue liberal democracy enhances economic rights but undermines African state autonomy.

### Critique of Liberal Democracy
- Iwara expresses skepticism towards liberal democracy as detrimental to African autonomy.
