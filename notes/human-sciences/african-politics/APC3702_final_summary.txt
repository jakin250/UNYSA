## Nationalism
- **Definition**: Ideology promoting the nation as the primary community in a state, expressing a single national culture.
- **Key Characteristics**:
   - Nation as a group sharing cultural, ethnic, and linguistic traits.
   - Originated from Western Europe.

### African Nationalism
- **Concept**: A movement advocating for independence across the continent.
- **Ideology**: Embraces all people within a state, regardless of cultural or ethnic differences.

- **Resistance to Colonialism**:
   - Question of defining "foreignness" and the implications of historical resistances (e.g., against Romans, Ottomans).
   - Smith (1983:66-67): Resistance may define a colonized people's political unity against "foreigners".

### Capitalist Imperialism Theory
- **Overview**: Colonizers exploit territories to resolve internal issues.
- **Objective of European Imperialism**: Extract profit from labor and control political/military machinery (Smith 1983:67).

### Stages of Anti-Colonial Reaction in Sub-Saharan Africa
- Developed as:
   1. Elite nationalism from educated urban classes.
   2. Mass nationalism involving broader classes, contrasting European nationalism focusing on pre-existing nationhood.
- Common goals: Independence, decolonization, and development (Smith 1983:68-69).

### Interest Group Thesis
- **Definition**: Explains how industrialization affects politics in underdeveloped societies.
- Kautsky illustrates: Modernization unity against colonial powers prevails over internal conflict (Smith 1983:71).
- **Nationalism**: Manifestation of intellectuals’ frustrations aligned with urban classes challenging colonialism (Smith 1983:71-72).

### Nationalism and Social Revolutions
- Nationalist movements occasionally trigger social revolutions (e.g., in China, Cuba, France).
- Varying degrees of nationalism’s role in change and penetration of social groups.
- Nationalism can demand political reorganization; often backed by potential violence or demonstrative acts (Smith 1983:79-82).

## Democracy and Constitutionalism
- **African Perspective on Democracy**: Linked to communal egalitarianism; governance through consensus.
- **Defining Democracy**:
   - Requires adherence to human dignity, political participation, and limits on government actions.
- **Key Aspects of Democratic Theory**:
   - Justice Hugo L. Black on citizen participation in elections (Murphy 1993:3).
   - Viable democracy needs representative electoral processes, freedom of communication, and public debate.

### Constitutional Democracy
- **Constitution Definition**: Fundamental framework governing a political system.
- **Functions**:
   - May serve as a sham or reality; essential for political legitimacy, governance processes, and rights protection (Murphy 1993:7-8).
   - Should outline government authority, terms of officials, and citizen rights.
   - Acts as a covenant, embodying collective national identity.

### Constitutional Change
- **Process**: Inevitable and needs substantive limits (Murphy 1993:13-14).
- **Interpretation**: Must involve public approval or referendums, distinct from official interpretations.

### Conclusion
- Constitutionalism: Balances governance and protection against tyranny, with an emphasis on participatory democracy and individual autonomy.

## 2.6 CONSTITUTIONS AND CONSTITUTIONALISM IN AFRICA

### CONSTITUTION
- Authoritative document establishing highest law.
- Set of rules for government institutions, regulating relationships and defining state-individual interactions.  
  **(Heywood 2002:294)**

### CONSTITUTIONALISM
- Practice of limited government via constitution.
- Exists when political processes are effectively constrained by constitutional rules.  
  **(Heywood 2002:297)**

### Disregard for Constitutions in Africa
- **Reasons for Violation of Constitutions:**
  - Ideological: Constitutions seen to limit government authority.
  - Analytical: Law’s relationship to power inadequately conceived; constitutions fail to regulate power effectively.
- Post-independence constitutions demonstrate a lack of respect; viewed as rhetorical by elites.  
  **(Okoth-Ogendo 1993:65-66)**

### Dilemma of Constitutionalism
- **Key Dilemmas:**
  - Abandon constitutionalism study due to lack of development.
  - Continue theorizing hoping elites will recognize constitutional values.
- African elites exhibit a commitment to constitutionalism while rejecting classical liberal notions.

### Historical Context and Legacies
- **Legacies Inherited from Colonial Rule:**
  1. **Legal Order vs. Constitutional Order**: Colonial systems maintained coercive power.
  2. **Fragmented Political Culture**: Conflicts institutionalized; political parties disassociated from constitutional values.
  3. **State's Economic Power**: Dominance in policy and development questions.
  4. **Colonial Constitutional Models**: African constitutions adapted from Western models, lacking democratic essence and rationale.

### Manipulation of Constitutional Processes
- Strategies to undermine constitutions:
  - Coups, delayed elections, and selective candidacy.
- **Politicization of Constitutions**: Used as tools for manipulation by granting extensive powers to executive and limiting political freedoms.
  - Declaring constitutions a liability helped facilitate control.

### Centralization of Power
- Shift of power towards a single executive undermines separation of powers.
- Presidents enjoy immunity and indefinite eligibility via constitutional changes.
- Political parties become instruments of the presidency, shrinking political discourse and control.
- Security powers exercised without emergency declarations, justified by national security claims.

### Legislative and Judicial Influence
- Increasingly, power is concentrated in the executive, challenging rule of law and effective governance.

### Key References
- **Okoth-Ogendo (1993:65-79)**: Analysis of constitutionalism's challenges and manipulation in Africa.

# MAU MAU REBELLION

## Overview
- Mau Mau originated as a Kikuyu rebellion, evolving into a nationalist movement.
- Lacked active support from other ethnic communities; however, it challenged White colonial rule.
- Causes of rebellion remain debated; Kenyatta's involvement is unclear.
- Resulted in significant violence, predominantly affecting Africans. 

## Key Factors Leading to the Mau Mau Rebellion
- **Land question:** Long-standing grievances over land ownership and use.
- **Economic grievances:** Overcrowded Kikuyu reserves created dissatisfaction.
- **Racial discrimination:** Growing complaints from educated Africans regarding systemic inequalities.

## Impact of the Mau Mau Rebellion
- Revealed the need for the British to maintain a military presence to address African nationalist demands.
- Economic considerations prompted reconsideration of British colonial strategies.
- Shifted the colonial discourse from European supremacy to the necessity of representative governance.

## Political Awakening
- The rebellion catalyzed political engagement across all ethnic groups.
- Significant relaxation of apartheid practices and increased political fragmentation among settlers.
- Emergence of a national consciousness among previously politically inactive tribes, notably after the banning of the Kikuyu political vehicle (KAU) during the emergency period.

## New Leadership and Political Dynamics
- Emergence of a pan-Kenyan leadership: 
  - **Tom Mboya (Luo):** Prominent union leader and political advocate; addressed broader political issues beyond labor.
  - **Oginga Odinga (Luo):** Key political figure opposing government policies; later vice president of independent Kenya.
  - **Daniel arap Moi (Kalenjin):** Future president; part of the legislative council.

## Legislative Council and Political Development
- Legislative council membership provided a platform for African political leaders to influence discussions on independence.
- Calls for the release of Kenyatta became central to political strategy.
- Fragmentation led to the creation of the **Kenya African National Union (KANU)** and **Kenya African Democratic Union (KADU)**, with foundational leadership roles established.

## Independence Negotiations 
- 1960 Lancaster House Conference aimed at negotiating peaceful transition to an African-majority government.
- Conflict between KANU and KADU over constitutional form:
  - **KANU:** Advocated for strong unitary government.
  - **KADU:** Preferred a federal system to protect minority rights.
- Agreement reached mixed federal and centralist principles, later abandoned by KANU.

## Conclusion
- The Mau Mau rebellion significantly influenced Kenya's path to independence, addressing issues of land, political representation, and racial dynamics.
- KANU's ultimate rise solidified political power, reflecting a shift towards majority rule and independence from colonial governance.

### 4.1 EMERGENCE OF KANU AS THE RULING PARTY  
- **Political Context:**
  - Debates among political parties on minority rights before independence.  
  - KANU's 1963 manifesto: committed to a "democratic African socialist" state (Sanger & Nottingham 1964:9).

- **KANU's Policy Framework:**
  - Major policy statement: African Socialism (Gertzel, Goldschmidt & Rothchild 1969:138-139) included:
    - Political democracy.
    - Mutual social responsibility.
    - Various ownership forms.
    - Controls for property use.
    - Diffusion of ownership to prevent economic concentration.
    - Progressive taxation for equitable wealth distribution.

- **Ideological Foundations:**
  - Policies rooted in African traditional values.
  - Constitution grants rights regardless of individual backgrounds.

- **Leadership Ambiguity:**
  - Socialist commitment not fully doctrinal; Tom Mboya’s statement illustrates ideological indecisiveness (Gertzel 1970:52).

- **Opposition to Kenyatta’s Policies:**
  - Criticism came mainly from KANU backbenchers; KADU disbanded in 1964.
  - Formed opposition: Kenyan People's Union (KPU) led by Oginga Odinga.
  - Odinga’s manifesto criticized KANU for failure on land redistribution (Odinga 1967:303).

- **Land Redistribution Issues:**
  - Dispute between conservatives and radicals within KANU over land ownership and wealth distribution remained pivotal.

- **Impact of Trade Unionism:**
  - Government's restriction on trade unions to contain opposition.
  - Unions adopted a focus on economic gains rather than ideological conflicts (Markovitz 1977:270).

- **Constitutional Implications:**
  - Resignation of KPU MPs required mini-elections; KANU's victory highlighted Kenyatta’s prestige.

- **Ethnic Politics:**
  - KANU dominated by Kikuyu, while Odinga represented Luo minorities.
  - Kenyatta’s centralized power and elimination of opposition questioned as means of unity post-independence.

- **Kenyatta's Leadership and Critiques:**
  - Kenyatta viewed as a unifying figure; however, criticisms arose regarding his disconnect from political realities (Tamarkin 1978:298-299).
  - Kenyatta's perceived charisma was arguably a myth; observed failures weakened his influence among Kikuyu (Throup 1985:429-432).

## 5.2 SECURITY FORCES

- Post-independence military structure based on British model, separating military from civil government.
- 1964 coup attempts in East Africa prompted government to address ethnic imbalances.
- Initial military had majority Kamba and Kalenjin, perceived as loyal to British, avoiding Kikuyu due to their militant history.
- Ethnic redress initiated in 1964, accelerated after 1971 coup attempt by non-Kikuyu officers.
- Establishment of the **General Services Unit (GSU)** as paramilitary force for regime protection.
- GSU seen as coercive arm against opponents, predominantly Kikuyu members.
- **Tamarkin (1978:301)**: Mixture of British tradition, ethnic solidarity, and material interests solidified military as regime’s stability factor.

## 5.3 PRESIDENCY

- Kenyatta’s presidency began as Prime Minister while **Governor-General** represented the monarch.
- December 1964: Kenyatta abolishes regional constitution, declares Kenya a republic, aggregates head of state and executive powers.
- Presidential authority emerged as primary power source, with cabinet ministers acting primarily as advisors.
- Formation of the "inner circle"/"kitchen cabinet" comprised of Kikuyu ministers and allies, influencing policy formulation (Tamarkin 1978:302).
- Parliament functions as a safety valve for dissent and represents public demands, with MPs needing to be responsive to their constituencies (Tamarkin 1978:305).
- Presidency prioritized existing colonial civil service and provincial administration, with commissioners holding significant control (Tamarkin 1978:307).

## 5.4 DYNAMICS OF PARTICIPATION 

- Kenyatta established a closed inner circle of power; elections were nominally competitive within KANU only.
- Only KANU could nominate presidential candidates; membership restrictions limited participation by less affluent individuals.
- Enabled support through upper and middle-class loyalty (Tamarkin 1978:309).

## 5.5 ETHNIC IMBALANCES

- Regime stability often relied on suppressing Kikuyu militancy and ignoring the Kenya Land Freedom Army's demands (Tamarkin 1978:316).
- Consolidation of power through elite patronage systems, primarily favoring Kikuyu groups over other ethnicities.
- Conflict arose from ethnic patronage dynamics (Tamarkin 1978:318).
- **Barkan (1992:167)**: Effective political systems have legitimate authority fostering accountability and trust.

## 5.6 KENYATTA AND PATRONAGE POLITICS

### PATRONAGE

- Political decisions often driven by personal and ethnic ties; entrenchment of party loyalty in appointments.
- Elections, though controlled, retained competitive elements and accountability to constituencies; emphasis on distributive politics (Barkan 1992:172).
- Kenyatta’s patronage methodology involved increasing ministerial posts, incentivizing MPs for community responsiveness.
- Maintained civil service autonomy via the **Public Service Commission**, protecting against political pressures.
- Educational advantage in civil service aligned with promoting Kikuyu interests; political participation allowed limited dissent (Kenyatta’s governance period).

- Emergence of voluntary associations and local harambee groups served as counterweights to state power while promoting community engagement in governance (Barkan 1992:177).
- KANU supported by prosperous tribes; contrast with KADU representing marginalized communities (Barkan 1992:169).

## STUDY UNIT 6: Daniel arap Moi's Ascendancy to Power

### 6.1 Introduction
- **Transition of Power**: Following Kenyatta’s death in 1978, Daniel arap Moi became president, having proven his loyalty as vice president.
- **Kenyatta’s Governance**: A blend of power-centred politics; permitted some authority among subordinates (Barkan 1992:175).
- **Moi’s Objectives**: Aimed to reduce the Kikuyu-led patronage system, position himself as leader of the ``wananchi'' (common people), and diminish the power of traditional ethnic elites.
- **Anti-Kikuyu Bias**: Early initiatives included dissolving large ethnic welfare unions.
- **Political Control**: Political debate was framed as disloyalty; culminated in declaring Kenya a de jure one-party state in 1982.

### 6.2 Moi and "Nyayoism"
- **Contrast with Kenyatta's "Harambee!"**: 
  - **Harambee**: Emphasized local collective efforts and bargaining for development (Widner 1992:130-161).
  - **Nyayo**: Encouraged compliance with presidential directives, marking a shift to a more controlled political environment.
- **KANU Restructuring**: 
  - Initiatives to prevent independent power bases, control party elections, and strengthen state party relations (Widner 1992:144-146).
- **Political Dynamics**: 
  - By 1985, KANU became an extension of presidential authority; elections largely ceremonial with restricted local input.
  - **Fear and Intimidation**: Underground opposition efforts led to restrictions on political space, suffocating broader representation (Widner 1992:164-174).
- **Opposition Suppression**: 
  - Extended political restrictions included curbing press freedoms and declaring opposition illegal, particularly following the establishment of rival parties in 1982.
- **Controversial Ballot System**: 
  - Introduction of a public queuing system for voting created an environment marked by fear and eventual removal in 1990 due to international scrutiny (Widner 1992:171-172).
- **Calls for Multiparty Democracy**: 
  - Rising demands from clergy, judiciary, and international donors for reforms; set stage for 1992 multiparty elections (Widner 1992:166-170).

  - Pre-election violence attributed to both opposition and Moi’s incitement, leading to a severe political atmosphere in 1992 (Widner 1992:177-197).

# STUDY UNIT 12: Tanganyika's Path to Independence

## 12.1 Legal Status Change
- Transition from mandated territory under League of Nations to **trust territory** under the **United Nations**.
- **Trusteeship Committee** advocated for enhanced political participation for Africans; influenced rapid move towards independence.

## 12.2 Legislative Council
- British response: establishment of a **legislative council** with 42 members (21 appointed by the governor, 21 elected representatives).
- Under-representation of Africans and over-representation of minorities (Europeans and Indians) caused strong objections from African leaders.
- Supporters of independence movement, particularly **TANU** (formed by **Julius Nyerere** in 1954), criticized council for not adhering to **UN commitments** towards self-government and participation.

## 12.3 British Policy and Opposition
- British government showed reluctance for rapid independence; focused on responsible local governance to prepare citizens for self-management.
- Resistance from the **Tanganyika Federation of Labour** (est. 1955) using economic influence for political goals.
- Minimal violence during the independence struggle; contrasts with violent opposition (e.g., **Mau Mau** rebellion in **Kenya**).

## 12.4 TANU and Independence
- TANU's electoral success in 1958 led to constitutional changes and increased African representation.
- **Independence** granted in December 1961, with Nyerere as prime minister; followed by **Zanzibar's** independence on 10 December 1963.
- Formation of the **United Republic of Tanzania** in 1964; Nyerere becomes the first president after Tanganyika's transition to a republic in December 1962.

# Study Unit 4: Rural Socialism and Ujamaa Policy in Tanzania

- **Demographics and Ujamaa Principles**
  - Majority of Tanzanians live in rural areas; rural socialism is vital for a socialist nation (Minogue & Molloy 1974:90±91).
  - Reactivation of extended family structures into larger community village units.
  - Aim: Villages operate collectively (farming, marketing, local service provision) for equality.

- **Villagisation Programme**
  - Initial voluntary compliance led to 531,000 Tanzanians in 1,956 villages.
  - Dissatisfaction with progress initiated "Operation Tanzania" in 1974, involving coercion.
  - By 1978, 85% of rural population (13 million) in 7,300 villages; average membership ~1,849 (Urther, Munnik 1986:83).
  - The project ultimately failed due to lack of incentives and support leading to food shortages and economic decline (Coulson 1982:262).

- **Economic Nationalisation and Dependency**
  - Failure of rapid nationalisation undermined rural services (Ergas 1982:300).
  - Ujamaa's reliance on aid led to increased dependence on foreign donors (Lofchie 1976:486).
  - By mid-1980s, dire economic assessments noted frequent famine, infrastructure decay, and decline in social services (Lofchie 1985:159-163).

- **Shift in Economic Policy**
  - Early 1980s economic pressures: IMF aid contingent on market liberalisation.
  - Transition in leadership: Mwinyi's reforms promoted free enterprise and foreign investment (African Connection 1993:99).

# Study Unit 14: The One-Party System under Nyerere

- **Support for One-Party Democracy**
  - Nyerere's justification rooted in traditional African consensus decision-making (Duffy & Manners 1961:30-33).

  - Transition to multiparty politics post-Nyerere, with ongoing influence in reform advocacy.
  - Nyerere's legacy as a moral leader; advocated for anti-colonialism and social justice.

## Post-independence politics: the kabaka and Obote

### 18.1 INTRODUCTION
- Uganda's independence was cooperative rather than conflict-driven.
- Political parties:
  - **Kabaka Yekka (KY)** opposed independence without specific conditions.
  - UPC-KY alliance was fragile due to regional political factions.
- UPC lacked urban organization pre-independence, relied on trade unions, which were hostile post-independence.
- Obote faced pressure to maintain coalition government and meet local interests while transitioning to a strong central government.

### 18.2 OBOTE AND BUGANDA
- Upon independence (1962), the kabaka becomes president with limited powers.
- **1962 Elections**: UPC - 43 seats, KY - 26 seats, Democratic Party - 24 seats.
- Obote shifts policy in **1963-1964**, aiming for a one-party socialist state, threatening Buganda's autonomy.
- Dependence on civil service and military increases amid economic redistribution tensions, leading to dependence on the army (e.g., Idi Amin's rapid promotions).
  
- **Crises**:
  - **1964 Mutiny**: Army demands lead to British troop intervention; Obote concedes to demands.
  - Tensions rise over “lost counties” dispute; plebiscite held, resulting in a majority for Bunyoro.
- By **1966**, Obote isolates KY, gaining majority support, allowing for a shift towards a one-party state.

- Political conflicts persist, resulting in instability within the UPC:
  - Amin's controversial banking incident sparks accusations of corruption against Obote.
  - February 4, 1966: No-confidence vote against Obote; rather than resign, he stages a coup, suspending the constitution.
- **1967 Constitution**: Abolishes federal autonomy of kingdoms including Buganda; centralizes power in the prime minister's office.
- Buganda responds with rhetoric; however, Obote sends Amin's forces to attack kabaka’s palace, resulting in the kabaka's exile.

# STUDY UNIT 19
## Overthrow of Amin's Regime

- **Smuggling Issue** 
  - Coffee producers resorted to smuggling, particularly to Kenya. 
  - Amin’s advisor, Bob Astles, was appointed to combat this issue, leading to orders to shoot smugglers on sight (Grahame 1980:128-132; Gupta 1983:102-103).

- **Internal Opposition**
  - By 1973, increasing opposition due to economic decline; Amin responded with repression, public executions, and mass murders against dissenters.
  - Antagonism towards Christians through the promotion of Islamic interests.

- **Counterattack by Obote**
  - Former president Obote attempted to invade Uganda from Tanzania after the expulsion of Indians in 1972.
  - The invasion aimed to incite a general uprising but resulted in a failed capture at Masaka; plans for the Entebbe airport seizure aborted (Gupta 1983:26; Martin 1974:170-176).

- **Amin’s Decline**
  - By 1978, Amin's inner circle dwindled; troops loyal to his vice president, General Mustafa Adrisi, mutinied.
  - Attempted to divert attention by invading Tanzania on November 1, 1978, claiming Nyerere instigated troubles (Grahame 1980:224-235; Jackson & Rosberg 1982:263-265).
  
- **Tanzanian Response**
  - Nyerere mobilised forces and counterattacked. Amin’s forces retreated, resulting in the fall of Kampala in April 1979.
  - Amin fled to Libya and later to Saudi Arabia; his downfall attributed to his military overreach (Grahame 1980:224-235).

- **Post-Amin Government Challenges**
  - Task ahead: rebuilding a country devastated by Amin’s regime, resulting in ~300,000 deaths.

## The Interim Period (1979-1980)

- **Unity Conference**
  - Representatives from 22 Ugandan groups convened in Moshi, Tanzania, to establish an interim government, forming the Uganda National Liberation Front (UNLF). Dr. Yusuf Lule was appointed leader (Ingham 1994:154-164).

- **Conflict in Leadership**
  - Tensions within the NCC; Lule viewed as conservative; subsequently removed and exiled with Nyerere’s approval.

- **Binaisa’s Government**
  - Godfrey Binaisa, a former UPC member, took over but struggled with military power and internal conflicts (Ingham 1994:158-163).
  
- **Militarisation of Politics**
  - Museveni and General Ojok built private armies which threatened Binaisa's administration.
  - Binaisa was overthrown in a military coup on May 10, 1980, led by Ojok and Museveni.

- **1980 Elections**
  - Obote returned from exile; national elections held December 10, 1980, after 18 years.
  - The UPC, under biased conditions, emerged victorious, overriding DP claims of success, leading to civil war (Amaza 1998:14-21; Ingham 1994:166-176).

  - Reintroduction of Amin's soldiers harmed Okello’s government image.
  - Began cease-fire negotiations but was overwhelmed by Museveni’s forces.

  - Took Kampala in January 1986 with popular support; Okello fled north.

### Learning Unit: Museveni’s Movement Democracy and No-Party System (1986 onwards)
- **Transition to Power:** 
  - Rapid change with Okello's withdrawal; Museveni established the NRM.
  - Focused on stability, resulting in law and order; significant GDP growth reported.
  
#### The Ten-Point Programme
1. **Restoration of Democracy:** Free elections contingent on living standards.
2. **Restoring Security:** Pledge to secure lives after Obote and Amin atrocities (800,000 deaths).
3. **National Unity:** Fight against tribalism and sectarianism.
4. **National Independence:** Strong leadership as a resourceful nation.
5. **Integrated Economy:** Structural changes to agriculture; reliance on local industry.
6. **Social Services Rehabilitation:** Focus on war-affected areas.
7. **Combat Corruption:** Eliminate misuse of public office.
8. **Redress Population Dislocation:** Aid to displaced communities.
9. **African Cooperation:** Promote regional economic unity and cooperation.
10. **Mixed Economy Strategy:** Private sector-led growth with strategic government involvement.

### **Conclusion**
- Museveni's administration leveraged the Ten-Point Programme for governance and stabilization post-civil conflict.
- Transition from Museveni’s guerrilla era to governing legitimacy through integration of various factions into governance structures.

# Study Notes on Uganda's Political System and Elections

### Chapter 23: Uganda's Political System

#### 23.1 The Movement and Political Developments
- From 1981 to 1985, opposition forces, **FEDEMO** and **UNRF**, merged into the **NRM**.
- The **1995 Constitution** adopted by an interim **284-member Constituent Assembly**.
- **Article 70** outlines principles of Movement democracy:
  - Participatory democracy
  - Accountability and transparency
  - Accessibility to leadership for all citizens
  - Merit-based election criteria
- **Article 72** guarantees the right to form political parties; defines them as uni-ideological organizations with specific agendas and membership criteria.

#### 23.2 Political Parties in Uganda
- **Democratic Party (DP)**: 
  - Founded 1954; advocates for multiparty democracy.
- **Uganda People's Congress (UPC)**: 
  - Founded 1960; has faced bans and re-emergence.
- **Kabaka Yekka (KY)**: 
  - Dissolved in 1966; absorbed into the People's Congress.
- **Conservative Party**: 
  - Founded 1979; attracts royalist support.

#### 23.3 The "No-Party" System
- **1996 Elections**: 
  - Criticism of corruption delegitimizing outcomes (William 1997).
  - Museveni elected with 74% of the vote.
  - The **"no-party" system** aimed to foster national unity but criticized for suppressing fundamental rights.
- Argument for a middle path between Movement system and multiparty democracy to avoid a "winner takes all" scenario (Barya).
- NRM's approach and constitutional efforts deemed an extensive consultation process, although criticized for not truly reflecting public views (Furley & Katalikawe).

#### 23.4 Constitutional Developments
- Legislation established a 21-member constitutional commission designed to propose rights protections, democratic governance, and public accountability.
- **Referendum (2000)**: Over 90% supported the "no-party" system; low voter turnout interpreted as lack of support for Museveni's system.
- **2001 Elections**: Museveni wins with 69.3% amidst allegations of malpractice (Kizza Besigye's claims).
- **2002 Political Parties Act**: Established stringent controls over political parties; deemed illegal by Constitutional Court in 2003, yet government acted against the ruling.

### Chapter 24: The 2006 Elections

#### 24.1 Challenges Facing NRM Leadership

- Achievements juxtaposed with major challenges for the NRM in maintaining stability and transitioning to democracy.

- **Political Parties and Organizations Act (2002)**: Regulated political party operations.
- **Constitutional Court Ruling (2003)**: Declared the Political Parties Act illegal.

**TOPIC 5: Uganda's Political Landscape**

**25.1 Informal Political Institutions**
- Presidentialism persists, but accountability has increased under Museveni.
- Clientelism and emotional ties are informal institutions impacting politics; however, tribal dynamics have intensified since 1986.
- Changes in informal political institutions since 1986 show mixed impacts on constitutional rule (Prabahakara 1999-2000).

**25.2 Corruption Allegations**
- Recent corruption charges against Museveni’s government have affected his anti-corruption image; need for trust-building with opposition and supporters.
- Fears of transitioning to a one-party state post-2001 elections.

**25.3 Ethnicity and Stability**
- NRM (National Resistance Movement) has achieved relative peace but politicizes ethnicity and limits opposition activity.
- While security has improved, conflict persists, notably the LRA (Lord's Resistance Army) issues in northern Uganda; historical context dates back to the 1986 rebellion involving the Holy Spirit Movement.

**25.4 Decentralization Efforts**
- The Ugandan government’s decentralization initiative aims to empower local governance but suffers due to inadequate capacity and ongoing regional conflicts, hindering economic progress and poverty alleviation.

**25.5 Multiparty Democracy Issues**
- Concerns over commitment to multiparty democracy; current system draws parallels to the one-party regimes of Obote and Amin.
- The Movement system's 2000 referendum victory reflected limited political diversity, with many conditions since 1986 having changed.
- Improved economic and social development under the Movement, yet political legitimacy remains spurious.

**25.6 Civil Society Organizations**
- Growth of civil society organizations reflects progress under the no-party democracy; lingering question on readiness for multipartyism remains.

**25.7 Presidential Term Limits and the 2006 Elections**
- Article 105(2) of the 1995 Constitution limits presidential terms to two. Museveni elected in 1996 and 2001.
- Museveni's 2006 promise to step down was violated; NRM resolved to abolish term limits in March 2003, influenced by committee votes in May 2005, claiming it represented the electorate's will (Makara, Rakner & Svasand 2007:11).
- Reasons for Museveni's continued presidency include support from party members and concerns over personal and party security during election periods (Tangari 2006:178).

**25.8 Obstacles to 2006 Elections**
- The Political Parties and Organisations (PPO) Act imposed stringent registration requirements, impeding opposition.
- Election schedule changes limited campaign opportunities for opposition, enhancing challenges (Makara et al. 2007:11).

**25.9 Election Results and Implications**
- Museveni won the 2006 elections with 59.3% of votes; opposition faced hurdles, questioning the democratic nature of government transitions.
- Removal of term limits underscores concerns over democratic consolidation; personalisation of the state remains a major hindrance (Mwenda 2007:28, 33).

# Study Notes on African Integration and the East African Community

## 25.2 THE EVOLUTION OF INTEGRATION IN AFRICA
- **Pan-African Conferences (1920s - 1960s)**:
  - Advocacy for liberation from colonialism.
  - Economic cooperation urged for newly independent states.
- **Formation of the OAU (1963)**:
  - Economic cooperation as key objective.
  - OAU summits (1970, 1973, 1977, 1979) formulated guidelines towards an African economic community through stages.
- **Stages of Economic Integration**:
  - Preferential trade area → Free trade area → Customs union → Common market → Economic community.
- **Sub-regional initiatives**:
  - Focus on establishing economic blocs in West, East, Southern, Central, and North Africa.
- **Lagos Plan of Action (1980)**:
  - Encouragement of sub-regional economic blocs aimed for an African economic union by 2000.
- **Transition to the African Union (2002)**:
  - OAU transformed into AU to create new economic and political organs to harmonize agendas.

## 25.3 CHALLENGES
- **Multiplicity of Organizations**:
  - Over 100 organizations complicate cooperation and harmonization.
- **Size and Population Constraints**:
  - Small populations (e.g., 22 countries with 5-10 million) limit development.
- **Historical Pan-Africanism**:
  - Early political unity efforts faltered (e.g., Ghana-Guinea union).
- **Economic Reality**:
  - Food insecurity, agricultural crisis, liquidity issues, and ongoing poverty highlight the need for regional cooperation.

## 25.4 OBSTACLES
- **Lack of Commitment**:
  - Uneven implementation of agreements undermines collective efforts.
- **Grassroots Awareness**:
  - Low promotion of economic integration impacts private sector involvement.
- **Skills Shortage**:
  - Insufficient local entrepreneurs and technical skills lead to reliance on public sector.
- **Economic Dependency**:
  - High dependence on imported goods from developed countries diminishes intra-African trade potential.
- **Linguistic and Cultural Divisions**:
  - Differences hinder cohesive economic arrangements (e.g., Anglophone vs Francophone challenges).
- **Infrastructure Challenges**:
  - Inadequate mechanisms hinder movement of goods/services.
- **Customs and Trade Barriers**:
  - Complications in customs duties and revenue sharing.
- **Equitable Distribution Issues**:
  - Economic advantages concentrated in advanced countries impact equitable growth.
