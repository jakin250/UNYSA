### 1.2 DEFINING AFRICAN POLITICAL ECONOMY

- Lack of a universally accepted definition; often linked to negative aspects like corruption and authoritarianism.
- Borrow from traditional definitions focusing on the state and governance.
- **Marxist critique**: highlights power relations in market and state dynamics.
- Political decisions influenced European colonization and capitalism spread.
- Liberal economists (e.g., **Ricardo, Mills, Malthus**) focused on conditions for liberal trade, while **Marx** emphasized class inequalities.
- Definition derives from a combination of classic liberal and Marxist discourses:
  - Form: Understanding policy roles in economic development (liberal discourse).
  - Function: Class relations and corruption within the global economic system (Marxist discourse).
- Political economy analysis includes dynamics between state and market affecting production, distribution, and consumption.

### 1.3 CONTEXTUALISING AFRICA’S POLITICAL ECONOMY

- Global politics evolved from bipolarity to multipolarity.
- Emerging global economic rivalries overshadow ideological conflicts.
- Historical context includes Cold War influences on African politics, where superpowers intervened for strategic interests.
- Economic performance trends in African economies show substantial decline post-1970s, leading to increased poverty and political instability.
- Historical factors behind this include:
  - Over 200 coups d’état as a political change method in Africa until the 1990s.
  - Increased foreign debts consuming 47% of Africa's export earnings and leading to impoverishment.
  - Statistics exemplifying intra-country income disparities, highlighting economic inequality further exacerbate current challenges.

### LEARNING UNIT 2: Concepts in Political Economy

#### Key Concepts
- **Capital Flight:** Economic instability prompting exit of financial assets; impacts growth negatively.
- **Clientelism:** Patron-client networks shaping political landscape.
- **Conditionality:** IMF-imposed requirements for financial aid; aims for balanced payments support.
- **Comparative Advantage:** Producing goods/services at a lower opportunity cost; enhances trade potential.
- **Deindustrialisation:** Decline of industrial sector within economic activity; regression in economic growth.
- **Developmental State:** State with capacity for rapid transformation and citizen participation (e.g., South Africa).
- **Free Trade Zones:** Areas promoting trade without tariffs or quotas; contrasts protectionism.
- **Mode of Exchange:** Framework for examining commodity exchange relations under specific constraints.
- **Mode of Production:** Concept from Marx; refers to systems of producing means of subsistence.
- **Nationalisation:** State takeover of private industries; transition to public ownership.
- **Neo-colonialism:** Economic exploitation post-independence; continuation of colonial structures.
- **Privatisation:** Transfer of public sector ownership to private entities; diminishes government control.
- **Relations of Production:** Marxian concept; social relations specific to modes of production.
- **Resource Curse:** Challenges faced by resource-rich nations; links to conflict, authoritarianism, and poor growth.
- **Trade Liberalism:** Reduction of trade barriers; promotes free exchange.
- **Trade Protectionism:** Efforts to limit imports; employs tariffs and quotas.

### 3.3 CLASSICAL APPROACH (LIBERALISM)
- Emphasis on depoliticization of society.
- Focus on economic growth through individual interests (Adam Smith, 18th century).
- Transition from international to national economic policies.
- **Key Principles**: Private property, unhampered marketplace.
- **Example**: British Corn Laws (1815-1846) aimed to protect domestic agriculture but led to social unrest.

#### 3.3.1 Corn Laws Overview
- Statutes maintaining high corn prices to protect local farmers post-Napoleonic Wars.
- Benefited landowners; adversely affected urban working class and manufacturers.

#### 3.3.2 The Age of Mercantilism
- Economic relations shaped by colonialism.
- Repeal of Corn Laws marked shift from mercantilism to liberalism.
  
### 3.4 MARXIAN APPROACH
- Organization of production influences social/political structures.
- Focus on conflict between capitalists and workers; exploitation at the core.
- **Key Argument**: Economy and politics interdependent (Marx, 1818-1883).
  
#### 3.4.1 The Industrial Revolution (1700-1900)
- Introduction of machine-made goods, agricultural methods, transportation advancements.
- Poor conditions for workers spurred critique of classical economics.
  
### 3.4.2 Marxist Theory of Transition
1. Capital accumulation leads to labor displacement.
2. Decline in profit rates leads to lower wages and search for cheaper labor markets.
3. Result: Concentration of capitalists, rise in unemployment, loss of production value.

- Emphasizes structural change to empower civil society in economic decision-making.
- Highlights relationship between political elites and economic interests.

## LEARNING UNIT 3: Approaches and theories relevant to Africa’s political economy

### 3.5 NEOCLASSICAL APPROACH
- **Core Idea**: Human nature determines economic outcomes; emphasis on rationality.
- **Goal**: Achieve maximum wealth and well-being for the majority.
- **Key Figures**: John Maynard Keynes, Milton Friedman, Walter Rostow.
- **State's Role**: Advocate for minimal state intervention in the economy ("rolling back the state").
- **Primary Tasks of the State**:
  - Provision of public goods.
  - Correction of externalities.
  - Management of collective action.
- **Consumer and Market Dynamics**:
  - Consumer preference affects supply and demand.
  - Profit maximization is key for companies.
  - Market equilibrium occurs when both consumer satisfaction and company profit goals align.

#### 3.5.1 Assumptions of neoclassical theory
- Decisions made rationally with full information.
- Consumers compare goods based on utility.
- Objectives: consumer satisfaction; company profit maximization.
- Market equilibrium is achieved through these objectives.
  
### 3.6 POSTMODERNISM APPROACH
- **Concept**: Critique of modern economic theories, focusing on plurality and questioning technology's role.
- **Power Shift**: Advocates for empowerment of the oppressed.
- **Characteristics**:
  - Economic theories are viewed as modernist (classical, Marxist, neoclassical).
- **Critique of Modernism**: Claims to challenge previous norms and concepts shaped by capitalism and colonialism.
  
### 3.7 DECIMALITY APPROACH
- **Definition**: Movement to address the consequences of colonialism on modern structures, influenced by historical patterns of power.
- **Colonialism vs. Coloniality**:
  - Colonialism: Political/economic control by one nation over another.
  - Coloniality: Persistent power structures emerging post-colonialism.
- **Key Focus by Quijano**:
  - Control over economy, authority, gender/sexuality, knowledge production.
- **Objective**: Reveal and dismantle colonial remnants in the political economy.

### 3.8 THEORIES OF POLITICAL ECONOMY
- **Purpose**: Conceptual frameworks that structure research, identifying key variables and limits in models.
- **Application**: To understand Africa's political economy shaped by various theoretical influences.

### 3.9 STATE AND MARKET SOCIALISM
- **Definition**: Economy where firms are state-owned, but operate in competitive markets (Shleifer & Vishny, 1994).
- **Characteristics**:
  - Central planning in allocation and distribution of goods (Nee, 1989).
  - Aims to enhance producer incentives and restore market coordination.
  
### 3.10 AFRICAN SOCIALISM
- **Central Idea**: An anti-imperial economic theory focused on state control of production and addressing inequalities.
- **Influential Figures**: Leopold Senghor, Ahmad Sekou.
- **Goals**: Establish a society devoid of exploitation, ensuring fair distribution of wealth and resources.
- **Three Features**:
  - Non-exploitation society.
  - Fair wealth distribution.
  - Improvement of living standards through production.

- Implementation of **taxation** (e.g., poll taxes from 1913) incentivized forced labor.

- **4.4 The Atlantic Slave Trade**
  - Significant role in global economic integration over 500+ years.
  - Integral to European economic expansion based on Africa's resources.
  - Resulted in suffering and exploitation of African societies; contributed to systemic racism.
  - Triangular trade linked Africa, America, Asia, and Europe; critical for 18th-century global economy.

  - Slaves primarily labored in mining and plantation systems.
  - Portugal’s early maritime advancements; reliance on precious metals for trade.
  - Slave trade profitability was high but risky due to disease, shipwrecks, and piracy.
  - Controversial debate on extent of slave trade's role in European capital accumulation.
  - Important ports intertwined with slave trade: Nantes, Bristol, Liverpool.
  - Conventional views on trade may overlook African underdevelopment causes.
  
- **4.5 The Impact of Colonisation and European Settlement in Africa**
  - 1870: Europeans controlled 10% of Africa; by 1900, control expanded to 90%.
  - Major land appropriations between 1878-1914, affecting global land distribution and population control.
  - Colonization justified by racial superiority ideologies; Kipling’s "white man’s burden".

- **4.6 The Rationale Behind European Colonisation in Africa: Establishing Export Markets**
  - Imperialism drove colonization between 1870s-1900.
  - Resistance from African societies met European military and diplomatic pressures.
  - Key motives: political, economic, social.

  - Nationalism drove competition among European powers (e.g., Bismarck’s strategy).

## LEARNING UNIT 5: Africa’s early involvement and integration in the international economy: classified into four linkages
- **Page 35**

### 5.1 Four Linkages During Colonial Period
- Linkages are defined as connections leading to economic pressures (Sender & Smith, 1986:10).
  - **Linkages developed:**
    1. Transformation of subsistence goods into commodities
    2. Backward linkages (development of production markets)
    3. Forward linkages
    4. Fiscal (public revenue) linkages

### 5.2 Transformation of Subsistence Goods into Commodities
- Expanded export production converted subsistence goods to commodities.
- Wage earners and specialized producers increased demand for consumer goods.
- Examples include:
  - Nigeria (palm oil), Gold Coast (cocoa), Mali (cotton).
- Internal trade grew, diversifying domestic production.
- Dependency on international trade emerged due to colonial market control.

### 5.3 Backward Linkages
- Sender & Smith (1986:14): Three sets of backward linkages include:
  - **Transport:**
    - Expansion of railways (e.g., Mozambique from 456 km in 1905 to 2,384 km in 1945).
    - Facilitated national markets and shifted prices.
  - **Domestic Transformation of Inputs:**
    - Growth in domestic production of goods required for export sectors.
    - Example: Firestone rubber plantations in Liberia employed 50,000 people yearly.
  - **Transformation of Land Tenure:**
    - Changed rights to land use, allowing property transactions and capitalist ownership (e.g., King Leopold II in Congo Free State).

### 5.4 Forward Linkages
- Expansion of exports led to new industries (e.g., Senegal groundnut oil production, East African Tobacco Company in Uganda).

### 5.5 Fiscal Linkages
- Increased export taxes provided significant public revenue.
  - Forms of taxation included export duties, import and excise duties, railway tariffs, and poll taxes.
- Example: Export duties contributed up to 50% of public revenue in the old Coast.
- Increased tax revenue necessitated state intervention in the economy.

### 5.6 Interventionist Postcolonial State
- European firms sought state intervention to secure favorable conditions (Sender & Smith, 1986:31).
- Even local African producers faced competition requiring state protection.
- Pressure for investment in infrastructure came from rising political demands for social services.

### 5.7 Monetisation and Integration into the World Capitalist Economy
- Precolonial currencies were replaced by European currencies, tightening economic ties.
- Integration made African economies vulnerable to fluctuations in the international economy.

### 5.8 Conclusion
- Colonial economies entrenched dependency on European systems.
- Continued integration into global economy makes disassociation challenging.

- **Formation of State Elite**:
  - Links between political office and wealth.
  - Social mobility tied to state access.

  - Disadvantages in the international economy.

  - Over-reliance on primary sector exports.

### 6.3 Bretton Woods Institutions and Western Capital in Africa
- **Bretton Woods Conference**: July 1944; established the World Bank and IMF.
  - Purpose: Rebuild post-war economies, promote cooperation, prevent economic crises.
  - Limited African representation (Egypt & South Africa).

- **Key Figures**: Henry Morgenthau (US), Harry Dexter White (US), John Maynard Keynes (UK).
- **World Bank**: Channels funds to developing states; emphasis on private sector financing.
- **IMF**: Manages fixed exchange rates, provides short-term loans.

- **Impacts of Private Financing**:
  - High debt service burden for LDCs.
  - Limited internal resource allocation; reliance on foreign loans.
  
- **Debt Relief Efforts**:
  - Political pressure culminated in initiatives like Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) initiative.

### 6.4 Structural Adjustment Programmes (SAPs)
- **Political and Economic Influence**: SAPs impacted economies across Africa due to high levels of debt.
- **Key Characteristics**:
  - Minimal state role in economy; reliance on market mechanisms.
  - Emphasis on privatisation and deregulation.
  
- **Assumptions**:
  - States blamed for economic decline; external factors also considered.
  - IMF/World Bank heavily involved in restructuring economies.

- **Specifics of SAPs**:
  - Currency devaluations; market-driven exchange rates.
  - Anti-inflation measures; expenditure controls.
  - Restoration of market mechanisms with potential political backlash.

### 6.5 Political Constraints on Implementing SAPs
- **Political Will**: Essential for successful SAP implementation; obstacles can derail progress.
- **Conditionality**: Aid tied to meeting specific political/economic goals.
  - Emphasized good governance as key to economic success.

- **Social Implications**: Sometimes resulted in privatisation of basic services.
  
### 6.6 Privatisation of Basic Services
- **Definition**: Transfer of government functions to private sector initiated in the 1980s.
- **Debt Crisis**: Led to budget contractions; push for public service restructuring.

- **Outcomes**:
  - Mixed results; privatisation often did not yield expected improvements.

## 7. Dynamics of the Regional Political Economy
- **GDP Overview**: Nigeria leads with US$480 billion GDP; South Africa at US$350 billion (Barungi et al. 2015).

### 7.4 Regional Economic Communities (RECs) in Africa
- **Post-Independence Establishment**: RECs emerged in the 1960s aimed at poverty eradication, economic development, and peace. 
- **Key RECs**:
  - Arab Maghreb Union (AMU/UMA)
  - Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS)
  - East African Community (EAC)
  - Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD)
  - Southern African Development Community (SADC)
  - Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa (COMESA)
  - Economic Community of Central African States (ECCAS)
  - Community of Sahel-Saharan States (CENSAD)
- **Role of RECs**: Act as building blocks for the African Economic Community under the 1991 Abuja Treaty; collaborate with the African Union (AU) for peace and stability.
- **Key Programs**: NEPAD (2001) and AU Agenda 2063 (2015).

### 7.5 Migration and Economic Development
- **Projection**: Africa's population growth to impact migration and economic development by 2050.
- **UNCTAD Report Key Findings**:
  1. Predominantly intracontinental migration.
  2. Intra-African migration enhances economic growth.
  3. Migration yields durable growth benefits.
  4. Trade increases due to migration.
  5. Women migrants gain broader opportunities.
  6. Both semi- and low-skilled migrants boost destination economies.
  7. Migration neither solely beneficial nor detrimental for Sustainable Development Goals.
  8. Align migration policies with long-term development objectives.
- **Recommendations from UNCTAD**:
  - Coherent migration, trade, and technology policies for structural transformation.
  - Implement free movement frameworks.
  - Regional mechanisms for intra-African migration benefits.
  - Integrate gender perspectives in migration policy.
  - Global compact on migration.

- **Congo-Brazzaville Example**:

- **Comparison with Norway**: Norway manages resources effectively; contrasts with Nigeria's issues of transparency and governance.

### 9.2.4 Nyerere’s implementation of Ujamaa
- **Period of Implementation:** 1964 - 1985
- **Core Ideas:** 
  - Collective farming and "villagisation".
  - Nationalisation of banks/industry; enhanced self-reliance at individual/national levels.
  
- **Objectives:**
  - Recreate precolonial traditions; promote moral lifestyle through settled communities.
  - Shift urban population to newly created villages for collective agriculture (Boddy-Evans 2019).

  - Started slowly; forced participation by the 1970s with ~2,500 villages involved.
  
- **Economic Theory Rejection:**
  - Rejected capitalism in favor of socialism. 
  - Ended in 1985 with Nyerere's departure.
  
- **Post-Implementation Survey (2014):**
  - Villagisation failed to yield expected economic results; increased urban reliance (Boddy-Evans 2019).
