# STUDY UNIT 1: The Development of the South African State  

## 1.1 Introduction  
- Focus on the historical influence on the South African political landscape  
- Political Science perspectives on state development may differ due to unique South African context  

## 1.2 The Pre-Imperialist or Traditional Period  
- South Africa as a non-consolidated state until 1910; characterized by autonomous entities  
- Main inquiry: Political organization of early inhabitants, not their origins  
- Archaeological evidence indicates early populations like Khoikhoi and San  
  - Khoikhoi: Stock farmers, settled in coastal areas; trade with Bantu-speaking neighbors  
  - San: Nomadic hunter-gatherers with limited political structure   
- Late Iron Age: Rising Bantu-language-speaking population; complex social structures and trade routes  
  - Key trade centers: Phalaborwa, Great Zimbabwe, Mapungubwe  

## 1.3 The Imperialist Situation  
- Imperialism began with Dutch settlement in 1652; over four centuries of colonial rule in South Africa  
- Key issues: Land claims, citizenship, economic, and political rights - viewed variably (legitimate/illegal)  
- Phases of colonialism:
  - **Dutch Period (1652–1806)**
    - Limited initial objective of DEIC; established land allotments for free burghers  
    - Political structure under DEIC: little power; governed by Political Council subordinate to DEIC  
    - Expansion of free burghers laid groundwork for later political features  

  - **British Period (1806–1910)**
    - Divided into phases: First and Second British occupation, colonization of Natal and other regions  
    - Influences of British rule:
      1. Introduction of British state ideas  
      2. Formation of government institutions  
      3. Nationalist resistance by Afrikaners for self-governance  
      
### 1.3.1 The Dutch Period  
- Initial settlement and land allocation in 1657 post-arrival of Jan van Riebeeck  
- Basic political framework subordinate to the DEIC; lack of separation of powers  
- Notable conflicts: Barbier rebellion over agricultural monopolies and petitions for economic representation  
- European concepts introduced: state boundaries and land ownership conflicts with indigenous groups  

### 1.3.2 The British Period  
- **1.3.2.1 British Ideas about the State**  
  - **Separation of Power (Trias Politica)**: Legislative, executive, and judiciary established, albeit with some overlap  
  - **Parliamentary Sovereignty**: Parliament as supreme authority; courts lacked power to contest legislation  
  - **Political Supremacy**: Parliament accountable to electors; not equal to constitutional supremacy  
  - **Supremacy of Law**: Ideal of law as foundation not realized; discrimination persisted  

- **1.3.2.2 Nationalist Response of Republicanism**  
  - Rise of republican sentiment post-British rule, with early rebellions in Swellendam and Graaff-Reinet  
  - Establishment of Boer republics during the Great Trek driven by anti-British sentiment  
  - Legacy of republicanism continued into the 20th century despite earlier defeats; motivations shifted against majority rule

### 1.3.2.3 The republics of Transvaal, Orange Free State and Natal
- **Voortrekker Settlements**: 
  - Increasing white settlement in interior South Africa due to the Great Trek.
  - Hendrik Potgieter declares Vaal River area as conquered Voortrekker territory.
  - Incorporation of the territory between Vet and Vaal rivers in 1838 post-Bataung agreement. 

- **Formation of Republics**:
  - Republic of Potchefstroom-Winburg formed; borders fixed to Orange River (1844).
  - Unification of four communities into the Zuid-Afrikaansche Republiek (ZAR) in 1860.
  - Republic of Natal established, uniting Potchefstroom-Winburg and Natal in 1840.

- **British Intervention**:
  - Voortrekkers' requests for British recognition of independence denied (1840-1841).
  - British annexation of Natal occurs post-Voortrekker defeat at Congella (1842).

- **Independence Movements**:
  - Potchefstroom-Winburg declared independent in 1844 with defined borders.
  - Conflicts arise between Voortrekkers and British, culminating in annexation of territory by Sir Harry Smith (1848).
  - The Sand River Convention (1852) recognized independence north of Vaal River.

- **Post-Convention Developments**:
  - Bloemfontein Convention (1854) recognizes Republic of Orange Free State.
  - Governance: Elected state president, executive council, unicameral Volksraad, judicial system managed by supreme court and magistrates.

- **Exclusivity of Franchise**:
  - Only whites eligible for voting; black populations governed under trusteeship.
  
- **Transvaal Annexation**:
  - British annexed it in 1877; temporary independence regained post-Amajuba battle (1881).
  - South African War (1899-1902) leads to ultimate loss of independence for both republics.

- **Legacy**:
  - Foundations for modern provinces established (Gauteng, Limpopo, Mpumalanga, North-West, Orange Free State, KwaZulu-Natal).
  - Role of former republics in calls for volkstaat.

### Summary of "White" State Development (1806-1910)
- **Colonial Changes**:
  - Cape Colony (British, 1806), Natal (British, 1842), Transvaal (independent, 1852), Orange Free State (independent, 1854).
  
- **Territorial Status Timeline**:
  - 1877-1881: Transvaal under British.
  - 1881-1902: Both Transvaal and Orange Free State as independent.
  - 1902-1910: All territories British colonies.
  
- **Unification**:
  - Formation of the Union of South Africa (1910), marking recognition of one state.

### 1.4 RESTRUCTURING AND SUBJUGATION OF TRADITIONAL AUTHORITY

#### 1.4.1 Mfecane/Difaqane and the Restructuring of the Political Terrain
- **Mfecane Overview**:
  - Early 19th-century unrest among Bantu-speaking communities; significant power restructuring.
  - Northern Nguni conflicts led to the rise of larger units such as Ngwane, Ndwandwe.

- **Key Leaders**:
  - Shaka Zulu rises post-Dingiswayo's death; leads extensive campaigns (1817-1828).
  - Moshoeshoe I unifies groups in present-day Lesotho; crucial for Basotho formation.
  - Mzilikazi establishes power among the Ndebele, facing defeat by Voortrekkers.

- **Impact**:
  - Major redistribution of Bantu language groups and power dynamics among chiefdoms.

#### 1.4.2 The Chiefdoms in South Africa
- **Colonial Influence**:
  - Mfecane and colonial settlement radically alter chiefdom power structures; some chiefdoms resist, others fold.

- **Notable Figures**: 
  - Kgama of Ngwato (protects against settlers), Moshoeshoe I (unification of Sotho groups).

- **Survivability Issues**:
  - Many chiefdoms succumb to colonial forces (e.g., Zulu, Xhosa).

- **Chiefdom Characteristics**:
  - Kinship replaced by territorial governance; chiefs manage local justice, legislation, and military.
  - Traditional authorities essential for community cohesion; modern state structures follow territorial demarcations.

- **Traditional Councils and Governance**  
  - Traditional leaders relied on councils for advice: 
    - Pitso (Sotho), Imbizo (Nguni), Libandla (Swazi).  
  - Governance by discussion; leaders must consult and gain consensus.  
  - Example: Moshoeshoe I emphasized consultative governance.  
  - Ignoring council advice could lead to political consequences.  

- **Succession of Chiefs**  
  - Elder son of a designated wife (often of royal birth) is usual successor.  
  - Chief’s council must approve successor choice.  

- **Philosophical Background**  
  - **Hobbes**: Justified state creation via social contract; no nature state in South Africa upon European arrival.  
  - South Africa’s state development:  
    1. Colonial (Western) tradition since the **Treaty of Westphalia (1648)**.  
    2. Traditional African governance.  

- **Political Dualism**  
  - Regions vary in political culture:  
    - Predominantly traditional governance in former homelands.  
    - Western influence in metropolitan areas.  
  - South Africa faces a dual challenge of democratization and modernization.

**THE CONSTITUTIONAL DEVELOPMENT OF THE APARTHEID STATE**  

- **Constitutional Summaries**:  
  - **1909 Constitution**: First constitution of a unified SA; British monarch as ceremonial head; South African PM and Cabinet exercise executive powers.  
  - **1961 Constitution**: Established a republic outside the Commonwealth; ceremonial state president; mirrored 1909's structure.  
  - **1983 Constitution**: Proposed reforms post-1976 Soweto uprising; created three parliamentary houses for whites, coloureds, Indians; consolidated powers in an executive president, abolished prime minister role.  

- **Common Undemocratic Features**:  
  - Exclusion of black population from political rights.  
  - Framework of reserves and homelands from 1913; separate black local authorities.  
  - Internal revolts in 1980s, notably led by United Democratic Front, impacted National Party's demise.  

- **Constitutional Practices Influencing Current State**:  
  - Parliamentary sovereignty enabled easy constitutional amendments.  
  - Courts lacked power for judicial review; no enforceable bills of rights.  
  - Prime minister and Cabinet accountable to parliament, could be removed via no-confidence motions; executive president replaced PM in 1983.  
  - Limited separation of powers; Cabinet members as parliament members.  
  - Two-house parliament until 1980; Westminster electoral system led to two-party dominance.

## STUDY UNIT 2: Restructuring of the South African state  

### 2.2.2 Political Deadlock  
- Political deadlock inherited by FW de Klerk from PW Botha.  
- Era of technocratic reform under Botha:  
  - Abandonment of radical partition.  
  - Creation of black local authorities, Regional Services Councils.  
  - Partial multiracial parliament, abolition of pass laws.  
- NP's ideological direction diminished, leading to loss of unity and government role.  
- Presidential authority used to marginalize parliament, NP caucus, increasing power of State Security Council.  
- States of emergency (1985-1990) escalated militarization, especially among whites.  
- Reform suggested necessity for a unified state with non-racial citizenship. NP aimed to maintain white political control.  
- Proposed National Council excluded banned organizations (e.g., ANC, SACP).  
- Political deadlock exacerbated by Mandela's conditional release offer (renounce violence).  
- ANC suspended armed struggle August 1990; deadlock persisted into de Klerk's era.  

### 2.2.3 General Election of 1989  
- NP lost support: from 57% (1981) to 48% (1989).  
- Policy costs led to Conservative Party formation in 1982, attracting NP supporters.  
- Economic discontent fueled alienation of white working class, farmers, public servants.  
- De Klerk's 1990 reforms were reactions to NP’s incapacity in white politics, driven by external pressures.  

### 2.2.4 Unplanned Internal Pressure: Urbanisation  
- Unequal population growth challenged white domination.  
- Urbanisation made apartheid policies on housing, education, health unworkable.  
- Regime's intolerance of black urbanisation further intensified internal unrest and demands for political change.   

### 2.2.5 Planned Internal Pressure: Unrest  
- 1980s: Mass internal resistance against the government (e.g., ANC's strategy to disrupt governance).  
- State of emergency declared by NP in response to unrest.  
- Black local authorities ineffective due to rent boycotts and violence.  
- Education crisis heightened since Soweto uprising (1976).  
- Formation of Mass Democratic Movement (MDM) as a response to government repression.  
- ANC positioned as critical to breaking the political deadlock and restructuring.  

### 2.2.6 Unplanned External Pressure: USA and USSR  
#### 2.2.6.1 Superpower Cooperation  
- Late 1980s: US-Soviet rapprochement shifted global conflict resolution focus from military to political solutions.  
- ANC faced weakened capacity for armed action, necessitating negotiations.  
- NP interpreted Eastern European socialist declines as a reduced threat, enabling political engagement.  

### 2.2.7 Planned External Pressure: International Isolation  
- Participation in Angolan war tied to Namibia’s independence, considered unlawful internationally.  
- International sanctions intensified post-1985, especially financial and arms embargoes cutting off loans and military trade.  
- Sports, diplomatic, cultural, and academic boycotts contributed to stigma and isolation.  

### 2.3 Transition and Negotiation  
- South Africa's transition was gradual and negotiated, not revolutionary.   
- Objectives of negotiation: end internal conflict and democratize governance.  
- Theoretical foundations: game theory and labour relations model.  
- **Sources for negotiation phases:**  
  - Pierre du Toit and Jannie Gagiano  
  - Frederik Van Zyl Slabbert  
  - Harare Declaration  

### 2.3.1 Du Toit and Gagiano  
1. **Bargaining about Bargaining**  
   - Parties' choice: victory on unilateral terms vs. mutually advantageous settlement.  
   - Negotiations aimed to commit leadership to a collaborative resolution.  
2. **Preliminary Bargaining**  
   - Parties determine agendas, negotiating conditions, and participant status.  
   - Decisions included negotiating format (round table), forum name (Codesa), and participant types.  
3. **Substantive Bargaining**  
   - Focus on resolving disputes, essential for formulating a new constitution and restructuring sectors like the economy.  

### 2.3.2 Van Zyl Slabbert  
1. **Normalisation**  
   - Transition from polarized to legitimate political activity.  
   - Key issues: managing political violence, ensuring consensus, and developing mutual trust.  
2. **Democratisation**  
   - Emphasis on democratizing not just the constitution but the state and economy.  
   - Recognized need for a final constitution to lend legitimacy to governance.

### 2.5 Phases of the Negotiations in South Africa

- Characterized by **bilateral discussions** between **NP** and **ANC**.

#### 2.5.2 Groote Schuur Minute

  - Laid foundation for the transitional process.

## STUDY UNIT 3: Theories of democratic development

### Rational Choice Theory
- Assumptions:
  - (1) Decision-maker is aware of all available options.
  - (2) Informed about costs and benefits of each option.
  - (3) Preferences ranked from most to least preferred.
  - (4) Chooses the option with the most advantages.
  
- **Application**: Game Theory
  - Simulates situations involving two or more political actors.
  - Decision-makers anticipate opponents' responses.
  - Types of outcomes: win-win, win-lose, zero-sum, lose-lose.

- **South African Context**:
  - Timothy Sisk's analysis (*Democratization in South Africa: the elusive social contract*, 1995):
    - (1) Shift from zero-sum perceptions to positive-sum possibilities.
    - (2) Pacts between ANC and NP reduced uncertainty and incentivized cooperation.
    - (3) Agreed outcomes led to a social contract to institutionalize cooperation.
    - (4) Social contract aimed to remove conflict causes and establish inclusive hegemony.

### Constitutionalism
- Formal approach focusing on political structures.
- Changes to institutions can resolve political issues.
- Institutionality determines human behaviour; reform-oriented actions suggested.
  
- **Examples in South African Politics**:
  - Associated with PFP, DP, commissions of inquiry, and early stages of constitutional negotiations.

- **Criticism**:
  - Excludes analysis of deeper political conflicts.
  - Seen as formalistic and abstract (Howarth & Norval, 1998).

### Theories about Democratic Consolidation
- Abstract and complex; lacks measurable standards.
- **Key Scholars**:
  - O’Donnell (1996): Power alternation, system stability, and economic support essential for consolidation.
  - Schmitter (1992): Emphasis on democratic values within society.
  - Przeworski (1991): Consolidation occurs when democracy becomes the only viable institutional framework.

- **Key Dimensions in the Political Landscape**:
  - Political Competitiveness:
    - Jung & Shapiro (1995): Lack of opposition diminishes competition.
  
  - Horizontal Accountability:
    - Post-apartheid Constitution emphasizes limited separation of powers; executive dominance concerns raised.

  - Political Legitimacy:
    - Economic inequalities and unrealized expectations challenge legitimacy post-1994.

### Democratic Justice Theory
- Ian Shapiro (2008): Caution against unrealistic expectations from democracy; demands stem from injustice experiences.
- Risks: Unrealistic expectations can lead citizens to blame democracy for unmet needs.

### 5.5 THE BILL OF RIGHTS
- Guarantees fundamental rights to all individuals in South Africa (Chapter 2).
- **Rights included**: 
  - Individual rights: life, freedom, security, dignity, privacy
  - Collective rights: cultural, religious, linguistic community rights; labour relations
- **Limitations**:
  - Section 36 limitation clause allows for reasonable, justifiable restrictions.
  - Limitations must be:
    - General in application
    - Justifiable in an open society

- **Key Cases**:
  - **State v Makwanyane (1995)**: Death penalty violates rights to life and dignity.
  - **State v Jordan (2002)**: Overturned constitutionality of prostitution provision in the Sexual Offences Act (1957), noting rights to dignity and privacy.

### 5.6 APPLICATION OF THE BILL OF RIGHTS
- Applies vertically (state vs. individuals) and horizontally (individuals vs. individuals).
- Courts ensure enforcement of rights, determining the reasonableness of limitations.

### 5.7 ENFORCEMENT AND INTERPRETATION OF THE RIGHTS
- Any individual or group can approach courts on rights infringement.
- Constitutional Court interprets rights, ensuring laws align with the Constitution.

### 5.8 THE FORM OF STATE
- Description: The South African state structure is unitary with federal elements.
- **Power Distribution**:
  - **Exclusive powers** (Schedule 5): Provincial powers without a national list.
  - **Concurrent powers** (Schedule 4): Shared between national and provincial authorities (e.g., education, housing).
- **Intergovernmental Relations**:
  - Mechanisms for cooperation established post-1994 (e.g., MINMEC forum).

### 5.9 INTER-GOVERNMENTAL RELATIONS AND CO-OPERATIVE GOVERNANCE
- Defines relationships among national, provincial, and local governments.
- Cooperative governance emphasizes:
  - Indivisibility of the state.
  - Independence of spheres.
  - No secession rights for provinces.
  - Encroachments on each other's jurisdiction or authority are prohibited.

**STUDY UNIT 6: The separation of powers in South Africa**

**6.1 INTRODUCTION**
- Separation of powers as a fundamental principle for liberty and restrainment of power.
- Essential for preventing abuse through effective checks.

**6.2 THE DOCTRINE OF THE SEPARATION OF POWERS (TRIAS POLITICA)**
- Originated from philosophers John Locke and Montesquieu’s *L’esprit des lois* (1748).
- Prevents concentration of power and promotes constitutional safeguards.
- Establishes:
  - Legislative, executive, and judicial branches (trias politica).
  - Different officials staffing each branch.
  - Core functions assigned distinctly (legislation, administration, adjudication).
  - Checks and balances to maintain equilibrium.

**6.3 THE SEPARATION OF POWERS IN SOUTH AFRICA**
- Transition from apartheid to democracy reallocated sovereignty to the 1996 Constitution.
- Interim Constitution introduced separation between legislature, executive, and judiciary.
- 1996 Constitution outlines powers without explicit reference to ‘separation of powers’:
  - Legislative power to Parliament (s. 43).
  - Executive power to President and cabinet (s. 85).
  - Judicial power to courts (s. 165).
- The Constitution prescribes partial separation, lacking absolute separation like the US.
- Retains Westminster-style parliamentary system, merging executive and legislative branches.
- Dominance of ruling party has weakened checks and balances.

**6.4 CONCLUSION**
- Separation of powers ensures accountable governance. Future study units will detail the judiciary, executive, and legislature.

### 7.2 Structure of the Judiciary
#### 7.2.1 Constitutional Court

  - Addresses failures by Parliament or the President in constitutional obligations.
  - Certifies provincial constitutions for compatibility.

#### 7.2.2 Supreme Court of Appeal
- Consists of president, deputy president, five judges.
- Highest court of appeal, excluding constitutional matters.
- Location: Bloemfontein.

#### 7.2.3 High Court
- Known previously as the Supreme Court.
- Hears serious criminal and civil cases, including constitutional matters.
- 13 courts across South Africa; provinces have High Courts.

#### 7.2.4 Magistrates’ Court
- Entry point for most court users.
- Two types: regional courts (higher rank, serious cases) and district courts.
- Cannot decide on constitutional matters.

#### 7.2.5 Other Courts
- **Small Claims Court:** Minor financial disputes.
- **Electoral Court:** Electoral issues.
- **Labour Court:** Labour relations.
- **Land Claims Court:** Land restitution matters.

### 7.3 The Judicial Service Commission (JSC)
- Formed by the Constitution; chaired by the Chief Justice.
- Composition:
  - Chief Justice, President of Supreme Court of Appeal, judge president, Minister of Justice, legal practitioners, educators, and members from National Assembly and Council.
- **Tasks:**
  - Prepare candidates for Constitutional Court judges.
  - Advise president on all judicial appointments.
  - Handle complaints against judges.
  - Advise on judiciary and justice administration matters.

### 8.1 Powers of Parliament  
- Joint exercise of powers by both houses:

### 8.3 The National Assembly  
#### 8.3.1 Composition  
- **Members**: 350-400, directly elected by voters via proportional representation.  
- **Proportional representation**: Parties represented as per votes received—contrasts with winner-takes-all system (e.g., UK, USA).  

#### 8.3.2 Proceedings  
- **Speaker**: Chairperson; does not vote but casts deciding vote in tie.  
- Decisions require absolute majority (50% + 1); constitutional matters need two-thirds majority (except Article 1).  

#### 8.3.3 Role of Committees  
- Defined in Sections 55-57 of the Constitution; mechanisms for executive accountability.  
- Committees viewed as "engine room" of Parliament; conduct oversight and accountability.  
- Committees make recommendations through reports to Parliament; they do not decide.  
- Examples include portfolio, select, and joint committees.  

**Committee Functions**:  
- Initiate legislation.  
- Debate and oversee departmental budgets and policies.  
- Hold public hearings and investigate executive functions.  

#### 8.3.4 Types of Committees  
- **Portfolio and Select Committees**: Shadow respective government departments (e.g., Basic Education).  
- **Provincial Committees**: Similar functions at the provincial level; do not always align with MECs.  
- **Ad Hoc Committees**: Temporary, focus on specific issues; terminate after mandate completion.  
- **Standing Committees**: Permanent structures (e.g., SCOPA); monitor expenditure of taxpayers' money; can call officials for evidence.  

*Joint Committees*: Composed of members from both Parliament houses; can summon individuals and collect submissions.

### 8.4 The National Council of Provinces (NCOP)  
- Represents interests of nine provinces, replacing the Senate from the interim Constitution (1993).  
- **Section 44(1)(b)**: Powers include participation in constitutional amendments, passing bills affecting provinces (Section 76), and considering ordinary bills (Section 75).  
- All provinces represented equally; each has ten delegates (4 special, 6 permanent).  

#### 8.4.1 Proceedings  
- The NCOP elects a chairperson and two deputy chairpersons.  
- Decisions require an absolute majority; each province casts one vote.  
- Local government representatives (part-time) can participate but not vote.  
- Functions similar to National Assembly committees.  

### 8.5 The National Legislative Process  
- Reviewed under:  
  - **Section 74** (constitutional amendments)  
  - **Section 75** (ordinary bills not affecting provinces)  
  - **Section 76** (ordinary bills affecting provinces)  
  - **Section 77** (money bills)  

- General process: All legislation (except money bills) debated and approved by both houses. Introduced by Cabinet members or parliamentary members.  
- Final approval from President required for all bills.

#### Section-Specific Insights  
- **Section 74**: Amendments need two-thirds majority in National Assembly and at least 6 province votes in NCOP.  
- **Section 75**: Ordinary bills passed by absolute majority; can be rejected by NCOP but must be reconsidered by National Assembly.  
- **Section 76**: Both houses need to agree; mediation committee resolves deadlocks.  
- **Mediation Committee**: 9 from National Assembly and 9 provincial delegates; requires supportive votes from both sides for decisions.  
- **Section 77**: Money bills (e.g., Appropriation Act); National Assembly has veto power on these.  

### 8.5.1 Parliament and the Budget  
- Parliament's key function: Discuss and pass the annual Budget introduced by the Minister of Finance.  
- **Budget**: Critical economic instrument detailing government's plans and priorities; affects resource allocation.  
- Indicates priorities of government while addressing deficit spending.

### 13.1 INTRODUCTION  
- Electoral systems vary among democracies; crucial for vote-to-seat conversion in Parliament  
- South African Constitution outlines the electoral framework; sections 46(1), 105(d), and 157(3) promote proportional representation  
- National and provincial elections utilize a closed-list PR system; local elections use a mixed-member proportional (MMP) system  
- Elections must be “free and fair” managed by the Independent Electoral Commission (IEC)  

### 13.2 NATIONAL AND PROVINCIAL ELECTIONS  
- Elections held every five years; president proclaims election date per Constitution  
- IEC facilitates free and fair elections with two ballot papers: national and provincial  
- **Proportional-Representation (PR) System**  
  - List PR aims for proportional allocation of seats to valid votes cast  
  - **Bicameral Parliament**: National Assembly (NA) and National Council of Provinces (NCOP)  
    - NA: 350-400 seats; MPs elected directly  
    - NCOP: 90 seats; indirectly elected by provincial legislatures  
  - NA party lists: divided into National-National (200 seats) and Provincial-National (200 seats); seats allocated based on party vote proportion  
  - Seats allocated via the **Droop quota**; no explicit minimum vote threshold for seat allocation  
  - Advantages: simplicity, proportionality; Disadvantages: voter disengagement, fragmentation of parties  

### 13.3 LOCAL GOVERNMENT ELECTIONS OR MUNICIPAL ELECTIONS  
- Municipalities are crucial for local governance; oversee service delivery and community representation  
- Elections every five years; councillors elected to local councils  
- **Types of Municipalities per Municipal Structures Act**:  
  - Category A: Metropolitan  
  - Category B: Local  
  - Category C: District  
- **Mixed-Member Proportional System (MMP)**  
  - Utilizes two voting systems: plurality-majority and PR  
  - Section 157(3) mandates proportional representation in municipal councils  
  - The MMP system divides votes 50:50 between wards and proportional representation  
  - Ward councillors elected via “first-past-the-post” system; independent candidates allowed in ward elections  

### 13.3.1 Proportional Representation Elections: Metropolitan, Local and District Municipalities

### STUDY UNIT 13: South African electoral system in context  

**Electoral Systems Overview**  
- Metropolitan and local councils use a proportionality formula.
- Votes from ward and PR ballots combined to determine total votes.
- Total votes divided by total council seats (excluding independent seats) gives implied quota.
- Quota determines seat allocation to parties (rounded up).
- Ward votes impact total PR seats for parties (compensation for disproportionality).

**Metropolitan and Local Council Formulae**  
1. Total votes from ward and PR ballots
2. Total council seats minus independent seats
3. Implied quota calculation
4. Seats allocated based on party votes
5. PR seats applied to ranked PR list

**District Municipality Composition**  
- 44 district municipalities within 205 local municipalities.
- District councils elected through a parallel system:
  - 60% councillors appointed by local municipality councils
  - 40% directly elected via PR system
- Voters receive three ballots during local government elections.

**Legislative Framework**  
- **Local Government Municipal Systems Act** mandates council meetings within 14 days post-election.
- Speaker of council elected from members, presided over by municipal manager or designated official.

### 13.3.3 Coalitions in Local Government  
- Lack of majority results in coalition formation by leading parties.
- Example: 2016 local elections saw 27 hung councils.
- Coalition typically seeks support for speaker and executive mayor elections.
- No legal provisions govern coalitions, posing risks of instability. 

### Voter Turnout in South Africa  
- Key indicator of democracy's vitality; reflects political system legitimacy.
- Voter turnout: votes cast as a percentage of registered voters.
- Decline in turnout post-1994; 16% decrease from 1994 to 2014.
- 2014 NPE: ANC garnered 62.1% of votes, equating to 36.4% of eligible voters.
- Highest turnout was 86% in 1994, attributed to historical significance.

### Voter Participation Patterns  
- Higher turnout in national elections vs. local elections.
- Local election turnout stabilized at 58% during 2011 and 2016.
- Lower turnout potentially harms social cohesion; abstention linked to ANC voter behavior.

### 14.5 PERSPECTIVES ON THE NATURE OF THE STATE AND SOCIETY

- **Kotzé (2011:9)**: Political parties define themselves beyond the left-right spectrum, focusing instead on the nature of society and the state.

#### 14.5.1 The nature of society

- **Atomistic Perspective**:
  - Individualistic, linked with liberalism emphasizing individual freedom.
  - Advocates personal responsibility and private initiative as drivers for societal development.
  - Stresses judicial procedures and the importance of the rule of law, judicial independence, and separation of powers.
  - **Associated Parties**: DA, IFP, FF+.

- **Organic Perspective**:
  - Views society holistically and emphasizes community interdependence for self-realization.
  - Prioritizes equity and equality over individual freedom and responsibility.
  - Critiques the profit-driven private sector; supports public-private partnerships and state enterprises for development.
  - Justice is viewed socially, focusing on transformation rather than strict legal interpretations.
  - **Associated Parties**: PAC, AZAPO, ACDP, EFF.

- **Hybrid Position**:
  - Combines communitarian views with free-market principles.
  - **Examples**:
    - IFP: Traditional constituency, supports free-market strategies.
    - FF+: Socially conservative, endorses group and cultural rights while advocating for a strong private sector and acknowledging state social responsibilities.

#### 14.5.2 The function and purpose of the state

- **State Views**: Opposition parties oscillate between viewing the state as:
  - A **social contract** limiting state functions or
  - A **public resource** for social transformation.

- **Social Contract Tradition**:
  - Views the state as controlled and scrutinized, providing essential services and security but limited in reaching into the private sector.
  - Emphasizes constitutionalism and checks on government power.
  - **Associated Parties**: DA, COPE, FF+, IFP.

- **Welfare Tradition**:
  - Considers the state a resource for social development, offering welfare, job creation, housing, and health services.
  - Associated with the **developmental state** concept adopted by ANC and endorsed by PAC, AZAPO, EFF.

### 14.6 UNDERSTANDING POPULISM

- Populism signifies a decline in traditional political ideologies and becomes a key feature of modern democracies.
- Observed rise of populism in Western Europe as significant in various electoral democracies.
- **Moffitt & Tormey (2014)**: Describes populism not as an ideology but as a political style characterized by:
  - Appeal to “the people”.
  - Reaction to crises, emphasizing urgency.
  - Disregard for conventional political norms (i.e. “bad manners”).

#### 14.6.1 Appeal to “the people”

- Populist leaders claim to solely represent the people, differentiating them from elites, and often use inclusive language to emphasize this divide.

#### 14.6.2 Crisis, breakdown, and threat

- Populism thrives on perceived crises, positioning leaders as decisive problem-solvers during political turmoil.
- Examples include the formation of the EFF driven by crises within the ANC.

#### 14.6.3 “Bad manners”

- Populists often challenge political conventions, creating an image distinct from elites to appeal to the general populace, evident in EFF’s disregard for parliamentary rules.

### 14.7 PARTY SYSTEMS

- Determined largely by the electoral system, impacting how votes translate into seats.

#### Party System Types:
- **One-party system**: Only one party is legally dominant.
- **Two-party system**: Dominance by two parties.
- **Dominant-party system**: Multiple parties exist, but one dominates.
- **Multiparty system**: Many parties have equal chances at power.

- **Duverger's Law**: States single-member constituency systems favor two-party systems while proportional representation leads to multiparty systems.
- **SADC Context**: Mostly multiparty systems, with dominant-party systems in countries like South Africa.

### 14.8 SINGLE-DOMINANT PARTY SYSTEM

- Defined as one party enjoys consistent majority support in regular elections, distinguishing it from authoritarian systems, which may lack genuine electoral competition.

#### Examples:
- **Botswana Democratic Party** since 1965.
- **Conservative Party** in Britain (1979-1997).
- **CDU/CSU** in Germany (1945-1966, 1982-1999).
- **Congress Party** in India (1947-1991).
