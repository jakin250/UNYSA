### 1.1 Introduction
- Critical reasoning enables analysis of arguments encountered in daily life.
- Rhetoric defined: persuasive attempts lacking substantial argumentation (*Bowell & Kemp, 2015*).

### 1.2 What is Critical Reasoning?
- Definition: Active, reflective, informed thinking that involves questioning and evaluating beliefs (*Van den Berg, 2010*).
- Critical reasoning vs. dogma:
  - Dogma: Unquestioned belief from authority, e.g., traditional gender roles.
  - Critical reasoning involves questioning these beliefs, promoting independent thought.

#### Core Components of Critical Reasoning
- Thinking for oneself
- Informed reasoning
- Critical self-reflection

### 1.3 Critical Reasoning, Informal Logic, and Formal Logic
- Critical reasoning and informal logic: Synonymous terms.
- Formal logic: Studies argument structures within logical languages/symbols.
  - Example of informal logic: “If it is raining, then my garden will be wet.”
  - Formal logic representation: P → Q

### 1.4 Critical Reasoning Competence – Thinking for Yourself
- Engaging critically in various life areas and questioning accepted dogma.
  - Encouraging exploration of alternatives (e.g., diverse relationship types).
- Historical examples: South African struggle heroes challenged dogmatic beliefs on apartheid laws.

### 1.5 Critical Reasoning Competence – Thinking in an Informed Way
- Informed opinions require substantiation based on facts.
  - Example: Rejecting evolution must be informed by scientific understanding, not mere opinion.

## LESSON 2: Different kinds of writing and definitions

### 2.2 Different Types of Writing

#### 2.2.1 Descriptive Writing
- Creates a language-based picture of an object/place/person/situation.
- Main characteristic: vivid description.
- Example: Travel narrative about the DRC.

#### 2.2.2 Narrative Writing
- Tells a story or expresses emotion/experience.
- Dominated by storytelling rather than description.
- Example: Richard Wright’s emotional expression in *Native Son*.

#### 2.2.3 Explanatory Writing
- Also known as informative writing.
- Seeks to explain or inform; definitions and clarifications fall here.
- Example: Definition of epistemology as a philosophical study of knowledge.

#### 2.2.4 Instructive Writing
- Provides clear instructions for completing a process or achieving goals.
- Example: Directions for writing an academic essay.

#### 2.2.5 Argumentative Writing
- Presents positions supported by justified evidence.
- Engages in philosophical discourse through arguments.
- Example: Discussion on Western empiricism and its impact on colonized knowledge systems.

### 2.3 Different Types of Definitions

- Importance of clear definitions to avoid vagueness in critical reasoning.
- A definition clarifies rather than serves as a premise.

#### 2.3.1 Stipulative Definition
- Introduces a new word or usage; highly subjective.
- Example: Thomas Kuhn’s definition of paradigms differs from everyday usage.
- Context: Legal rights for animals by advocating a new definition of personhood.

#### 2.3.2 Persuasive Definition
- Aims to persuade the reader of a viewpoint; often includes emotional appeals.
- Example: Defining abortion biasedly as "the murdering of innocent little babies".

#### 2.3.3 Lexical Definition
- Also called logical definition; reflects standard usage or "dictionary definition".
- Example: A textbook defined as "a book used as a standard work for study."

#### 2.3.4 Enumerative Definition
- Defines by providing examples; can be vague due to reliance on shared experiences.
- Example: Defining kwela music by citing specific artists, which may not clarify for everyone.

### 3.1 INTRODUCTION
- **Definition of Argument**:
  - A set of propositions where one is a conclusion and others are premises (Bowell & Kemp, 2015).
  - A group of statements affirming a claim’s truth or acceptability (Van den Berg, 2010).

### 3.2 THE STATEMENT OR PROPOSITION
- **Definition**:
  - Proposition: Factual content expressed by a declarative sentence (Bowell & Kemp, 2015).
  - Statement: An assertion that is either true or false (Van den Berg, 2010).

- **Examples**: 
  - Propositions include factual statements like "Robert Mugabe is no longer president of Zimbabwe."
  - Non-propositions include commands, questions.

- **Importance**: Only propositions contribute to argument analysis.

### 3.3 THE CONCLUSION
- **Definition**: A conclusion is a statement that premises support (Van den Berg, 2010).
- **Identifying Conclusions**:
  - Ask the main point of the argument.
  - A variety of propositions can serve as conclusions.
  - Multiple conclusions possible in extended arguments (sub-conclusions).
  - Look for conclusion indicators:
    - Therefore, Consequently, Thus, etc. (Van den Berg, 2010).

### Summary: 
- Understanding types of writing and definitions is crucial for evaluating arguments and reasoning in legal contexts.
- Familiarity with arguments, propositions, and conclusions helps in critical analysis and debate.

## LESSON 3:  The Argument

### 3.4 The Premise
- **Definition**: A premise is a statement offered to support a conclusion (Van den Berg, 2010, p. 43).
- **Identification Guidelines** (Bowell & Kemp, 2015, pp. 16-18):
  - Ask for reasons supporting the conclusion.
  - Premises need not be universally accepted; controversial statements can serve as premises.
  - **Premise Indicators**: 
    - Because
    - For
    - If
    - Moreover
    - Since
    - For the reason that
    - Given that
    - Whereas
    - Insofar as
    - Firstly, secondly, thirdly, etc.
    - Seeing that
    - In the light of (Van den Berg, 2010, p. 50).
  - Add premise indicators where missing to check for sense.

### 3.5 The Complex Argument
- **Definition**: A complex argument contains a sub-argument, often called a chain argument.
- **Example Structure**:
  - **Premise**: All toddlers are between 2 and 5 years old.
  - **Premise**: Pharelo is between 2 and 5 years old.
  - **Sub-conclusion**: Pharelo is a toddler.
  - **Conclusion**: Pharelo loves ice cream (with prior premises supporting it).

### 3.6 The Implicit Premise and Conclusion

#### 3.6.1 The Implicit Premise
- **Definition**: Missing premises expected to be inferred by the audience (assumptions).
- **Example**: 
  - Original: "I am a good parent. My children never watch more than an hour of TV a day."
  - **Missing Premise**: "No good parent would allow excessive TV." 
  - **Standard form**:
    - P1: No good parent would allow excessive TV.
    - P2: My children watch less than an hour.
    - C: I am a good parent.

#### 3.6.2 The Implicit Conclusion
- **Definition**: Missing conclusions that need to be inferred.
- **Example**: 
  - Original: "Voluntary euthanasia decreases suffering. People should decide how to end their lives."
  - **Approximate Conclusion**: "Voluntary euthanasia should be permissible."
  - **Standard form**:
    - P1: Voluntary euthanasia decreases suffering.
    - P2: People should decide on their end of life.
    - C: Voluntary euthanasia should be permissible.

### 3.7 Conclusion
- An argument consists of premises supporting a conclusion.
- Important to identify premises, conclusions, and sub-arguments for analysis.
- Evaluates strength or weakness of arguments.

### 4 Different types of arguments  

#### OUTCOMES OF THIS LESSON  
- Distinguish between value and empirical arguments  
- Distinguish between inductive and deductive arguments  
- Understand the difference between soundness and validity  
- Identify arguments that are value, empirical, inductive, deductive, sound and/or valid  

### 4.1 INTRODUCTION  
- Basic building blocks for an argument: premises and conclusions.  
- Types of arguments: value vs empirical, inductive vs deductive.  
- Importance of truth in logic.  

### 4.1.1 What is truth?  
- Objective truth vs subjective truth.  
- Common-sense understanding of truth: e.g., "fish live in water" vs "abortion is morally acceptable."  
- Truth-value: the truth or falsity of a claim; can vary by context.  

### 4.2 VALUE ARGUMENTS  
- Claims about how things ought to be (norms, morals, aesthetics, beliefs).  
- Not empirically verifiable.  
- Fields of study: psychology, sociology, political science.  
- Example comparison:  
  - *Empirical*: "Sugar sweetens coffee."  
  - *Value*: "Sweetened coffee tastes better than unsweetened coffee."  

### 4.3 EMPIRICAL ARGUMENTS  
- Arguments verifiable by senses or scientific knowledge (facts).  
- Premises verifiable by direct observation.  
- Examples:  
  - P1: "I saw a stray cat in the parking lot."  
  - C: "Therefore, there are stray cats on campus."  
  - P1: "Raw fish can carry Salmonella."  
  - P2: "Salmonella is dangerous."  
  - C: "Eating raw fish may cause illness."  

### 4.4 DEDUCTIVE ARGUMENTS  
- Conclusion guaranteed if premises are true; contains conclusion within premises.  
- Example:  
  - P1: "All humans need oxygen to survive."  
  - P2: "Tshepo is a human."  
  - C: "Tshepo needs oxygen to survive."  
- Properties of a successful deductive argument:  
  1. Conclusion follows from premises.  
  2. Premises are true.  
- Common forms: syllogisms (categorical, hypothetical).  

#### 4.4.1 Hypothetical syllogism  
- Includes a conditional statement ("if-then").  
- Antecedent (IF) and consequent (THEN) parts.  
- Examples:  
  - "If it is raining, then it is cloudy."  
  - "If Sam does not practice, she will fail."  

### Modus Ponens  
- Argument pattern:  
  - P1: "If P (is true), then Q (is true)."  
  - P2: "P (is true)."  
  - C: "Therefore, Q (is true)."  
- Example:  
  - P1: "If it is raining, then my car will be wet."  
  - P2: "It is raining."  
  - C: "My car is wet."

### 4.1 Arguments in Standard Form
- **Modus Ponens**
  - Rewrite arguments:
    - (a)  
      - P1: If a team wins the league, then they are the best soccer team in the country.  
      - P2: Kaizer Chiefs won the league.  
      - C: Kaizer Chiefs is the best soccer team in South Africa.  
      - *Type*: Value argument, empirical premise.  
   
    - (b)  
      - P1: If a person finds ice cream too sweet, then they have more taste buds than the average person.  
      - P2: I find ice cream too sweet.  
      - C: I have more taste buds than some others do.  
      - *Type*: Empirical argument.  

    - (c)  
      - P1: If a fetus is a human being, then abortion is akin to murder, and therefore immoral.  
      - P2: A fetus is a human being.  
      - C: Abortion is akin to murder and therefore immoral.  
      - *Type*: Value argument.  

### 4.2 Modus Tollens
- **Hypothetical syllogism**: 
  - P1: If P, then Q  
  - P2: Not Q  
  - C: Not P  
- Example structure:  
  - P1: If it is raining, then my car will be wet.  
  - P2: My car is not wet.  
  - C: It is not raining.  

### 4.3 Categorical Syllogism
- Defined as a three-line argument with statements starting with all, some, or no.  
- Standard form:  
  - P1: All M are P.  
  - P2: All S are M.  
  - C: All S are P.  
- **Terms**:  
  - Middle term (M): Occurs in both premises.  
  - Major term (P): First premise, predicate of conclusion.  
  - Minor term (S): Subject of conclusion, in second premise.  

- **Examples**:
  1. P1: All birds have feathers.  
     P2: Some dinosaurs were birds.  
     C: Some dinosaurs have feathers.  
     - Terms: Minor - Dinosaurs; Major - Have feathers; Middle - Birds.  

  2. P1: All dinosaurs were cold-blooded.  
     P2: No mammals are dinosaurs.  
     C: Therefore, no mammals are cold-blooded.  
     - Terms: Minor - Mammals; Major - Cold-blooded; Middle - Dinosaurs.  

  3. P1: All dinosaurs are reptiles.  
     P2: All crocodiles are dinosaurs.  
     C: Therefore, all crocodiles are reptiles.  
     - Terms: Minor - Crocodiles; Major - Reptiles; Middle - Dinosaurs.  

### 4.5 Inductive Arguments
- Conclusion follows with strong probability, not absolute certainty.  
- Example: Hearing thunder implies it may rain later.  
- Sometimes, conclusions may amplify beyond the premises.  
- Weakness in arguments leads to varying support for conclusions.

### 4.6 Deductive Validity
- **Definition**: A valid deductive argument has premises that cannot all be true while the conclusion is false.  
- *Important Note*: Validity is technical; it does not equate to “true” in everyday language.

## 4.0 Lesson 4: Different types of arguments

### 4.1 Importance of Validity
- Validity is crucial for rigorous reasoning aimed at truth discovery.
- A valid argument maintains the truth of its premises in its conclusion.
- Valid argument can be **unsound** if premises are false (e.g., **P1**: If a star is fairy dust → **C**: Moon made of gorgonzola.)

### 4.2 Types of Deductive Arguments
- Focus on two deductive argument types:
  - **Hypothetical Syllogisms** (Modus Ponens, Modus Tollens)
  - **Categorical Syllogisms**

#### Valid Deductive Patterns
- **Hypothetical Syllogisms**
  - **Modus Ponens**:  
    - **P1**: If P then Q  
    - **P2**: P  
    - **C**: Q  
  - **Modus Tollens**:  
    - **P1**: If P then Q  
    - **P2**: Not Q  
    - **C**: Not P  

- **Categorical Syllogisms**
  - **Type 1**:  
    - **P1**: All M are P  
    - **P2**: All S are M  
    - **C**: All S are P  
  - **Type 2**:  
    - **P1**: All M are P  
    - **P2**: Some S are M   
    - **C**: Some S are P  
  - **Type 3**:  
    - **P1**: All M are P  
    - **P2**: No S are M  
    - **C**: No S are P  

### 4.3 Soundness
- Distinction between validity and soundness:  
  - **Validity**: Structure of argument.  
  - **Soundness**: Truth of premises (e.g., **P1**: If star → moon made of cheese → unsound).  

#### Validity vs. Soundness Summary
- **Valid and Sound**: Valid structure + True premises  
- **Valid and Unsound**: Valid structure + False premises  
- **Invalid and Sound**: Invalid structure + True premises  
- **Invalid and Unsound**: Invalid structure + False premises  

### 4.4 Conclusion
- Understanding argument types crucial for identification in analysis and assessments.
- Validity and soundness are foundational concepts in argument evaluation.

## LESSON 5: Fallacies  
---
### 5.1 Overview  
- **Fallacies**: Misuse of language and evidence in arguments.  
- **Types**:  
  - **Informal fallacies**: Errors in reasoning (substantive).  
  - **Formal fallacies**: Logical errors affecting validity.    

### 5.2 Informal Fallacies  
- Failures of reasoning rather than logic.   

#### 5.3.1 Informal Fallacy – Slippery Slope  
- **Definition**: Exaggerates consequences from an initial action.  
- **Structure**: Chain of conditionals (e.g., "if… then…").  
- **Example**: Not studying leads to homelessness.  
- **Identification**:  
  - Are events connected?  
  - Is the conclusion exaggerated?  

#### 5.3.2 Informal Fallacy – Straw Man  
- **Definition**: Misrepresenting an opponent's argument to attack a weaker version.  
- **Example**: Child seeks a gap year; parent claims child wants to leech money.  
- **Identification**:  
  - Is the argument against a misrepresented position?  

#### 5.3.3 Informal Fallacy – False Dilemma  
- **Definition**: Presents limited choices when more exist (excluded middle).  
- **Example**: "Support the ruling party or agree with corruption."  
- **Identification**:  
  - Are complex alternatives ignored?  

#### 5.3.4 Informal Fallacy – Begging the Question (Petitio Principii)  
- **Definition**: Circular reasoning; conclusion assumed in premises.  
- **Example**: "The Bible is true because it says so."  
- **Identification**:  
  - Does the conclusion rephrase premises without progress?  

#### 5.3.5 Informal Fallacy – Ad Hominem Argument  
- **Definition**: Attacks the person rather than the argument.  
- **Example**: Attacking Dumisani's character instead of his argument against fracking.  
- **Identification**:  
  - Is an argument undermined by personal attacks?  

#### 5.3.6 Informal Fallacy – False Appeal to Authority  
- **Definition**: Citing an authority irrelevant to the subject matter.  
- **Example**: Neurologist on land reform vs. cardiologist on diet.  
- **Identification**:  
  - Is the cited authority an expert in the relevant field?

### LESSON 5: Fallacies

#### 5.3.7 Informal fallacy – Hasty Generalization
- General conclusion drawn from a biased or small sample.
- Example: Concluding that "Fords are bad cars" from two negative experiences.
- Hasty generalization occurs when conclusions are made without sufficient evidence.
- **Key References:**
  - Bassham et al. (2008, p. 155)
  - Dowden (2019)

#### 5.3.8 Informal fallacy – Red Herring
- Defined as a digression that distracts from the relevant issue.
- Example: Politician sidetracks criticism by discussing personal farming experience.
- Red herring attempts to claim that an irrelevant issue resolves the original argument.
- **Key References:**
  - Bassham et al. (2008, p. 135)
  - Dowden (2019)

#### 5.3.9 Informal fallacy – Complex Question
- Known as "loaded question"; contains unfair assumptions.
- Example: "Are you still cheating on your spouse?"
- Forces a responder into admission regardless of the answer.
- **Key References:**
  - Bassham et al. (2008, p. 152)

#### 5.3.10 Informal fallacies – Equivocation
- Occurs when a word or expression shifts meaning across premises.
- Example: Difference between "miracle" in scientific and supernatural contexts.

#### 5.1 Bandwagon Argument  
- Bandwagon argument (argumentum ad populum): Appeal to popular belief rather than the strength of the argument.

#### 5.2 Informal Fallacy: False Analogy  
- Analogy usage: Represents comparisons for understanding; a weak analogy is a false or faulty analogy.  
- Example: Misinterpretation by white anthropologists of Australian parental terms highlights the fallacy of false analogy.  

#### 5.3 Summary of Informal Fallacies  
- **Slippery slope**: Exaggerated consequences from trivial premises.  
- **Straw man**: Misrepresenting an opponent's position.  
- **False dilemma**: Presenting only two alternatives when more exist.  
- **Begging the question**: Conclusion assumes truth of premises.  
- **Ad hominem**: Attacking the person instead of the argument.  
- **False appeal to authority**: Citing non-expert authorities.  
- **Hasty generalisation**: Conclusion drawn from insufficient evidence.  
- **Red herring**: Distracting with irrelevant issues.  
- **Complex question**: Combining multiple questions into one.  
- **Equivocation**: Using ambiguous terms in different senses in an argument.  
- **Appeal to force**: Using threats to support an argument.  
- **Bandwagon argument**: Persuasion based on populist sentiment.  
- **False analogy**: Weak comparison in an argument.  

#### 5.4 Structural or Formal Fallacies  
- Structural fallacies arise from argument form rather than content.  
- Key formal fallacies: **Affirming the consequent** and **denying the antecedent**.  

##### 5.4.1 Formal Fallacy: Affirming the Consequent  
- Occurs when the consequent of a conditional statement is affirmed, mistakenly concluding the antecedent.  
- Example structure:  
  - P1: If P, then Q.  
  - P2: Q (consequent affirmed).  
  - C: Therefore, P (incorrect conclusion).  
- Implication: For instance, speaking isiZulu does not necessarily mean one is South African.

- **Definition of Fallacy**: An argument that is flawed in its reasoning.
- **Types of Fallacies**:
  - **Formal Fallacies**: Errors in logical structure.
  - **Informal Fallacies**: Errors in reasoning that do not involve a flaw in logical structure.

#### Formal Fallacies
- **Denying the Antecedent**:
  - Structure:
    - P1: If P, then Q
    - P2: Not P
    - C: Not Q
  - Example: If it is raining (P), then my car will be wet (Q). It is not raining (Not P), therefore, my car is not wet (Not Q).

- **Summary of Formal Fallacies**:
  - **Modus Ponens**: 
    - Valid Argument
      - P1: If P, then Q
      - P2: P
      - C: Q
  - **Affirming the Consequent**: 
    - Invalid Argument
      - P1: If P, then Q
      - P2: Q
      - C: P
  - **Modus Tollens**: 
    - Valid Argument
      - P1: If P, then Q
      - P2: Not Q
      - C: Not P
  - **Denying the Antecedent**: 
    - Invalid Argument
      - P1: If P, then Q

      - C: Not Q

### 6.3 HOW TO EVALUATE AN ARGUMENT

- Good vs. Bad Arguments (Bassham, et al., 2008, p. 203):
  - Good argument ≠ agreement with personal views.
  - Good argument ≠ merely persuasive.
  - Good argument ≠ well-written/spoken.

- Characteristics of a Good Argument:
  - **Absence of Fallacies**: Fallacies indicate errors in reasoning; presence means argument is bad.
  - **Validity**:
    - **Deductively Valid**: Impossible for premises to be true and conclusion false (Bassham, et al., 2008, p. 76).
    - **Inductively Strong**: Conclusion likely true if premises are true.
  - **Tendency to Truth (Soundness)**:
    - Assess truth of premises (Van den Berg, 2010, p. 91).
    - Empirical claims easier to verify than value claims.

- Evaluating Arguments (Bassham et al., 2008, p. 206):
  - Are the premises true?
  - Is reasoning correct (valid/strong)?
  - Are any fallacies committed?
  - Are premises relevant to conclusion?
  - Is all evidence accounted for?

- Two critical reasoning processes:
  - Analyzing arguments.
  - Evaluating arguments.
