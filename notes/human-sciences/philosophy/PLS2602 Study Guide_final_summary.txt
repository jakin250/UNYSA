## 2.1 The Doctrine of Discovery  
- Important for colonization ethos; evolved from European Middle Ages.
- Papacy held absolute authority supporting the Doctrine.
- Papal Bulls granted lands to European monarchs disregarding Indigenous rights.  
- Claims to territorial rights by Europeans without Indigenous consent.

## 2.2 Papal Bulls and Colonial Justification  
- **Romanus Pontifex (1455)**: Granted Portugal authority to claim lands, subjugate 'Saracens and pagans' (Miller 2011:857–858).
- Divine gifts of land legitimized violence against non-Christians.
- **Dum Diversas**: Allowed for killing and expropriation of non-believers (Mudimbe 1988:45).

## 2.3 Territorial Rivalry among European Powers  
- Holland vs England: Claim rights based on occupation and first discovery principles.
- Conflicted claims led to wars (e.g., Anglo-Boer War).
- **Berlin Conference (1884)**: Peaceful partition of Africa; arbitrary boundaries formed.

## 2.4 Colonial Borders in Africa  
- Philosophers like Nkrumah challenged the colonial boundaries, asserting they hinder development (Obeng 1979:63).
- AU/OAU treaties uphold colonial boundaries despite critiques.
- Ethical dilemma concerning the retention of colonial borders addressed.

## 2.5 Ethical Implications of the Doctrine  
- Doctrine contradicted **just war principles** (ius ad bellum and ius in bello).
- Focused on war permissibility and humane conduct; violated principles of dispute resolution and just cause.
- Claims based on desires for political power and economic benefits deemed unethical.

## 2.6 Epistemicide and Colonial Language  
- Imposition of European values epitomized the Doctrine.
- **Civilization** element justified paternalism and denigration of Indigenous cultures (Miller 2011:854).

## 2.7 Naming and Implications of South Africa  
- Name “South Africa” reflects colonial logic; ignores Indigenous presence.
- **Terra nullius**: Lands considered vacant despite Indigenous governance (Miller 2011:900–901).
- **1961 Constitution**: Echoes papal claims framing land as divine gift.

## 2.8 Recognition of Historical Injustices  
- Call for acknowledgment of terra nullius before true reconciliation can occur (Krog 2009:209).
- Ethical questions regarding decolonization; ongoing struggle for justice.

## 2.9 Conclusion  
- Colonization undermined humanity's ontological equality; Africa, the origin of humanity, subjected to marginalization.
- Ethical concerns persist regarding historical injustices from colonization.

# Chapter 4: Perspectives on the Meaning of Ethics: The African Standpoint

## 4.7.1 Benezet Bujo on African Ethics
- **Elements of African Ethics**:
  - **Theonomy**: Moral norms are believed to come directly from God (Bujo 1998:25).
  - **Community Understanding**: 
    - Comprises three dimensions:
      1. Living here and now
      2. Living-dead
      3. Yet-to-be-born
    - **Obligations**: 
      - Living must protect community wellbeing.
      - Breach of obligations leads to punishment from the living-dead.
      - Healthy individuals must bear children; unwillingness to do so is viewed negatively.
      - Community wellness is linked to fertility and family lineage.
  - **Anthropocentrism**: Human-centered ethics recognizing the interdependence of individuals and the community.
  - **Memory**: Re-membering the living-dead and yet-to-be-born link individuals within the community.
    - Bujo refers to African ethics as a **"morality of memoria."**
    
- **Ethical Foundations**: 
  - Morality is located in the human heart; virtues and vices relate to the heart's influence.
  - Emphasis on **“Promote life and avoid killing”** (Bujo 1998:77) linked to the clan's survival.
  - Comparison with Western ethics (abstract principle: “Do good and avoid evil”).
  
- **Knowledge of Ethical Principles**: 
  - Bujo conflates ethics and morality, despite indicating awareness of their distinction, particularly in titles of his works.

## 4.7.2 The Necessity for God and Its Elusive Identity: A Critique of Bujo
- **Gilson’s Argument**: God as a metaphysical necessity doesn't alter scientific structures; any identity attributed to God is subjective (Gilson 1941:141).
- **Identity Issues**: 
  - God can possess multiple identities or none, complicating ethical understanding.
  - Critique of God’s identity leads to feminist and liberation theologies, questioning inherent ethical knowledge.
  
- **Conscience vs. Divine Ethics**: 
  - Ethical principles may be seen as innate, yet conscience can err, complicating the divine implantation of morality.

## 4.7.3 African Ethics: A Philosophical Perspective
- **Bujo’s Background**: Focuses on Bantu-speaking communities in the Democratic Republic of the Congo.
- **Connection to Ubuntu**: 
  - Ubuntu as a central philosophy in African ethics, highlighting community interconnectedness.

#### 5.1 Ethical Considerations of Truthfulness

- **Key Points by Kung (1968)**:
  - Openness in negotiations without deceit is crucial; trust is fundamental.
  - Untruthfulness as a character trait is worse than a lie.
  - **Mendacity**: Worst form of deception; person identifies with an oppressive system, denying their truth.

- **Degrees of Truthfulness (Kung, 1968)**:
  1. Lying is bad.
  2. Untruthfulness as a person’s essence is worse.
  3. Mendacity is the worst.

- **Opportunism**:
  - Seen as a variant of mendacity; adapts irresponsibly to prevailing situations.
  - Ethical implications: no obligation to disclose truth at all times.
