# OBSTACLES TO CLEAR THINKING

## 2.2 Fallacies

### 2.2.1 Slippery Slope Argument
- Form: 
  1. Event X occurs.
  2. Therefore, event Y will follow inevitably.
- Fallacy: Lacks argument linking X and Y directly.

### Examples:
- Tuition fees escalating to R10,000.
- South Africa's foreign troop involvement leading to extensive military presence.
- Giving leeway leading to being taken advantage of.
- Banning one form of literature leading to broader censorship.

### Extended Example:
- Preventing smoking in cinemas threatens personal liberty and democratic principles.

### 2.2.2 Straw Man Argument
- Definition: Misrepresenting an opponent's argument to defeat it easily.
- Example: Responding to animal rights by claiming animals cannot claim rights due to perceived stupidity.
- Identification: Requires familiarity with the topic to recognize misrepresentation.

### 2.2.3 Begging the Question
- Definition: Conclusion presupposed in premises (circular reasoning).
- Example: "Deliberate ending of a foetus is murder" used to define abortion without new support.

### 2.2.4 Equivocation
- Definition: A word used with different meanings in the same argument.
- Example: Alvaré’s argument on "humanity" of embryos versus the right to life.
- Explanation: Ambiguity not due to grammar but to distinct meanings of words.

### 2.2.5 Complex Question
- Definition: Two or more questions disguised as one.
- Example: "Is it government’s unlawful conduct or moral decline causing economic issues?"
- Another Example: "Have you stopped beating your wife and children?"
- Implication: Assumes answer to one question within the other.

### 2.2.6 Faulty Analogy
- Definition: Comparing two dissimilar things without relevant similarities.
- Example: Comparing dog breeding to child rearing—oversimplifies both.

### General Note on Fallacies
- Categorization of fallacies as distraction fallacies; deeper exploration available in prescribed materials.
- Emphasis on identification and understanding of various fallacies in debates and discussions.

### Examples of Additional Distractive Fallacies:
1. Right to bear arms linked to overreach.
2. Illegal sales practices presumptions.
3. Global warming skepticism due to lack of proof.
4. Existence of ghosts not disproven, thus believed to exist.
5. Risks of gambling leading to crime.
6. Promises of honesty in used car sales.
7. Mischaracterization of gun control advocates.
8. Flawed analogies (e.g., marriage without love and driving without brakes).

# OBSTACLES TO CLEAR THINKING

## 2.2.7 Ad Hominem Argument
- **Definition**: Attacks the character of the person making a claim instead of the claim itself.
- **Forms**:
  - Personal attack on character.
  - Attack on circumstances of the claimant.
  - Attack on claimant's interests.

## 2.2.8 False Appeal to Authority
- **Definition**: Citing an authority not relevant to the context to justify a claim.
- **Example**: A medical doctor asserting moral justification for human cloning is a false appeal to authority if they lack expertise in ethics.

## 2.2.9 False Dilemma
- **Definition**: Presents only two alternatives when more exist.
- **Example**: Suggesting only aggressive or submissive responses ignores complexities.

## 2.2.10 Hasty Generalisation
- **Definition**: Generalizing based on insufficient evidence.
- **Example**: Claiming all good engineers are men based on limited observations is hasty.

### Summary
- **Common Fallacies**: Ad hominem, false appeal to authority, false dilemma, hasty generalisation.
- **Focus**: Develop a sense of recognizing bad arguments through practice, beyond specific fallacy identification.

# STUDY NOTES ON IDENTIFYING AND ANALYSING ARGUMENTS

### 3.1 What is an argument?

- **Definitions of Argument**:
  - (1) Quarrel between individuals.
  - (2) Group of statements aimed at establishing truth or acceptance of a claim.
  - (3) Exchange of differing opinions where reasons are provided for positions.

- **Context**: In critical reasoning, "argument" refers to senses (2) and (3).

- **Example Uses**:
  - Descartes on existence.
  - Plato’s Republic dialogue regarding justice.

- **Argument Definition**:
  - A group of statements; one is the conclusion, others are premises aimed to support said conclusion.

### 3.2 Analysing Arguments

#### 3.2.1 Identifying Premises and Conclusions

- **Premises**:
  - Support statements in an argument.
  - Provide reasons for accepting the conclusion. Not all reasons are valid or relevant.

- **Conclusions**:
  - Statements intended to be supported by the premises.
  - The objective of an argument is to affirm the truth or acceptability of the conclusion.

- **Example Argument**:
  - Premise: "My nose itched this morning."
  - Conclusion: "So I am going to be upset later today."
  - Analysis: No valid connection between premise and conclusion; conclusion is unsupported.

### Key Concepts

- **Sound Argument**: Conclusion is true or acceptable due to the premises and valid structure.

### Structure of Arguments
- Identifying how conclusions relate to premises:
  - Independent support: Each premise supports the conclusion separately.
  - Interdependent support: Premises combined provide conclusive support.

#### Example of Independent Support
- **Argument**: 
  - 1. Abortion preserves mother’s rights.
  - 2. Abortion prevents unwanted children.
  - **Conclusion**: Abortion should be legally available.

#### Example of Interdependent Support
- **Argument**: 
  - 1. All human beings are mortal.
  - 2. Sue is a human being.
  - **Conclusion**: Sue is mortal.

- Arguments can have omitted premises required for sufficiency of support.
  - Example: Peter is a poor student because he reads comics.

# EVALUATING ARGUMENTS

---

## 4.1 Different types of arguments

- **Understanding Argument Structures**
  - Evaluating arguments requires knowledge of argument types and soundness criteria.
  
- **Types of Arguments**
  - **Empirical Arguments**: Based on observable facts.
  - **Value Arguments**: Pertaining to moral judgments or preferences.
  - **Deductive Arguments**: Conclusions necessarily follow from premises.
  - **Inductive Arguments**: Conclusions extend beyond premises.

---

- **Characteristics of Argument Types** (Refer to Chapter 6)
  - Empirical arguments focus on facts.
  - Value arguments focus on opinions and moral positions.
  
- **Additional Observations**: 
  - Recognizing argument type is crucial for appropriate analysis.

## Activity Responses

1. **Empirical vs. Value Arguments**:
   - Empirical arguments assert factual states; correctness is based on external facts.
   - Value arguments make moral claims; correctness depends on reasoning.

2. **Argument Type Identification**:
   - (a) Empirical Argument
   - (b) Value Argument
   - (c) Empirical Argument
   - (d) Value Argument
   - (e) Value Argument
   - (f) Empirical Argument

---

## Deductive vs. Inductive Arguments

1. **Differences**:
   - **Deductive Arguments**:
     - Premises inherently contain the conclusion.
     - Valid if true premises guarantee the conclusion's truth.
   - **Inductive Arguments**:
     - Conclusions offer additional information beyond premises.
     - Based on reasonable expectations or observations.

2. **Examples**:
   - **Deductive Argument**:
     - "If A, then B. A is true, therefore B is true."
   - **Inductive Argument**:
     - "Specific observations suggest general conclusions."

---

## Valid vs. Invalid Deductive Arguments

1. **Valid Deductive Argument**:
   - Structure follows logical rules; premises sufficiently support conclusion.
   - Example:
     - "All humans are mortal; Socrates is human; therefore, Socrates is mortal."

2. **Invalid Deductive Argument**:
   - Structure is flawed; premises do not lead logically to conclusion.
   - Example:
     - "If A, then B; not A; therefore, not B."

---

## Conclusion

- **Sound Argument**: A valid argument with all true premises.
- **Evaluating Arguments**:
  - Identify argument type.
  - Assess validity and soundness based on premises and conclusion support.

### Page 103 - Conclusion and Arguments

### Page 104 - More Fallacies and Definitions

- **False dilemma**: Occurs when a reasoner presents a choice between two options when more alternatives exist.
- **Faulty analogy**: When a comparison is drawn between two different cases without relevant similarities.
- **Hasty generalisation**: Conclusion made based on insufficient evidence.
- **Inductive argument**: Argument where the conclusion is subject to probability.
- **Invalid deductive argument**: Argument where the structure is invalid; premises do not adequately support the conclusion.
- **Logical definition**: Definition based on properties shared by all items covered by the term.
- **Narrative writing**: Type of writing focused on unfolding a story or recounting a sequence of events.

- **Slippery slope argument**: Argument that leads from an apparently insignificant first premise to exaggerated consequences.

- **Sound**: Argument that is valid and has all premises accepted as true.
- **Soundness**: Relates to the truth or strength of the premises in an argument.
