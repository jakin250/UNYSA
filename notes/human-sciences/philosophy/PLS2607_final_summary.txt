### 2.1 THE OBJECTIVE SCIENTIST
- Science holds high prestige compared to other institutions.
- Traditional view: science as an objective, value-free activity; scientists portrayed as unbiased.
- Trust in scientists stems from their perceived credibility over others (e.g., politicians).
- Scientific conclusions are seen as products of the scientific method; believed to be free from ideology or prejudice.

### 2.2 THE LOGIC OF SCIENCE
- Importance of understanding scientific thought processes.
- The scientific method is seen as giving science its character and standing.
- Science regarded as rational: it effectively achieves discovery, explanation, and predictions.
- Claiming something is scientific gives it merit and reliability.
- Questions arise about the universality of rationality in science.

### 2.3 THE QUESTION OF “OBJECTIVITY”
- Belief in scientific inquiry as inherently objective.
- Prestige stems from perceived objectivity; objectivity defined through various lenses:
  - Accurate description of natural facts (scientific realism).
  - Objectivity derived from non-arbitrary, non-subjective criteria in hypothesis development (Longino 1998).
- According to Popper:
  - Scientific objectivity entails posing refutable hypotheses, testing them, and unambiguously reporting results.
- Objectivity achieved through critical scrutiny and peer review.
- Four criteria for an objectively operating scientific community (Longino 1998):
  1. Recognized avenues for criticism.
  2. Shared standards for critics.
  3. Serious consideration of criticism by the community.
  4. Equal intellectual authority among qualified practitioners.

### 2.4 IS “VALUE-FREE SCIENCE” POSSIBLE?
- The assumption of value-free science is debated.
- Hume's assertion: "ought cannot be derived from is," emphasizing the disconnect between facts and values.
- Ideal of value-free science posits that science should describe "what is" rather than "what ought to be."
- Hugh (1999:1) identifies two aspects of value-free science:
  1. Interaction of values with theory choice and consequences from accepted theories.
  2. The role of science in serving projects that express values.
- Methodologies and scientific inferences should be value-free.
- Common argument for value-free science: Science should be objective.

#### Experimental vs. Theoretical Laws
- Experimental laws can be directly falsified by empirical observations (e.g., Kepler's law).
- Theoretical laws (e.g., law of energy conservation) are intertwined with broader scientific theories.

### 3.2 The Hierarchy of Science
- Science as a developing system, not merely a collection of statements.

- **Logical Criteria**:

#### Core Principles of Logical Positivism

## STUDY UNIT 3: The Vienna Circle and Logical Positivism

### 3.1 Empirical Meaning and Theoretical Terms
- **Rules of Correspondence**: Provide empirical meaning to theoretical terms; analytic.
- **Theoretical Axioms**: Express observational portion of theory; synthetic.
- Deductive system required for formal distinction of sentences and terms.
- **Verifiability Principle**: Statement meaningful if verifiable relative to reality; non-verifiable statements (e.g., quantum particles) considered meaningless.

### 3.2 Approaches to Verifiability
- **Schlick (1931)**: Scientific principles as rules of inference; problem of meaning resolved.
- **Neurath (1931)**: Terms of abstract language defined via restricted, observable language.
- Shift to acceptance of observational vs. theoretical terms.
- **Carnap's Realization**: Theoretical terms indeterminable by observational terms; proposed reduction; ultimate elimination of theoretical terms.

### 3.3 Observation and Theory
- **Two-Language View of Science**: Science represents reality using observation language and theoretical language.
  - **Observation Language**: Reflects sense experience; describes phenomena (weight, color, shape, size).
  - **Theoretical Language**: Invented terms (wavelength, atomic weight) for unobservable phenomena (atoms, molecules).
- Science aims to organize experiential data for explanation and prediction; significant advances through theoretical laws.
  - **Example by Hempel (1965)**: Laws like “Wood floats on water and iron sinks” revised via theoretical term “specific gravity”: s = w/v.

### 3.4 Positivist Theory Structure
- Positivists classify terms: observational vs. theoretical.
- Theory depicted as rules (correspondence rules) where empirical data linked to logical calculus.
- **Scientific Theory**: Linguistic representation of human experience, capturing causal regularities in phenomena (Klee 1999).
- **Interpretation of Terms**:
  - Observational terms interpreted independently; theoretical terms gain meaning through correspondence rules.
  - Indeterminacy issues affect both types of terms; clear observational/theoretical distinction problematic.

### 3.5 Critique of Two-Language Structure
- **Popper**: All scientific concepts are hypothetical; every assertion entails hypotheses.
- **Quine**: Critiqued both observational/theoretical and analytic/synthetic distinctions.
- **Hempel**: Denied existence of observational terms; all observations mediated; terms theory-laden.
- **Carnap**: Emphasis on empirical laws directly confirmable vs theoretical laws only testable through empirical consequences.

### 3.6 Synthetic, Analytic, A Priori and A Posteriori Statements
- **Analytic vs. Synthetic Statements**: Analytic true by definition; synthetic defined beyond their terms.
- **Verifiability Principle**: Synthetic a priori statements lack meaning if not observed; assertions are either synthetic a posteriori or analytic a priori.
- Carnap's solution: Analyze theoretical terms against observational practices, separating synthetic from analytic statements.

### 3.7 Realism and Instrumentalism
- **Realism**: Claims entities exist independent of human perception; knowledge claims about reality possible.
  - **Scientific Realism**: Theories describe reality beyond observation; access to unbiased objects/properties.
  - Causal connections between theories and entities suggest likely truth of a theory.
  
- **Instrumentalism**: Contrasts realism; scientific theories viewed as mental constructs for organizing observable phenomena, relying on sensory observation for reality. 

**Critical Realism**: Recognizes subjective experience in developing scientific theory.

## STUDY UNIT 4: Induction, Popper and Falsificationism

### 4.1 Induction
- **Induction Process:**
  - Symbolization of observations: Object a (Fa), Object b (Fb) => Hypothesis: All objects possess quality F.
  - Inductive generalization proceeds from specific instances to universal conclusions.
  
- **Forms of Induction:**
  - **Statistical Generalization:**
    - Example: Testing population for blood group A; if 10% of tested population belongs to blood group A, we generalize that 10% of the entire population may belong.
  - **Analogy:**
    - Example: Similarities between Mars and Earth imply potential for life on Mars.
  - **Statistical Analogies:**
    - Example: 20% of women smoking during pregnancy leading to babies with low birth weight suggests similar outcomes for the broader population.

### 4.2 Arguing for Induction: Francis Bacon (1561–1626)
- **Definition of Induction:** (Bortolotti 2008:170)
  - Scientific reasoning based on observations leading to generalizations.
  
- **Bacon’s Inductivism:**
  - Inductive methodology focused on specific observations to derive general conclusions.
  - Critique of syllogisms for straying from experiential knowledge.
  
- **Replicability in Science:**
  - Emphasis on observation leading to confirmable laws and theories.
  
- **Induction vs. Deduction:**
  - Induction generates new knowledge; deduction confirms knowledge (universal premises leading to particular conclusions).

### 4.3 The “Problem” of Induction: David Hume (1711–1776)
- **Critique of Induction:**
  - Hume posits that induction lacks rational justification, arguing against the assumption that universal principles can be derived from particular observations.
  
- **Key Problems of Induction:**
  - **Uniformity of Nature:**
    - Assumptions about consistent natural laws cannot be verified; e.g., the sun rising.
  - **Cause-and-Effect Reasoning:**
    - Assumptions about causal connections may transcend observable evidence; e.g., discovering a watch on a deserted island does not confirm human presence as a cause.

### 4.3.1 The Problem of the Uniformity of Nature
- Hume questions reliance on uniformity for future predictions; the acceptance of future events remains unverified until they occur.

### 4.3.2 The Problem of Cause-and-Effect Reasoning
- Relationship between observed fact and inferred conclusion may not reflect actuality; assumptions may rely on unobserved connections.

## STUDY UNIT 4: Induction, Popper and falsificationism

### 4.1 Hume's Critique of Induction
- **Principle of Imagination**: Reason, not experience, infers conclusions.
- **Hume's Argument**: Cause and effect seen as unrelated; reality composed of causes without discernible effects (Hume 2009:221).
- **Billiards Example**: Various outcomes possible from one cause; cause-effect relationships require observation (Hume 2009:221).

### 4.2 The Problem of Reliance on Past Experience
- **Inductive Inference**: Past experiences can inform future predictions but do not guarantee truth (Hume 2009:223).
- **Assumptions in Inductivism**: Knowledge based solely on past experience lacks absolute certainty (Hume 2009:224).

### 4.3 The Swans Example
- **Historical Conclusion**: Premise that “all swans are white” based on observation lacks universality.
- **Counterexample**: Discovery of black swans invalidates the universal claim.
- **Hume’s Conclusion**: Induction cannot yield universal truths without complete observation.

### 4.4 Attempting to Counter Hume
#### 4.4.1 Reliance on Past Experience
- **Repetition**: Verifiable outcomes lead to accepted scientific theories (e.g., carbon dioxide test with limewater).
- **Limitations**: Observations only reflect current states, uncertain for future outcomes (Hume 2009:223).

#### 4.4.2 Uniformity of Nature
- **Premises**:
  - Premise 1: Historical consistency (e.g., sun rising).
  - Premise 2: Uniformity means future resembles past.
- **Hume’s Challenge**: Justification for assuming future conformity to past is weak; cannot be derived just from experience.

### 4.5 Reichenbach’s Vindication of Induction
- **Reichenbach's Position**: Induction as a speculative method rather than a source of certainties (Reichenbach 2009:368).
- **Best Assumption**: Inductive inference should derive from established knowledge.
- **Example**: Medical emergency scenario justifies inductive inference (Reichenbach 2009:368).
  
### 4.6 Mid-20th Century Critique of Induction
- **Naive Inductivism**: Criticism of science purely as observation without subjective factors.
- **Selection of Facts**: Challenges in determining relevant facts for observation; reliance on hypotheses to guide fact selection.

### 4.7 Falsificationism
- **Observational Limitations**: Induction cannot provide universal conclusions; observations can never be identical.
- **Popper's Rejection of Inductivism**: Emphasis on theories shaped by interpretations and expectations rather than repeated observations (Popper 2009:476-477).

### Key Cases and Legislation
- **Hume**: Explores the nature of induction and causality.
- **Popper**: Introduction of falsificationism emphasizing the limitations of inductive reasoning.

### Points to Ponder
- Are true universals possible in nature?
- How do we know which facts to select during scientific inquiry?
- Can a "wager" adequately support scientific methodology?

### Conclusion
Induction faces significant philosophical challenges, particularly through the critiques of Hume and Popper, highlighting its limitations in establishing absolute knowledge and leading to universal conclusions.

## STUDY UNIT 4: Induction, Popper and Falsificationism

### Observations and Theory-Laden Insights
- Observations influenced by pre-existing knowledge and theories (Jones 1982:335).
- **Inductivism**: Conclusion content not in premise content.
- Popper prioritizes prior knowledge in filtering observations over induction.
- **Popper 2009:478**: Pure observations without theories are considered absurd.

### Role of Theories in Observation
- Observations cannot be isolated; require a point of view based on existing theories and interests (Jones 1982:336).
- **Barrow 2007:192**: Science cannot rely solely on observation without theory.

### Popper’s Critique of Induction
- Induction viewed as misleading; scientific theories arise from conjectures, not observations (Prevos 2005).
- Science advances through **falsification**, not induction.
- Acceptance of counterinstances falsifies general laws (e.g., all swans are white).

### The Scientific Method
- Emphasizes conjecture and rigorous testing over observation and induction.
- **Popper 2009:478**: Hypotheses must explain observations but depend on theoretical frameworks.

### Problematic Nature of Induction 
- Induction misrepresents observation's role. Experiments test hypotheses, not justify them.
- Popper insists observations are inherently theory-laden (Popper 1972:107).

### Problem-Solving in Science
- Problems drive scientific inquiry; theory is crafted to solve observed issues.
- New theories generate further problems, fostering continuous inquiry.

### Distinctions of Discovery and Justification
- Discovery involves psychological processes; logic applies only post-discovery.
- Logical justification follows discovery, focusing on deduction over induction.
- Popper: logical tests are crucial for justifying scientific theories, emphasizing context separation.

### Corroboration vs. Confirmation
- **Corroboration**: Survives critical tests; reflects quality, not quantity.
- **Confirmation**: Inductive inference; lacks the depth of corroboration.
- Popper emphasizes severe testing as significant for corroboration.

### Testability and Theory Preference
- Theories must possess high empirical content for robust testing.
- Theories that withstand severe scrutiny are viable; those that persist beyond simpler tests are preferred.
  
### Conclusion on Popper’s Philosophy
- **Corroboration**: A measure of acceptability among scientific communities.
- Non-scientific theories lack testability; bold theories are favored.
- Popper rejects modifications that shield theories from falsification.

## STUDY UNIT 4: Induction, Popper and Falsificationism

### 4.1 Introduction  
- Theories may incorporate auxiliary hypotheses to avoid falsification.
- Example: Neptune's discovery confirmed Newton's theory regarding Uranus's orbit.

### 4.2 Popper’s Methodological Rules  
- Auxiliary hypotheses should not weaken a theory's falsifiability.
- Falsificationism argues for logical deductive testing instead of induction.

### 4.3 The Problem of Induction  
- Induction relates to demarcating science from non-science (Popper, 2009).
- Essential questions:  
  - What makes a theory scientific?  
  - How do we differentiate science from pseudoscience?

### 4.4 Demarcation Criteria  
- Popper critiques traditional inductive methodology. 
- Comparison of theories (e.g., Marxism, psychoanalysis) to primitive myths due to reliance on verification.
- Criteria for scientific theories:  
  1. Seek confirmations often yield findings confirming theories.  
  2. "Risky predictions" enhance scientific credibility.  
  3. Stronger theories are less porous.  
  4. Theories must be refutable.  
  5. Testability is key to scientific nature.  
  6. Corroborating evidence must arise from failed attempts to falsify.

## 5.4 Critique of Kuhn’s Paradigmatic View of Science  
- Kuhn’s historical approach is debated; his view emphasizes structure within science (Chalmers 2009:119).

- Kuhn asserts that paradigm shifts signify non-cumulative progress and differing perceptions of reality (Chalmers 2009:120-121).  
- Example: phlogiston vs. oxygen paradigms; previous theories lead to anomalies enabling new theories.    

### 5.4.3 Change in Standards of Evaluation Across Paradigms  
- Paradigm metrics (criteria, evaluation standards) are relative to the paradigm (Chalmers 2009:122).  
- Lack of universal standards raises questions about adequate paradigm replacement and relative comparison.  

### 5.4.4 “Kuhn-Loss”  
- New paradigms may fail to explain certain observations as well as the previous ones (Worrall 2002:32).  
- Kuhn’s method of selecting paradigms does not provide a logical necessity for abandoning old paradigms.

- In “Objectivity, Value Judgment, and Theory Choice,” Kuhn defends the notion of collective scientific community decisions (1998:212).

- **Hard Core**: Elemental principles defining a science; deemed "irrefutable" by its proponents (Lakatos, 1998).

### 6.2 Development of Research Programmes
- **Progressive Problem Shift**: A successful research programme should lead to development; degenerating programmes lack this progression (Lakatos, 1998).
- **Historical Examples**: 
  - **Copernican Programme**: Needed auxiliary modifications (epicycles).
  - **Newton’s Gravitational Theory**: Demonstrated problem shift by overcoming counterexamples.
- **Theoretical vs. Empirical Progress**: 
  - Theoretical progress discernible quickly, empirical slower; research must expand content consistently (Lakatos, 1998).

### 6.3 Abandonment of Research Programmes
- **Criteria for Termination**:
  - Failure to produce novel predictions and explanations indicates degeneration (Lakatos, 1998).
  - Comparison with rival programmes provides objective standard for rejection (Lakatos, 1998).

### 6.4 Critique of Lakatos
- **Vagueness in Replacement Criteria**: No clear rules for determining when to abandon degenerating programmes (Nickles, 2001).
- **Subjectivity in Choices**: Decisions to abandon based on individual researchers; lacks consistent objective standards.
- **Inadequate Instruments**: Transitioning theories may rely on evolving methods of measurement.
- **Over-reliance on Future Potential**: Non-progressive theories may still be chosen based on expected future insights.
  
### 6.5 Comparison with Kuhn
- **Kuhn's Paradigm vs. Lakatos's Research Programmes**: Both address theory evolution; however, Lakatos offers more structured testing against independent facts, unlike Kuhn's relativism.

### 6.6 Realism vs. Idealism
- **Kuhn’s Position**: Science operates within paradigms, yielding no common standards for inter-paradigm comparison; knowledge is subjective.
- **Lakatos’s Realism**: Argues for objective evaluation of scientific progress; knowledge relates directly to external reality, contrary to Kuhn's idealism.

### Conclusion
- **Philosophical Divergence**: The conflict between falsificationists like Lakatos and idealists like Kuhn represents an ongoing inquiry into the fundamental nature of scientific theory and reality.

- Western scientific norms stem from a specific cultural context, challenging claims of objectivity and universality in science.

## STUDY UNIT 7: Socially relevant philosophy of science: feminist and African philosophy of science

- **Cultural Context of Science**
  - Viewed as singular knowledge source; reflects European expansionism.
    - Value-neutrality in Western science as culturally specific (Harding, in Okere et al. 2005:56).
    - Most cultures favor their own values over neutrality (Confucian, Indigenous American, Islamic, Maori).

- **Cultural Influence on Knowledge Production**
  - Cultural dimensions manifest in scientific research practices.
    - Geography affects knowledge priorities (e.g., water conservation in desert).
    - Examples: Eugenics research by Nazi scientists exemplifying cultural biases (2005:58).
  
- **Localization vs. Universal Application**
  - Knowledge emerges from specific contexts but can transcend locality (2005:59).
    - Diversity in scientist backgrounds; effective modeling of natural laws leads to broader relevance (2005:62).
  
- **Compatibility of African and Western Science**
  - Theoretical constructions of African thought contrast with Western "local knowledge" (Ogunniyi 1988:5).
    - Mechanistic (Western) vs. anthropomorphic (African) explanatory models (Ogunniyi 1988:6).
    - Coexistence of scientific and traditional perspectives (1988:6-7).
    - Understanding human experience requires integrating both knowledge systems (1988:7).
  
- **Cultural Affiliation in Science**
  - Caution against abandoning African cultural perspectives for Western science.
    - Need for integration to avoid superficiality and oppression (1988:8).
  
- **Alternative Epistemologies**
  - Critiques of positivism by feminism and African philosophy underscore need for pluralism in scientific understanding.
    - Questioning absoluteness and acknowledging multiple accounts of phenomena (1988).
