## SECTION A: AFRICAN METAPHYSICS

### 1. Themes in African Metaphysics
#### 1.1 Introduction
- African metaphysics is a significant branch of African philosophy.
- Debate previously existed regarding African philosophy's existence, now widely accepted.
- Central questions relate to human experiences: success, illness, and misfortunes.
- Different cultures attribute varying significance to these questions based on their reality conception.

#### 1.2 Understanding Reality
- Metaphysics attempts to understand ultimate reality (Teffo & Roux 2003:162).
- Defined as a branch of philosophy addressing existence, essence, space, time, and causality.
- Cultural influences shape perceptions of reality and subsequent philosophical inquiries.

#### 1.3 Rationality
- Rationality is perceived differently across cultures; historical issues tied to eurocentrism.
- Claims of rationality have been used to deny African philosophy.
- Alternatives to universal truth include contextually constructed truths (Wiredu 1980).

### 2. Is There an African Metaphysics?
- Existence of African philosophy implies African metaphysics exists.
- African metaphysics reflects a particular way of conceptualizing reality shaped by history, location, and belief systems (Teffo & Roux 2003).
- Central themes include religion, self, causality, nature, and interconnectedness.

#### 2.1 Holism vs. Dualism
- Holism signifies interconnectedness among all entities; contrasts with Western dualism (mind vs. body).
- Causality is essential; African view is more teleological.
- Events are explained with purposeful reasoning, not chance.

### 3. Themes in African Metaphysics
#### 3.1 God
- God is central, interlinked with the universe (Teffo & Roux 2003).
- The Supreme Being coexists with creation, emphasizing unity in existence.

#### 3.2 Ancestors
- Ancestors play an important role; their world is akin to that of the living.
- Rituals maintain communication between the living and spirits.

#### 3.3 Witchcraft
- Belief in witchcraft is pervasive; used to explain misfortunes.
- Witchcraft fits within causal explanations in African metaphysics.

#### 3.4 Personhood
- The African understanding of personhood emphasizes relational existence: “I am because we are.”
- Challenges the individualistic Western perspective.

### 4. Destiny
- Concepts of destiny shape individual life paths; compatible with personal agency.
- Teffo and Roux argue that destiny and freedom coalesce in African thought.

### 5. Summary/Conclusion
- African metaphysics addresses fundamental existential questions.
- Emphasizes interconnectedness, causality, and unity of existence.
- Highlights the significance of ethical and cultural considerations in understanding reality.

### STUDY UNIT 2: The Concept of Cause in African Thought
#### 2.1 Introduction
- Centrality of causality in the African worldview; everything occurs for a reason.
- Causal explanations integrate spiritual and material dimensions.
  
#### 2.2 Sogolo’s Analysis
- Explores the dilemma of invoking non-material entities (spirits, gods) as causal agents.
- Seeks to reconcile traditional beliefs with observable events.

#### Key Points
- Understanding cause requires comprehensive explanations.
- African metaphysics sees events as interconnected and informed by both physical and spiritual causes.

**STUDY UNIT 2: The concept of cause in African thought**

- **Key Questions:**
  - Why did the fisherman drown despite his skills?
  - Role of spirits and ancestors in causality.
  - Nature of causal explanation in African societies.

- **Causal Explanation in African Thought:**
  - Traditional beliefs invoke the invisible to explain events.
  - Causation raises philosophical issues; Hume's thoughts on cause and effect.
  - **Sogolo (2003)** distinguishes causality by:
    - Nature of the event.
    - Importance and perspective on the event.
    - Whether causes are singular or multiple.
    - Compatibility of causes.
    - Sufficiency vs. necessity of causes.

- **Two Understandings of Causality:**
  1. **Primary/Non-mechanistic**: Holistic view of causality.
  2. **Secondary/Mechanistic**: Empirical cause-and-effect.

- **Horton’s Position (1998):**
  - Minimal difference between African traditional explanations and Western science.
  - Cultures shape explanatory theories.
  - Commonalities between religious thought and scientific inquiry.
  - Theory seeks unity amidst diversity; parallels common across cultures.
  
- **Critiques of Horton (Sogolo, 2003):**
  - Traditional explanations seek avoidance, not control, of natural events.
  - Causal interest is perspective-dependent (e.g., fire explanation from varied professional lenses).

- **Health and Illness:**
  - **Primary Causes**: Supernatural/spiritual (e.g., emotions, witchcraft).
  - **Secondary Causes**: Physical (e.g., social relations causing stress).
  - Herbalism combines medical knowledge with spiritual practices.

- **Causality and Culture:**
  - No strict dichotomy between natural and supernatural explanations.
  - Beliefs about causality are integral to diagnostic processes in African thought.
  - Conflict between science and traditional beliefs is exaggerated.

---

**SUMMARY/CONCLUSION:**
- African notions of causality incorporate physical and spiritual dimensions.
- Two views: primary (non-mechanistic) and secondary (mechanistic) relate to diverse causal explanations.
- Common sense underlies both scientific and traditional explanatory frameworks.

**STUDY UNIT 3: African conceptions of a person: A critical survey**

- **Overview:**
  - Analysis of various African philosophical traditions regarding self and personhood.
  - Importance of conception of self in dignity, rights, and identity.

- **West African Conceptions of Person:**
  - **Akan Perspective (Wiredu & Gyekye)**:
    - Personhood composed of:
      1. **Okra (soul)**: Divine essence, distinct from Western soul concept.
      2. **Sunsum**: Individual personality traits; views differ on immateriality.
      3. **Body (honam)**: Flesh, bones; clan identity linked to maternal lineage.

- **Yoruba Conception of Person (Gbadegesin):**
  - Eniyan composed of:
    1. **Ara (body)**: Physical vessel.
    2. **Okan (heart)**: Emotional and psychic responses; source of consciousness.
    3. **Emi (soul/spirit)**: Divine breath, lifegiving principle.
    4. **Ori**: Physical and spiritual, determining destiny.

- **Eastern and Southern African Conceptions:**
  - **Force Thesis (Tempels)**: Vital force as essence of being.
  - **Communalism Thesis (Mbiti)**: Person defined within community; individual exists through collective.

- **Critiques of Communalism:**
  - Potential denial of individual autonomy and rights.
  - Kaphagawani's analysis underscores cultural narratives in philosophy.

**4.1. Epistemic Threads in Custom**

- Avoid dichotomy of tradition vs. modernity; African traditions adapt and endure.

1. Must encompass both similarities and differences across cultures.
2. Must integrate advancements from Western science for contemporary relevance.

**4.5. Epistemological Authority**
- Authority may shift from elders to literate individuals, impacting knowledge production.

**4.6. Rational Appreciation of Traditional Beliefs**
- Examine contemporary relevance of traditional beliefs and practices.
- Culture appreciation requires comprehensive understanding.

**4.7. Philosophical Sage vs. Folk Sage**
- Philosophic sagacity: method to derive philosophy from community sages.
- Role of sages in community knowledge and cultural renewal.

### STUDY UNIT 5: Is there one science, Western science?

**5.1. Context of Science Inquiry**
- Okere (2005) explores notions of Western dominance in knowledge.
- Hegemony of science inhibits diverse contributions; global coexistence might depend on emancipation of different knowledge forms.

**5.2. Definition of Science**
- Etymological roots: Latin 'scientia' - knowledge; historical context complicates its meaning.

**5.3. Terms “West” and “Western”**
- Historically constructs; ambiguous notions influencing perceptions of superiority.

**5.4. Science Classification**
- Two understandings: general knowledge vs. systematic specialized knowledge.
- Western science does not encompass all knowledge.

**5.5. Western Contributions to Science**
- Acknowledges Western achievements but warns against exclusivity.

**5.6. Collective Human Achievement in Science**
- Emphasizes that all cultures contribute to knowledge; exclusive claims undermine cooperation.

**5.7. Strengths and Weaknesses of Western Science**
- Technology's success does not equate to comprehensive understanding of human experience; ethical dilemmas arise.

**5.8. Critique of Science**
- Kuhn’s perspective challenges linearity of scientific progress; suggests inherent cultural biases exist within scientific paradigms.

**5.9. Other Knowledge Traditions**
- Rejecting non-written traditions undermines the universal landscape of knowledge; exemplifies cultural contributions like medicine and agriculture.

**5.10. Validity of Igbo Medicine**
- Igbo medicine’s holistic approach contrasts with Western reductionism; acknowledges diverse forces at play in health.

- African epistemology, as a branch of African philosophy, addresses local and universal knowledge definitions.
- Universalist vs. particularist views on knowledge: former sees knowledge as universally applicable; latter emphasizes cultural contexts.
- Responsibility lies with African philosophers to revitalize indigenous knowledge systems for contemporary relevance.

## 1. GLOSSARY OF TERMS

- **Phronesis**: Practical wisdom distinguishing proper ends of conduct and means to attain them (Aristotle).
  
- **Platonic tradition**: Philosophical legacy of Plato, encompassing various approaches; associated with dualistic arguments.
  
- **Positivism**: Philosophy deeming science as the only true knowledge source; dismisses theology and metaphysics.
  
- **Postmodernism**: Reacts against epistemological modernity; accepts epistemological relativism—rationality is not absolute.
  
- **Pragmatism**: Defines truth by results; actions deemed good if they produce desirable outcomes.
  
- **Praxis**: Activity with an intrinsic goal, often associated with Marxist theories.
  
- **Premise**: Propositions supporting an argument; conclusion is grounded in premises.
  
- **Presupposition**: Unproven ideas influencing thoughts/actions.
  
- **Principle of excluded middle**: States a proposition is either true or false, no intermediate state allowed.
  
- **Principle of non-contradiction**: No statement may be both asserted and denied in the same context.
  
- **Proposition**: Something that can be true or false; differs from a sentence in grammar.
  
- **Reflection**: Consideration of a thinking activity.
  
- **Reductio ad absurdum**: Proving a statement by showing its negation leads to contradiction.
  
- **Reduction**: Simplifying complex events to simpler familiar kinds; evident in stringently reductive philosophies.
  
- **Relativism**: No absolute standards; truth/morality varies based on context.
  
- **Rheomode**: Language aiming to overcome fragmentation of being; focuses on flow rather than static definitions.
  
- **Structuralism**: Understanding social/linguistic phenomena through underlying structures; reduces perspectives to one valid view.
  
- **Surrealism**: Artistic movement accessing a 'real world' beyond ordinary experience through art.
  
- **System**: Interrelated elements functioning as a unit; systematic explanations arise from interrelations.
  
- **Telos**: Aim or purpose; teleological perspective attributes purpose to events.
  
- **Universal principles**: Apply universally, contrasting with particulars; addresses metaphysical questions.
  
- **Verification**: Conclusively proving a statement’s truth.
