# STUDY UNIT 1. CONQUEST AND THE HISTORY OF WESTERN EDUCATION AND PHILOSOPHY IN SOUTH AFRICA

## 2.1 Introduction
- Post-1990s: Rise of non-racialism in South African legal and academic discourse.
- Constitution Act 108 of 1996: Proclaims non-racialism as a founding principle.
- Courts’ interpretation: Ruling against "blacks only" organizations; outlawing certain liberation songs.
- Non-racialism definition: Resembles Theo David Goldberg's “anti-racialism,” distinct from anti-racism (Goldberg, 2006: 257).
- Critique: Anti-racialism fails to alleviate historical injustices and often blames victims of racism for social justice efforts.
- Historical origin: Dominant conception of non-racialism stems from English-speaking settlers; linked to Cape Liberalism and problematic “right of conquest” (Day, 2008, pp. 92-111).

## 2.2 The Study of Racism and Philosophy in South Africa
- Race and racism: Historically neglected topics in South African academia.
- Rise of Critical Philosophy of Race: Highlights philosophy’s complicity in racism.
- Key texts: Analyze racism within the "great Western tradition" (Eze, 1997; Serequeberhan, 2007).
- Philosophical contributions: Essential for understanding origins and implications of racism; philosophy should not be passive in these discourses.
- Other disciplines like political science, sociology: Also under-address racism; philosophy arguably worse (Mills, 1998).

## 2.3 Racism, the Eurocentric University, and the Marginality of African Philosophy in South Africa

### 2.3.1 Introduction
- Educational foundations: Established by European settlers, focused on maintaining cultural dominance.
- Curriculum influences: Reflects trends from the settlers' home, ignoring local context.
- Indigenous peoples: Admitted to education under systemic poverty and cultural disconnection.
- Education aimed at assimilation: Presents conquerors' culture as superior, disregarding indigenous identity (Loram, 1934; McKerron, 1934).
- Post-1994: Minimal change in university identity; Eurocentrism persists, despite integrated admission policies.

### 2.3.2 Some General Characteristics of South African Philosophy
- Overview: Not exhaustive, focuses on institutional philosophy influenced by colonial, Eurocentric structures.
- Apartheid: Seen as a continuation of long-standing colonial racism (More).
- Two colonial traditions: Dutch (1652) and British (1820s onward) influence philosophy and education.
- Philosophy's exclusionary practices: Systematic discrimination against indigenous peoples continues.

### 2.3.3 The Afrikaans-Continental Tradition

# Study Notes on Pre-Socratic Philosophy

## 3.5 Anaximenes
- Anaximenes shifted focus from Anaximander's abstract concepts.
- Proposed **condensation-rarefaction** as singular mechanism for physical change.
- Claimed all changes result from densities of an underlying element, which he called **air**:
  - Air → Compression (cold) → Water → Earth
  - Air → Rarefaction (hot) → Fire
- His analysis is the ultimate achievement of Milesian philosophy.

### Points to Ponder
- **Problem of the arche**: Why did the Ancient Greeks seek a singular cause in their understanding of nature?
- Anaximenes's assertion implies a spectrum where all elements are simply varying states of one another.

## 3.6 Other Significant Pre-Socratics
- Contrasted **Heraclitus** with **Parmenides**:
  - Both emphasize the arche as beyond sensory perception.
  - Offer opposing views on the nature of reality.

### 3.6.1 Heraclitus
- Argued for perpetual change; famously stated, "One cannot step into the same river twice."
- Identified **Fire** as the ultimate reality, representing constant change and renewal.
- Introduced the concept of **logos**, a principle that states all things pass into their opposites.
- **Cratylus**, disciple of Heraclitus, argued that constant change negates permanence, even in language.

### Implications of Cratylus's Thought
- Challenges traditional notions of knowledge as absolute and unchanging due to the fluidity of concepts and language.

### 3.6.2 Parmenides
- Asserts that change is an illusion; true change requires an entity to come into being, which is logically impossible.
- Concludes:
  - **Being** is one, uncreated, and indestructible.
  - All diversity is illusory; thus, movement and change are impossible.
- Differentiates between **The Way of Belief** (common views) and **The Way of Truth** (rational thought).
- Opposes Heraclitus; emphasizes reason over sensory experience.

## 3.7 The Sophists
- Group of 5th-century professionals focused on education, particularly in rhetoric and persuasion rather than seeking absolute truth.
- Emphasis on **moral relativism** and subjective opinion:
  - Knowledge is shaped by human perception and cannot yield universal truths.
- Sophists recognized the influence of the human mind in knowledge acquisition.

### Points to Ponder
- Consider the implications of the Sophists' views on moral relativism in contemporary society.

## 3.8 Socrates
- No direct writings; known through Plato's dialogues.
- Illustrated the **Socratic Method**, involving systematic questioning to reach definitions of concepts.
- Advocated against circular reasoning in defining ideas:
  - Rejects definitions that include the term being defined.
  - Tests definitions through reasoning and premises.
- Aim: To refine understanding by eliminating faulty premises.

### Example of the Socratic Method
- Socrates prompts Laches to define courage, revealing complexities and ambiguities in thought processes.

## 3.8 Plato's Theory of Knowledge

### 3.8.1 Overview of Platonic Theory
- Knowledge rests on intuitive beliefs that cannot be rationally justified.
- Distinction between the real world (Forms) and the world of sense perception (shadows).
- Objective truth discovered through understanding, akin to light in Plato's Allegory of the Cave.
- The soul, prior to earthly existence, knows the Forms, and knowledge is a recollection of this.

### 3.8.2 Doctrine of Anamnesis (Recollection)
- Dualism: Immaterial soul superior to material body; influence on later philosophies (e.g. Descartes).
- Soul belongs to the changeless world of Forms; forgetfulness due to pollution from material existence.
- Knowledge arises from remembering the absolute Forms through imperfect sense perception.

### 3.8.3 Summary of Plato's Theory of Knowledge
- Knowledge is permanent and certain, beyond correction.
- Claims of knowledge cannot base on sense perceptions; only true beliefs guide to Forms.
- Forms are eternal, changeless, and known through mental capacity for recollection.

### 3.8.4 Relationship Between Knowledge, Truth, Belief, and Justification
- Knowledge equals true mental states that conform to Forms; belief is distinct from knowledge.
- Knowledge requires justification; cannot be an accident or coincidence.

### 3.8.5 Criticism of Plato's Theory
- Challenges in explaining the relationship between Forms and particulars.
- Ambiguities surrounding the role and existence of multiple Forms (e.g., virtue, courage).
- Issues with the notion of participation: Forms may not correlate with particulars coherently.
- "Third Man Argument": Critique of Forms relating to particulars, proposing infinite regress.
- Difficulty in defending Forms as objects of true knowledge if separate from particulars.

## 3.9 Aristotle's Epistemology

### 3.9.1 Knowledge through Sense Perception
- Reality is perceived through senses but is ever-changing from potentiality to actuality.
- Reason uncovers unchanging qualities amidst changing phenomena.

### 3.9.2 Classification of Knowledge
- Categorization begins at contingent (changing) properties, moving to universal (genus) and essential (species).
- Essential properties defined by four causes of existence.

### 3.9.3 The Interdependence of Forms and Particulars
- Forms and particulars are not independent; they rely on one another for existence.
- Sense perception is crucial for understanding Forms; knowledge of universals stems from particulars.

### 3.9.4 Distinction Between Definitions and Descriptions
- Particulars cannot be defined but identified; essential properties belong only to the species (e.g., "Man" as rational animal).
- Received understanding of individuals through descriptive attributes rather than essential qualities.

### 3.9.5 Geometrical Examples
- Knowledge of geometrical forms built on defining and essential attributes, which allow logical deduction of further properties (e.g., circles).

### 3.9.1 Induction and Essential Attributes
- **Induction**: Generalizing properties by observing particulars; a guide, not definitive for essential attributes. 
- **Accidental Properties**: Observations (e.g., featherless bipeds) may be universal but not essential. Requires causal explanation.
- **Causal Connection**: Establishment is necessary to assert essential properties. E.g., planets do not twinkle due to proximity to Earth.
- **Nous**: Intuition as a means to understand causes and obtain knowledge, more reliable than deduction (compared to Descartes).

### 3.9.2 Summary of Aristotle's Analysis of Knowledge
- **Realism**: Forms exist objectively and interdependently with particulars.
- **Knowledge Acquisition**: Based on sense perception of particulars, unlike Plato’s recollection.
- **Necessary Attributes**: Discovery relies on observation and association of properties.
- **Reasoned Facts**: Derived from understanding causes and support scientific knowledge.
- **Empirical Inquiry**: Yields knowledge comparable in certainty to mathematical knowledge.
- **Knowledge Conditions**: Connected to truth, belief, and justification; emphasis on systems rather than individual mental states.

### 3.9.3 The Doctrine of the Four Causes
- **Material Cause**: The substance composition (e.g., bronze for a statue).
- **Formal Cause**: The shape or structure (e.g., the statue's specific design).
- **Efficient Cause**: The initiator of change (e.g., the sculptor creating the statue).
- **Final Cause**: The purpose or goal (e.g., aesthetic pleasure from a statue).

#### 3.9.3.1 Metaphysical Implications
- **Knowledge Accessibility**: Achievable through observation of physical laws, different from Plato’s mystical approach.
- **Causal Relationships**: Key to determine knowledge; chance events remain unexplainable.
- **Contrast in Metaphysics**: Aristotle's reality is tied to observable physical objects, contrasting Plato’s hierarchy of Forms.
- **Actuality and Potentiality**: Forms and matter are interlinked; change signifies a dynamic process involving both. 

### Conclusion
- **Entelechy**: Internal striving toward fulfillment of one’s nature. Distinction between potentiality (ability to become) and actuality (realization of potential).

# Study Unit 3: Medieval Philosophy (Augustine, Anselm, and Aquinas)

### 4.1 Introduction
- Transition from ancient Greek philosophy to medieval philosophy (400 CE - 1400 CE).
- Focus on St. Augustine and St. Thomas Aquinas as pivotal figures.
- Examination of the synthesis of Platonic ideas with biblical doctrine in Augustine's work.
- Anselm's rational proofs for God's existence reflect a resurgence of reasoning post-"Dark Ages."
- Aquinas's synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy with Christian theology marks a significant philosophical evolution.

### 4.2 The Historical Background
- St. Augustine (354-430) lived during the decline of the Roman Empire.
- Witnessed the Christianisation of the ancient world and the fall of Rome.
- His life represents the transition from antiquity to the Middle Ages, shaping Western thought.

### 4.3 Augustine's Theory of Knowledge
- Integration of Platonic and Biblical epistemology.
- Knowledge derived from divine illumination and inner reflection.
- Emphasis on faith as a pathway to understanding truth, contrasting with purely empirical methods.

### 4.4 Anselm’s Ontological Argument
- Anselm's argument posits the existence of God as a necessary being.
- **Key Principle**: God is "that than which nothing greater can be conceived."
- Critique: Addresses potential counterarguments against the necessity of God's existence.

### 4.5 Aquinas’s Five Proofs for the Existence of God
- **Proof 1**: Argument from Motion – Change necessitates an Unmoved Mover (similar to Aristotle).
- **Proof 2**: Argument from Causation – Every effect has a cause; this chain must lead to a first cause (God).
- **Proof 3**: Argument from Contingency – Contingent beings imply a necessary being for their existence.
- **Proof 4**: Argument from Degrees of Perfection – Objective gradation of qualities suggests a perfect model (God).
- **Proof 5**: Teleological Argument – Order and purpose in the universe indicate a Designer.

### 4.6 Comparison of Anselm’s Argument and Aquinas’s Proofs
- Anselm's argument is purely conceptual, while Aquinas provides empirical proofs.
- Both aim to affirm God's existence but differ in method and philosophical approach.

### 4.7 Theological Implications of Aquinas’s Conception of Nature
- Nature reflects God's rational order; natural law is divinely instituted.
- Explores the relationship between faith and reason in understanding the natural world.

### 4.8 Critical Observations
- Examination of potential limitations in Augustine's and Aquinas's theories.
- Discussion of the integration of philosophy and theology during the medieval period.

### 4.9 Summary
- Medieval philosophy represents a crucial link in the evolution of Western thought.
- The contributions of Augustine, Anselm, and Aquinas were foundational in shaping Western epistemology and metaphysics.
- Emphasis on the synthesis of reason and faith remains relevant in contemporary philosophical discourse. 

### References
- Notable references include Augustine's works, Anselm's *Proslogion*, and Aquinas’s *Summa Theologica*. 

### Conclusion
- The synthesis of philosophical thought during the medieval period set the stage for future developments in Western philosophy, influencing thinkers through the Renaissance and beyond.

### 4.3 AUGUSTINE’s Approach to Human Knowledge and the Nature of Reality

- Existence of ideas necessitates the existence of a corresponding reality.
- Greatest possible being (God) must exist beyond mere thought.

#### Knowledge and Sensation (4.3.1)
- Sensory knowledge provides minimal certainty due to:
  - Variability of objects and sensory organs.
- Augustine asserts:
  - Senses are reliable; errors arise from judgment, not sensation.
  - Judgments involve comparing sensory experience to higher ideals.
- Human beings, unlike animals, can make rational judgments beyond senses.
- Four elements of sensation:
  1. Object sensed.
  2. Bodily organ.
  3. Mind’s image formation.
  4. Immateriaral standards for judgment.
- Knowledge progresses from sensation to understanding eternal truths, with the knowledge of God as the highest form.

#### The Doctrine of Illumination (4.3.2)
- Challenges how finite minds access ultimate truths without divine assistance.
- Rejects:
  - Plato’s recollection.
  - Aristotle’s abstraction.
- Proclaims: "I believe in order to know."
- Mind requires divine illumination to understand eternal truths:
  - Divine light reveals the quality of truth in ideas.

#### God (4.3.3)
- Investigation of reality driven by religious faith rather than secular reasoning.
- God defined as ultimate reality, encompassing truth and being.
- God as source of unchangeable truth; knowledge reflects God's attributes.
- Augustine posits that to know God is to understand the world and humanity's nature.

#### The Created World (4.3.4)
- Recognizes the physical world’s importance while distinguishing it from true knowledge.
- Describes God’s unique act of creation.

##### Creatio ex nihilo (4.3.4.1)
- God created all from nothing, distinct from Plato's form extensions.
- All changes and forms are the product of God’s creative act.

##### Rationes seminales (4.3.4.2)
- Continuous species production based on implanted seminal principles.
- Existing things can redevelop forms but not create new species.
- Seminal principles as invisible potentials for change and development.

### Critical Observations (4.3.5)
- An intertwining of theology with philosophy; philosophy as a path to salvation.
- Augustine’s theories reflect historical context and the quest for peace amidst turmoil.
- Overlapping of metaphysical and biblical understandings of God, stressing the need for a coherent view of divine nature.
- Challenges in relating finite human experience to the infinite qualities of God.
- Critique of Augustine’s synthesis of personal God with metaphysical concepts as potentially inadequate.

  - Pessimistic view of humankind; this view persisted for over a thousand years.

# 4.4 ANSELM AND THE PROOFS FOR THE EXISTENCE OF GOD

## 1. Knowledge and Divine Illumination
- Physical world is changing; unaided knowledge is uncertain.
- God provides physical light and divine illumination to discern truth: “believing is seeing.”
- God harbors the seminal principle ensuring existence and diversity.

## 4.4.1 Anselm's Ontological Argument
- Developed in **Monologion** and **Proslogion**; premises include:
  1. **Goodness Argument**: 
     - Good things must stem from a common source, identified as God, who is Goodness itself.
  2. **Causality Argument**: 
     - Infinite regress of causes is impossible; requires singular or multiple causes leading to God as non-finite cause.

- **Ontological Proof**:
  - Concept of God as the greatest conceivable being necessitates existence in reality; otherwise, it is not the greatest.

## 4.4.2 Objections
- Critiques include:
  1. **Idea of Perfect Being**:
     - Gaunilo's objection on whether the concept of absolute perfection is universally accepted.
     - Anselm's counter: the existence of varying degrees of perfection implies a standard of absolute perfection.
  2. **Existence as Added Perfection**:
     - Questioning if existence adds to perfection; a perfect being must exist in reality.

## 4.4.3 Conclusion
- Anselm's proofs raise questions about the relationship between faith and reason.
- Notably influences the understanding of proof validity and the theological doctrines concerning faith and reason, paving the way for **Thomas Aquinas**.

## 1-6. Proofs for God's Existence

  3. **Possibility and Necessity**: Things can be contingent (possible) or necessary; necessary existence implies God.

### 4.5.1.1 Proof from motion
- "Whatever is in motion must be put in motion by another"; requires an initial unmoved mover (God).

### 4.5.1.2 Proof from efficient causality
- No infinite series of efficient causes; necessitates an uncaused cause (God).

### 4.5.1.3 Proof from possibility and necessity
- Contingent beings indicate a necessary being (God).

### 5.1 Introduction

#### 5.1.1 What is ethics?

- Ethical theory investigates what constitutes morally right and wrong actions.
- It aims to establish principles, values, and assumptions that inform moral conduct.
- Ethics examines moral duty, obligation, right and wrong, good and evil.
- Ethical principles should relate to ordinary moral experiences and human perspectives on the "good life."
- Theories face challenges in multicultural contexts with differing views of rationality and morality.
- *Difference between ethical theorist (examines moral conduct critically) and moralist (promotes moral quality in communities).*

---

### 5.2 Major Questions and Themes in Western Ethical Theory

#### 5.2.1 The Greek tradition: what is the highest Good?

- Central focus on the highest Good linked to moral perfection and political institutions.
- Ethics viewed as pursuit of the “good life” associated with human happiness.
- **Aristotle's view**: ethics addresses the question “How should I live?” connected to the highest Good (happiness).

#### 5.2.2 Ethics in the Judeo-Christian tradition: how is moral conduct to be defined?

- Introduces love (compassion) alongside Greek ethical considerations.
- Separation between ethical and political realms; authority of the church over the state.
- Ethical principles derived from righteousness before God and love towards others.
- **Saint Augustine**: ethics from Christian creed, emphasizing free will and love.
- **Thomas Aquinas**: moral laws as natural laws based on human inclinations.

#### 5.2.3 Ethics in the modern tradition: how is moral conduct to be defined?

**5.2.3.1 Consequentialist ethics**
- Rightness/wrongness based on consequences.
- **Utilitarianism**: right actions promote general happiness; **Bentham** and **Mill's** contributions.

**5.2.3.2 Non-consequentialist or deontological ethics**
- Rightness based on the significance of the action itself, independent of outcomes.
- **Immanuel Kant's** deontological approach; morality not solely determined by majority wellbeing.

---

### 5.3 SOCRATES (470-399 BC)

- Significant figure; influenced **Plato**; historical teachings known through Plato and Xenophon.
- Charged with impiety, sentenced to death; executed in 399 BCE.
- Employed dialectic method and Socratic irony, focusing on virtues like piety and temperance.
- Believed in objective virtues attainable through rational discourse.
- Confronted relativism of **Protagoras**; sought universal ethical standards.
- Advocated for the “improvement of the soul” through rational inquiry.
- **Philosophy of ignorance** led to questioning norms; aimed for rational foundations of ethics.
  
---

### 5.4 PLATO (428-347 BC)

- Born into a distinguished Athenian family; influenced by Athenian culture.
- Plato seeks the universal “highest Good,” moving beyond Socratic definitions of virtues.
- Overcomes Socratic ignorance with the **theory of recollection**; ethical knowledge linked to memory.
- Ethical theory intertwined with metaphysics and epistemology; believes in eternal Ideas.
- Views moral perfection linked to knowledge of the Form of the Good.
- Philosophers as moral instructors; rational insight superior to mysticism.
- Challenges in identifying knowledge claims that transcend human condition are prominent.

# PLATONIC AND ARISTOTELIAN ETHICS

## 5.4 PLATO (427-347 BC)

### 5.4.1 The Highest Good
- **Purified Soul**: Central to Plato’s ethics. Knowledge attained by distancing oneself from bodily influences.
- **Allegory of the Cave**: Humanity's ignorance compared to prisoners in a cave, mistaking shadows for reality.
- **Realm of Being vs. Becoming**: The soul seeks knowledge in the realm of being (Parmenidean), while the body is tied to the inferior realm of becoming (Heraclitus).
- **Moral Existence**: Abstaining from physical pleasures aligns with the pursuit of the highest Good.
- **Reincarnation**: Represents the soul's journey towards perfection, achievable through the process of recollection.

### 5.4.2 Search for Truth
- **Form of the Good**: Represents an ideal; knowledge and virtue correlate with insight into the highest Good.
- **Alienation**: Disconnect from one's true Self represents moral imperfection.
- **Justice**: Practical implications of ethics translated into political theory.

### 5.4.3 Allegory Interpretation
- **The Sun**: Represents the Form of the Good; knowledge derived from understanding this source leads to moral perfection.
- **Moral Guidance**: Understanding the highest Good is essential for rational choices in private and public life.

### 5.5 ARISTOTLE (384-322 BC)

### 5.5.1 Ethical Framework
- **Nicomachean Ethics**: Formulated alongside Politics; explores human conduct and practical reasoning.
- **Practical Reason vs. Theoretical Reason**: Differentiates between rationality in moral-political conduct (praxis) and scientific knowledge (episteme).
- **Empirical Basis**: Ethics grounded in inductive reasoning over deductive.

### 5.5.2 Defining the Highest Good
- **Chief Good**: All actions aim at a higher end; knowledge of this Good influences life choices.
- **Human Happiness**: Considered the highest Good but varies by individual perspective.
- **Human Nature**: Unique capacity to reason distinguishes humanity; education fosters moral excellence.

### 5.5.3 Characteristics of Virtue
- **Deliberate Choice**: Virtue arises from reasoned decisions.
- **Moral Excellence**: Recognized virtues include friendship, fairness, self-discipline, and courage.

### 5.5.4 Friendship
- **Types of Friendship**: 
  - Genuine: Based on mutual goodness.
  - Strategic: Based on personal gain.
- **True Friendship**: Only possible between good individuals; predicated on moral choice.

### 5.5.5 The Contemplative Life
- **True Happiness**: Achieved through intellectual excellence and contemplation, rather than physical pleasures.
- **Pleasure and Happiness**: Contemplative activities provide lasting joy and align with human nature.

### 5.5.6 Summary
- **Determining the Good Life**: Influenced by nature, custom, and education.
- **Political Science and Practice**: Correct judgment in governance requires both theoretical knowledge and practical experience.
- **Role of the Soul**: True happiness rooted in rational function and spiritual as well as sensuous dimensions.

# STUDY UNIT 5: MEDIEVAL ETHICS [AUGUSTINE AND AQUINAS]

## 6.1 St AUGUSTINE (354-430 CE)

- **Background:**
  - Born in Numidia; father pagan, mother Christian.
  - Bishop of Hippo (396 CE); focused on personal destiny.

### 6.1.1 Introduction
- Concern: Moral evil's existence vs. a good God.
- Initial turn to **Manichaeans** for dualistic approach (light vs. darkness).
- Shift to **Platonism** in 386 CE; philosophy consistent with Christian faith.
- Philosophy merges faith and reason.

### 6.1.2 Augustine's Ethical Theory
- Morality as foundational; pursuit of happiness central.
- **6.1.2.1 God as Our Chief Good**
  - Happiness attained by turning to the supernatural.
  - Highest good is God, source of all goodness (aligned with biblical teachings).

- **6.1.2.2 The Role of Love**
  - Human beings inherently love; we can misplace our love.
  - Disordered love causes misery; little `good` compared to God.
  - Example: Overeating exemplifies disordered love through misplacement.

- **6.1.2.3 Free Will as the Cause of Evil**
  - Free will is a gift; misused free will leads to moral evil.
  - Contrast with **Manichaeans** (evil from dualism) and **Plato** (evil as ignorance).
  - Story of Adam and Eve represents choice and consequence of free will.

- **6.1.2.4 The Two Cities**
  - Concept of two cities: **City of God** vs. **Earthly City**.
  - Aligning with the City of God represents focusing on eternal goodness.
  - Final judgment reflects choices made in life.

### 6.1.3 St. THOMAS AQUINAS (1225-1274)

- Education under Albert Magnus; early influence from **Aristotle**.
- Harmony between Aristotle's philosophy and Christian theology.

### 6.1.4 Introduction
- Aquinas views morality as happiness-seeking; welcomes both natural and supernatural ends.
- Ethical theory builds upon **Aristotelian** foundations, but engages with faith.

### 6.1.5 Aquinas's Ethical Theory

- **6.1.5.1 The Concept of Law**
  - **Natural law** as a core foundation; law defined as a dictate from a ruler.
  - Types of law: **Eternal Law, Natural Law, Human Law, Divine Law**.

- **6.1.5.2 The Concept of Natural Law**
  - Morality isn't arbitrary; deeply rooted in human nature.
  - Human inclinations allow participation in eternal law (example: gravity).

- **6.1.5.3 The Precepts of Natural Law**
  - Basic inclination to "seek our human good"; fundamental precept: "do good and avoid evil."
  - Three levels of human inclinations:
    - 1. Self-preservation (substances)
    - 2. Species preservation (animals)
    - 3. Truth-seeking and societal living (rational animals).

## 6.2 A Brief Overview of Our Focus
- **Origins**: Political philosophy in the Western tradition begins with ancient Greece, focusing on justice in community life.
- **Philosophers**: Emphasis on Plato and Aristotle regarding governance and justice.
- **Key Questions**: What is the best form of government? How can harmony be established amidst individual differences?

## 6.3 PLATO
- **The Republic**: Plato's exploration of justice within the state.
- **Justice and Happiness**: Individual happiness is contingent on adherence to justice, rooted in knowledge of the highest Good.
- **State as Individual**: State reflects individual needs and psychology, proposing three classes: 
  1. Artisans (economic class)
  2. Auxiliaries (military class)
  3. Guardians (political leadership)

- **Three Parts of the Soul**:
  - Physical (needs like food and shelter)
  - Emotional (protection)
  - Intellectual (reason as guiding principle).
- **Principle of Specialization**: Each class should function according to their strengths, contributing to the common good.

### 6.3.2 Concept of Justice
- **Definition**: Harmony in individual and community where each part does its best.
- **Role of Guardians**: Those who understand the highest Good to guide society.
- **Meritocracy**: Justice requires individuals to fulfill roles suited to their capabilities.

### 6.3.3 Practical Implementation 
- **Myth as Persuasion**: Utilizes myths to support authority and convey moral values due to varying capacities for rational persuasion.
- **“Noble Lie”**: Society structured on a belief in inherent qualities (gold, silver, bronze) determining roles in governance.

- **Plato 1955**: Discusses justice and governance throughout "The Republic", with significant passages providing insights into Plato's theory.

### 7.3.2 The Rule of the “Philosopher-King”

- Glaucon's challenge reveals the ideal nature of Plato's political theory.
- **Ideal State**: A reflection of justice and harmony, a vision that can only be approximated but not fully realized (Plato 1955:472).
- **Philosopher-Kings**: Philosophers as the rightful rulers due to understanding of the highest Good (Plato 1955:473).
  - Need for philosophers to assume responsibility in politics for societal happiness.
  - Political authority resides with educated rulers to prevent power abuse.

- **Paradox of Philosophy vs. Politics**: Philosophical knowledge often contrasts with political aspirations driven by power, wealth, etc. (Plato 1955:521).
- **Role of Government**: A well-governed state requires leaders who prioritize moral over mere political interests.

#### Key Summary of Plato’s Political Theory:
1. The ideal state embodies ideals of justice and collective harmony.
2. Constructs a hierarchical meritocracy based on understanding and knowledge.
3. Leaders, as philosopher-kings, utilize religious narratives to mandate societal structure.

### 7.5 Aristotle

- **Complementary Works**: Aristotle's *Politics* and *Ethics* both seek to define a good life, emphasizing the role of the legislator in achieving communal well-being.

#### 7.4.1 Aristotle's Theory of the State

#### Key Summary of Aristotle's Political Theory:
