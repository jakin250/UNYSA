### STUDY UNIT 1: Defining African Philosophy

#### 1.1 Introduction and Learning Outcomes
- African philosophy situated within global philosophies: African, Arabic, Chinese, Indian, Western, etc.
- Defines African philosophy through the lens of African experiences; a “pre-text” shaped by African history.
- "Africa" and "African" extend beyond geographical senses, including diaspora.
- Philosophy described as both philosophical and typically African (Osuagwu, vol 4:28).
- Aim to critique ethnocentrism, Eurocentrism, and understand "Africa" historically.

#### 1.2 The Controversy of the Term “Africa”
- Linguistic, historical, philosophical meanings of “Africa” are contested.
- Dispute reflects natural and historical justice; questioning the term is crucial.
- Indigenous peoples may challenge the label “Africans” assigned by outsiders.
- Geographical meaning considered settled; however, variations like “sub-Saharan Africa” complicate understanding.
  
- **Contentions Against Geographical Meaning**:
  - Historical context challenges understanding of Africa; Pangea theory indicates all continents were once a single entity.
  - Naming emerged through historical events: Romans named the northern regions based on interactions (Libya, Africa).

- **Historical Naming**:
  - The term “Africa” derives from antiquity (Greek and Roman contexts).
  - Refers primarily to the northern part viewed as a southern extension of Europe.
  - Emphasizes a description based on European conquests rather than indigenous naming.

- **Imposed Naming and Identity Crisis**:
  - Name changing at independence reflects the ongoing struggles with identity.
  - The analogy of a child discovering its biological parents correlates with African nations reclaiming identity.
  - Question of if "Africa" genuinely reflects indigenous perspectives or is merely a Western construct.

- **Discussion on Philosophy**:
  - Critique of the assumption that Africa shares a singular philosophy; each ethnic group has unique philosophies.
  - The term "Africa" emphasizes Western experience rather than authentic African self-understanding.
  
- **Historiographical Critique**:
  - Accounts like those in *Encyclopaedia Britannica* often misrepresent Egyptian knowledge and philosophy.
  - Historical ties between ancient Egypt and Greek philosophy are systematically disregarded.
  - The tendency within Western philosophy to appropriate reason and wisdom while denying it to non-Western knowledge systems.
  
- **Continued Relevance**:
  - Philosophy of colonization and its modern implications. 
  - Questions regarding the existence and authenticity of African philosophy raise both empirical and existential doubts regarding cultural identity.

# Study Notes on African Philosophy

## 1. Understanding African Philosophy  
### 1.1 General Overview  
- Bondy (1986) presents the concept of underdevelopment and domination in Hispanic America and parallels conditions in the Third World.
- Philosophy in Hispanic America can achieve authenticity amid surrounding unauthenticity. It serves as a critical awareness of subjugation and a means to overcome it.
- Key works for further reading:  
  - The African Origin of Civilization (Diop 1974)  
  - The African Origin of Greek Philosophy (Onyewuenyi 1993)  
  - Other relevant titles by Van Sertima, Bernal, and Van Binsbergen.
  
### 1.2 Science and African Philosophy  
- The meaning of "science" is often taken for granted; it is not neutral or objective.
- Bondy emphasizes the need to critically examine "science" in relation to the African experience.
- The question of whether there can be an African philosophy is primarily ontological, linked to the idea of rationality.

### 1.3 Africanity of African Philosophy  
- **Africanity** refers to characteristics that distinguish African philosophy from others (Osuagwu).
- Key factors of Africanity:  
  - **Ethno-African**: Origin or nature of an African author.  
  - **Geo-temporal African**: Context of African philosophy's occurrence.  
  - **Lego-African**: Inclusion of expatriates recognized by legal frameworks.  
  - **Techno-African**: Engagement by expatriates in African philosophical discourse.

### 1.4 Categories of Africans (Mazrui)  
- **Africans of the blood**: Racial definition; the black race.  
- **Africans of the soil**: Geographical identity; those on the African continent.  
- **Both**: Many might fall under both categories.  
- **Africans of the soil by adoption**: Examples include white South Africans and others of non-African descent.

### 1.5 Philosophicality of African Philosophy  
- Philosophy, from Greek "philosophia," emphasizes questioning knowledge and truth claims.
- Argumentation is essential across disciplines, parallel in philosophy and law.
- Osuagwu’s view of African philosophy as formal, systematic, and community-focused contrasts common interpretations.

## 1.6 Defining African Philosophy  
- **Kwasi Wiredu** distinguishes African philosophy from African traditional worldviews, emphasizing contemporary philosophical production.
- **Tsenay Serequeberhan** posits that African philosophy must be based on lived historical experiences and written texts, dismissing oral traditions as philosophical.
- **Paulin Hountondji** confines authorship of African philosophy to Africans, emphasizing the significance of texts.

## 1.7 Key Discussions  
- The term "Africa" holds implications for identity and representation; debates about whether to retain or redefine it persist.
- Questions arise on the authorship and validity of philosophy as expressed through written texts versus oral traditions.

## 1.8 Conclusion  
- African philosophy intersects with diverse socio-cultural realities and actively engages with historical contexts.

## Chapter 2: Cross-Cultural Cognition and the African Condition

### 2.1 Cultural Conquest 
- Cultural conquest leads to the replacement of indigenous religions and educational systems (Biakolo 2002:41).
- Acceptance of conqueror’s culture becomes a requirement for political and social inclusion.

### 2.2 Perceptual vs Conceptual 
- **Claude Lévi-Strauss**: Distinction between "perceptual" (magical) and "conceptual" (scientific) thought.
- **Bricolage**: Perceptual knowledge is reorganized from existing patterns; it conserves existing knowledge.
- Conceptual thought enables innovation and renewal.
- Biakolo critiques Lévi-Strauss for not extending binary forms of knowledge organization across cultures (Biakolo 2002:41-42).

### 2.3 Oral vs Written 
- Assertion that writing underpins Western civilization; crucial shift in knowledge retrieval and storage.
- Oral narratives connect temporally with events; substantial reliance on memory.
- Writing shifts focus to space, facilitating abstract knowledge presentation (Biakolo 2002:42).
- Critique: Only elevating written cultures as "civilized" and overlooking the significance of oral traditions (Biakolo 2002:43).
- Writing reduces the communal engagement found in oral traditions.

### 2.4 Religious vs Scientific 
- **Robin Horton**: Various positions on the rationality of "primitive thought":
  1. Irrational and illogical.
  2. Rational but not scientific.
  3. Equally rational within its context (Biakolo 2002:45).
- Critique of Western scientific criteria as subjective, reflecting cultural biases (Biakolo 2002:46).

### Conclusion 
- Biakolo proposes that Western cognitive frameworks do not encompass the African experience and potentially perpetuate colonial narratives.
- He aims to expose the limitations of Western methods in understanding African knowledge systems.

# Study Notes on African Philosophy

## 3.3 The Most General Classification of African Philosophy: Language
- **Challenges of Language**: Influence of colonial languages (English, French, Portuguese, German).
- **Divisions**: 
  - Anglophone African Philosophy
  - Francophone African Philosophy

## 3.4 Anglophone African Philosophy
- **Origin**: Emerged post-colonialism, influenced by analytical philosophy.
- **Reaction to Colonialism**: Critical of Eurocentrism; apologetic in nature, focusing on African worldviews.
- **Key Movements**:
  - **Anti-Colonial**: 
    - Key Figures: Kwame Nkrumah, Julius Nyerere, Kenneth Kaunda, Leopold Senghor, Nnamdi Azikiwe, Sekou Toure.
  - **Professionalisation**: 
    - Key Figures: John Mbiti, Odera Oruka, Kwasi Wiredu, Kwame Gyekye, Dismas Masolo, Mogobe Ramose, Barry Hallen.

## 3.5 Francophone African Philosophy
- **Dominant Perspectives**: Identity and modernity.
- **Identity Crisis**: Emerged from colonial realities, tied to negritude.
- **Responses to Modernity**: Philosophers critically engage with Western traditions; common ground found with European philosophers.
- **Prominent Features**:
  - **Ethno-philosophy**: Focus on African identity. Influenced by Placide Tempels’ *Bantu Philosophy*.
  - **Critique of Africanist Discourses**: VY Mudimbe critiques the disconnect between African thought and modernity.

## 3.6 Classification by Kwasi Wiredu
- **Traditional African Philosophy**: Communal, accessed via proverbs, myths, etc.
- **Contemporary African Philosophy**: Academic, embraces Anglophone and Francophone perspectives.

## 3.7 Classification by Oleko Nkombe and Alfons J. Smet
- **Trends**:
  - Ideological Trends: Philosophers improving political and cultural conditions.
  - Traditional Philosophy: Analyzing philosophical works in traditional Africa.
  - Critical School: Reacting to previous trends and Western science.
  - Synthetic Current: Hermeneutical approach to problem-solving.

## 3.8 H. Odera Oruka’s Four Trends in African Philosophy
- **Trends**:
  1. **Ethnophilosophy**: Reconstructing traditional worldviews.
  2. **Sage Philosophy**: Ideas from African sages.
  3. **Nationalist-Ideological Philosophy**: Reconstruction ideas post-colonialism.
  4. **Professional Philosophy**: Those trained in philosophical discourse.

## Analysis on Oruka’s Classifications
- **Support and Criticism**: Oruka’s framework influential, with various critiques regarding categorization applicability and philosophical distinctions.
- **Perception of Philosophy**: Oruka argues no significant difference between African and European philosophy.

## Additional Notes
- **Labeling**: Classifications serve as tools for understanding varying philosophical approaches, although simplifying complexities might obscure nuances.
- **Relevance of Language**: Language shapes philosophical discourse and understanding, particularly in multilingual contexts.

### 2. Study Unit 4: Philosophical Anthropology (pg. 31)
#### 4.1 Introduction and Learning Outcomes
- Investigate the problem of “self” from an African perspective.
- Essential reading: Kwame Gyekye’s “Person and community in African thought”.

#### 4.2 African Cosmology
- Understanding African anthropology within African cosmology or ontology.
- Cosmology vs. ontology:
  - **Cosmology**: Study of the universe and humanity’s place.
  - **Ontology**: Study of being and reality.
- African cosmos consists of:
  - World of spirits and world of matter
  - Hierarchical order: God; Ancestors; Spirits; Humans; Animals; Plants; Inanimate beings.
- Characteristics:
  - Dynamic beings in constant interaction.
  - Unitary ontology advocating holistic view—material and non-material as equal.
  - Persons are embedded in relations with others and the hierarchy.

#### 4.3 Two Poles of African Anthropology
- **Intra-personal Pole**:
  - Human person as composite of body and soul (balance of bodily and spiritual needs).
  - Importance of spiritual dimension for authentic existence.
- **Inter-personal Pole**:
  - Personhood defined by relationships with others.
  - Community as essential for self-realization.
  - Nature of person as "being-in-community" (Tom Mboya & Julius Nyerere).
  - Life's meaning measured by social contribution.

#### 4.4 Prescribed Reading: Kwame Gyekye
- Analysis of Gyekye on personhood and community:
  - Critique of Menkiti's emphasis on community defining personhood:
    - Menkiti's stance includes ideas that personhood must be achieved.
  - Gyekye's arguments:
    - Rights of individuals must be considered alongside communal duties.
    - Infants as persons regardless of social status or communal recognition.
    - Community not the sole determinant of moral capacities.
- **Key Differences**:
  - Gyekye argues for intrinsic personhood independent of communal validation.
  - Criticizes Menkiti’s processual view, reinforcing potential moral capacities in all human beings.

# STUDY UNIT 4: COMMUNITARIANISM AND GYEKYE’S MODERATE VIEW

### 4.1 Concept of Individuality and Community
- **Human Capacity for Choice**: Individuals, while part of a community, can question and reject communal practices.
- **Gyekye’s Argument**: Development of communal culture arises from individual self-assertion, enabling autonomous choice of life goals.

### 4.2 Gyekye's Moderate Communitarianism
- **Definition**: Acknowledges self as both communal and autonomous.
- **Comparison with Menkiti**: Menkiti views community as having total moral authority over individuals; Gyekye allows for individual rights.
- **Rights in Moderate Communitarianism**: Recognizes rights as intrinsic to individuals, essential for personal dignity and moral respect.

### 4.3 Role of Rights and Duties
- **Rights vs. Community Good**: While rights are acknowledged, they are not prioritized over communal values.
- **Duties Over Rights**: Priority is given to duties individuals owe to others, emphasizing communal well-being.
- **Major Duties Defined**: Include helping others in distress and ethical treatment of fellow community members.
- **Responsibility for Communal Living**: Individuals must acknowledge moral responsibility for others’ needs.

### 4.4 Ethical Values in Communitarianism
- **Moral Obligation**: Communitarianism views duties as central, promoting care and reciprocity.
- **Balanced Approach**: Encourages a balance between individual needs and communal obligations.

### 4.5 Conclusion on Philosophical Anthropology
- **Interconnectedness of Self and Community**: Explores diverse approaches to this relationship, leading into morality in African thought.

# STUDY UNIT 5: MORALITY IN AFRICAN THOUGHT

### 5.1 Introduction
- **Definition of Morality**: Normative standards for acceptable behavior; varies by society.
- **Key Discussion Topics**: Relationship between morality and religion, human nature, culture.
- **African Humanism and Communitarian Ethics**: Focus on community and individual roles.

### 5.2 Distinction Between Morality and Ethics
- **Terminology**: "Morality" (Latin *moralis*) vs. "Ethics" (Greek *ethos*).
- **Common Confusion**: Morality typically denotes conduct; ethics refers to the study of that conduct.
- **Ilori's Perspective**: Morality encompasses all human experience and choices involving values.
  - *Moral Decision-Making*: Involves selecting better alternatives based on value judgments.

### 5.3 African Morality Characteristics
- **Focus on Community**: Goodness tied to societal and individual enhancement—concepts like Ubuntu reflect this.
- **Organic Nature**: Morality derived from communal life; expressed through daily practices and proverbs.
- **Universality of African Concepts**: Concepts like Ubuntu have potential universal significance while retaining cultural specificity.

### 5.4 Additional Readings
- **Bewaji's Work**: "Ethics and morality in the Yoruba culture" focuses on cultural definitions of moral concepts such as Ese, eewe, abuku, aimo, and egbin.

## Page 43: Analysis of Bewaji’s Text
- **Significance of Morality**: Essential for adjusting individual interests for community well-being (p. 54).
- **Comparison of Morality**: Differentiates African moral systems from non-African ones.
- **Purpose of Essay**: Analysis of African morality foundations.

### Main Points of the Essay
- **Debunking Fallacies**:
  - African morality emphasizes social obligations over individual ones.
  - Morality reflects a religious foundation, with a belief that all aspects of life are interconnected with religion.

- **Two Important Points**:
  - **Basis of Morality**: Focus on human welfare rather than divine rewards; religion aids social cohesion.
  - **Pervasiveness**: Ethics influence all life aspects; gods and ancestors are also expected to uphold ethical conduct.

### Ethical Concepts in Yoruba Philosophy
- **Virtues of a Morally Upright Person**: Recognition and respect from society for virtuous behavior.
- **Key Moral Concepts**:
  - **Ese**: Infractions against the Supreme Being and society (p. 58).
  - **Eewe**: Prohibitions by the Supreme Being that incur sanctions (p. 58).
  - **Abuku**: Moral "blemishes" acquired from wrongdoing (p. 58).
  - **Aimo**: Ignorance as an epistemic and moral liability (p. 58).
  - **Egbin**: Warning against moral decay (p. 58).

### Conclusion (Page 44)
- **Comparison of Ethics**: Notable similarities and differences between African and Western ethics; community-focused ethics define individual roles through societal interaction.
