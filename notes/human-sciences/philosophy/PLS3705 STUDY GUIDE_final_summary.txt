### 1.1.1 What is Political Philosophy?
- Focuses on moral questions in politics, especially concerning good vs. evil and self-interest vs. community interests.
- Reflects on political change for improving quality of life and addressing injustice.
- **Moral Indignation:** Initial emotional response to perceived injustice leading to a desire for political change (e.g., human trafficking).
- Political change is inevitable due to the dynamic nature of society amidst suffering. 

#### Key Thinkers:
- **Plato**: Emphasized justice in *Republic* as crucial for a happy political community; ignorance of justice leads to suffering.
  - Justice is not merely individual but a societal construct influenced by political authority.
  
#### Core Questions:
- What constitutes the ideal political community?
- What form of political authority and leadership is preferable?
- Citizen's role and responsibilities towards the state?
- Justification of political authority and power by the state?
- Nature of political constitutions and their purposes?
- Justification of resistance against state authority by various groups in pursuit of justice?
- Definition of justice within a community.

---

### 1.1.2 The Relationship Between Philosophy and Politics
- **Politics**: Pervasive force affecting daily life; includes governance of community good.
- Need for critical analysis of the state and its moral obligations.
- The state should reflect the collective moral aspirations of its citizens.

#### Normative Engagement:
- Political philosophy seeks transcendent values to enable citizen potential within institutions.
- Distinctions in humanity rooted in ideologies, such as male supremacy, racism, and classism must be addressed.

#### State's Role:
- Acts as a collective agent of citizens; morality governs interactions.
- Responsibilities of the state include the ethical treatment of citizens.

### 1.1.3 Moral Philosophy: The Normative Dimension of Political Philosophy
- **Moral Philosophy**: Focuses on values, responsibilities, and ethical claims.
- Normative claims: Statements about how society should function (e.g., "ought" and "should").
- Normative authority often found in constitutions (e.g., liberal constitutional democracies vs. theocratic states).

#### Descriptive vs. Normative:
- **Descriptive Statements**: Empirical observations of political realities.
- **Normative Statements**: Proposals for moral standards guiding political action.

#### Distinctions:
- Political scientists focus on observing political situations factually.
- Political philosophers engage with moral questions about what the state ought to do.

### 2.1 Introduction
- Distinction between:
  - Descriptive research (political science)
  - Normative concerns (political philosophy)
- Liberalism's assumptions values include:
  - Individual freedom and autonomy
  - Minimal state to protect individual rights

### 2.2 What is Liberalism?
- Liberalism as a moral-political philosophy has various forms:
  - From social contract theory to free market economics
- Foundational elements of liberalism:
  - **Minimal State**: Role restricted to enabling individual freedom.
  - **Neutrality of the State**: Respect for diverse beliefs without favoritism.
  - **Toleration**: Acceptance of differing lifestyles in a pluralist society.
  - **Freedom**: Core social and political interaction principle.
  - **Individualism**: Focus on individual morality and rationality.
  - **Equality**: Equal protection and civil liberties for all citizens.
  - **Voluntary Consent**: Foundation of political authority morality.
- Economic system linked with capitalism, emphasizing private property and wealth pursuit.
- Distinction between public (political) and private (economic) spheres, addressing potential patriarchal and racial implications.
- Connected to **Social Contract Theory**:
  - Historical figures: **Plato, John Locke, David Hume, Jean-Jacques Rousseau**.
  - **John Rawls** positioned in contemporary discussions through *A Theory of Justice*.
  - Critiques present in works by **Carole Pateman** and **Charles Mills**.

### 2.3 John Stuart Mill on Toleration and Liberty
- **On Liberty** as foundational text of modern liberalism.
- Key themes:
  - **Toleration**: Acceptance of diverse actions and beliefs.
  - **Individuality**: Freedom to pursue personal visions of the good life.
  - **Liberty**: Limits on state and societal coercion.
- Mill's intentions:
  - Advocate for political liberty and against intrusive social control.
  - Warn against effects of societal pressure on individuality.
- Reflections on the importance of diversity and freedom for human growth and societal progress.

### 1. Mill’s Education and Influences
- John Stuart Mill’s upbringing by James Mill emphasized a rational approach to education, excluding romantic and metaphysical ideas.
- Beliefs: 
  - Human nature studied like natural sciences.
  - Education based on empirical foundations prevents ignorance and weakness.
- Early accomplishments:
  - Mastery in Greek, algebra, and Latin by early childhood.
  - Emotional detachment due to rigorous intellectual training.
- Crisis:
  - Agonizing self-analysis questioning the purpose of life and happiness.
  - Shift in values towards emotional depth and diversity.

### 2.4 Toleration
- Examines censorship of beliefs and practices.
- Acknowledges historical instances of banning based on moral grounds.
- Mill's focus: Defining the limits of societal power over individual liberty.
- Key concern: "Tyranny of the majority" vs. state oppression.
- Social mandates may enslave individual thought (Mill 1991:26).

### 2.5 The Principle of Liberty
- Mill critiques lack of rational standards in tolerance.
- Proposes a principle of liberty:
  - Legitimate interference in liberty is only for self-protection.
  - Individual good does not justify coercion (Mill 1991:30).
- Two crucial points in Mill's principle:
  - Interference occurs only if beliefs cause harm to others.
  - Individual autonomy is absolute in personal matters.

### 2.6 What is “Harm”?
- Definition of harm:
  - A person is harmed if made worse off due to another's actions.
  - Two perspectives:
    1. **Subjective**: Personal experience of feeling worse off.
    2. **Objective**: Being worse off per independent standards.
- Problem with strict subjective theory: Arbitrary criteria for harm.
- Normative concept of harm:
  - Distinction between distasteful actions and immorality.
  - Damaged autonomy, opportunities, and independence signify harm.
- Mill’s aim:
  - Protecting minority beliefs and individual freedom (Mill 1991:33).
- Summary of Mill's principle:
  - Individual action tolerated if it does not impede others' autonomy or cause harm.

## 2.7 Arguments in Support of the Principle of Liberty

### 2.7.1 The Moral Argument
- Mill's utilitarian basis: moral rightness judged by the effect on total utility.
- Actions maximizing happiness are morally good; those diminishing it are bad.
- Principle of liberty maximizes happiness by allowing individuals to live as they choose, provided they don't harm others.
- Quote: “Mankind are greater gainers by suffering each other to live as seems good to themselves than by compelling each to live as seems good to the rest.”

### 2.7.2 The Epistemological Arguments
- **First Argument**: Suppression of opinion assumes infallibility of the suppressor.
  - Historical context: many beliefs considered true have been overturned.
  - Claim: present beliefs may similarly be seen as false in the future.
  
- **Second Argument**: Certainty of truth should not prevent scrutiny.
  - Need for vigorous discussion to maintain opinions as living truths rather than dead dogmas.
  
- **Third Argument**: An opinion may contain only part of the truth.
  - Allows for the possibility of suppressing some truth if opinions are not tolerated.
  - Quote: “Popular opinions... are often true, but seldom or never the whole truth.”

## 2.8 Some Further Points Relating to Toleration
- Toleration defined as permitting unacceptable opinions/actions.
- It occurs when there's the power to suppress an opinion.
- Tolerant societies allow strong opinions but do not suppress opposing views.

## 2.9 The Paradox of Toleration
- Toleration arises from deep offense against opposing views, creating conflict.
- Committed beliefs challenge the rationale for toleration.
- Mill's response: No conviction is infallible, even strong beliefs may be wrong.
- Epistemic humility allows for toleration.

## 2.10 Individuality
- Mill defends individuality, autonomy, and self-determination.
- Argues individuality contributes to the common good:
  - Different opinions and experiments in living enhance society.
- Importance of choice:
  - "Man" is most himself when choosing freely.
- Denial of choice diminishes human character and faculties. 
- Quote: “In proportion to the development of his individuality each person becomes more valuable to himself, and is therefore more capable of being valuable to others.”

## 2.11 AUTONOMY AND ANTI-PERFECTIONISM
- **Autonomy**: Self-governing property of agents; authors of their own lives.
- **Kantian Tradition**:
  - Autonomy linked to reason; true autonomy comes from choices aligned with reason.
  - Distinction: empirical self (external/internal compulsion) vs. real self (reason).
  - **Rawls**: Original position aimed at revealing the real self beyond the empirical self.
  
- **Mill’s Perspective**:
  - Focus on individual non-interference; emphasis on individuals as best judges of their own good.
  - Autonomous agents choose freely and are self-determined.
  - Values spontaneity and individuality as part of autonomy.
  
- **Perfectionism**:
  - Certain states or characteristics viewed as intrinsically good; associated with figures like **Plato** and **Nietzsche**.
  - Liberalism (anti-perfectionism) values diversity; no singular ideal for human goodness.
  - Mill views characteristics as means to maximize happiness, advocating for experimental living.

## 2.12 THE REJECTION OF CUSTOM AND TRADITION
- **Spontaneity and Eccentricity**: Mill values these traits, allowing for different “experiments in living.”
- **Principle of Harm**: Experiments in living permissible only if they do not harm others.
- **Critique of Custom**:
  - Conformity hinders individual nature and capacities.
  - Mill critiques stagnancy due to customs, calling it a barrier to progress.

## 2.13 THE LIMITS TO THE AUTHORITY OF THE STATE
- **Liberty**: Defined as negative liberty; freedom from interference.
- **Neutral State**: State must remain neutral on competing ideas of the good; obligations to protect autonomous choices.
- **Pluralism**: Acknowledges multiple views of good, contrasting with monistic views.

## 2.14 SOME CRITICISMS OF MILL
- **Epistemological Concerns**: Validity of knowledge emerging from free discussion questioned (Isaiah Berlin).
- **Autonomy Concerns**: Not all individuals may benefit equally from autonomy; some may require external guidance.
- **Harm Principle Critique**: Inquires if any action can truly be without social impact.

## 2.15 CONCLUSION
- Discussion synthesizes elements of political liberalism and Mill’s philosophy.
- Highlights the liberal state’s role in relation to individual freedom and toleration.
- Aims for a balanced state that respects individual liberties while navigating societal diversity.

### 3.2 Justice and the Minimal State
- **Nozick's Philosophy**: Advocates a minimal state, opposing common ownership views.
- **Private Ownership**: Guarantees individual rights to property and wealth.
- **State Role**: Only justifies minimal intervention to enforce justice.
- **Quote**: “The minimal state... any state more extensive violates people's rights” (Nozick 1974:149).

### 3.3 Nozick’s Theory of Justice as Entitlement
- **Distributive Justice Critique**: Challenges extensive states that justify resource redistribution for equality, linked to **John Rawls'** theory.
- **Rawls' Justice Definition**: Social primary goods should be distributed equally unless unequal distribution benefits the least favored (Rawls 1971:303).
- **Inequality Removal**: Rawls argues that the disadvantaged should have a say in correcting inequalities.

### 3.3.1 Entitlement Principles
1. **Just Initial Acquisition**: Accountability for how ownership is established.
2. **Principle of Transfer**: Right to freely transfer property without governmental interference.
3. **Rectification of Injustice**: Address injustices in holdings.

- **Just Distribution Definition**: If goods are acquired justly, the resulting distribution is just.

### 3.3.2 Patterns of Justice
- **Opposition to Patterned Principles**: Nozick rejects predetermined distributions (patriarchal, utilitarian).
  - **Unhistorical Principles**: Focus on current distribution based on fixed criteria (e.g., utilitarianism).
  - **Historical Principles**: Assess distributions while still following a preconceived agenda (e.g., moral worth, societal usefulness).

### 3.4 The Influence of Immanuel Kant
- **The Role of Self-Ownership**: Just holds if acquired without coercion; redistribution constitutes a violation of moral intuitions.
- **Kant's Philosophy**: Treat humanity as “ends in themselves”, emphasizing freedom and autonomy (Kant 1959).

### 3.4.1 Self-Ownership  
- **Nozick's Principle**: Based on Kant's moral principle; individuals must be treated as ends, not means.
- **Rights**: Individuals have inherent rights that are inviolable; respecting rights reflects human dignity (Nozick 1974:ix).
- **Self-Ownership**: 
  - Individuals own themselves, their talents, and capacities.
  - Implicit assumption: gifted individuals are not obligated to share their achievements.
  - Disadvantaged individuals have no claim over the talents of the gifted.

### Page 40  
- **Redistribution**: Conflicts with Nozick's principle of self-ownership; requires limits to equality. Redistribution is likened to forced labour.
- **Taxation**: Nozick views taxation as involuntary and akin to slavery; it violates individual rights.
- **Equality and Rights**: Recognizing self-ownership equates to treating everyone as equals; redistribution undermines this.

### 3.4.2 Forced Labour  
- **Definition**: Situations wherein individuals must work for others' benefit without willing consent is termed forced labour.
- **Analogy**: Taxation viewed as mandated labour; everyone has the reasonable alternative to work less.
- **Nozick’s Perspective**: Unfair to seize wealth generated by hard work (e.g., cinema-goers taxed while sunset-gazers are not).

### Page 41  
- **Rights as Enforceable Claims**: Rights consist of definitive entitlements; infringements necessitate a violation of individual rights.
- **Redistribution and Ownership**: Redistribution equates to ownership claims on others' efforts, contradicting self-ownership.
- **Deontological vs. Teleological Views**: 
  - Nozick's stance aligns with deontological ethics; rights are unbreachable.
  - Opposed to utilitarian perspectives where rights can be overridden for greater good.

## 4 Michael Walzer: Theory of Distributive Justice

### 4.1 Introduction

- **Contrast with Nozick**:
  - Nozick: Focus on individual autonomy and minimal state intervention.
  - Walzer: Justice is culture-specific; challenges universal principles.

- **Main Claims of Walzer**:
  - Justice is not universal.
  - Each community has distinct social goods requiring different distribution norms.
  - Dominance in social life is avoided by maintaining the autonomy of distributive spheres.

### 4.2 Rawls’s Universalism

- **Rawls's Framework**:
  - Hypothetical “veil of ignorance” ensures no bias in choosing principles of justice.

- **Core Concepts**:
  - Original position as a tool to derive fair principles.
  - Distinction between social and natural primary goods crucial for justice.

### 4.3 Walzer’s Critique of Universalism

- **Critique of Rawls**:
  - Rawls's abstract universalism overlooks historical and cultural contexts.
  - Argues for recognition of the plurality of social goods rather than a singular good.

## Chapter 4: Walzer's Theory of Distributive Justice

### 4.1 Overview

- **Philosophical Context**: Emphasizes the historical and cultural environment of the philosopher.
- **Walzer's Critique**:
  - Critiques Rawls’s *universalism*—justice principles must reflect cultural significance.
  - Justice cannot be abstracted from community norms.

### 4.2 Goods and Their Value

- **Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Goods**: 
  - **Intrinsic Good**: Valued for its own sake.
  - **Extrinsic Good**: Valued for what it can achieve (e.g., wealth for social esteem).
- **Cultural Significance**:
  - Goods derive significance from "shared understandings" in a community.
  - Distributions rely on community values rather than individual ownership.

### 4.3 Complex Equality

- **Complex Equality Defined**: 
  - Prevents one distribution sphere from dominating another.
  - Eg. healthcare should be based on need, irrespective of wealth.
  
- **Political Egalitarianism**: 
  - Aims for a society free from domination across all aspects of life.
  - Equality does not eliminate differences but ensures non-domination.
  
### 4.4 Anti-Universalism

- **Critique of Abstract Theories**: Rejects claims that universal principles can be applied across cultures.
- **Cultural Mediation**: 
  - Humans are shaped by cultural contexts—not a common humanity.
  - *Walzer (1983:15)*: Rights arise from shared goods within communities.

### 4.5 Pluralism and Particularism

- **Community-Based Justice**:
  - Justice requires understanding cultural meanings of social goods.
  - Identifies a plurality of social goods unique to each community.

- **Kymlicka’s Interpretation**:
  - No universal theory of justice exists; justice must reflect community understandings.

### 4.6 Implications of Walzer’s Theory

- **Link Here**: Social meaning tied to distribution.
- **Identity**: Community identity relates to social goods.
- **Plurality**: Existence of diverse goods warrants different distribution criteria.

### 4.7 Dominance and Monopoly

- **Definitions**:
  - **Dominance**: Control over diverse goods.
  - **Monopoly**: Control held against rivals, exploiting social goods.
  
- **Political Unjust Behavior**: Exploiting dominance for influence in unrelated spheres (e.g., wealth impacting education).

### Key Legislation and Cases

- **Referencing Walzer (1983)**: Central texts for deeper understanding of theory.
- **Case Studies**: Evaluative scenarios to consider application of Walzer’s principles in modern contexts. 

### Conclusion

- Walzer posits that a just society is reliant on shared community values and cultural meanings of goods.
- Principles of justice must be grounded within the specific historical and cultural constructs of a community, rather than overarching universal ideals.

## 4.4.1 Principles of Just Distribution of Social Goods
- **Situation Examples**: 
  - Wealthy family gaining political favors (e.g., positions and contracts).
  - University access criteria: matric exemption, proof of financial support.

- **Walzer's Perspective**:
  - Issues in distinguishing monopoly vs. dominance cause social conflict.
  - Creation of ideology justifies unjust practices, leading to dominance across distribution spheres.
  - Opposes conversion of monopoly to dominance, promotes success based on natural abilities.
  - Advocates for distribution that aligns with specific sphere meanings, not equal distribution.

- **Ideological Persuasion**:
  - Aristocracy uses noble birth; religious leaders use divine supremacy; educators use meritocracy.

- **Complex Equality**:
  - Concept is rooted in egalitarian reciprocity and opposes social domination.

## 4.4.2 Simple and Complex Equality
- **Simple Equality**:
  - Defined by equal access to a shared good (resource).
  - Walzer critiques the ineffectiveness of simple equality in achieving moral equality.
  - Example: Equal salaries for medical doctors yield inequality over time due to different financial decisions.

- **Complex Equality**:
  - Requires multiple distributions resulting in inherent inequalities.
  - Focuses on reducing social dominance and maintaining autonomy of spheres.

- **Tyranny and Wealth**:
  - Monopolization of goods results in access to other goods, leading to inequality.
  - Key example: Tyranny linked to the pursuit of wealth, undermining political efficacy for poor citizens.

- **Community and Justice**:
  - The community's values inform distribution principles.
  - Just society requires a focus on community context, not universal standards.

## 4.5 Shared Understandings and the Common Life
- **Walzer's Thesis**:
  - Shared community understandings shape justice principles.
  - Justice rooted in respect for different social meanings and goods.

- **Communitarianism vs. Liberalism**:
  - Communitarianism emphasizes the community's role in justice and critiques liberal individualism.
  - Political liberalism viewed as neglectful of historical and communal contexts.

- **Cultural Relativism**:
  - Justice is context-dependent, evaluated through community standards.
  - Emphasizes community as the normative source for justice definitions.

- **Politics of Recognition**:
  - Acknowledgement of different cultural values as a foundation for justice.

- **Impacts of Neglect**:

  - Scientific advancement limits creativity.

  1. From product of labor.
  2. From process of production (commodification).
  3. From human essence (species being).

- **Human Potential**: Work as self-preservation defining human dignity.

### Communism and Justice

#### Communism as Positive Transcendence
- **Definition**: Communism as the appropriation of human essence, resolving conflict between man and nature, existence and essence, objectification and self-confirmation.

#### Marxist Critique of Justice
- **Core Rejection**: Rejection of liberal ideas of equality as a normative basis for justice.
- **Problem of Equality**: Marx argues that liberal equality perpetuates systemic inequalities; differences in talents and needs negate true equality.
- **Equity Misconception**: Liberal juridical equality can render some individuals "more equal," perpetuating poverty and systemic inequality.

#### Principles of Marxist Justice
- **Equal Regard**: Proposes “equal regard” instead of liberal juridical equality.
- **Rejection of Redistribution**: Redistribution cannot solve class divisions; actual justice requires structural removal of conditions necessitating it.

#### Transition to Communism
- **Means of Production**: Calls for the proletariat's ownership transfer of production means as a step toward communism, leading to a classless society.
- **Social Solidarity**: Advocates for a society founded on mutual respect and cooperation, akin to familial ties.

#### Nature of Justice in a Communist Society
- **Elimination of Justice Need**: In a sufficiently abundant society, the need for justice as a regulatory principle diminishes.
- **Marx's Vision**: Communism envisions a society without the state or formal categories of justice.

### The Role of the State

#### Marx's View of the State
- **Instrument of Power**: Considered an instrument for ruling class economic interests; formal justice merely upholds bourgeois interests.
- **Predicted Withering**: Engels predicts the state's eventual disappearance following the transition to socialism and communism.

#### Political Order vs. Economic Order
- **Aron’s Counterpoint**: The political order remains autonomous and is not solely an extension of economic interests.
- **Reciprocal Influence**: Production and resource distribution affect authority dynamics, and vice versa.

### Conclusion: The Marxist Critique
- **Irreparability of Capitalism**: Structural issues like alienation and exploitation cannot be remedied within a capitalist framework.
- **Proletariat as Catalyst**: The proletariat will lead to the disappearance of the bourgeois state and realization of a communal ethos.

### 9.2 THE PROBLEM OF RACE AND PHILOSOPHY
- Philosophical Concern: Racism's relevance across all philosophical disciplines, not limited to ethics.
- **Critical Philosophy of Race**: A movement recognizing racism's implications across epistemology and metaphysics; aims to dismantle the foundational racism within philosophical discourse.
  
### Key Points Highlighted by Bernasconi et al (2013)
- **Opposition to Racism**: The critical philosophy of race rejects biological racialism and recognizes race as a valid analytical category.
- **Philosophical Complicity**: Western philosophy's complicity in racism historically; connection to major philosophical figures (Hume, Kant, etc.).
- **Racial Dynamics in Philosophy**: The predominance of "whiteness" deters diversity in philosophical discussions and academia.

### Challenges in South Africa
- Lack of comprehensive engagement with racism within South African philosophy curricula.
- Underrepresentation of African voices and philosophies perpetuates ignorance and colonial legacies in academic thought.

### Objective of the Unit
- To rectify inadequacies regarding race in philosophy and explore Black Consciousness as a pivotal critical philosophy of race within South Africa's socio-political context.

### Liberalism's Ideological Characteristics
- Azanian critique posits liberalism as a “racism without races” concept; attributes humanity to blacks contingent on Western civilizational adoption.

- **Right of Conquest:** Critiques ethical and legal foundations supporting colonial practices; references Ramose’s argument on the flawed basis of Western law.

### Conclusion on Liberalism
- Mills’ work emphasizes that liberalism is fundamentally intertwined with racial inequality and that the liberation of non-whites is systematically undermined within liberal frameworks.
- South African context illustrates how white supremacy is historically and structurally linked to the denial of indigenous rights.

## 9. Non-Racialism among Liberals and Black Consciousness

### 9.1. Key Quotations from Biko

- Critique of liberal perspective on apartheid:  
  - Liberals see the thesis as apartheid; anti-thesis as non-racialism; synthesis poorly defined.  
  - Biko's thesis: strong white racism as primary issue; anti-thesis: strong black solidarity.  
    - **Source:** Biko (2004:99).

- Arrogance of liberal ideology:  
  - Liberals insist on bilateral approaches; integration viewed as both end and means.  
  - Skepticism towards the authenticity of liberal non-racialism suggested by quotation marks.  
    - **Source:** Biko (2004:22).

### 9.2. Distinction between Liberal and Liberatory Non-Racialism

- **Liberal Non-Racialism:**  
  - Defined by integration into existing white-dominated structures.  
  - Viewed by Biko as protection of white supremacy; promotes assimilation rather than true equality.  
    - **Source:** Biko (2004:26).

- **Liberatory Non-Racialism (Black Consciousness):**  
  - Acknowledges and resists white supremacy; promotes African cultural values in post-colonial society.  
  - Focuses on a radical restructuring toward justice and equality.  
    - **Source:** Biko, referenced thinkers (e.g., Serequeberhan, Lembede).

### 9.3. Conceptual Differences in Integration

- **Liberal Integration:**  
  - Promotes assimilation into a normativity established by whites, inhibiting true diversity.
  
- **Liberatory Integration:**  
  - Advocates for free and equal participation; emphasizes expression of African identity and values.
  
### 9.4. Historical Context of Non-Racialism

- **David Everatt’s Distinction:**  
  - Multi-racialism (cooperation of racially separated entities) vs. Non-racialism (inclusive parties like the Liberal Party, SACP).
  - Non-racialism not feasible within white supremacist societal structures; foundational changes required.  
    - **Source:** Everatt (2009).

### 9.5. Conclusion on Non-Racialism

- A dual conception exists:  
  - **Liberal Non-Racialism:** Reflects interests of colonizers; mandates superficial integration without deeper justice.
  - **Liberatory Non-Racialism:** Calls for genuine equality and dignity for indigenous peoples, representing historical justice.
- Constitutionally claimed non-racialism is questioned:  
  - Who benefits? Requires redress for indigenous communities still facing inequalities.
  
### 9.6. Philosophical Considerations

- Importance of addressing historical injustices and understanding the ongoing struggle for true liberation.
- Notion of non-racialism remains hollow without action against colonial legacies of power and land ownership.  

### 9.7. Influential Theoretical Perspectives

- **Key Considerations:**  
  - True liberation demands dismantling of white supremacy and recognition of African cultural values.
  - Movement and discourses such as #RhodesMustFall exemplify ongoing struggles.
