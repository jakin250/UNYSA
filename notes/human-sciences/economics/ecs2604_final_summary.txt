## GRAPHICAL ANALYSIS OF THE LABOUR MARKET
- **Movements along a Curve vs. Shifts of the Curve**
  - Movements depict changes in quantity supplied/demanded due to wage rate changes.
  - Shifts indicate changes in overall labour supply/demand caused by factors other than wage rates.

### Determinants
- **Movement along supply curve**: Result of wage rate changes.
- **Shift of supply curve**: Influenced by factors such as:
  - Population and demographic changes (increased workforce).
  - Health impacts (e.g., HIV/Aids reducing workforce).
  - Compensation changes in other occupations affecting attractiveness.
  - Non-monetary job benefits improving job appeal (safety, benefits).

## Chapter 1: Introduction to Labour Economics

### 1.1 Possible reasons for a change in demand:
- **Export Orders**: Increase leads to a rightward shift in demand curve.
- **Decrease in Product Demand**: Product downturn shifts demand curve left.
- **Substitute Factor Price Change**: Lower machinery costs may decrease labour demand (left shift).
- **New Labour Substitute**: Introduction of alternatives (e.g., ATMs) decreases demand for specific labour roles (left shift).

### Key Distinctions:
- **Supply vs. Quantity Supplied**: Differentiate between overall labour supply and specific quantities offered.
- **Demand vs. Quantity Demanded**: Recognize distinction in labour demand versus specific quantities required.
- **Movements vs. Shifts**: Understand the difference between shifts and movements in demand/supply curves (label axes as L or E).

### 1.2 Characteristics of a Perfectly Competitive Labour Market:
- **Perfect Knowledge**: Full transparency of market information among participants.
- **Mobility of Workers**: Assumes workers can relocate freely for employment.
- **Wage Determination**: Equilibrium wage set by intersection of supply and demand.

### 1.3 Key Characteristics of the South African Labour Market:
- Increased supply of labour yet low demand for unskilled/semi-skilled workers.
- High unemployment linked to poverty and inequality.
- Rising preference for skilled employees affecting labour demand.
- Inequality in income distribution persists with high labour costs and low productivity.

### 1.4 Important Characteristics:
- **Labour Market Dynamics**: Increased supply and low demand lead to segmentation and inequality.

# STUDY NOTES

## 2.1 Introduction
- Individual labor supply decisions: work or leisure hours
- Influencing factors: personal preferences, wage rate, non-labor income, money needed

## 2.2 The Labour Force as the Supply of Labour
- **Labour Force (LF)**: Economically Active Population (EAP), ages 15-64
- Definitions: 
  - *Total labour force*: Employed + self-employed + unemployed
  - Discouraged work-seekers: Unemployed persons not actively seeking work; excluded from strict definition
- Data reliability: Change in methodology post-2008 affects comparisons
- South African LF demographics:
  - Gender: Women comprise ~45%
  - Age: High dependency ratio due to youth population
  - Education: Increased from 34% (1995) to ~50% (2016)
  - Ethnic demographics: 2016 - 75% African, 9.5% white
  - **Figure 2.1**: Graphical determinants of labour force

## 2.3 Labour Force Participation Rate
- **Labour Force Participation Rate (LFPR)**: Proportion of working-age population (15-64) engaged in labor
  - South Africa: ~55% LFPR, lower than international standards
  - Factors: Social welfare, contradictory effects on female participation
- Demographic breakdown:
  - Highest participation: Whites, increasing rate for Africans
  - Age-based participation peaks in 35-44 age group
  - Education: Nearly 90% LFPR for post-matric qualifications

## 2.4 Population and Population Growth
- South Africa population (2016): ~56 million; 81% African
- Key concerns: High youth population, exacerbated by HIV/AIDS impact
- Characteristics of growth:
  - Declining fertility rates, higher emigration rates
- Demographic factors influencing growth: fertility, mortality, migration

## 2.5 Total Fertility Rate
- **Total Fertility Rate (TFR)**: Average number of children born to a woman (ages 15-49)
  - Estimation challenges in developing countries
  - TFR decline from 6.7 (1960s) to 2.84 (2011 Census), 2.4 (2016)
  - **Demographic Transition Theory**: Stages of population growth
- Influencing factors on TFR: 
  - Higher education, social status of women, urbanization
  - Current high TFRs for Africans and Coloureds above replacement level (2.1)

## 2.6 The Impact of HIV and AIDS
- Significant implications for labor market: population size, inequalities, productivity
- High incidence rates: ~13% overall, ~19% adult population
- Causes: Safe sexual behavior, elimination of discrimination
- Consequences: Dependency ratios, poverty, employment impact, human capital depletion, health costs

## 2.7 Immigration, Emigration and Migrant Labour
- Trends: Increased migration post-1970; brain drain effect
- Skilled immigration benefits vs. losses for origin countries
- South Africa: Net loss of skilled workers since 1994 exacerbates skills shortage
- Data reliability issues: Lack of post-2003 immigration/emigration stats 
- Comparative example: USA's utilization of skilled immigrants (1/3 professionals born abroad)

### 3. **2.8 Deeper Insight: From Individual Decision to Market Supply of Labour**

#### 2.8.1 Basic Concepts and Important Notations
- **Opportunity cost of leisure:** Value of best foregone opportunity
- **Substitution effect:** Wage increase leads to work hour increase
- **Income effect:** Income increase leads to leisure hour increase if leisure is a normal good

#### 2.8.2 Worker Preferences: Indifference Curves
- Utility function: U = f(Y, L) (Y = income, L = leisure)
- **Indifference curve:** Points of constant utility
- Properties of indifference curves studied; refer to Figures 2.9, 2.10, and 2.11

#### 2.8.3 Budget Constraint
- Budget constraint shows combinations of income and leisure
- Movement of budget line:
  - **Parallel shift:** Increase in non-labour income
  - **Swivel:** Increase in hourly wage
- Distinction between work hours and leisure hours on graphs

#### 2.8.4 Utility Maximisation
- Maximum utility occurs when indifference curve is tangent to budget line
- Study Figure 2.16; leave out Figure 2.17

#### 2.8.5 Income Effect with a Non-Labour Increase
- Non-labour income increase shifts budget line upwards
- **Normal good vs. inferior good:** Effects on work hours
- Figure 2.18 for analysis of income effects; Figure 2.19 for supply curve shifts

#### 2.8.6 Substitution and Income Effects with a Wage Increase
- Budget line shifts upwards on wage increase
- Relative strength of substitution vs. income effects determines work hour adjustments
- Figures 2.20 and 2.21 guide understanding of effects and curves

#### 2.8.7 Reservation Wage
- **Reservation wage:** Minimum wage acceptable for employment
- Equilibrium distinctions:
  - **Interior solution:** Positive work hours if market wage > reservation wage
  - **Corner solution:** Zero work hours if market wage ≤ reservation wage

#### 2.8.8 The Individual Labour Supply Curve
- Derived curve showing relationship between wage and hours of work
- Figures 2.24 and 2.25 illustrate upward and backward-bending supply curve
- Substitution and income effects guide shape; combination leads to backward-bending curve at higher wages

## 3.4 Deeper Insight: Deriving the Demand for Labour

- **Overview**
  - Perfect competition assumed in the goods and labour markets.
  - Demand for labour is derived from demand for products/services.

### 3.4.1 The Production Function
- Short-run production function key inputs: 
  - Employment (E) - variable input
  - Capital (K) - fixed input
- Concepts:
  - Total Product (TP), Average Product (AP), Marginal Product (MP) of labour.
  - Importance of understanding the **law of diminishing marginal product**.
- References: 
  - Figure 3.7 (a) & (b) for production function diagrams and definitions.

### 3.4.2 The Short-Run Demand for Labour
- **Value of Marginal Product of Labour (VMPE)**:
  - Derived from product price (P) and MP: \( VMPE = P \times MP \).
  - Downward-sloping VMPE curve indicates firm's short-run demand for labour.
- References: 
  - Table 3.14 for calculation; Figures 3.8 & 3.9 for graphical representation.

### 3.4.3 The Long-Run Demand for Labour
- Both capital and labour are variable.
- **Isoquants & Isocost Curves**:
  - Isoquants depict combinations of inputs yielding constant output.
  - Isocost curves represent equal cost combinations of inputs.
- Profit maximization occurs where isoquant is tangent to isocost.
- References: 
  - Figure 3.11 (isoquants), Figures 3.13 & 3.14 (isocosts).

### 3.4.8 Factors Influencing Market Labour Demand

## Key Definitions
- **Law of Diminishing Marginal Product**: Decreasing additional output from increased input.
- **Isoquant Curve**: Combinations of inputs producing same output.
- **Isocost Curve**: Combinations of inputs that cost the same.

  - **Frictional**: Short-term; transitional.
  - **Cyclical**: Economic downturns.

  - **Seasonal**: Demand fluctuations based on seasons.
- **Measurement Methods**: Census, registration, sample surveys; sample surveys offer most reliable stats.
- Comparison of unemployment across countries can be misleading due to differing methodologies.

## 7.3 Unemployment in South Africa
- Official unemployment rate: 27.1% (2016) → 5.6 million unemployed; nearly tripled since 1995.
