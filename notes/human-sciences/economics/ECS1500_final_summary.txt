# LEARNING UNIT 1: THE ECONOMIC PROBLEM

## 1.1 INTRODUCTION
- The economic problem: Tension between unlimited wants and limited resources.
- Key questions in economics:
  - What should be produced?
  - How should it be produced?
  - For whom should it be produced?
- Scarcity exists when resources are insufficient to satisfy all needs and wants.

## 1.1.1 UNLIMITED WANTS VS. LIMITED MEANS
- Scarcity applies universally; wants exceed available resources.
- Distinction between wants (evolving human desires) and needs (essential for survival).
- Resources are limited: 
  - **Natural resources** (e.g., minerals, fertile soil).
  - **Human resources** (labour).
  - **Manufactured resources** (machinery, tools).
- Factors of production:
  1. **Labour**: Human productive effort (manual and professional).
  2. **Capital**: Means of production (equipment, buildings; **not** money).
  3. **Land**: Natural resources (climate, minerals).
  4. **Entrepreneur**: Visionary who combines resources and bears risk.

## 1.1.2 CHOICE AND OPPORTUNITY COST
- Scarcity leads to choices: Satisfying one want often means sacrificing another.
- Opportunity cost: The value of the next best alternative sacrificed when making a choice.
- Importance of opportunity cost:
  - Measures cost of production in terms of forgone alternatives.
  - Both explicit and implicit costs are considered in economic analysis.

## 1.1.3 IMPORTANT DECISION-MAKERS IN THE ECONOMY
- **Households**: Individuals or groups making decisions as consumers (what to buy, income earn).
- **Firms**: Organizations producing goods and services, deciding on production processes and pricing.
- **Governments**: Responsible for legislation, regulation, and provision of public goods (e.g., infrastructure).

# ECS1500/1 - Learning Unit 1: The Economic Problem

## 1.1 Introduction
- Governments tax households and firms for essential services (e.g., defence, law and order).
- Payments are made to households or firms to finance expenditure and achieve economic objectives.
- Conflicts arise between decision-makers: 
  - Households want lower prices.
  - Firms seek higher prices for goods/services.
  - Governments impose taxes to fund services.

## 1.2 Different Economic Systems
### 1.2.1 The Production Problem
- Scarcity of resources necessitates choices about:
  - What goods to produce.
  - Quantities of goods.
  - Production techniques (labour vs. capital-intensive).
  - Allocation of tasks based on individual abilities and interests.
  - Timing and location of production.

### 1.2.2 The Distribution Problem
- Post-production, the focus shifts to the distribution of goods/services.
  - Distribution systems: equality vs. merit-based.
  - Allocation roles for the state.

### Types of Economic Orders
- **Command Economy**: Decisions made by central authority. 
  - Power enables enforcement; intertwined politics and economics.
  - Answers to economic problems stemming from central planning.
  - Utilizes committees for decision-making (e.g., resource distribution, capital vs. consumer goods).

- **Market Economy**: Decisions derived from markets and prices (market mechanism).
  - Individuals' self-interest drives production/distribution.
  - Market ensures no single entity exploits others; competition regulates profits.

- **Mixed Economy**: Combination of command and market elements.
  - No economy is purely command or market.
  - Limited market use in planned economies (e.g., former Soviet Union).
  - Government interventions address market imperfections.

#### South Africa's Economic Composition
- Characterized as a mixed-market economy.
- Significant government involvement (e.g., state enterprises like Eskom, price controls).

## 1.3 The Miracle of the Market Economy
- Market mechanisms allow for efficient organization of economic activities without a central authority.
- Coordination occurs through millions of actors pursuing individual interests, leading to societal wellbeing (Samuelson and Nordhaus).

### 1.3.1 Markets
- Defined as venues for buyers and sellers to trade and establish prices; can be physical or abstract.
- Types include:
  - Local markets for goods.
  - Global markets, including foreign exchange.
- Markets exist for both primary and manufactured products.

### 1.3.2 Prices
- Prices signal value, guiding economic participants' reactions.
- Established prices indicate terms for exchanges between buyers and sellers.

## 1.4 A MODEL OF THE MARKET ECONOMY

### 1.4.1 Developing a model
- Economic model: Simplified representation focusing on essential factors.
- Market economy: Known as the economic circuit or circular-flow model.
- Simplifying assumptions:
  - Excludes government sector; focuses on households and firms.
  - Considers two markets: consumer goods/services and factors of production.
- Aggregation: Combining individual markets into a whole.
- Graphical representation (Figure 1.1): 
  - Households own factors of production; offer labor, resources, capital to firms.
  - Money flow from firms to households as income; goods flow from firms to households.
  - Complete circular flow of goods and money: (P = E = I).

#### Diagram Reference
- Figure 1.1: Circular-flow model of the economy.

### 1.4.2 Limitations of the model
- Simplified version of reality; excludes:
  - Government’s role: Influences market via buying goods/services; provides services and taxes.
  - Foreign sector: Impacts from imports/exports not included.
  - Financial sector: Banking and financial intermediation effects are omitted.

- Ethical considerations:

  - Responsible resource use; consider long-term societal impact, not just immediate profit.

- Vertical axis (Y): Measures another variable; increases upward.

## 3.2.3 A Change in Demand
- Negative relationship: Price of a good ↔ Quantity demanded
- Other factors influencing demand include:
  - Income of consumers
  - Consumer preferences
  - Prices of related goods

- **Income effect**: Increase in income typically leads to increased demand for goods.  
- Example: Increase in demand for potatoes when income rises, or due to rising prices of substitutes, population growth, or preference changes.

---

### **Demand Schedule and Demand Curve Analysis**
- **Table 3.2**: Shows new quantity demanded at various prices.
  
- **Shift in Demand Curve**:
  - Increase in demand: Rightward shift (e.g., demand curve shifts from d to d').
  - Decrease in demand: Leftward shift.
  
- **Implications**: At each price level, higher or lower quantities are demanded post-shift.

---

### **Movement along vs. Shift of Demand Curve**
- **Movement along demand curve**: Caused by price changes.
  - Decrease in price → Increase in quantity demanded.
  
- **Shift of demand curve**: Caused by changes in non-price determinants (income, preferences, etc.).
  - Right shift: Increase in demand.
  - Left shift: Decrease in demand.

- **Figures 3.5, 3.6**: Visual representations illustrating the differences.

---

### **Key Determinants Affecting Demand**
- **Table 3.3(a)**: Price changes result in movements along the demand curve.
  
- **Table 3.3(b)**: Non-price factors causing shifts in demand.
  - **Prices of Substitutes**
    - Increase → Right shift of demand curve.
    - Decrease → Left shift of demand curve.
  
  - **Prices of Complements**
    - Increase → Left shift of demand curve.
    - Decrease → Right shift of demand curve.
  
  - **Income Effect**
    - Normal goods: Income increase → Right shift; Income decrease → Left shift.
  
  - **Taste/Preferences**:
    - Increased desire → Right shift; Reduced desire → Left shift.
  
  - **Population**:
    - Increase → Right shift; Decrease → Left shift.
  
  - **Expected Future Price**:
    - Expected increase → Right shift; Expected decrease → Left shift.

---

### **Substitutes and Complements**
- **Substitutes**: Goods that can replace one another (e.g., potatoes and rice).
  - Price of rice increases → Demand for potatoes increases (shift right).

- **Complements**: Goods used together (e.g., fish and potatoes).
  - Price of fish increases → Demand for potatoes decreases (shift left).

---

### **Conclusion**
- Understanding the dynamics of demand involves recognizing:
  - Distinction between movements along the demand curve vs. shifts.
  - The multi-faceted influences that affect consumer choices beyond price.

### LEARNING UNIT 3: DEMAND, SUPPLY AND PRICE DETERMINATION

#### Change in Supply vs. Change in Quantity Supplied
- **Change in Quantity Supplied**:
  - Triggered by price changes.
  - Illustrated by movements along the same supply curve (e.g., decrease in quantity due to price drop).
  
- **Change in Supply**:
  - Influenced by factors other than price.
  - Results in shifts of the supply curve (leftward for decreased supply, rightward for increased supply).

#### Factors Resulting in a Change in Supply
- **Substitutes in Production**:
  - Definition: Alternative products that can be produced with the same resources (e.g., potatoes vs. carrots).
  - Price increase of related product causes a quantity increase in that product.
  - Movement along supply curve illustrated.

- **Complements in Production**:
  - Definition: Jointly produced products (e.g., beef and leather).
  - Price increase of one product increases the supply of both.

- **Changes in Input Prices**:
  - Increase in input prices shifts supply curve leftward (decrease in supply).
  - Decrease in input prices shifts supply curve rightward (increase in supply).

- **Expected Future Prices**:
  - Anticipation of future price increase shifts supply curve rightward.
  - Anticipation of future price decrease shifts supply curve leftward.

- **Technological Changes**:
  - Cost-reducing technology leads to an increase in supply (rightward shift).
  - Cost-increasing technology leads to a decrease in supply (leftward shift).

- **Number of Firms**:
  - Entry of new firms increases supply (rightward shift).
  - Exit of firms decreases supply (leftward shift).

#### Tables
- **Table 3.6 (a)**:
  - Price increase: Upward movement along supply curve (increase in quantity supplied).
  - Price decrease: Downward movement along supply curve (decrease in quantity supplied).

- **Table 3.6 (b)**:
  - Substitute price increase: Leftward shift of supply curve (decrease in supply).
  - Complementary price increase: Rightward shift of supply curve (increase in supply).
  - Input price increase: Leftward shift of supply curve (decrease in supply).
  - Input price decrease: Rightward shift of supply curve (increase in supply).
  - Technological improvement: Rightward shift of supply curve (increase in supply).

#### Impacts of Price Changes
- Increase in input costs → Shift leftward (decrease in supply).
- Decrease in input costs → Shift rightward (increase in supply).

### LEARNING UNIT 5: ELASTICITY

#### 5.1 PRICE ELASTICITY OF DEMAND
- **Formula**
  - \( e_p = \frac{\text{Percentage change in quantity demanded}}{\text{Percentage change in price}} \)
- **Calculation Example**
  - \( e_p = \frac{20\%}{-10\%} = -2 \)
  - Interpretation: A 1% decrease in price leads to a 2% increase in quantity demanded.
- **Categories of Price Elasticity**
  - Perfectly inelastic demand: \( e_p = 0 \)
  - Inelastic demand: \( 0 < e_p < 1 \)
  - Unitary elastic demand: \( e_p = 1 \)
  - Elastic demand: \( 1 < e_p < \infty \)
  - Perfectly elastic demand: \( e_p = \infty \)

#### 5.2 INCOME ELASTICITY OF DEMAND
- **Definition:** Measures responsiveness of quantity demanded due to income changes.
- **Formula**
  - \( e_y = \frac{\text{Percentage change in quantity demanded}}{\text{Percentage change in income}} \)
- **Normal Goods**
  - **Luxury Goods:** \( e_y > 1 \) 
  - **Essential Goods:** \( 0 < e_y < 1 \)
- **Inferior Goods**
  - Negative income elasticity: \( e_y < 0 \)

#### 5.3 CROSS ELASTICITY OF DEMAND
- **Definition:** Measures responsiveness of quantity demanded of one good to price changes of another good.
- **Formula**
  - \( e_c = \frac{\text{Percentage change in quantity demanded of Product A}}{\text{Percentage change in price of Product B}} \)
- **Complementary Goods:** Negative cross elasticity (\( e_c < 0 \)).
- **Substitute Goods:** Positive cross elasticity (\( e_c > 0 \)).
- **Example Calculation:**
  - If a 10% price increase in fish leads to a 5% decrease in potatoes:
    - \( e_c = \frac{-5\%}{10\%} = -0.5 \)
  - If a 10% price increase in rice leads to an 8% increase in potatoes:
    - \( e_c = \frac{8\%}{10\%} = 0.8 \)

## 5.1 Cross Elasticity of Demand
- **Definition**: Measures the responsiveness of the quantity demanded of one good to a change in the price of another good.
- **Positive Cross Elasticity (Substitutes)**: 
  - Example: Butter and margarine.
  - Calculation: 
    - % Change in Qd (margarine) = \( \frac{100,000}{800,000} \times 100 = 12.5\% \)
    - % Change in Price (butter) = \( \frac{10}{50} \times 100 = 20\% \)
    - Cross Elasticity: \( ec = \frac{12.5}{20} = 0.625 \)
- **Negative Cross Elasticity (Complements)**:
  - Example: Butter and hot-cross buns.
  - Calculation:
    - % Change in Qd (buns) = \( \frac{-50,000}{300,000} \times 100 = -16.67\% \)
    - Cross Elasticity: \( ec = \frac{-16.67}{20} = -0.834 \)
- **Zero Cross Elasticity** (Unrelated Goods):
  - Example: Butter and bicycles.
  - Calculation: 
    - % Change in Qd (bicycles) = 0%; 
    - Cross Elasticity: \( ec = \frac{0}{20} = 0 \)

## 5.2 Price Elasticity of Supply
- **Definition**: Measures responsiveness of the quantity supplied to price changes.
- **Formula**:
  - \( es = \frac{\text{Percentage change in quantity supplied}}{\text{Percentage change in price}} \)
- **Categories of Price Elasticity of Supply**:
  - **Perfectly Inelastic (es = 0)**: No change in supply with price change.
  - **Inelastic (0 < es < 1)**: Supply changes less than price change.
  - **Unit Elastic (es = 1)**: Supply changes equal to price change.
  - **Elastic (es > 1)**: Supply changes more than price change.
  - **Perfectly Elastic (es = ∞)**: Quantity supplied changes instantly with any price change.

## 5.3 Examples of Price Elasticity of Supply
- **Example**: Fido Dog Food.
  - Price increased from R50 to R55, supply increased from 500,000 to 510,000.
  - Elasticity: Indicates inelastic supply (0 < es < 1) due to production constraints.

  - **Vertical**: Perfectly inelastic.
  - **Horizontal**: Perfectly elastic.
  - **Flatter curves** represent more elastic demand.
- **Supply Curve Characteristics**:
  - **S2 (Inelastic)**: Smaller % change in quantity.

## LEARNING UNIT 5: ELASTICITY

### 5.1 Price Elasticity of Demand
- **Price Elasticity**: Measures responsiveness of quantity demanded to price changes.
- **Inelastic Demand**: Demand curve steep; significant price drop needed to increase sales (e.g., product A).
- **Elastic Demand**: Demand curve flatter; small price drop leads to substantial sales increase (e.g., product B).
- **Substitutes**: More available substitutes lead to higher price elasticity.
- **Complementary Products**: Generally low price elasticity; demand curve relatively steep (e.g., car tyres and cars).
- **Basic Needs vs. Luxury Goods**: Basic needs (staples) exhibit less price elasticity than luxury goods.

### 5.2 Price Elasticity of Supply
- **Supply Reaction**: Measures responsiveness of supply to price changes; dependent on time.
- **Short Run**: Supply typically inelastic; producers have limited ability to change output.
- **Long Run**: Producers can adjust capacity, making supply more elastic.

### 5.3 Income Elasticity of Demand
- **Income Elasticity**: Measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to changes in income.
- **Positive but <1**: Indicates normal goods; Positive and >1 indicates luxury goods.
- **Inferior Goods**: Negative income elasticity, indicating demand decreases as income increases.

### 5.4 Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand
- **Cross Price Elasticity**: Measures the responsiveness of the quantity demanded for one good when the price of another good changes.
- **Substitutes**: Positive cross elasticity.
- **Complements**: Negative cross elasticity (e.g., increase in electricity price decreases T-shirt demand).

### Key Findings in Demand Elasticity
- **High Substitutability**: Leads to greater elasticity.
- **Complementarity**: Products purchased together have low elasticity.
- **Basic Needs Demand**: Generally inelastic due to necessity.

## 6.2.2.2 Three methods to calculate GDP
- **Expenditure Approach**
  - Counts final transactions; e.g., furniture store to customers (R88,000).

  - Total expenditure equals total production.

- **Income Method**
  - Compensation to factors of production (wages, interest, rent, profit) calculates GDP.
  - Identity: **PRODUCTION (P) = INCOME (Y) = EXPENDITURE (E)**.
  
- **GDP Definition**
  - Includes only goods and services produced within a specific year; excludes second-hand sales and stock market activity.

#### 7.2 INFLATION
- **Definition**: Increase in prices leads to a decline in money's purchasing power.
- **Problems with Inflation**:
  - **Redistribution Effects**: Affects debtors and creditors; necessitates higher interest rates.
  - **Bracket Creep**: Nominal income rises, leading to higher taxes despite stagnant real income.
  - **Impact on the Poor**: Disproportionately affects low-income groups; they benefit less from interest rate rises.
  - **Investment Shifts**: Drives investment into non-productive assets.
  - **Export Competitiveness**: Increases domestic prices, reducing export competitiveness.
  - **Socio-economic Effects**: Reduces purchasing power, leading to social unrest.

#### 7.2.1 Consumer Price Index (CPI)
- **Definition**: Index showing average price changes of goods/services in a household over time.
- **Calculation**: Annual percentage change in CPI.
- **Fixed-Weight Index**: Allocates constant weights for items in the CPI basket over time.
- **Data Collection**: Monthly retail prices, categorized by relative importance.

- **Monopolies**: Sole supplier in the market with price and quantity influence.
- **Natural Monopoly**: One firm satisfies total demand, e.g., power stations.
- **Government Solutions**:
  - No intervention relying on market profits.
  - **Price Control**: Regulate prices to prevent exploitation.
  - **Taxation**: Impose taxes on monopolist's excess profits.
  - **Production Regulation**: Government may produce good or subsidize private firms.

### 8.1.2.2 Public Goods
- **Characteristics**: Non-excludable benefits (e.g., streetlights).
- **Free Riding**: Individuals benefit without paying; government can finance through taxes.
- **Privatisation**: Possible for public goods; efficiency expected from the private sector.

### 8.1.2.3 Externalities
- **Definition**: Costs/benefits of economic activities not reflected in prices.
  - **Negative Externalities**: e.g., pollution (government regulation required).
  - **Positive Externalities**: e.g., vaccinations (wider community benefits).
- **Intervention Methods**:
  - Regulation on permissible production levels.
  - Taxation for negative externalities to internalize costs.

### 8.1.2.4 Income Inequalities
- **Inequitable Distribution**: Market outcomes may lead to income disparities.
- **Government Action**:
  - Progressive tax systems to redistribute income.
  - Funding of social services (education, housing).

### 8.1.2.5 Economic Instability

- **Government Policies**:

#### 8.3.1 Neutrality

# Chapter 9: The Financial Sector

## 9.1.1.2 Money as a Store of Value
- Function: Serves to conserve wealth.
- Forms: Money, fixed property, real assets, stocks, and shares.
- Advantages: Convenience and immediate usability.
- Limitations: Ineffective during high inflation; purchasing power decline.

## 9.1.2 The Evolution of Money
- Historical forms: Beads, tea, cattle, silver, cigarettes.
- Commodity Money: Initial form where intrinsic and exchange value aligned.
  - Characteristics: Uniformity, durability, divisibility, portability.
- Transition to coins: Metals (iron, copper, gold, silver) due to durability and scarcity.
- Introduction of Paper Money: 
  - Originated in England, 16th century.
  - Certificates of deposit as initial paper money.
- Gold Standard: Partial backing by commodities (gold).
  - Emergence of fiduciary or credit money.
- Current form: Banknotes as legal tender, value based on governmental trust.

## 9.1.3 The Money Supply in South Africa

### 9.1.3.1 The Conventional Measure (M1)
- Definition: Medium of exchange function.
- Includes: Coins, notes in circulation, demand deposits (domestic private sector).
- Demand Deposits: Immediate withdrawal capability.
- Exclusion: Government and foreign sector deposits; cash in banks not counted.

### 9.1.3.2 A Broader Definition of Money (M2)
- Definition: M1 + short-term and medium-term deposits (savings).
- Characteristics: Not immediately available; requires notification for withdrawal.

### 9.1.3.3 The Most Comprehensive Measure of Money (M3)
- Definition: M2 + long-term deposits.
- Importance: Evaluates monetary policy efficacy.

### 9.1.3.4 Medium of Exchange vs. Store of Value
- M1 focuses on the medium of exchange.
- As financial assets are added in M2 and M3, emphasis shifts to store of value.

### 9.1.3.5 Money and Other Related Concepts, and the Equation of Exchange
- Distinction: Money ≠ income/wealth. Wealth includes all forms of assets.
- Stock vs. Flow Concepts: Wealth and money (stock) vs. income (flow).
- GDP vs. Money Supply: M1 (R1,700,965 million) vs. GDP (R4,651,785 million).
- Velocity of Circulation (V): Frequency of money supply circulation.
- Equation: M x V = nominal GDP.

### Comparative Advantage
- **Definition**: Ability of a country to produce a commodity at a lower cost than another country.
- **Principles**:
  - Specialization leads to efficiency and lower production costs.
  - Countries benefit from specializing in goods they produce more efficiently.
  
### Example Scenario
- **Countries**: A and B producing rice and wine.
  - **Country A**:
    - Rice: 40 bags/person-hour
    - Wine: 8 bottles/person-hour
  - **Country B**:
    - Rice: 8 bags/person-hour
    - Wine: 4 bottles/person-hour
- **Advantages**:
  - Country A has an absolute advantage in both (more production capabilities).
  - Country A specializes in rice, B in wine.
  
### Trade Benefits
- Both countries gain by trading ratios that fall between their production cost ratios (5:1 for A, 2:1 for B).
- Example Trade Ratio: 4 bags of rice for 1 bottle of wine.
- Benefits:
  - **Country A**: Gains bottles of wine by trading rice.
  - **Country B**: Gains more bags of rice by trading wine.

### Free Trade vs Protectionism
- **Free Trade**: Natural specialization without interference, enhancing economic growth.
- **Protectionism**: Policies imposing tariffs/subsidies to protect local industries, distorting market prices.
- **Policies**: Tariffs increase prices of imports, while subsidies lower production costs domestically.

### Effects of Tariffs and Subsidies
- **Tariffs**:
  - Example: 25% tariff on imports (price increase).
  - Protects local production at the cost of higher consumer prices.
  
- **Subsidies**:
  - Example: 25% subsidy on renewable energy sector salaries lowers production costs.
  - Encourages local production but can lead to inefficiencies.

### South Africa's Foreign Trade
- **Export Composition (2012-2017)**:
  - Main trading partners (e.g., China, US, Germany).
  - Notable growth in exports, particularly in agriculture and mining, despite fluctuations due to global market changes.

## 10.4 Exchange Rates
### 10.4.1 Definition, Methods of Quotation
- **Definition**: Exchange rate is the rate at which one monetary unit can be exchanged for another.
- Notable currencies: Rand (R), Dollar (US$), Euro (€), Pound (£), Yen (¥), Pula (BWP).

### 10.4.2 Direct and Indirect Quoting
- **Direct Method**: Local currency needed to purchase one unit of foreign currency.
  - Example: R11.50 = $1.00 (Rand/Dollar).
  
- **Indirect Method**: Foreign currency needed to purchase one unit of local currency.
  - Example: $1.00 = R11.50 (Dollar/Rand).

- **Conversion Calculation Example**: For an import priced at £500, R19.50 = £1.00 leads to R9,750 needed (R19.50 * £500).

### 10.4.3 Market for Foreign Exchange
- **Participants**: Include importers, exporters, banks, forex brokers, etc.
- **Equilibrium**: Established where quantity of foreign currency demanded equals quantity supplied (Figure 10.1).

- **Changes in Demand/Supply**:
  - Increased South African exports = increased supply of dollars.
  - Increased South African imports = increased demand for dollars.
  
- **Price Changes**:
  - **Depreciation**: Currency value decreases; R/$ increases.
  - **Appreciation**: Currency value increases; R/$ decreases.
  
- **Impacts**:
  - Depreciation leads to increased exports and decreased imports, contributing to inflation.
  - Appreciation results in decreased exports and increased imports, contributing to lower inflation (Table 10.5). 

### Table 10.5: Impact of Changes in R/$ Exchange Rate
- Rand depreciates:
  - Export prices (dollars): Decrease
  - Import prices (rands): Increase
  - Domestic prices: Rise
  
- Rand appreciates:
  - Export prices (dollars): Increase
  - Import prices (rands): Decrease
  - Domestic prices: Fall

### 10.5 THE BALANCE OF PAYMENTS

#### 10.5.1 What is the balance of payments?
- Summary of international transactions in balance of payments accounts.
- Divided into:
  - **Current Account**: Goods and services exported/imported.
  - **Capital Transfer Account**: Minor significance.
  - **Financial Account**: International transactions in assets/liabilities.
- Positive balance = surplus; negative balance = deficit. 
- Trade balance = total exports – total imports.

#### 10.5.2 The items that make up South Africa's balance of payments
- **Merchandise exports (2017)**: 
  - Total exports = R1 108,322 million (goods) + R66,411 million (gold).
- **Trade balance calculation**: 
  - (Exports - Imports) = R68,857 million.
- Service and income receipts add to inflow:
  - Service receipts = R210,238 million.
  - Income receipts = R81,637 million.
- Negative amounts = outflows for imports and foreign payments: 
  - Merchandise imports = R1 105,876 million.
  - Service payments = R214,544 million.
  - Income payments = R221,201 million.
- **Current account deficit (2017)**: R114,317 million.

- **Financial Account**:
  - Categories:
    - **Direct Investment**: R80,472 million outflow.
    - **Portfolio Investment**: R220,371 million inflow.
    - **Financial Derivatives**: R4,247 million outflow.
    - **Other Investment**: R9,002 million outflow.
- Investment funds inflow = R126,650 million (2017) counters current account deficit.

- **Unrecorded Transactions**: Adjust errors/omissions in balance of payments.

### 10.5.3 The link between exchange rates, the balance of payments, and economic growth in South Africa
- Exchange rates affect balance of payments:
  - Depreciation of rand leads to reduced imports; increased exports → improves current account.
  - Appreciation of rand leads to increased imports; reduced exports → harms balance of payments.
- **Economic Growth**: 
  - Growth leads to increased imports without corresponding export increases → may deteriorate balance of payments and cause currency pressures.
