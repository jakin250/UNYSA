### 14.6 The Demand for Money
- **Key Concepts**:
  - **Demand for Money (L)**: The desire to hold money as an asset.
  - **Transaction Demand for Money (L1)**: Depends on income level (Y), constant irrespective of interest rate.
  - **Speculative Demand for Money (L2)**: Dependent on interest rates, inversely related to interest rate levels.

- **Diagram References**:
  - **Diagram for L1 and L2**: Illustrate differences in demand based on income (Y) and interest rates.
  - **Total Demand**: L = L1 + L2
  
- **High Interest Rates**: 
  - Opportunity cost of holding cash increases; people prefer bonds.
  
- **Low Interest Rates**: 
  - Opportunity cost decreases; demand for money increases.

- **Effects of Income Changes**:
  - **Increase in Income**: Rightward shift of L1, L shifts right.
  - **Decrease in Income**: Leftward shift of L1, L shifts left.

## 2.6 The Demand for Money

- **Effect of Interest Rate Changes on Money Demand (L):**
  - **Decrease in Interest Rate:**
    - Opportunity cost of holding money decreases.
    - Passive balances increase (M2 to M3).
    - Total money demanded increases (M4 to M5).
  - **Increase in Interest Rate:**
    - Opportunity cost of holding money increases.
    - Passive balances decrease (stay at M3).
    - Total money demanded decreases (M5 to M4).

- **Interest Rate Understanding:**
  - General interest rate level influences money demand.

- **Inverse Relationship (Box 14-4):**
  - Between bond interest rate and bond price.

### Contents

#### 3.1 Role of Government in the Economy: An Overview

- Mixed economies include government, private sector, and market forces.
- Government intervention justifiable due to:
  - Contract enforcement necessity.
  - Market failure impact on social costs/benefits.
- Three broad government functions:
  - **Allocative Function**: Address market failures for efficient resource allocation.
  - **Distribution Function**: Ensure equitable income distribution beyond market-generated outcomes.
  - **Stabilisation Function**: Promote macroeconomic stability (full employment, price stability).

#### 3.2 How Does Government Intervene?

- Government intervention methods:
  - **Government Spending**: Direct investment in services and infrastructure.
  - **Transfer Payments**: Social welfare and support programs.
  - **Provision of Public Goods & Services**: Ensure goods/services not provided by the market, e.g., education, healthcare.
  - **Market Participant Role**: Engage in purchasing goods/services or employing factors of production.

#### 3.3 Government Failure

- Government can fail due to:
  - **Politician Behavior**: Self-interest motivated by vote maximization.
  - **Bureaucratic Issues**: Budget-maximizing tendencies lead to inefficiencies.
  - **Special Interest Groups**: Influence may create economic rent, resulting in misallocation.

#### 3.4 Nationalisation and Privatisation

- Compare arguments for/against nationalisation and privatisation:
  - **Nationalisation**: State ownership, intended to control resources for the public good.
  - **Privatisation**: Transfer ownership to the private sector, focused on efficiency but may impact employment.

#### 3.5 Fiscal Policy and the Budget

- **Fiscal Policy**: Key economic policy related to government spending and taxes.
  - Distinction between fiscal and monetary policy.
  - Types:
    - **Expansionary Policy**: Increase spending or decrease taxes.
    - **Contractionary Policy**: Decrease spending or increase taxes.
- Budget influences:
  - Production, income, and employment levels.

#### 3.6 Government Spending

- South Africa's increasing government spending:
  - Reflects changing social priorities and pressures from interest groups.
  - Political shocks and expectations for improved public services increase expenditure.
  - Rapid population growth and urbanization also contribute to spending pressures. 

### Key Points to Remember
- Government plays a crucial role in addressing market failures and ensuring economic stability.
- Intervention methods must balance efficiency with social equity.
- Both nationalisation and privatisation have merits and drawbacks.
- Fiscal policy is fundamental in managing economic fluctuations through government financial decisions.

## 3.7 Financing of Government Spending

- **Ways to Finance Government Spending:**
  - Income from property
  - Taxes (mainly sales tax and income tax)
  - Borrowing (internal or external)
  
- **Budget Deficit:**
  - Difference between government spending and current revenue.

  - Rising, with nearly 20% of tax revenue used for interest payments.

## 3.8 Taxation

- **Criteria for a Good Tax:**
  - **Neutrality:** Minimal distortions in resource allocation to avoid discouraging labor employment.
  - **Equity:** Fairness in tax burden distribution.
  - **Administrative Simplicity:** Low administrative and compliance costs.

- **Types of Tax:**
  - **Direct Taxes:** Levied directly on income (e.g., personal income tax).
  - **Indirect Taxes:** Levied on goods/services (e.g., VAT).
  - **Progressive Taxes:** Higher rates on higher income groups.
  - **Proportional Taxes:** Same rate regardless of income.
  - **Regressive Taxes:** Higher percentage on lower income groups.

- **Taxation in South Africa:**
  - Main taxes: Personal income tax, company tax, VAT.
  - Increase in overall tax burden noted in recent decades.

## 4.1 Why countries trade
- **Comparative Advantage**:
  - Basis for trade, allowing countries to consume beyond their production possibility curve.
  - Countries should specialize in goods with the least opportunity costs relative to trade partners.
  
- **Key Concepts**:
  - Opportunity costs reflect the cost of production.
  - **Absolute Advantage**: Ability to produce more of a good than competitors.
  - **Relative Advantage** (Comparative Advantage): Specialization based on lower opportunity costs.

## 4.2 The balance of payments
- **Definition**:
  - Summary record of a country's transactions with the rest of the world during a period.

- **Components**:
  - **Current Account**: Records flow of goods and services.
  - **Financial Account**: Records financial transactions and investments.
  
- **Key Considerations**:
  - Deficits can be sustained if matched by financial account surpluses.
  - South African Reserve Bank (SARB) holds gold as part of foreign reserves.

## 4.3 Exchange rates
- **Definition**:
  - Price of one currency in terms of another. 

- **Determining Factors**:
  - Application of microeconomic theory (demand and supply).
  
- **Key Points**:
  - Exports create supply, imports create demand for foreign exchange.
  - Appreciation: Currency strengthens against another.
  - Depreciation: Currency weakens against another.
  - SARB's intervention in foreign exchange market depends on foreign reserves.

## 4.4 Terms of trade
- **Definition**:
  - Represents the rate at which one good is exchanged for another, highlighting the significance of export prices in the economy.

- **Importance**:
  - Critical for economic performance and policy implications related to trade and currency stability.

**Chapter 4: Comparative Advantage and Exchange Rates**

**4.1 Absolute and Comparative Advantage**
- **Absolute Advantage:** One party can produce more of a good than another.
- **Comparative Advantage:** Evaluated by opportunity cost;
  - **Jackie's Production:**
    - Knits 1 jersey = sacrifices 1¼ dresses.
    - Specializes in knitting jerseys (relative advantage).
  - **Susan's Production:**
    - Sews 1 dress = sacrifices ½ jersey.
    - Specializes in sewing dresses (relative advantage).
  
**4.2 Trade-offs and Specialization**
- Trade-offs determine which good to produce based on lower opportunity cost.
- **Country A (Tractors)**: Sacrifices more for maize than Country B.
- **Country B (Maize)**: Sacrifices less for maize, thus has relative advantage in maize production.

**4.3 Equilibrium Exchange Rate**
- **Supply Increase**: Rightward shift in supply curve of dollars due to increased South African exports.
- New equilibrium indicates depreciation of dollar against rand.
- **Demand Increase**: Rightward shift in demand curve for dollars; dollar appreciates against rand.

## Chapter 7: Economic Models and Fiscal Policy

### 7.1 Effect of Tax Changes on Economic Equilibrium

- **Impact of Increase in Proportional Tax Rate**
  - Decrease in disposable income → decrease in induced consumption.
  - Downward swivel of:
    - Consumption function (to C’)
    - Aggregate spending function (to A’)
  - Effects:
    - Decrease in aggregate spending leads to increased inventories → decreased production.
    - Reduced employment of factors of production → decreased income.
    - Continuous cycle until new equilibrium at lower income level (Y2).

- **Impact of Decrease in Proportional Tax Rate**
  - Increase in disposable income → increase in induced consumption.
  - Upward swivel of:
    - Consumption function (to C”)
    - Aggregate spending function (to A”)
  - Effects:
    - Increase in aggregate spending leads to decreased inventories → increased production.
    - Increased employment of factors of production → increased income.
    - Continuous cycle until new equilibrium at higher income level (Y3).

- **Keynesian Model Analysis**
  - Understand how tax rate changes influence the economy.
  - Be able to illustrate using the Keynesian model.

### 7.2 Fiscal Policy and Equilibrium Income

- **Government Sector Impact on Equilibrium Income**
  - Government spending: Injection into circular flow, raises aggregate spending (A).
  - Taxes: Leakage from circular flow, decrease disposable income, reducing induced consumption, making multiplier smaller.

- **Analysis of Fiscal Policy Effects**
  - Effects of government expenditure and tax changes on:
    - Aggregate spending
    - Size of the multiplier
    - Equilibrium income level
  - Key changes to consider:
    - Increase in government expenditure.
    - Decrease in government expenditure.
    - Increase in proportional tax rate.
    - Decrease in proportional tax rate.

### 7.3 Introducing the Foreign Sector: Open Economy Model

- **Exports**
  - Treated as autonomous; depend on global economic conditions, competitiveness, and exchange rates.

- **Autonomous Imports**
  - Assumed independent of income; subtracted from total expenditure as a leakage.

- **Induced Imports**
  - Increase with rising domestic income, linked to marginal propensity to import (m).
  - Impacts multiplier negatively as less income is spent locally.

### 7.4 Summary of Government and Foreign Sector Impacts

- **Government**
  - Fiscal policy can modify equilibrium income.
  - Adjusting government spending or tax rates can directly impact economic equilibrium.

- **Foreign Sector**
  - Exports are injections, imports are leakages.
  - The balance of trade affects aggregate spending thresholds.

#### 9.1 Definition of Inflation

- Inflation is commonly termed "public enemy number one."
- Key aspects of inflation:
  - **Measurement**: Utilizes indexes such as CPI, PPI, and GDP deflator.
  - **Causes**: Can stem from demand-pull or cost-push factors.
  - **Effects**: Erodes purchasing power; impacts savings, investments, and costs of living.
  - **Policies**: Requires strategic fiscal and monetary responses.

---

#### Principles and Theories

- **AD-AS Model**:
  - Equilibrium illustrated by intersections (e.g., E0, Y0, P0).
  - **Expansionary Fiscal Policy**:
    - Increased government spending or reduced taxes → Rightward shift of AD curve → Higher income (Y1) but also higher price level (P1).
    
- **Expansionary Monetary Policy**:
  - Decrease in repo rate leads to lower interest rates → Increased borrowing and investment → Shift of AD curve to the right → Higher income (Y1) and increased price level (P1).
  
- **Balance of Payments**:
  - Equilibrium through exports=imports; shifts in AD affect imports/exports.
  - Policy implications involve trade-offs between aggregate demand, unemployment, and balance of payments deficit.

---

#### Inflation Measures

- **Consumer Price Index (CPI)**:
  - Measures average change over time in prices paid by consumers for goods and services.
  
- **Production Price Index (PPI)**:
  - Measures average change in selling prices received by domestic producers for their output.

- **Implicit GDP Deflator**:
  - Reflects the price change in all domestic production, provides a broader inflation measure than CPI.

---

#### Types of Inflation

- **Demand-Pull Inflation**:
  - Occurs when demand exceeds supply; depicted in graphical analyses (diagrams referenced).
  
- **Cost-Push Inflation**:
  - Arises from rising costs of production (e.g., wages, materials).

- **Stagflation**:
  - Combination of stagnation (slow economic growth) and inflation.

- **Contractionary Policies**:
  - Aim to reduce AD to combat inflation; involve higher taxes or reduced government spending.
  
- **Expansionary Policies**:
  - Designed to increase AD; however, can exacerbate inflation if not managed correctly.

- **Trade-off Principle**:
  - Balancing inflation control with unemployment reduction poses significant challenges for policymakers.

- **Implicit GDP Deflator**:

### 9.3 Effects of Inflation
- **Distribution Effects**: Benefits debtors; reduces purchasing power.
- **Economic Effects**: 
  - Governments may benefit; private sector impacted negatively.
  - Income redistribution from fixed income groups (e.g., elderly) to those with increasing nominal incomes.
  - Tax implications (bracket creep).
- **Social & Political Effects**: High inflation can lead to speculative activities, disrupt productivity.

### 9.4 Causes of Inflation
- **Demand-Pull Inflation**:
  - Excess demand for goods/services over production capacity.
  - **Trade-off** between inflation and unemployment.
- **Cost-Push Inflation**:
  - Caused by rising production costs.
  - Imbalance between stakeholder demands and production capacity.
- **Conflict Approach**:
  - Stakeholders seek larger economic "slices"; leads to price level rises without real GDP increase.

**10.1 Unemployment**

- **Definition**: Unemployment is classified as individuals who are willing and able to work but cannot find a job.
  
- **Cost of Unemployment**:
  - **Individual Costs**: Loss of income, skills erosion, psychological effects.
  - **Societal Costs**: Resource diversion to unemployment benefits, reduced economic growth.

- **Classification of Unemployment**:
  - **Frictional Unemployment**: Short-term unemployment during transitions between jobs.
  - **Seasonal Unemployment**: Unemployment that occurs during certain times of the year.
  - **Cyclical Unemployment**: Unemployment due to economic downturns and insufficient demand.
  - **Structural Unemployment**: Mismatch between skills and job requirements; often requires retraining.

- **Measuring Unemployment**: Rate of unemployment = (Number of unemployed / Labour force) × 100%

---

**Costs of Inflation**

- **Categories**:
  - **Distribution Effects**: Impact on income distribution, e.g., inflation may benefit debtors at the expense of creditors.
  - **Economic Effects**: Distortion of spending and saving, unpredictability in price levels.
  - **Social and Political Effects**: Heightened inequality, potential for civil unrest.

---

**Types of Inflation**

- **Demand-Pull Inflation**:
  - Characterized by rightward shift of Aggregate Demand (AD).
  - Increases employment and production until full employment is achieved.
  
- **Cost-Push Inflation**:
  - Characterized by leftward shift of Aggregate Supply (AS).
  - Results in higher prices and decreased production; often called stagflation.

**Policies to Combat Inflation**:
- **Demand-Pull**: Apply contractionary monetary/fiscal policy.
- **Cost-Push**: Increase aggregate supply through structural policies, as contractionary measures worsen unemployment.
