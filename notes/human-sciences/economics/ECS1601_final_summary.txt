  - Economic growth.
  - Unemployment reduction.
  - Price stability.
  - Balance of payments stability.
  - Equitable distribution of income.

### 1.5 Common Mistakes in Economic Reasoning

- **Fallacy of Composition**: The erroneous assumption that what is true for an individual is true for the group.
  
- **Correlation vs. Causation**: 
  - Correlation: Two variables moving together without implying cause.
  - Causation: One variable directly affects another.

- **Level vs. Rate of Change Distinction**:
  - Level: Current value at a point in time.
  - Rate of Change: Measurement of growth or decline over time.

### Circular Flow Model

- **Injections**:
  - Investment.
  - Government spending.
  - Exports.

- **Leakages**:
  - Savings.
  - Taxes.

### Components of Total Spending in a Domestic Economy

- **Equation**: 
  - \(C + I + G + (X - M)\)
  - where \(C\) = Consumption, \(I\) = Investment, \(G\) = Government Spending, \(X\) = Exports, \(M\) = Imports.

#### 2.3 Money in South Africa

- **Characteristics for Commodity to Serve as Money**:
  - Must be easily divisible
  - Must be durable
  - Must be portable
  - Must have a stable intrinsic value

- **Development of Paper Money**:
  - Evolved from commodity money
  - Trust in issuer's promise of value
  
- **Fiduciary Money**:
  - Money based on trust and credit rather than physical backing
  
- **Value of Money in Modern Economy**:
  - Derived from public trust and government backing
  - Not directly tied to physical commodities (e.g., gold)
  
- **Cheque and Cheque Account**:
  - A cheque is an order to a bank to pay a specified amount from an account.
  - Cheque accounts are demand deposit accounts allowing for withdrawals and deposits.

- **Credit Cards**:
  - Not money as they represent a line of credit, not a payment medium.
  
- **Electronic/Virtual Money**:
  - Not traditionally regarded as money since it lacks physical characteristics and legal tender status.

---

#### Measuring Money Supply

- **M1, M2, M3 Measurement**:
  - **M1**: Coins, banknotes, demand deposits.
  - **M2**: Includes M1 plus savings accounts, short-term deposits.
  - **M3**: Includes M2 plus long-term deposits and large time deposits.

- **Functions of Money**:
  - Medium of exchange
  - Store of value
  - Unit of account

---

#### 2.4 Financial Intermediaries

- **Surplus and Deficit Units**:
  - Surplus units: Entities with excess funds.
  - Deficit units: Entities in need of funds.

- **Role of Financial Intermediaries**:
  - Facilitate the flow of funds between surplus and deficit units.

- **Securities**:
  - Financial instruments representing ownership (stocks) or creditor relationships (bonds).
  - Interest rates on securities reflect the cost of borrowing.

- **Direct vs. Indirect Financing**:
  - Direct financing: Borrower directly receives funds from lenders (without intermediaries).
  - Indirect financing: Involves an intermediary (e.g., banks).

---

#### 2.5 The Demand for Money

- **Definitions**:
  - **Bonds**: Debt securities that pay interest over time.
  - **Demand for Money**: Represents the desire to hold liquid assets.

- **Liquidity Preference**:
  - Households and firms hold money for transactions (active balances) and as an asset (passive balances).

- **Demand for Money Curve**:
  - Inverse relationship between the quantity of money demanded for speculative purposes and interest rate.
  
- **Shifts in Demand Curve**:
  - Movement along the curve due to interest rate changes.
  - Shifts in the curve due to income changes.

- **Liquidity**:
  - Money is the most liquid asset, easily transferable to cash.

- **L1 Curve**  
  - Vertical representation indicates demand for transaction balances is interest rate independent (i).
  - Curve position affected by income level (Y).
  - Income increase from Y1 to Y2 shifts L1 right (higher demand for transaction balances).
  - Income decrease from Y1 to Y3 shifts L1 left (lower demand for transaction balances).
  
- **Speculative Demand for Money**  
  - Referral to demand for money as an asset; related to passive balances.
  - Choice between money (no interest) and bonds (interest-bearing, relatively liquid).
  - Money's liquidity enables immediate transactions; other assets require conversion to money.
  - Opposite side: holding cash incurs opportunity cost (interest lost if held in bonds).
  
- **Interest Rate and Speculative Demand**  
  - **High-interest rate (ihigh)**: higher opportunity cost leads to increased bond holdings, reducing demand for money (Mdlow).
  - **Low-interest rate (ilow)**: lower opportunity cost increases money demand for speculation (Mdhigh).
  - **L2 Curve**: depicts speculative demand; negative relationship with interest rates.
  
- **Total Demand for Money (L)**  
  - Derived from L=L1+L2.
  - Active balances (L1) remain constant regardless of interest rate.
    - At interest rate i1, L2 demand is M2.
    - Total demand L at i1 equals M1+M2=M3.
  - At interest rate i2:  
    - Active demand remains M1; passive demand rises to M4 (decreased opportunity cost).  
    - New total demand L equals M1+M4=M5.

- **Impact of Income Changes on Money Demand (L)**  
  - Income increase: shifts L1 right, increasing total demand L.
  - Income decrease: shifts L1 left, decreasing total demand L.
  
- **Impact of Interest Rate Changes on Money Demand (L)**  
  - **Decrease in interest rate**: decreases opportunity cost, increases passive balance demand; total money demand increases (downward L curve movement).
  - **Increase in interest rate**: increases opportunity cost, decreases passive balance demand; total money demand decreases (upward L curve movement).

- **General Interest Rate Definition**  
  - Represented in diagrams; dictates relationship between interest rates and money demand.

- **Inverse Relationship**  
  - Between bond interest rates and bond prices.

## 2.7 THE ROLE OF THE SOUTH AFRICAN RESERVE BANK IN THE ECONOMY

- **Objectives of the SARB**
  - Influences economic stability per the **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa**.

- **Functions of the SARB**
  - Monetary policy formulation.
  - Supervising bank activities.
  - Maintaining the National Payment System.
  - Acting as banker to other banks.
  - Financial stability through currency issuance.

- **Monetary Policy**
  - Aimed primarily at **price stability** and **financial stability**.
  - **Defined**: Central bank actions to manage the economy via interest rates and liquidity.

- **Accommodation Policy**
  - Provides loans to banks to address daily liquidity deficits.

- **Repurchase Agreement**
  - A financial transaction where the borrower sells securities and agrees to repurchase them at a higher price.

- **Liquidity Deficit**
  - SARB influences banking sector liquidity deficits to promote enough liquidity for the economy.
  - Uses **open market operations** to manage liquidity.

- **Quantitative Easing**
  - Central bank buys financial securities to increase liquidity, especially during economic downturns.

- **Transmission Mechanism of Monetary Policy**
  - Process by which monetary policy decisions affect the economy and inflation.

### Key Points
- SARB prefers banks to maintain liquidity deficits, sometimes necessitating additional bank liquidity through quantitative easing.

## 3.1 ROLE OF GOVERNMENT IN THE ECONOMY: AN OVERVIEW

### Functions of Government
- **Allocative Function**: Corrects market failure for efficient resource allocation.
- **Distribution Function**: Aims for equitable income distribution beyond market forces.
- **Stabilisation Function**: Promotes macroeconomic stability (full employment, price stability, balance of payments).

### Market Failure Causes
- **Imperfect Competition**: 
  - Monopoly formation influences supply/prices.
  - Natural monopoly arises from high capital costs limiting market entry.
  - Government responses:
    - Do nothing and trust market entry.
    - Introduce regulation (e.g., price control).
    - Impose taxes reducing monopoly profits.
    - Produce goods directly.

- **Public Goods**: Non-excludable benefits; leads to free-riding. 
  - Government provides and finances through taxation or pays private providers.

- **Externalities**: 
  - Negative (e.g., pollution) not reflected in prices; regulation/taxation needed.
  - Positive (e.g., inoculation benefits those not inoculated); government intervention can be beneficial.

### Distribution Function
- Government corrects income inequality:
  - Progressive income tax system where wealthier pay more.
  - Tax revenue funds transfer payments to poorer households.

### Stabilisation Function
- Business Cycle Dynamics:
  - Phases include upswing, peak, downswing, and trough.
  - Employment lost during downward phase; inflation during upward phase.
- Government uses fiscal and monetary policy to stabilize cycles (Keynesian model discusses fiscal policy).

### Regulatory Environment
- Necessary rules for property rights and contracts for market function.

## 3.2 HOW DOES GOVERNMENT INTERVENE?

- **Monetary and Fiscal Policy**: Tools for market failure correction.
  
### Fiscal Policy
- **Definition**: Government's use of spending and taxation to influence economy.
- **Instruments**: Budget and policy variables (e.g., tax rates, spending levels).
- **Demand Management**: Budget shapes variables like production, income, employment, and income redistribution.
  
### Policies
- **Expansionary Fiscal Policy**: Increases demand through spending or tax cuts.
- **Restrictive Fiscal Policy**: Decreases demand through spending cuts or tax increases.

## 3.6 Taxation

- **Criteria for a Good Tax**  
  - Equity  
  - Efficiency  
  - Simplicity  

- **Different Types of Tax**  
  - **Direct Taxes**: Taxes levied directly on income or profits (e.g., personal income tax, company tax).  
  - **Indirect Taxes**: Taxes levied on the production and consumption of goods (e.g., VAT).  

- **Types of Taxes by Structure**  
  - **Progressive Taxes**: Lower income groups pay a smaller percentage (e.g., personal income tax).  
  - **Proportional Taxes**: Uniform tax rate applied (e.g., company tax).  
  - **Regressive Taxes**: Lower income groups pay a higher percentage (e.g., certain VAT structures).  

- **Taxation in South Africa**  
  - Main taxes: Personal Income Tax, Company Tax, VAT.  
  - Key characteristics:  
    - Personal Income Tax: Progressive  
    - Company Tax: Proportional  
    - VAT: Regressive

### 12.2 THE BUSINESS CYCLE

#### Definition
- Business Cycle: A cycle of economic expansion and contraction characterized by fluctuations in production and employment.

#### Phases of the Business Cycle
- **Four Phases**:
  - **Expansion**: Increased economic activity and employment.
  - **Peak**: Maximum output and employment.
  - **Recession**: Decline in economic activity.
  - **Trough**: Lowest point of output before recovery.

---

### 12.3 SOURCES OF ECONOMIC GROWTH

#### Supply Factors
- Factors influencing production capacity (e.g., land, labor, capital, entrepreneurship).

#### Demand Factors
- Components of aggregate spending impacting overall demand in the economy.
- **Key Points**:
  - Sustained economic growth necessitates expansion in both aggregate supply and demand.
  - Growth contingent upon quality/quantity enhancement of production factors.

---

### 12.4 THE GROWTH DEBATE IN SOUTH AFRICA

#### Economic Policies and Strategies
- **Key Strategies**:
  - **RDP (Reconstruction and Development Programme)**: Aimed to address socio-economic disparities.
  - **GEAR (Growth, Employment and Redistribution)**: Shift from growth through redistribution to redistribution through growth.
  - **NDP (National Development Plan)**: Framework for long-term development.
- **Critical Issues**:
  - Impact of government debt ratings on the economy.
  - High inequality influencing economic policy design.
  - Strategies for alleviating poverty and providing basic services.
