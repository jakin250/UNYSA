### 1. Money vs. Bonds
- **Money**: Assumed to earn no interest; held for transaction purposes and as an asset.
- **Bonds**: Interest-earning; relatively liquid.
- **Types of Demand for Money**:
  - **Transactions Demand**: For transaction purposes (L1).
  - **Speculative Demand**: As an asset (L2).

---

### 2. Demand for Money  

#### 2.1 Transaction Demand for Money
- **Symbol**: L refers to liquidity; money is the most liquid asset.
- **Active Balances Demand (L1)**: 
  - Dependent on income level (Y); shifts right with income increase.
  - Vertical curve (L1) indicates no dependence on interest rate level (i).
- **Income Changes**:
  - Increase (Y1 to Y2): L1 shifts right; more money held.
  - Decrease (Y1 to Y3): L1 shifts left; less money held.

---

#### 2.2 Speculative Demand for Money
- **Speculative balances (L2)**: Assumes choice between money and bonds.
- **Liquidity**:
  - Money: Immediate accessibility for transactions.
  - Bonds: Require selling to convert to money.
- **Opportunity Cost**: Interest relinquished by holding money instead of bonds.
- **Interest Rate Impact**:
  - High interest rate (ihigh): Lower speculative demand (Md:low).
  - Low interest rate (ilow): Higher speculative demand (Md:high).
- **Inverse Relationship**: Between interest rate and speculative demand.

---

### 3. Total Demand for Money
- **Total Demand (L)**: L = L1 + L2.
- **Interest Rates**:
  - At i1: Active balances (M1) unchanged; passive balances (M2).
  - Decrease to i2: Active balances (M1) unchanged; passive balances increase to M4.
- **Income Changes**:
  - Increase: Total demand shifts right (from L1 increases).
  - Decrease: Total demand shifts left (from L1 decreases).

---

#### 3.1 Effects of Income Level Changes
- **Income Increase**: 
  - L1 increases (shifts right); total demand increases (M4 to M5).
- **Income Decrease**: 
  - L1 decreases (shifts left); total demand decreases (M5 to M4).

---

#### 3.2 Effects of Interest Rate Level Changes
- **Interest Rate Decrease**: 
  - Passive demand increases; total demand increases (downward slide).
- **Interest Rate Increase**: 
  - Passive demand decreases; total demand decreases (upward climb).

---

### 4. Interest Rates
- **Concept**: General interest rate reflected in diagrams.
- **Inverse Relationship**: Between bond interest rates and bond prices; understanding essential.

### Chapter 2.6: How is Money Created?

- **Role of Banks in Money Creation**  
  - Banks create money primarily through the extension of loans.
  
- **Money Creation Process**  
  - Money creation occurs when banks issue loans that generate deposits (credit creation).
  
- **Endogenous Money**  
  - Refers to money supply that is determined by the banking system in response to the demand for loans.

- **Factors Affecting Money Supply**  
  - Demand for loans
  - Interest rates
  - Economic activity levels

- **Money Market Dynamics**  
  - Changes in interest rates influence the money supply.
  - Increase in interest rates = decrease in demand for loans.
  - Decrease in interest rates = increase in demand for loans.

- **Impact of Income Level on Money Market**  
  - Increase in income level raises demand for transactions, shifting demand for money rightward.
  
- **Central Bank Influence**  
  - Central banks control money supply through monetary policy tools.

   - 4: F - Income level affects money creation.

2. **Money Creation by Banks**  
   - Money is created when banks provide loans, which manifests as deposits.
  
3. **Endogenous Money Explanation**  
   - Money supply is tailored to meet the needs of the economy as dictated by lending and borrowing activities.

4. **Illustrations of Changes in the Money Market**  
   - **Increase in Income Level**

   - **Decrease in Income Level**

### 3.1 Role of Government in the Economy: An Overview

- **Classification of Economies**: Mixed economies involve government, private sector, and market forces.
- **Functions of Government**:
  - **Allocative Function**: Correcting market failures for efficient resource allocation.
  - **Distribution Function**: Achieving equitable income distribution through government actions.
  - **Stabilisation Function**: Promoting macroeconomic stability (full employment, price stability).

- **Market Failure**: 
  - **Imperfect Competition**: monopolistic tendencies, natural monopolies.
    - Government can either intervene through regulation, taxation, or direct production.
  - **Public Goods**: Non-excludable benefits (e.g., streetlights), often financed through taxes.
  - **Externalities**: Positive (e.g., inoculation) or negative (e.g., pollution) effects not reflected in market prices; requires government intervention.

- **Distribution Function**:
  - The free market does not ensure equitable wealth distribution.
  - Progressive tax systems ensure richer individuals contribute more, financing social expenditures.

- **Stabilisation Function**:
  - Business cycles: phases of economic growth and decline, impacting employment and pricing.
  - Government utilizes fiscal and monetary policy to smooth these cycles.
  - **Diagram Reference**: Figure 3.1 illustrates the business cycle.

- **Regulatory Environment**:
  - **Necessity of rules**: Critical for functioning economies (property rights, contracts).

---

### 3.2 How Does Government Intervene?

  - **Fiscal Policy**: Government spending and revenue collection.

    - **Policy Variables**: Tax rates and public expenditure levels.
    - Differentiate between expansionary and restrictive fiscal policy.
  
- **Monetary Policy**: Actions taken to regulate the economy's money supply and interest rates.

## 3.7 Taxation

### Criteria for a Good Tax
- Equity
- Efficiency
- Certainty

### Different Types of Tax
- **Direct Taxes**: Levied directly on income or profits (e.g. personal income tax, corporate tax).
- **Indirect Taxes**: Levied on goods and services (e.g. Value Added Tax - VAT).

### Tax Structures
- **Progressive Taxes**: Higher percentage rates on higher income (e.g. personal income tax).
- **Proportional Taxes**: Same percentage regardless of income (e.g. corporate tax).

## 6.7 THE MULTIPLIER

- The **multiplier** is a key concept in macroeconomics, reflecting the impact of changes in autonomous spending on the equilibrium level of income.
  - **Calculation Formula**: Multiplier = 1 / (1 - c) where c = marginal propensity to consume (MPC).
  - Size of the multiplier depends on the **MPC**; the higher the MPC, the larger the multiplier.
  - Graphical representation: Depicts shifts in the aggregate spending curve.

# SECTION 10.3: THE EFFECTS OF INFLATION

- **Distribution Effects**
  - Benefits debtors at the expense of creditors.
  - Reduces the real value of money.
  - Benefits government over the private sector.
  - Redistributes income and wealth from the elderly (fixed incomes) to younger individuals (growing incomes).
  - May cause bracket creep due to taxing nominal incomes.
  
- **Economic Effects**
  - Stimulates speculative activities over productive activities.
  - Causes balance of payments issues, especially when national inflation exceeds that of trading partners.

---

# SECTION 10.4: THE CAUSES OF INFLATION

- **Demand-Pull Inflation**
  - Defined as inflation resulting from increased aggregate demand.
  - Caused by rises in consumption (C), investment (I), government spending (G), and exports (X).
  - Illustrated by a rightward shift of the Aggregate Demand (AD) curve.
  - Combated using contractionary monetary and fiscal policies.

- **Cost-Push Inflation**
  - Defined as inflation driven by an increase in production costs.
  - Illustrated by a leftward shift of the Aggregate Supply (AS) curve.
  - Causes include increased profit margins, decreased productivity, and higher costs of imports.
  - Often leads to stagflation; price level rises while production falls.

- **Limitations**
  - Shortcomings exist in both demand-pull and cost-push models regarding comprehensive explanation of inflationary dynamics.

## 12.1 GDP and Economic Growth

- **Nominal vs Real GDP**
  - Nominal GDP: Measured using current prices (e.g., GDP for 2004 at 2004 prices).
  - Real GDP: Adjusted for inflation; expressed in constant prices (e.g., GDP for 2004 at 2000 prices).

- **Gross National Income (GNI)**
  - Real GNI expressed at constant prices (e.g., 1995 prices).

- **Economic Growth Measurement**
  - Adjustments must consider price changes and population shifts.
  - GDP limitations:
    - Non-market transactions complicate value estimation.
    - GDP is not a comprehensive indicator of economic welfare.
    - Environmental factors and pollution impacts are not accounted for.

## 12.2 The Business Cycle

- **Definitions and Phases**
  - Business Cycle: Fluctuations in economic activity over time, consisting of four phases: 
    1. Expansion
    2. Peak
    3. Contraction/Recession
    4. Trough

- **True/False Statements on Business Cycles**
  - Economic growth is characterized by non-uniformity.
  - Expansion phases conclude at economic peaks; their duration does not equate to recession durations.

## 12.3 Sources of Economic Growth

- **Factors of Production**
  - Supply Factors: Productivity increase via enhanced quality/quantity of resources (labor, capital, land).
  - Demand Factors: Aggregate spending components critical for sustained growth.

- **Economic Growth Conditions** 
  - Requires simultaneous expansion of aggregate supply and demand.
  - Capital widening: Increases employment capacity without capital deepening.
  - Capital deepening: More labor-intensive production methods.
