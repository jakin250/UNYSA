# ECS1501/002 Study Notes on Price Ceilings

## Chapter 1: Price Ceilings

### 1.1 Price Ceilings Defined
- **Price Ceiling**: Legal upper limit on price for goods/services.
- **Purpose**: To enhance affordability for households (e.g., medication, fees).
  
### 1.2 Rent Controls
- **Definition**: Type of price ceiling on housing rents.
- **Economic Debate**: Economists agree on negative consequences of rent controls.
  
### 1.3 Impact of Price Ceiling
- **Market Operation**: Efficient markets do not require government intervention.
- **Effects in South Africa**: 
  - Protects households from exploitation.
  - Limits housing availability, potentially increasing homelessness.
  - Decreases housing quality.
  - Property owners may incur losses.

### 1.4 Rent Control Analysis
- **Equilibrium without Intervention**: Price adjusts where quantity supplied equals quantity demanded (E at R3,000 with 30,000 apartments).
- **Imposition of Price Ceiling at R2,000**:
  - **Quantity Demanded**: 40,000 apartments.
  - **Quantity Supplied**: 20,000 apartments.
  - **Excess Demand**: 20,000 apartments.

### 1.5 Comparison of Scenarios
- **Price Ceiling Effects**:
  - Decrease in supplied apartments from 30,000 to 20,000 (10,000 fewer).
  - Increase in demand for apartments from 30,000 to 40,000 (10,000 more).
  
### 1.6 Price Ceiling Above Equilibrium Price
- **Example**: Ceiling at R4,000 (higher than R3,000).
- **Outcome**: No impact; suppliers willing to supply at R3,000 will not raise prices.

## Chapter 2: Consequences of Price Ceilings

### 2.1 Rationing Problem
- **Need for Mechanism**: Excess demand forces allocation decisions.
- **Potential Methods**:
  - First-come-first-served basis.
  - Random selection.
  - Supplier or government controls.
  - Waiting lists.
  
### 2.2 Inefficiencies and Consequences
- **Rationing Methods**: Lead to inefficiencies (bribery, luck, connections).
- **Quality and Availability Issues**: 
  - Higher finding costs and resource waste.
  - Potential decrease in apartment construction/quality.

### 2.3 Black Markets
- **Emergence**: Caused by persistent shortages.
- **Example**: Subletting rent-controlled apartments at higher rates.
  
### 2.4 General Impacts of a Price Ceiling
- Persistent shortage (excess demand).
- Increase in rationing costs.
- Rise of illegal markets.
- Greater opportunities for corruption and bribery.

# Study Notes on Price Ceilings and Floors

## Chapter 9: Price Ceilings and Floors

### 9.1 Price Ceilings
- Price ceilings create **winners** (consumers acquiring goods at lower prices) and **losers** (producers supplying less due to lower prices).
- Results in:
  - **Excess demand** requiring rationing mechanisms (first-come-first-served, production quotas).
  - Potential development of black markets.

#### Legislation and Cases
- None specifically mentioned.

### 9.2 Market Effects of Price Ceilings
- Demand and supply graphs illustrate demand change under a price ceiling (e.g., R2,500 course fee).
- Statements regarding price ceilings include:
  - Rationing needed due to excess demand (True).
  - Price would be the rationing method without the ceiling (True).
  - More students enrolled due to the ceiling (False).

### 9.3 Price Floors
- **Definition**: Legal minimum price enforced by government (e.g., minimum wage).
- Commonly seen in agricultural price supports to stabilize income fluctuations.
  
#### Market Dynamics
- Example: Minimum price of R2.00 vs. equilibrium price of R1.50 for eggs:
  - **Equilibrium price**: R1.50; **Equilibrium quantity**: 3 million eggs.
  - **At price floor of R2.00**:
    - Quantity supplied: 4 million
    - Quantity demanded: 2 million
    - **Excess supply**: 2 million eggs.
  
#### Key Outcomes of Price Floors
- Benefits mainly producers; consumers face higher prices and lesser consumption.
- Conditions for a binding price floor:
  - Must be set above equilibrium price.

### 9.4 Consequences of Price Floors
- Creates **surplus** requiring action on excess supply.
- Possible resolutions:
  - Producers store excess at their cost.
  - Government purchases surplus.
  - Destruction of surplus products.
  - Quotas to limit production.

#### Negative Implications
- Consumers face higher prices.
- Inefficient producers remain in business.
- Disposal of surplus incurs costs to taxpayers and societal welfare losses.

#### Diagram References
- Supply and demand graphs illustrating price ceilings and floors.
- Example diagrams of market scenarios for eggs and milk.

### Conclusion
- Both price ceilings and floors interfere with equilibrium, resulting in excess demand or supply, impacting consumers and producers differently, necessitating various government interventions to manage the outcomes.

### 2. Price Elasticities

#### 2.1 The Price Elasticity Coefficient
- Measures sensitivity of quantity demanded to price changes.
- Formula:  
  \[
  e_p = \frac{\% \Delta Q_d}{\% \Delta P}
  \]
- Example calculations derived from percentage changes in price and quantity.

#### 2.2 Calculate Percentage Change
- Example Data:  
  Price (P): 8 to 10  
  Quantity Demanded (Qd): 100 to 90  
- Percentage Change Calculations:  
  - Price Increase:  
    \[
    \text{Percentage increase} = \frac{2}{8} \times 100 = 25\%
    \]  
  - Quantity Decrease:  
    \[
    \text{Percentage decrease} = \frac{10}{100} \times 100 = 10\%
    \]  
- Price Elasticity Coefficient Calculation:  
  \[
  e_p = \frac{10\%}{25\%} = 0.4
  \]

#### 2.3 Arch Elasticity: Midpoint Method
- Averages used to eliminate bias of starting points.
- Formula for Midpoint Method:  
  \[
  e_p = \frac{Q_2 - Q_1}{(Q_1 + Q_2)/2} \div \frac{P_2 - P_1}{(P_1 + P_2)/2}
  \]
- Step-by-step calculations yield elasticity that is consistent regardless of change direction.

- Example Calculation Steps:  
  1. Midpoints for P and Qd:
     - \( P_{midpoint} = 9 \)
     - \( Q_{midpoint} = 95 \)
  2. Calculate percentage changes using midpoints.
  3. Final elasticity result indicates responsiveness of demand.

#### 2.4 Types of Price Elasticity
- Descriptions of various elasticities based on elasticity coefficients:
  - < 1: Inelastic demand (e.g., Salt: 0.1)
  - > 1: Elastic demand (e.g., Fresh Tomatoes: 4.6)
- Classification highlights consumer responsiveness to price changes.

### 3. Conclusion
- Understanding price elasticity aids in predicting consumer behavior.
- Distinct types of elasticity reflect varying sensitivities across markets.

### 1. Price Elasticity Formula
- **Formula**: 
  \[
  e_{p} = \frac{\% \Delta Q_d}{\% \Delta P}
  \]
- Price elasticity outcomes:
  - \( e_{p} < 1 \): Price change > Quantity demanded change (Inelastic)
  - \( e_{p} > 1 \): Price change < Quantity demanded change (Elastic)

### 2. Types of Elasticity

#### Relatively Inelastic Demand
- **Definition**: When price elasticity coefficient \( e_{p} < 1 \)
- **Characteristics**: 
  - Goods: Salt, medical care, tobacco, fuel
  - Low sensitivity to price changes
- **Examples**:
  - Salt (0.1), Electricity (0.13), Fuel (0.6)
- **Graphical Representation**: Steeper demand curve

#### Relatively Elastic Demand
- **Definition**: When price elasticity coefficient \( e_{p} > 1 \)
- **Characteristics**:
  - Goods: Private education, restaurant meals, fresh tomatoes
  - High sensitivity to price changes
- **Examples**:
  - Restaurant meals (2.27), Fresh tomatoes (4.6)
- **Graphical Representation**: Flatter demand curve

#### Unitary Elasticity
- **Definition**: When price elasticity coefficient \( e_{p} = 1 \)
- **Characteristics**: 
  - Equal percentage change in price and quantity demanded (1:1 relationship)
- **Example**: Shoes (≈ 1.0)
- **Graphical Representation**: Linear demand curve at 45° angle

### 3. Extreme Cases

#### Perfectly Inelastic Demand
- **Definition**: No change in quantity demanded despite price change
- **Characteristics**: 
  - \( e_{p} = 0 \)
  - Occurs for essential goods (e.g., life-saving medication)
- **Graphical Representation**: Vertical line

#### Perfectly Elastic Demand
- **Definition**: Any change in price leads to infinite change in quantity demanded
- **Characteristics**:
  - \( e_{p} = \infty \)
- **Graphical Representation**: Horizontal line

### 1. Introduction to Price Elasticity of Demand
- Price elasticity measures responsiveness of quantity demanded to price changes.
- Elasticity > 1: elastic demand (sensitive to price changes).
- Elasticity < 1: inelastic demand (less sensitive to price changes).

### 2. Factors Influencing Price Elasticity

#### 2.1 Proportion of Income
- Higher proportion spent on a good/service leads to higher price elasticity.
- Example: Fridge (R4,500) has high elasticity; matches (R2.80) has low elasticity.

#### 2.2 Availability of Substitutes
- More substitutes lead to higher elasticity.
- Example: Cigarettes have different elasticities for teenagers (higher) vs. adults (lower) due to income sensitivity.

#### 2.3 Time Period
- Elasticity varies between short-run and long-run.
- Long-run options increase (e.g., changing travel habits).
- General rule: Longer time period = more elastic demand.

#### 2.4 Nature of the Product
- Necessities (e.g., water, food) tend to have inelastic demand.
- Luxuries (e.g., vacations) tend to have elastic demand.

### 3. Household Spending and Price Elasticity of Demand

#### 3.1 Total Spending and Price Changes
- **Total Spending = Price (P) × Quantity (Q)**
- Spending impacts based on price elasticity:
  - Increase in price & inelastic demand: total spending increases.
  - Decrease in price & inelastic demand: total spending decreases.

#### 3.2 Price Inelastic Demand and Total Spending
- Price inelastic demand: elasticity < 1.
- Example: Thabo's smoking habits with a price increase of cigarettes.

  - After 10% price increase to R33, quantity (9.5 packets).

  - Firms analyze revenue changes based on price adjustments.

    - Does a price decrease affect total revenue?

### Total Revenue and Inelastic Demand
- **Inelastic Demand Characteristics**
  - Demand is price inelastic (elasticity < 1).
  - Price increase leads to:
    - Increased total spending by households.
    - Increased total revenue for firms.

    - Decreased total spending by households.
    - Decreased total revenue for firms.

- **Case Study: Cigarettes**

  - Demand is price elastic (elasticity > 1).

    - Decreased total spending by households.
    - Decreased total revenue for firms.
  - Price decrease leads to:
    - Increased total spending by households.
    - Increased total revenue for firms.

1. **Price Inelastic Goods:**
   - Limited responsiveness of quantity demanded to price changes.
   - Price increase → Total revenue increases.
   - Price decrease → Total revenue decreases.

   - Significant responsiveness of quantity demanded to price changes.
   - Price increase → Total revenue decreases.

## 11.1 Income Elasticity  
- Measures responsiveness of quantity demanded to changes in consumer income.  
- Formula for income elasticity:  
  \[ e_y = \frac{\% \text{ change in } Q_d}{\% \text{ change in income}} \]

### Normal Goods  
- Demand increases with income increase.  
- Most goods (positive elasticity): demand curve shifts right.  
- Classification:  
  - **Necessities** (Income elasticity < 1): Demand increases but less than proportionally.  
  - **Luxury goods** (Income elasticity > 1): Demand increases more than proportionally.

### Impact of Economic Growth  
- **Luxury goods**: Demand rises significantly with income, sensitive to economic changes.  
- **Necessities**: Demand less sensitive; constant need even in economic downturns.  

### Inferior Goods  
- Demand decreases as income increases, indicating negative income elasticity.  
- Example: Preference shifts from box wine (inferior) to bottled wine (normal) with increased income.  
- Formula: for inferior goods, demand curve shifts left.  

## 11.2 Cross Elasticity  
- Describes how the demand of one good changes when the price of another good changes.  
- Formula for cross elasticity of demand:  
  \[ e_{AB} = \frac{\% \text{ change in } Q_d A}{\% \text{ change in price of } B} \]

### Complements vs. Substitutes  
- **Complements (e.g., bread and peanut butter)**: Price drop of one increases demand for the other.  
- **Substitutes (e.g., Coke and Pepsi)**: Price drop of one decreases demand for the other.  
  - Positive cross-elasticity indicates substitute goods; larger values indicate closer substitutability.  

### Example: Coca-Cola and Pepsi  
- Both identified as substitutes with a positive cross-elasticity.  
- Cross-elasticity magnitude shows the degree of substitutability.

## Chapter 11: Elasticity Concepts
### Learning Unit 1: Cross-Elasticity of Demand
- **Complementary Goods**
  - Negative cross-elasticity (ec < 0)
  - Demand for good A decreases as the price of good B increases.
  - Strength of complements:
    - Weak complements: ec close to zero (slightly negative)
    - Strong complements: ec significantly negative
  - Example: Cell phones and airtime (strong complements)

- **Independent Goods**
  - Cross-elasticity equals zero (ec = 0)
  - No relationship between price changes in goods A and B.

- **Cross-Elasticity Formula**
  - ec = % change in quantity demanded of A / % change in price of B

### Learning Unit 2: Characteristics of Goods
- **Complement Goods**
  - Demand decreases when the price of another increases.
  - Cross elasticity: negative (ec < 0)

- **Substitute Goods**
  - Demand increases when the price of another increases.
  - Cross elasticity: positive (ec > 0)

- **Unrelated Goods**
  - Demand does not change when the price of another changes.
  - Cross elasticity: zero (ec = 0)

## Chapter 11: Price Elasticity of Supply
### Learning Unit 3: Price Elasticity of Supply
- **Definition**
  - Measures sensitivity of quantity supplied to price changes.
  - Formula: 
    \[
    e_s = \frac{\% \text{ change in quantity supplied}}{\% \text{ change in price}}
    \]

- **Nature of Price Elasticity of Supply**
  - Positive relationship: as price increases, quantity supplied increases.
  - Value ranges from 0 (perfectly inelastic) to ∞ (perfectly elastic).

- **Elasticity Categories**
  - Perfectly inelastic: \(e_s = 0\)
  - Inelastic: \(0 < e_s < 1\)
  - Unit elastic: \(e_s = 1\)
  - Elastic: \(1 < e_s < \infty\)
  - Perfectly elastic: \(e_s = \infty\)

### Learning Unit 4: Determinants of Price Elasticity of Supply
- **Time Periods**
  - Short-run: Inelastic supply due to limited ability to change production.
  - Long-run: Elastic supply as production capacity can be adjusted.

- **Expected Future Prices**
  - Temporary price changes: Inelastic supply; producers hesitate to adjust.
  - Anticipated long-term price increases: More elastic supply; increase production.

- **Production Processes**
  - Labour-intensive goods: More inelastic due to hiring/training time.
  - Capital-intensive goods: More elastic because of equipment flexibility and excess capacity.

- **Agricultural Products**
  - Generally more inelastic due to perishability vs. storable goods, which are more elastic.

### 14.3 Total Revenue, Average Revenue and Marginal Revenue of the Perfectly Competitive Firm

- **Total Revenue (TR)**
  - Calculation: TR = Price (P) × Quantity (Q)

### 14.5 Profit Position of the Firm

- **Profit Maximization Condition**
  - Total profits decline when **Marginal Revenue (MR) < Marginal Cost (MC)**.
  
- **Key Concepts**
  - **Normal Profit**: Minimum return needed by owners, covering opportunity costs.
  - **Economic Profit**: Revenue exceeding total costs; also known as excess profit.
  - **Economic Loss**: Occurs when price falls below average cost.

#### Normal Profit
- Normal profit is calculated where **Average Revenue (AR) = Average Cost (AC)**.
  - This indicates the break-even point (Point E).
  - **Characteristics**:
    - U-shaped **AC curve**.
    - Horizontal **AR = MR** curves.

#### Economic Profit
- Economic profit occurs when **AR > AC**.
  - Profit position at output level Q where:
    - Total profit area: \( C-P-E-M \).
    - **Profit Formula**: Total Revenue (TR = P × Q) exceeds Total Cost (TC = AC × Q).

#### Economic Loss
- Economic loss occurs when **AR < AC**.
  - Loss at output level Q with:
    - Total loss area: \( P-C-M-E \).
    - **Loss Analysis**: If price (P) is lower than AC, the firm incurs a loss.

---

### 14.6 The Shutdown Point

- **Concept**: A firm should consider shutting down when **Total Revenue (TR) < Total Cost (TC)**.
  - Making a loss does not automatically lead to shutdown; distinction between fixed costs (FC) and variable costs (VC) must be understood.

#### Fixed Costs (FC)
- Remain constant regardless of output level.
- Must be paid even at zero production (e.g., maintenance costs).

#### Variable Costs (VC)
- Change with production levels.
- Include costs for raw materials and labor.
- Referred to as direct or avoidable costs.

## Chapter 14: Market Structures

### 14.9 Imperfect Competition

**Overview**
- Market models spectrum: Perfect competition → Monopoly
- Two intermediate structures: Monopolistic competition, Oligopoly

### Characteristics of Monopoly, Monopolistic Competition, and Oligopoly
#### Monopoly
- Single firm, no rivals, unique product
- Price setter, determines its own prices based on output
- High barriers to entry prevent rivals

##### Sources of Monopoly Power
- **Barriers to Entry**: Characteristics blocking new entrants
  - **Economies of Scale**: Long-run average costs decline with increased output; leads to natural monopoly (e.g., utilities)
  - **Location Advantages**: Isolated markets (e.g., small town theaters) can establish monopolies
  - **Sunk Costs**: High initial costs that are unrecoverable hinder new firms’ entry
  - **Ownership of Inputs**: Control of essential resources can lead to monopoly
  - **Government Restrictions**: Exclusive franchises, licenses, and patents create barriers

#### Monopolistic Competition
- Many firms, differentiated products (advertising, quality, location)
- Limited price-setting power due to availability of close substitutes
- Easy entry and exit (e.g., restaurants, retail)

#### Oligopoly
- Few firms, recognizing mutual interdependence
- Industry actions influence and respond to competitors
- Produces standardized (e.g., steel) or differentiated products (e.g., automobiles)

### Summary of Efficiency in Perfectly Competitive Markets
- **Technical Efficiency**: Producing goods without waste; price equals minimum long-run average cost.
- **Allocative Efficiency**: Price equals marginal cost, ensuring consumer benefits align with production costs.
- Real-world deviations: Market dynamics often violate perfect competition assumptions; efficiency does not guarantee equity.

### Important Notes

## 15.1 The Demand for Labour
### Demand for Labour as a Derived Demand
- Demand for labor is contingent upon demand for the products/services produced by that labour.
- **Key Concept**: If demand for a product decreases, demand for labor to produce that product decreases (e.g., steel, haircuts).

### Marginal Revenue Product of Labour (MRP)
- **MRP Definition**: Contribution to revenue by an additional unit of labor.
- Calculated as:
  \[
  \text{MRP} = \text{Marginal Product of Labour (MP)} \times \text{Price (P)}
  \]

---

### Total and Marginal Product Observations
- Total product (TP) increases with more labor employed; however, MP eventually decreases due to the **law of diminishing returns**.

### Marginal Revenue Product Calculation
- Table illustrating MRP and how it declines with increased labor employment.
- Example components:
  - Unit Price (e.g., R5)
  - Marginal Product related to each labor unit.
  
---

### Impact of Marginal Revenue Product
- **Demand for Labour**: MRP curve represents the demand for labor.
- Downward Sloping Curve: Indicates an inverse relationship between wage rate and quantity of labor demanded.

- **Marginal Revenue Product (MRP)**:
  - Represents **demand for labour**.

- **Determining Employment of Labour**:
  - **Marginal Cost of Labour (MCL)** = Wage.
  - Profit-maximizing firms hire until **MRP = MCL**.
  - Rule: MRP > MCL => Hire additional worker.

  - Worker 3: MRP = R7,500 > MCL = R2,500 (Hired)

  - Worker 6: MRP = R1,000 < MCL = R2,500 (Not Hired)
  
- **Graphical Representation**:
  - MCL curve intersects with MRP at employment decisions.

### 15.3 Supply of Labour
- **Defining Supply of Labour**:
  - Distinct from product supply; influenced by household decisions.

- **Factors Influencing Labour Supply**:
  - Wage earned.
  - Goods/services purchasable with wages.
  - Sacrifice of leisure time.
  - Required working hours.

### Individual Supply of Labour
- **Decision-Making**:
  - Analysis of marginal utility between leisure and work hours.
  
- **Example - Kevin's Decision**:
  - Marginal utility from working (R100/hour) vs. lost leisure hour (opportunity cost).
    - Advice: Work extra if utility from goods/services exceeds leisure.

- **Impact of Wage Increase**:
  - **Substitution Effect**: Incentive to work more due to higher income, increasing opportunity cost of leisure.
  - **Income Effect**: Higher income allows for fewer working hours if preferred.
  
- **Behavioural Outcomes**:
  - If substitution effect > income effect, labour supply increases.

### Change in Supply of Labour
- **Market Factors**:
  - Non-wage factors (preferences, population changes, wealth, working conditions) shift the supply curve.
  
- **Supply Curve Shift**:
  - Rightward shift indicates an increased supply.

### Equilibrium in the Labour Market
- **Definition**:
  - Equilibrium occurs when quantity of labour demanded = quantity supplied.

- **Illustration**:
  - Point E where demand and supply curves meet.
  - Equilibrium quantity: 2000; Equilibrium wage: R100.

### Excess Supply of Labour
- **Wage above Equilibrium (R200)**:
  - Leads to decreased quantity demanded and increased quantity supplied.
  - Results in excess supply; wages decline until equilibrium is restored.

### Excess Demand for Labour
- **Wage below Equilibrium (R50)**:
  - Leads to increased quantity demanded and decreased quantity supplied.
  - Results in excess demand; wages rise until equilibrium is restored.
