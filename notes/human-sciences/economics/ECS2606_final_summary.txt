## Chapter 1: Introduction to Environmental Economics

### 1.1 Neoclassical Environmental Economics
- Natural entities lack intrinsic value; valued by economic contribution.
- Environmental degradation may be compensated by man-made capital (Tietenberg 2014).

### 1.2 Natural Resource Economics
- Study pages 21–23.
  
### 1.3 Ecological Economics
- Views economy as a subsystem of the environment.
- Environment holds intrinsic value; economy cannot fully substitute for ecological loss.
- An interdisciplinary focus is essential, combining economics, ecology, and sociology (Perman et al. 2011).
- **Figure 1.1** illustrates economy within the environment.

### 1.4 Views on the Relationship between Environment and Economy
- Differing perspectives: **Pessimist Model** vs **Optimist Model**.

#### 1.4.1 The Basic Pessimist Model
- Systems analysis highlights environmental depletion; published in "The limits to growth" (Meadows et al. 1972).
- Concluded environmental policies and economic growth are incompatible.
- Indefinite growth impossible due to finite resources; laws of thermodynamics applied.
- Hostile response from traditional economists did not diminish report's impact.

#### 1.4.2 The Basic Optimist Model
- Supported by Julian Simon: economic adaptability can mitigate resource scarcity.
- Advocated in "The Skeptical Environmentalist" (Lomborg 2001); highlighted global resource improvements.
- Principles of improvement:
  1. Historical and current trends comparison.
  2. Global analysis over single-case studies.
  3. Long-term versus short-term evaluation.
  4. Relative importance of environmental issues.
  5. Human needs as assessment yardstick.
- Critique: Neoclassical perspective overlooks biophysical limits, focusing instead on overcoming limits through technology and institutions.
  
#### 1.4.3 Comparison of Neoclassical and Ecological Economics
- **Neoclassical:**
  - Anthropocentric; human economy viewed as separate.
  - Substitutes for nature; technological solutions.
- **Ecological:**
  - Human economy as part of a larger ecosystem.
  - Nature as the ultimate wealth source.
  - Emphasizes energy resources and environmental significance.

### 1.5 Conclusion
- Environmental Economics studies human-environment interactions.
- Divergent perspectives on future resource sustainability: optimists vs pessimists regarding depletion and economic impacts.

**LEARNING UNIT 2: The economy and the environment**  
- **2.1 Materials Balance Principle**  
  - Based on first and second laws of thermodynamics.  
  - **First Law:** Energy conservation; energy is constant in a closed system, changing form but not created or destroyed.  
  - **Second Law:** Entropy; increases as matter becomes less ordered during usage (e.g., burning coal).  
  - Recycling can reduce entropy, but requires energy. Overall entropy must increase over time, suggesting economic activity must eventually decline.  
  - Earth is not a closed system; it exchanges energy with external systems.  

- **2.2 Reducing Raw Material Flow**  
  - Three methods to reduce raw material extraction:  
    - Reduce production quantity.  
    - Reduce residuals of production.  
    - Increase recycling.  

- **2.3 Environment as an Economic Asset**  
  - Sustainability involves short-term decisions that do not harm long-term viability.  
  - Principle Seven of UNGC supports precautionary approaches to environmental challenges.  
  - Production without environmental consideration degrades capacity; illustrated through production possibility curve (PPC).   

- **2.4 Types of Pollutants**  
  - Pollution alters ecosystems; forms include:  
    - Air, water, soil, radioactive, noise, heat/thermal, light.  
  - **Sources:**  
    - Point sources (easier to control).  
    - Non-point sources (harder to control).  

- **2.5 Conclusion**  
  - Economy and environment are interlinked; human economy is a subsystem of the natural ecosystem.

# 4 Economic Efficiency and Markets

## 4.1 INTRODUCTION
- Economic efficiency used to evaluate economic performance.
- Economic equity includes social values and environmental consequences.
- Market role in achieving economic efficiency acknowledged despite market failures.

## 4.2 ECONOMIC EFFICIENCY
- Economic efficiency defined as effective input usage and output determination.
- Focus on output levels, particularly environmental quality.
- Key questions:
  - What should be produced?
  - What is produced?
- Socially efficient output determined by equality of marginal willingness to pay and production cost.

## 4.3 EFFICIENCY AND EQUITY
- Efficient production occurs when marginal benefits equal marginal costs.
- Efficiency does not equate to equity; efficiency can lead to unequal benefit distribution.
- Equity pertains to income and wealth distribution.

## 4.4 MARKETS
- Market’s role: determines production allocation, despite environmental drawbacks.
- Market system assumed to yield better outcomes than alternatives.
- Market definition: institutional context for buyer-seller exchanges.
- Market competition essential to reach equilibrium (pm = qm).
- Diagram Reference: Figure 4.2 (Field & Field, 2016:64).

## 4.5 MARKETS AND SOCIAL EFFICIENCY
- Social efficiency occurs when marginal willingness to pay equals market demand and marginal cost equals supply.
- Market failures impede valuation of environmental goods; case study of **Brenton Blue butterfly** illustrates this failure.
- External costs (negative externalities) and benefits cause market distortions.
- Diagram Reference: Figure 4.3 (Field & Field, 2016:67–70).
- External costs: differences between private costs and true social costs.
- Common problem with open-access resources and improperly defined property rights.
- Positive externalities lead to demand-side failures.
- Public goods issue: “free riding” incentivizes non-contribution.

## 4.6 CONCLUSION
- Markets determine socially efficient levels of production, assuming all values and costs are reflected in demand/supply dynamics.

# 5 The Economics of Environmental Quality

## 5.1 INTRODUCTION
- Introduction of a general pollution control model focusing on trade-off between damage and abatement costs.

## 5.2 POLLUTION DAMAGES
- Relationship between pollution levels and corresponding damages described via damage functions.
- Marginal damage functions indicate changes in damages due to emission fluctuations.
- Notable difference in damage functions can occur even for the same pollutant.
- Assumed reversibility of damage functions contradicted by potential irreversible effects of certain pollutants (e.g., greenhouse gases).
  
## 5.3 ABATEMENT COSTS
- Abatement costs defined as expenses involved in reducing emissions or pollutants.
- Similar variability in marginal abatement cost curves as seen in marginal damage functions.

## 5.4 THE SOCIALLY EFFICIENT LEVEL OF EMISSIONS
- Efficient emissions defined where marginal damages equal marginal abatement costs.
- Trade-off exists: increased damage reduction leads to higher abatement costs.
- Introduction of enforcement costs as a necessary public expenditure for regulatory compliance.
- Diagram Reference: Figure 5.6 (Field & Field, 2016:95).

## 6 LEARNING UNIT 6: Framework of Analysis

### 6.1 INTRODUCTION
- Examines frameworks for analyzing environmental matters.
- Previous units focused on abatement costs/damages without measurement.
- Emphasis on anthropocentric economic analysis.
- Economic tools (macro & micro) applied to solve environmental problems.
- Acknowledges multidisciplinary approaches to environmental issues.

### 6.2 IMPACT ANALYSIS
- Discusses three methods of impact analysis.
- Economic impact analysis focuses on economic consequences of activities (e.g., plastic bag regulations under **Section 24 of the Environmental Conservation Act (73 of 1998)**).
- Environmental impact assessments (EIA) to consider social costs and benefits.

### 6.3 COST-EFFECTIVENESS ANALYSIS
- Estimates costs of different alternatives to compare effectiveness.
- Aim to determine costs per functional unit (e.g., cost per million gallons of water).
- Equimarginal principle is significant in cost-effectiveness studies.

### 6.4 DAMAGE ASSESSMENT
- Used in liability cases to hold responsible parties accountable.
- Two types of damages:
  - Lost value of the resource.
  - Cost to restore resource to original condition.

### 6.5 BENEFIT-COST ANALYSIS
- Developed by **A-J-E-J Dupuit**; dormant until flood control initiatives revived interest.
- South African manuals developed for BCA, including guidelines in **1989** and **2002** (Water Research Commission).
- Uses of BCA:
  - Acceptance/rejection of projects; comparison of alternative projects; scale/timing decisions.
- Important to consider:
  - Accuracy of monetary values on environmental assets.
  - Sensitivity analysis for efficiency levels.
  - Discounting future costs/benefits using the formula **PV = m/(1+r)t**.
  - Choice of discount rate influenced by time preference/marginal productivity.
  - Acknowledgment of future generations and distributional issues (horizontal & vertical equity).
- Major criticisms of neoclassical approaches:
  1. Environmental values cannot be reduced to monetary terms.
  2. High uncertainty renders total value measurements meaningless.
  3. Survey techniques may confuse preferences with beliefs.
  4. Important ecological connections may be overlooked.

### 6.6 RISK ANALYSIS
- Necessity to assess, evaluate, and manage risks due to environmental uncertainties.

### 6.7 CONCLUSION
- Overview of frameworks and primary approaches in resource/environ. economics.
- Key conceptual issues relating to BCA discussed:
  - Analytical steps, project/program size, net benefits vs benefit-cost ratios, discounting, distributional issues, risk analysis.
- Frameworks assist policymakers in generating/presenting information.
- Political decisions require nuanced monetary valuation of environmental inputs.

- Three primary methods to gauge public willingness to pay:
  - Direct expenditures revealing preferences.
  - Price variations of goods reflecting environmental attributes.
  - Contingent evaluation (surveys asking willingness to pay).

##### 7.4.1 Willingness to pay: revealed preference methods
- Six indirect methods identified; market choices reveal values on environmental factors.

##### 7.4.2 Willingness to pay: stated preference methods
- Used when no market mechanism exists to ascertain values (e.g., for wildlife).
- **Contingent valuation**: used to estimate value through direct questioning.
- **Key components** of contingent valuation:
  - Questionnaire design
  - Informed respondents' significance
  - Changing preferences and potential for misrepresentation.

#### 7.5 PROBLEMS IN BENEFIT ESTIMATION
- **Challenges identified**:
  - Discounting issues (previously discussed).
  - Willingness to accept vs. willingness to pay disparity.
  - Non-use values must be included (bequest value and existence value).

- Principle: Polluters held accountable for damages, incentivizing careful decision-making.
- Transforms environmental damages into costs influencing production choices (Refer to Figure 10.1, Field & Field, 2016:186).

## 11 LEARNING UNIT 11: Command-and-control strategies: the case of standards  

### 11.1 INTRODUCTION  
**Study pages 202–212**  
- **Command-and-Control Policies**: Detailed regulations stipulating correct conduct for behavior, such as the management of elephants in South Africa.  
- **Examples of Regulations**: National norms for waste disposal (Government Gazette No. 36784, 23 August 2013).  
- **UNGC Principle 7**: Advocates for a precautionary approach in environmental policymaking.  

### 11.2 STANDARDS  
**Study pages 202–212**  
- **Definition**: Mandated performance levels enforceable by law.  
- **Example**: Speed limits act as a traditional standard with set maximums.  
- **Nature**: Command-and-control strategy; firms incur costs to comply.

### 11.3 TYPES OF STANDARDS  
**Study pages 202–207**  

#### 11.3.1 Ambient Standards  
- Definition: Qualitative limits on pollutants in the environment; requires monitoring emissions to enforce compliance.  

#### 11.3.2 Emission/Effluent Standards  
- Definition: Quantitative limits on pollutants released directly from sources; often measured over time.  

#### 11.3.3 Technology Design/Engineering Standards  
- Definition: Dictate enforcement through specific technologies or practices that must be used.  

### 11.4 THE ECONOMICS OF STANDARDS  
**Study pages 207–212**  

#### 11.4.1 Setting the Level of Standards  
- Challenges due to lack of complete information on marginal abatement costs and damages.  
- **Precautionary Principle**: Advocates for zero-risk levels when uncertainty exists.

#### 11.4.2 Uniformity of Standards  
- Question of equal application across regions; efficiency may vary geographically; tailored standards can increase enforcement costs.

#### 11.4.3 Standards and the Equimarginal Principle  
- Importance of balancing the marginal abatement costs across multiple pollution sources for efficiency.  

### 11.5 STANDARDS AND INCENTIVES  
**Study pages 212–215**  
- Short-term absence of incentives; long-term incentives vary based on input/output focus of standards.  
- Potential for perverse incentives or “technology forcing” through regulatory measures.

### 11.6 THE ECONOMICS OF ENFORCEMENT  
**Study pages 215–219**  
- **Emission Standards Enforcement**: Marginal penalty function determines compliance penalties—penalties increase with the level of violation.  
- **Technology Standards Enforcement**: Focus on initial and continued compliance with straightforward enforcement mechanisms.

### 11.7 CONCLUSION  
- Standards represent a command-and-control strategy enforced by public authorities.  
- Challenges include cost-effectiveness and adherence to the equimarginal principle; compliance is compulsory via legal frameworks.

# Learning Unit 12: Incentive-based strategies: emission charges and subsidies

## 12.1 Introduction 
- **Carbon Tax Act** (No 15 of 2019) signed into law, effective from 1 June 2019.
- Objective: Reduce **greenhouse gas (GHG)** emissions sustainably.
- South Africa's commitment aligns with **National Climate Change Response Policy (NCCRP)** and **National Development Plan (NDP)**.
- Emphasizes the **polluter-pays-principle**.
- Policies discussed include emission charges and abatement subsidies.

## 12.2 Emission Charges
- **Emission charges**: Cost imposed on emissions to internalize environmental costs.
  
### 12.2.1 The economics of an emission charge
- Goal: Minimize costs by balancing marginal abatement costs and tax costs.
- Emissions decline until marginal abatement costs = emission charge.

### 12.2.2 The level of the charge
- Difficult to set; ideal level where marginal damages = marginal abatement costs.
- Total costs include abatement and tax payments.

### 12.2.3 Emission charges and cost-effectiveness
- Uniform tax across sources equalizes marginal abatement costs.
- **Equimarginal principle**: Maximize reductions in emissions at equal abatement costs across sources.

### 12.2.4 Emission taxes and non-uniform emissions
- Single charge inefficient for varied emissions; does not account for different marginal damages.

### 12.2.5 Emission charges and uncertainty
- Poor knowledge of marginal abatement costs complicates implementation.
  
### 12.2.6 Emission charges and tax revenues
- Tax revenue impacts depend on elasticity of the marginal abatement cost curve.

### 12.2.7 Emission charges and the incentive to innovate
- More cost-effective than standards; encourages firms to reduce emissions voluntarily.

### 12.2.8 Emission charges and enforcement costs
- Requires accurate tax item information and strict monitoring.

### 12.2.9 Other types of charges
- **Carbon footprint**: Measures total CO2 emissions from a product/activity.

## 12.3 Distributional impacts of charges
- Impact on income distribution through price/output changes and tax revenue allocation.

## 12.4 Abatement Subsidies
- Act as charges to incentivize firms towards cleaner practices.

## 12.5 Conclusion
- Emission charges as an effective policy tool in environmental economics; retail market plays a significant role over direct policies.

### LEARNING UNIT 13: Incentive-based strategies: market trading systems

#### 13.1 Essential Issues
- **Initial Rights Allocation**: Distribution of initial permits among polluters.
- **Trading Rules Establishment**: Governed by agency or market-driven; aim to lower transaction costs.
- **Permit Reduction**: Efficient emissions should decrease post-initial distribution; mechanisms for reduction required.
- **Non-uniform Emissions**: Similar issues to effluent charges.
- **Cap-and-Trade Competition**: More market participants enhance functionality; potential for wider geographical distribution without reducing pollution.
- **Enforcement in Cap-and-Trade**: Monitoring permits and emissions essential; prevents excess emissions.
- **Incentives for R&D**: Effective environmental policies stimulate innovation; emission standards vs. charges.
- **Uncertainty in Emission Control**: Fluctuations in permit prices due to cap settings; balances between too high/low caps.
- **Offset Trading**: Purchasers compensate for emissions increases by buying offsets from sellers reducing emissions.

# LEARNING UNIT 15: Economic Development and the Environment

## Learning Outcomes
- Differentiate between economic growth and economic development.
- Analyze and illustrate the relationship between economic growth and the environment in developing countries.
- Evaluate long-term relationships between the economy and environment.
- Discuss approaches to sustainable development.
- Examine environmental policy choices for developing countries.
- Analyze the role of developed countries in aiding developing nations with environmental issues.

### 15.2 ENVIRONMENTAL DEGRADATION IN DEVELOPING ECONOMIES
- Developed and developing countries face similar environmental challenges including climate change and species extinction.

### 15.3 ECONOMIC GROWTH AND THE ENVIRONMENT
- Relationship views: 
  - Developing countries prioritize monetary income over environmental quality.
  - Dependence on primary production (agriculture) risks environmental degradation.
- Long-term sustainability outlines three relationships:
  1. Weak sustainability: natural and human capital are substitutes (Hartwick’s rule).
  2. Strong sustainability: natural and human capital are complements; sustainability implies non-declining natural capital.
  3. Safe Minimum Standard (SMS): hybrid approach; favors weak sustainability when impacts are superficial, strong sustainability when uncertainty is high.
- **Brundtland Report (1987)** defined sustainable development focusing on present and future needs. 

### 15.4 ENVIRONMENTAL POLICY CHOICES IN DEVELOPING COUNTRIES
- Policy strategies to resolve environmental issues:
  - Benefit-cost analysis.
  - Institutional policies: property rights.
  - Balancing population and environmental policy.
  - Instrument choice for environmental management.

### 15.5 THE ROLE OF DEVELOPED COUNTRIES
- Developed countries must aid developing nations in transitioning to modern economies and addressing environmental challenges.

### 15.6 CONCLUSION
- Frameworks exist for developing countries to engage in responsible international trade while conserving environmental resources. Developed nations also contribute to pollution.
