### Sociology of Families

1. **Definition of Family**
   - Family as a social institution shaped by social change and societal institutions (economy, polity, judiciary, religion).
   - Focus on nuclear family as a dominant model in Western societies.
   - **Elliot (1986:4)**: Nuclear family defined as marriage-based grouping with biological ties, sharing residence and identity; however, this view fails to encompass diverse family types, particularly in South Africa.
   - Modern families exhibit various structures beyond the conventional nuclear model, making it increasingly less common.
   - Definitions impact perceptions of normality in intimate relationships, determining legal/social rights and obligations.
   - Lack of a universal definition complicates the understanding of diverse family structures, leading to stigmatization of non-nuclear configurations.
   - Emphasis on recognizing 'families' instead of 'the family' to capture diversity, including adoptive and single-parent families.

2. **Distinction Between Concepts**
   - **Household**: Spatial concept where individuals share a living space, not necessarily related (e.g., communes).
   - **Families**: Groups related by blood/marriage (including adoption) residing together; can vary in structure.
   - **Kinship**: A broad network formed by marriage or blood ties, encompassing various familial setups.
   - **Marriage**:
     - **Giddens**: Defined as a socially recognized sexual union, connecting broader kin networks.
     - Types:
       - **Monogamy**: One woman to one man.
       - **Polygamy**: Concurrent marriage to multiple partners; includes:
         - **Polygyny**: Man with multiple wives.
         - **Polyandry**: Woman with multiple husbands.
   - **Conjugal Family**: Focus on the marital relationship within a nuclear family.
   - **Extended Family**: Includes relatives beyond the nuclear unit; affects resource sharing and household dynamics.
   - **Gender**: Biological distinctions vs. socioculturally defined roles; gender inequality arises from societal power relations.

3. **Family Structures**
   - Evolution towards diverse family patterns; notable rise in female-headed households and single-parent families in South Africa.
   - **Elliot**: Decline of two-parent households attributed to social changes (e.g., unmarried childbirth, divorce).
   - Emergence of child-headed households due to HIV/AIDS impacts.
   - Economic change as a factor influencing family structure.

4. **Sex and Gender**
   - **Sex**: Biological differences; **gender**: cultural constructs of femininity and masculinity.
   - **Sexual division of labor**: Differentiation of tasks by gender; roles historically led to power imbalances.
   - Changes in gender roles in South Africa: increasing equality in job opportunities for women.
   - Gender roles instilled through socialization; constructed and reinforced by societal beliefs and values.

## Theoretical Perspectives on Sociology of Families

### Functionalism
- Focus on family’s role in societal stability and individual well-being.
- Families socialize children, meet societal needs, and reproduce members.
- Functions of families: sexual, reproductive, economic, and educational.
- Family viewed as adaptive to societal changes; changes in one part affect others.
- **Parsons and Murdock** emphasize nuclear family for societal stability and integration.
- Family systems are interrelated; each member has a role for social order.
- Disruption in one institution (like family) leads to social disorganization.
- Families evolve in form with societal changes (e.g., from extended to nuclear).

#### Functions of the Family according to Murdock
1. **Sexual Regulation**: Orderly sexual relations via marriage; prevents promiscuity.
2. **Reproduction**: Biological reproduction crucial for societal continuity.
3. **Economic Production**: Families generate economic necessities.
4. **Socialization**: Transmit societal culture and values, promoting social order.

#### Functions of the Family according to Parsons
- Emphasizes nuclear family’s role in industrial economies and psychological needs.
- Functions: primary socialization of children and stabilization of adult personalities.
- Gender division of labor: men as instrumental, women as expressive; complementary roles for societal function.
- Four functional prerequisites (AGIL):
  - **Adaptation**: Economic institution fulfills needs.
  - **Goal attainment**: Political institution sets goals.
  - **Integration**: Judiciary minimizes disputes.
  - **Latency**: Family and education maintain norms/values.

#### Criticism of the Functionalist Perspective
- Presents idealized pictures of families, overlooks conflict.
- **Conflict theorists** and **feminists** argue conjugal families oppress individuality and women.

---

### Marxist Perspective on the Family
- Family viewed as a social institution that perpetuates inequality.
- Conflict theorists interpret families as reflections of societal power struggles.
- **Karl Marx** posits that economic structures shape social institutions.
- Division between **bourgeoisie** (owners) and **proletariat** (workers) leads to family conflict.
- Family structure mirrors workplace dynamics; women’s unpaid labor reinforces capitalism.
  
#### Engels’ View on Family
- Explains family origins via economic conditions; family as a class system.
- Monogamy enforced for paternity assurance post-private property establishment.
- Marriage symbolizes male control over female sexuality; family as microcosm of class struggle.

#### Infrastructure and Superstructure
- Economic base (infrastructure) influences social institutions (superstructure).
- Families sustain capitalist production and societal inequalities.

#### Role of the Proletarian Family in Reproducing Capitalist Order
- **Ideological Function**: Socializes children to accept subservient roles in a stratified society.
- Families condition children to be obedient workers; create compliance with capitalist hierarchy.
- **Economic Function**:
  - Biological reproduction ensures labor supply.
  - Families as consumption units promote capitalist market expansion and profit maximization.

# UNIT 5
## Families and Socio-Economic Realities

- Family dynamics interact significantly with the economic sector; occupational status and income shape family lifestyles.
- **Class**: Indicator of social stratification; characterized by income, education, and lifestyle.
- **Underclass**: Individuals living below the societal average income level.

### 1. FAMILIES AND THE ECONOMIC SECTOR

- Historically, pre-industrial households operated as economic units for survival.
- Women primarily managed childcare, while children began working early.
- Industrialization caused a separation of work from home, defining men as breadwinners in individual employment roles.
- In South Africa, labor divisions were influenced by both gender and race; black women often worked for white women.

#### a) Women, Men, and Employment

- Feminists criticized the patriarchal structure, viewing housewives' roles as fostering dependence.
- Since the 1970s, more women entered the paid labor force, including mothers with preschoolers.
- In the 2004 SA elections, gender equity was highlighted with the appointment of 12 women as ministers.
- South Africa has a high proportion of women in management roles, yet men still hold more economic power.
- **Gender Equality**: Extends beyond equal positions and incomes; includes balancing employment with domestic responsibilities, resulting in a "double burden" for working women.

- **Dual-earner and Dual-career Families**:
  - Dual-earner families consist of both spouses working out of necessity or choice, impacting job prospects and responsibilities.
  - Employment policies like flexible schedules benefit working parents but may lead to perceptions of lesser commitment in women.

- Need for **equal parental responsibilities** in childcare recognized; domestic obligations affect economic roles for both genders.

#### b) Work-Life Integration

- Industrialization disrupted family life; work commitments impose on personal lives due to global market demands.
- Increased contractual positions afford workers less bargaining power, along with longer hours.
- Technological advancements blur work-life boundaries; new strategies are needed for integration of paid work and personal life.
- Perceptions exist that men are more reliable in workplaces; women need fair task distribution for successful careers.

### 2. UNEMPLOYMENT

#### a) Men and Unemployment

- Men's inability to provide leads to feelings of despair; unemployment trends have affected traditional roles of male breadwinners.
- **Breadwinner ideology** may contribute to perceived crises in masculinity, challenging fatherhood roles dependent on stable income.
- Unemployment negatively impacts family living standards; difficulty re-employing middle-aged men leads to family strain.

#### b) Women and Unemployment

- Increased female employment reflects a shift in labor dynamics; single mothers are often linked to poverty.
- Economic restructuring has heightened unemployment among unskilled workers, affecting women disproportionately.
- Females experience not only economic but also psychological impacts of unemployment; isolation and dependency may arise.
- Women's experiences of unemployment differ from men's; femininity is less threatened by unemployment than masculinity.

#### c) Youth and Unemployment

- Youth unemployment correlates with family structures; employment signals adult responsibilities like marriage and parenting.
- Unemployed youths are viewed as unsuitable for marriage; linked to rebellious behavior and crime.
- Family expectations instill structure and encourage job-seeking among youth; rural youths face higher unemployment rates.

### 3. SOCIAL CLASS AND FAMILY LIFE

- **Marxist Theory**: Families perpetuate the class system; social class underpins family dynamics.
- Social class distinctions affect family roles, responsibilities, and lifestyle choices.

#### Class Positions:

1. **Upper-Class**: Inherited wealth; involvement in charities; significant investments in children’s future.
2. **Lower-Upper-Class**: Newly wealthy; model after upper-class norms; aim for social mobility.
3. **Upper-Middle-Class**: Professionals with dual-career families; significant emphasis on education and child-rearing.
4. **Lower-Middle-Class**: Limited education; characterized by stable employment; extended family importance.
5. **Working-Class**: Gender-defined labor divisions; reliant on female social networks in child-rearing.
6. **Lower-Class**: Unskilled, poorly paid work; women often more employed; social networks crucial for support. 

- **Underclass**: Severely disadvantaged, often reliant on state welfare; excluded from mainstream economy.

### Conclusion

- Women often experience a double burden of responsibilities, while men may face exclusion from family tasks due to work demands.
- Variability in unemployment affects men, women, and youth differently, particularly among breadwinners.

# Study Notes: Understanding Social Problems

## 1. Introduction
- Personal problems vs. social problems: Personal issues stem from individual circumstances; social problems affect collective well-being, necessitating group action.
  
## 2. PERSONAL VS SOCIAL PROBLEMS
- **Personal Problem**: Rooted in the individual (e.g., unemployment impacting nutrition).
- **Social Problem**: Engages collective action, e.g., unemployment at 37% in South Africa creating broader societal issues.

## 3. CONSIDERATIONS IN DEFINING A SOCIAL PROBLEM
- **Social Problem Definition**: Condition threatening societal norms and quality of life.

### a) Social Problems: A Definition
- Social problems arise when a significant segment of society recognizes a condition that threatens values and believes it can be resolved collectively.

### b) Awareness of a Social Condition
- Example: Environmental pollution negatively impacts health and nature.

### c) A Pressure Group Defines a Problem
- Influential groups (e.g., Greenpeace) identify issues, impacting public discourse and policy.

- **Power to Define Issues**:
  - **Numerical Strength**: Larger groups can outvote smaller ones.
  - **Organization**: Coordinated efforts can influence actions (e.g., movements for pollution control).
  - **Access to Resources**: Groups can leverage resources (money, media) to promote agendas.

- **Authority to Define Issues**:
  - Traditional: Customary belief systems (e.g., parental norms).
  - Rational: Understanding through reasoning.
  - Charismatic: Leadership based on personal qualities (e.g., Nelson Mandela).

### d) Values and Norms Under Threat
- Societal values encompass perceptions of justice (e.g., abortion debates illustrate varying value hierarchies).

### e) Belief in Collective Action
- Societal belief exists that collective efforts can mitigate issues like unemployment and AIDS.

### f) Features of Social Problems
- **International Ideologies**: Issues like racism and gender-based violence.
- **Historical Context**: Problems can evolve (e.g., changing views on racism).
- **Wide Impact**: Problems like poverty affect global populations.
- **Uniqueness of Society**: Issues can be context-specific (e.g., apartheid).
- **Conflicting Values and Interests**: Interest groups may resist change that threatens their status quo.

## 4. RESEARCH ON SOCIAL PROBLEMS
- Focus areas: societal awareness, definitions of problems, values at risk, and planning responses.
- Contributing factors in South Africa: Urbanization, economic depression, racial conflict.
- Sociologists contribute solutions (e.g., addressing poverty, AIDS).

## 5. SOCIOLOGICAL THEORY AND SOCIAL PROBLEMS
### Theoretical Perspectives
- **Structural Functionalism**: Focus on societal stability and normative functions; rapid change leads to normlessness (anomie).
- **Conflict Theory**: Examines power disparities influencing crime; corporate crime highlights how elites navigate deviance.
- **Symbolic Interactionism**: Focus on individual interactions and social meanings; criminal behavior as learned through social contexts.

### Importance of Theory
1. Provides explanations for social phenomena.
2. Directs focus to significant aspects of issues.
3. Highlights knowledge gaps.
4. Aids in predicting future behaviors.
5. Facilitates finding solutions through understanding problem origins.

## Summary
- Social problems are defined collectively; pressure groups play a vital role in identification and resolution.
- Sociology aims to study and recommend solutions for social phenomena.

# UNIT 8: Marginalised Identities: Disabilities

## Introduction
- Marginalised identities relate to labeling individuals as handicapped or disabled.
- Such labels enhance feelings of "otherness" and can evolve to become part of mainstream identities.

### 1. LABELLING
- Labels important for healthcare planning and intervention.
- Positive consequences from correct labeling; negative from incorrect.
- Understanding disabilities essential for meeting the needs of disabled individuals.

### 2. GOFFMAN ON STIGMA
- Distinction between virtual and actual social identity (Goffman, 1963).
- Stigmas create discrepancies; lead to dehumanization and discriminatory practices.
- Coping strategies: Individual acceptance and interactions within stigmatized groups reduce stigma-related stress.

### 3. FOUR MODELS OF DISABILITY AND IMPAIRMENT (French & Swain)
- **Medical Model**: 
  - Views disabilities as individual problems requiring medical intervention.
- **Tragedy Model**: 
  - Defines disability as a personal tragedy rather than a social issue.
- **Social Model**: 
  - Emphasizes societal barriers rather than individual impairments.
- **Affirmative Model**: 
  - Seeks to affirm identities rather than deny harsh realities; recognizes intersectionality with other identities.

### 4. IDENTITY POLITICS AND DISABILITY
- Disability struggles began in the 1970s with increasing political and cultural attention.
- Movement towards recognition of positive identities and formal rights against discrimination.

### 5. THE DEAF COMMUNITY
- Models of deafness: medical interventions vs. recognition of linguistic and cultural identity.
- Membership criteria in the deaf community include audiological, linguistic, social, and political factors.
- The deaf community has developed a distinct culture with its own schools, clubs, and norms.
- Ongoing debates about integration into hearing communities and the importance of deaf identity.

## 2. Moral Conservative Perspective on AIDS
- **Cultural Context**: AIDS initially viewed as a byproduct of sexual permissiveness and homosexuality.
- **Evolution of Understanding**: Shift in perception due to increased knowledge about HIV transmission and control strategies.
- **Changing Accusations**: Decreased harshness in public attitudes towards homosexuals linked to better understanding of AIDS development.

## 3. Representation of AIDS in Gay Debates
- **Viral Origins**: Gay discourse emphasizes AIDS as a viral issue, not inherently linked to homosexuality.
- **Sexual Practices**: Connects AIDS to specific practices (e.g., anal) rather than identity.
- **Control Strategies**: Promotes lifestyle changes (e.g., condom use) for AIDS control.
- **Denial of Promiscuity**: Responses from gay writers to distance themselves from negative stereotypes regarding promiscuity and drug use.
- **Underlying Moral Conservative View**: Recognition of safer sex practices among gays contrasts with early accusations of promiscuity causing AIDS.

## 4. AIDS in Feminist Debates
- **Initial Misconceptions**: Perception of AIDS as a disease affecting only gay men; later recognized impact on heterosexuals.
- **Sexual Power Dynamics**: Feminist critique highlights how sexual privileges and ideologies propagate male control over women's sexual health.
- **Cultural Barriers to Safe Sex**: Women face cultural challenges in practicing safe sex, increasing vulnerability to infection.

## 3. Epidemiology of AIDS
- **Definition**: AIDS as a condition resulting from compromised immune system, leading to susceptibility to infections and other complications.
- **Transmission Routes**:
  1. Penetrative sex with an infected individual.
  2. Contact with infected blood (e.g., shared needles).
  3. Mother-to-child during birth or breastfeeding.
- **Influence of Socioeconomic Factors**: Malnutrition exacerbates HIV/AIDS progression in South Africa; average time from infection to death is 3-7 years.

### 5 Stages of HIV/AIDS Life Cycle
1. Precondition: Factors affecting susceptibility.
2. Infection: Acquisition of the virus.
3. Latency: Asymptomatic phase of infection.
4. Dependency: Acute care requirement.
5. Further Consequences: Impact of the disease on family and economic structures.

## 4. South African AIDS Scenario
- **Cultural Stigmas**: Resistance to condom use linked to cultural beliefs and gender dynamics; women disproportionately blamed for HIV spread.
- **Socioeconomic Disparities**: AIDS affects both rich and poor, primarily impacting individuals aged 30-34.
- **Impact on Families**: Early maternal mortality leads to increased responsibility for elderly caretakers, affecting child development.
- **Life Expectancy**: Average age of 47 years; economic implications are severe.

### Problem Areas in SA Health Care
- **Unequal Distribution**: Health services and staff unevenly dispersed, exacerbated by racial and geographic inequalities.
- **Curative vs. Preventative Focus**: High disease incidence linked to poor living conditions; need for improved social determinants of health.
- **Access Inequities**: Segmentation of services based on geography, race, and socio-economic status.

### a) Distribution Inequalities
- Importance of reasonable access to health services (hospitals, clinics).
- Differences in facility distribution highlight social and racial inequities.
- Free-market dynamics leads to disparities in care access.

### b) Curative vs. Preventative Emphasis
- Poor living conditions contribute to high disease incidence.
- Need for health care improvements linked to broader social issues.

### c) Access Barriers
- Geographical challenges hinder access to health care.
- Financial, class, and racial barriers further complicate access.

## 5. Different Health Care Systems
### Ideal Type of Health Care
- Abstract model representing various health care systems globally.

### Free-Enterprise System
- Characterized by:
  - Two-tier health care system: private vs. public.
  - Insufficient preventive focus.
  - Concentrated facilities in urban areas.
  - Profit-driven motives leading to high costs.

### Welfare-State System
- Features include:
  - Mixed public/private health services.
  - Accessible health care for all population segments.
  - Emphasis on preventive care and better distribution of resources.
- Challenges include:
  - Motivation of public sector workers.
  - Overwhelmed public facilities.

### Socialist System
- Characteristics:
  - State-owned health services.
  - Healthcare perceived as a human right.
  - Collective community involvement in health care delivery.
- Challenges include:
  - Lack of patient choice.
  - Equal salary for health professionals relative to other sectors.

## 6. Vision for Health in South Africa
### Equity
- Improvement of health through equitable social and economic development.

### Right to Health
- State's responsibility to provide optimal health conditions for all citizens.

### Primary Health Care Approach
- Emphasizes prevention and community involvement in health system restructuring.

### National Health System
- Comprehensive integration of health services under a single governance structure.

### Coordination and Decentralization
- Healthcare provision coordinated among various governmental levels with decentralization of authority.

### Priorities
- Focus on vulnerable groups, maternal and child care, and prevention of major health risks.

### Promotion of Health
- Importance of health education and integration of traditional healing practices.

### Respect for All
- Implementation of a Charter of Patients’ Rights to ensure dignity in treatment.

### Health Information System
- Requirements for systematic data collection for effective health system management and planning.
