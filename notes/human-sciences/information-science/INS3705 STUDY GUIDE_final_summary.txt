# Study Notes on Data, Information, Knowledge, and Wisdom

## Chapter 1: Understanding Data and Information

### 1.2.1 What is Information?
- **Definition**: Information is data presented comprehensively with meaning in context (Reitz, 2004).
- **Formula**: DATA + (Meaning) = INFORMATION
- **Characteristics** of Information:
  - Transformative nature linked to decision-making in contexts (e.g., temperatures, electricity supply).

### 1.2.2 Nature of Information
- **Information Economy**:
  - Information as a valuable resource (Meyer, 2005).
- **Unique Characteristics**:
  - Non-physical output (e.g., software vs. hardware).
  - Expandable, sharable, and non-depleting through use.
  - Increases in value through sharing.
- **Burk and Horton’s Attributes**:
  - Measurable cost, definable value, quantifiable consumption.
  - Clear life cycle: collection, processing, storage, dissemination.
  - Requires management approaches.

## Chapter 1.2.3 What is Knowledge?
- **Elusive Definition**: Philosophical debates on truth and belief.
  - **Plato’s Definition**: Knowledge as justified, true belief.
- **Conceptualization**:
  - Evaluated and organized information, internalized (Feather & Sturges; Wiig, 1993).
  - Knowledge informs situation interpretation and decision-making.

### 1.2.3.1 The Need for Knowledge
- Essential for daily management and success.
- Higher knowledge demands deeper understanding for competitive advantage.

### 1.2.3.2 Nature of Knowledge
- **Epistemology**: The study of knowledge's nature.
- **Unique Characteristics**:
  - Personal, internalized, and expandable like information.
  - Deemed a resource in management contexts.

## Chapter 1.2.4 What is Wisdom?
- **Definition**: Application of knowledge effectively (Wehmeier, 2000).
- **Role**: Best use of knowledge for organizational objectives.

## Chapter 1.3 The Knowledge Pyramid (DIKW Model)
- **DIKW Hierarchy**: Illustrates relationships among data, information, knowledge, and wisdom.
- Two representations:
  - Linear relationship versus the **Knowledge Pyramid**.

### Learning Unit 1.1: DIKW Hierarchy
- **Figures:** 
  - **Figure 1.1:** DIKW Hierarchy (Bellinger, Castro & Mills 2004)

- **Knowledge Pyramid (Mchombu 2007:16):**
  - Bottom Layer: Data (symbols, facts, figures)
  - Middle Layer: Information (processed data providing meaning)
  - Top Layer: Knowledge (evaluated information organized for purpose)
  - Wisdom (top of pyramid): Skill to discern right from wrong (Bellinger et al. 2004).
  
- **Transformation Processes:** 
  - **Understanding** is the facilitator between data, information, knowledge, and wisdom.
  - Definitions by Bellinger et al. (2004):
    - Data: Fact without relationship.
    - Information: Understanding relationships.
    - Knowledge: Predictable patterns based on information.
    - Wisdom: Understanding fundamental principles behind knowledge.

### Learning Unit 1.2: Information and Knowledge as Assets
- Importance of **information and knowledge** as resources akin to land, money, and equipment (Meyer, 2005).
- **Gangatharan (2004)** identifies components of information resources:
  - Information content: Characteristics and uses.
  - Information technologies: Hardware and software.
  - People: Personnel managing information (technical staff, knowledge workers).
  - Facilities: Libraries, communication centres, etc.

- **Knowledge Assets (Handzic & Zhou 2005:109):**
  - Human capital (employees’ knowledge).
  - Knowledge artefacts (databases, manuals).
  - Structural assets (culture, strategy).
  - Customer capital (knowledge about customers).

### Learning Unit 1.3: Information Society
- **Definitions:**
  - **Information Society:** Utilizes ICT for societal benefit (Information Society Commission, 1999).
  - **WSIS (2003) Definition:** Society where individuals freely create, receive, share, and utilize information for development.

- **Differences:**
  - Information Society: Social environment of information processes.
  - Information Economy: Economic activities focused on information.
  - **Information Age:** Current era emphasizing information over physical goods.

### Learning Unit 1.4: Characteristics of Information Society
- **Moore (1999:702):**
  - Information-intensive organizations utilize data for advantage.
  - Significant information sector contributes to economic growth.
  - Social use: Information aids decision-making.
  - Learning society values lifelong education and information literacy.

### Learning Unit 1.5: Knowledge Society
- **Key Concepts:**
  - Knowledge Society: People sharing and utilizing knowledge for societal benefit.
  - Knowledge Economy: Use of knowledge for economic gain (transition from industrial economy).

- **Characteristics:**
  - Global nature facilitated by technology (ICTs).
  - Enhanced opportunities for knowledge sharing and retrieval.
  - Knowledge recognized as vital capital for success.

### 1. Characteristics of the Knowledge Economy (pg. 19)
- **Knowledge-centric**: Services/goods rely on knowledge for production/distribution (e.g., consulting).
- **Increasing returns**: Production cost decreases after initial high cost (e.g., software).
- **Network effects**: Value of offerings increases with market share, often pursued at loss (e.g., free software).
- **Rapid changes**: Need for organizational adaptability and swift knowledge application.
- **Networked firms**: Enhanced transparency and effectiveness of knowledge through partnerships.
- **Differentiation**: Firms succeed based on knowledge assimilation and customer loyalty.
- **Innovation focus**: Success requires innovation in processes, industries, and clientele.
- **Obsolescence of knowledge**: Fast integration of new knowledge is critical.
- **Experience economy**: Products/services viewed as experiences.

### 2. MANAGEMENT (pg. 15)
#### 2.1 What is Management? (pg. 15)
- **Function**: Process of leading an organization through resource manipulation (human, capital, etc.).
- **Entity**: Personnel leading an organization; often refers to executive roles.
- **Historical Definition**: Follet (1918) defined as getting things done through and with people.
- **Contemporary Definitions**:
  - **Pettinger (2007)**: Combination of resources into effective activities.
  - **Schermerhorn (2005)**: Planning, organizing, leading, and controlling resources to achieve goals.

#### 2.2 Foundations of Management (pg. 16)
- Essential components include:
  - Achieving goals through collaboration.
  - Profit generation.
  - Resource utilization and development.
  - Managing change and uncertainty.

#### 2.3 Management Functions (pg. 16)
- **Planning**: Future decision-making and action planning.
- **Organising**: Efficient resource arrangement to meet plans.
- **Directing**: Guiding and overseeing employee participation to achieve goals.
- **Controlling**: Monitoring progress and making necessary adjustments based on feedback.

#### 2.4 Branches of Management (pg. 17)
- **Main branches**:
  - Human Resource Management
  - Operations/Production Management
  - Strategic Management
  - Marketing Management
  - Financial Management
  - Information Technology Management
- **Interrelated areas**: Includes administrative, communication, cost, crisis, environmental, and knowledge management.

### 1. Knowledge Management Definitions (Page 45)
- Knowledge Management (KM) is the process of identifying, organizing, and managing knowledge resources (Al-Hawamdeh, 2003:21).
- KM includes:
  - **Explicit knowledge**: information
  - **Know-how**: learning capacity
  - **Know-who**: customer-related knowledge
  - **Tacit knowledge**: skills and competencies
- Wiig (1999): KM systematic and deliberate, focused on maximizing effectiveness and returns from knowledge assets.
- Information management is a subset of KM, primarily involving explicit knowledge.

---

### 2. Types of Organisational Knowledge (Page 46)

#### a. Tacit Knowledge
- **Definition**: Personal, context-independent, hard to formalize, difficult to communicate (Metis, 2002).
- Characterized by subjective insights, technical skills ("know-how"), and intuitive knowledge.
- **Polanyi's Idea**: "We can know more than we can tell."
- Divided into:
  - **Technical Dimension**: Informal skills related to know-how.
  - **Cognitive Dimension**: Implicit mental models influencing perception.

#### b. Explicit Knowledge
- **Definition**: Formal, systematic, more easily communicated and shared.
- Examples: documented knowledge, patents, operational procedures.
- Considered intellectual assets, easily transmitted and processed.
- **Gladstone (2000)**: Captured and represented by artefacts; provides snapshots of tacit knowledge at the time of capture.

---

### 3. Components of Knowledge Management (Page 48)
- **Content**: Knowledge itself, processes for manipulation, and flow of information.
- **Human Resources**: Involvement of individuals in creating and valuing knowledge.
- **Technology**: Infrastructure necessary for storing and accessing knowledge; technology is an instrument for achieving goals, not a goal itself.

---

### 4. Drivers of Knowledge Management (Page 49)
- **Global Trends**: Digital and technological advancements facilitating new forms of organization and economy.
- **Shifting Economy**: Transition to a knowledge economy, where knowledge itself is a primary commodity (Stewart).
- **Changing Organisational Forms**: Growth in knowledge-intensive organizations.
- **Knowledge Work**: Emphasis on effective KM to enhance the production and reproduction of valuable knowledge.
  
**Main Reasons for Adopting KM** (Handzic and Zhou, 2005:26):
- Limited access to outside knowledge
- Poor connections with stakeholders
- Inadequate understanding of market dynamics
- Stagnating skills
- Ineffective product/service development
- Limited organizational knowledge

**Key Drivers of KM (Al-Hawamdeh, 2003:26)**:
- **Organizational Efficiency**: Need for organized information to support operations.
- **Competitive Advantage**: Understanding of customer/competitor dynamics through CRM and market intelligence.
- **Maximizing Potential**: Enhancing returns on R&D investments through effective knowledge recycling.

## Chapter 3: Knowledge Management Processes

### 3.7 KNOWLEDGE MANAGEMENT MANIPULATION PROCESSES
- Integration of various knowledge forms essential for transfer and creation of new knowledge (Handzic & Zhou 2005).
- KM processes classified into four categories:
  - **Knowledge creation process**
  - **Knowledge storage/retrieval process**
  - **Knowledge sharing/transfer process**
  - **Knowledge application process**

#### 3.7.1 Knowledge Creation Process
- Development or replacement of new content in collective knowledge (Handzic & Zhou 2005).
- **Nonaka and Takeuchi's model:**
  - Knowledge conversion through interplay of tacit and explicit knowledge.
  - **Processes:**
    - **Socialisation:** Sharing tacit knowledge informally.
    - **Externalisation:** Converting tacit to explicit knowledge.
    - **Combination:** Restructuring explicit knowledge into new forms.
    - **Internalisation:** Converting explicit knowledge back to tacit.

- Successful teamwork conditions include:
  - Small team size
  - Complementary skills
  - Shared purpose
  - Specific goals
  - Clear approach
  - Mutual accountability

#### 3.7.2 Knowledge Storage/Retrieval Process
- Organizing and retrieving knowledge in various forms (Handzic & Zhou 2005).
- Creation of knowledge repository recommended for knowledge reuse:
  - For document producers’ future use.
  - For others with similar or dissimilar backgrounds.
- Challenges:
  - Diverse user needs
  - Costs of maintaining repositories (Markus).

#### 3.7.3 Knowledge Sharing/Transfer Process
- Communication of all knowledge types, including explicit and tacit (Al-Hawamdeh 2003).
- Distribution of knowledge for application (Handzic & Zhou 2005).
- **Channels of knowledge transfer:**
  - **Informal:** Effective for small organizations but may inhibit diffusion.
  - **Formal:** Widely distributed but may hinder creativity.
  - **Personal:** Effective for context-specific knowledge, resource-constrained.
  - **Impersonal:** Easy access but may discourage use due to volume.

- Goal: Distribute right knowledge to right people at right time.
- Growing importance due to ageing workforce, necessitating knowledge codification.
- Effective transfer requires supportive environment and technical infrastructure:
  - Visibility of knowledge
  - Knowledge networks 
  - Organisational support
  - Coordinated knowledge flow

#### 3.7.4 Knowledge Application Process
- Application of knowledge in practice to achieve competent performance (Al-Hawamdeh 2003).
- Integrating knowledge mechanisms to create organizational capability:
  - **Directives:** Rules and procedures for specific situations.
  - **Organisational routines:** Task performance and interaction protocols.
  - **Self-contained task teams:** Groups for complex tasks.

### 3.8 IMPORTANT CORNERSTONES OF KNOWLEDGE MANAGEMENT
- Identification of content through knowledge audits to prevent information overload.
- Importance of organisational culture; continuous education programs essential for developing a knowledge-based culture.
- Logical sequence in KM process crucial for cost-effectiveness.
- Infrastructure consistency with knowledge needs and financial constraints; KM as dynamic process requiring future planning.

### 3.9 LEVELS AT WHICH KNOWLEDGE MANAGEMENT MAY TAKE PLACE
- KM occurs on multiple levels:
  - **Business Level:** Focus on business reasons for KM.
  - **Management Level:** Facilitation and monitoring of KM activities.
  - **Knowledge-Related Level:** Functions related to knowledge handling and application.

# Chapter 3: Knowledge Management

## 3.10 Barriers to Effective Knowledge Management
- Understanding barriers:
  - Problems/challenges in KM processes, primarily knowledge-related.
- Key issues identified by **Wiig (1995:9)**:
  - Knowledge not treated as valuable asset → ineffective use.
  - Insufficient knowledge at point-of-action → task performance difficulties.
  - Missed learning opportunities → lack of feedback on prior tasks.
  - Narrow knowledge transfer → limited task assignments.
  - Unnecessary task division → fragmented knowledge.
- Additional barriers per **Trivedi (2007)**:
  - Intellectual capital → challenge of managing tacit knowledge.
  - Culture → reluctance to share knowledge due to insecurity.
  - Obsolescence → fast information aging, difficult to disseminate.
  - Overloading → accumulation of irrelevant data leading to confusion.
  - Technology → rapid changes hinder effective KM application.

## 3.11 Enablers of Knowledge Management
- Definition: Factors that positively influence KM.

### Chapter 1: Introduction to Legal Principles

- **Definition of Law**
  - System of rules enforceable through institutions.
  
- **Functions of Law**
  - Maintain order, resolve disputes, protect liberties, establish standards.

- **Types of Law**
  - **Public Law**: Governs the relationship between individuals and the state (e.g., criminal law).
  - **Private Law**: Governs relationships between private individuals (e.g., contract law).

### Chapter 2: Law and Society

- **Relationship between Law and Society**
  - Law reflects societal values; changes with societal norms.

- **Role of Legal Institutions**
  - Courts, legislatures, and regulatory bodies in law-making and enforcement.

- **Impact of Social Changes on Law**
  - Legislation reflects cultural shifts (e.g., **Human Rights Act**).

### Chapter 3: The Judicial System

- **Structure of the Judiciary**
  - Hierarchical system: lower courts, appellate courts, supreme courts.

- **Key Judicial Principles**
  - **Judicial Independence**: Detached from political influences.
  - **Rule of Law**: Law applies equally to all.

- **Notable Cases**
  - **Marbury v. Madison**: Established judicial review.
  - **Brown v. Board of Education**: Addressed segregation in public schools.

### Chapter 4: Sources of Law

- **Primary Sources**
  - **Legislation**: Statutes enacted by legislative bodies.
  - **Case Law**: Legal principles established by court decisions.

- **Secondary Sources**
  - Commentaries, legal journals, and textbooks.

- **International Law**
  - Treaties and conventions (e.g., **United Nations Charter**).

### Chapter 5: Legal Reasoning and Interpretation

- **Principles of Legal Reasoning**
  - Logical reasoning based on legal precedents and statutes.

- **Interpretation of Statutes**
  - **Literal Rule**: Words taken at face value.
