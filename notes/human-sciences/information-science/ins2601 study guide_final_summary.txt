## 2.8 Information User Studies as a subject discipline
- User studies: empirical investigation of users; broader term includes Information User Studies.
- Focus on individuals as readers and users of various information records.
- "Read" encompasses interpretation beyond traditional definitions, inclusive of various media formats.
- Information literacy: ability to interpret diverse media; computer literacy: effective use of computers.
- Historical context: development of readership research since the late 1920s with cycles of interest.

## 2.9 Object of study of Information User Studies
- Emerged post-1948 Royal Society conference on information supply to scientists.
- Extensive studies conducted on user groups and their information behaviors (needs, seeking, usage).
- Distinct perspectives across disciplines (Psychology, Sociology, Information User Studies) shape study focus.
- **Definition**: Examines people as interpreters of all forms of recorded information, irrespective of context.

## 2.10 Key issues of Information User Studies
- Core questions to explore about information users:
  - User needs and motives (information needs)
  - User behavior (information usage)
  - Act of interpretation (information utilization)
  - Media effects/functions (effects of information usage)
- Hatt’s questions provide a framework:
  1. Who is the reader?
  2. What are the reader’s needs?
  3. What is the text?
  4. How do the reader and text interact?
  5. Nature of the reading act?
  6. Outcome of the reading act?

## 2.11 General theoretical framework for studying the information user
- Focus on the information user as an active participant in communication.

### 2.11.1 Model of the reading or media utilization event
- Fouché's "reading events model": identifies critical moments in user behavior:
  - Decision to read/use media
  - Selection of media
  - Reading/interpretive act
  - Outcomes of the reading act
- Influencing factors: content of reading matter, reader characteristics, environment.

### 2.11.2 Information usage model
- Addresses strategies users employ in electronic/virtual environments.
- Rephrased Hatt’s questions to fit contemporary contexts:
  1. Attributes and predispositions of users.
  2. Information needs/motivations.
  3. Source selection and usage.
  4. Search processes for information.
  5. Nature of interpretive acts.
  6. Outcomes of information usage.
  7. Contextual factors influencing use.
- **Figure 2.1 Information usage model**: Depicts components of information generation, user motivation, and contextual factors related to usage.

## 2.12 Information Usage Behaviour
- **Definition**: Extends beyond using information, including antecedents (needs, motives) and outcomes of information usage.
  
### 2.12.1 Motives for Information Usage
- **Needs and Motives**: Fundamental driver behind information needs; derived from deep-seated personal requirements (Hatt, 1986:41–44).
- **Decision to Use Information**: Influenced by personal needs and characteristics (age, sex, intelligence).

### 2.12.2 Information Seeking Patterns
- **Components**: Selection and acquisition of information.
- **Influences**: User's personal and social factors.
- **Examples**: Consulting colleagues, using libraries, internet.

### 2.12.3 Information Utilisation
- **Process**: Involves decoding and interpretation.
- **Interpretive Act**: Core element; requires decoding which involves perceptual and cognitive processes.
- **Influences**: User's context, predispositions, and cultural framework.

### 2.12.4 Effects of Information Utilisation
- **Outcomes**: Immediate reactions (knowledge increase, attitude change) or more permanent effects.
- **Variability**: Effects differ in duration, exposure, and scope; relates to satisfaction or gratiﬁcation of needs.

### 2.12.5 Contexts of Information Usage
- **Factors**:
  - User's attributes and circumstances.
  - Record/media characteristics and the information environment.

#### 2.12.5.1 User Attributes and Circumstances
- **Social Context**: Influences knowledge generation (culture, economics, politics).
- **Physical Context**: Geographic factors affect media availability.
- **Personal Context**: Biological, hereditary factors, personality impacting media usage.

#### 2.12.5.2 Record Characteristics and Information Environment
- **Factors**:
  - **Record Content**: Subject, theme, presentation, readability affect usability.
  - **Record Form**: Types (visual, auditory, audiovisual) require different skills.
  - **Availability and Accessibility**: Media accessed via various distribution channels affects choice.

## 2.13 Wilson’s Models of Information User Behaviour
- Focuses on user behaviour; comprehensive view compared to other models.
  
### 2.13.1 Wilson’s 1981 Model of Information Behaviour
- **Versions**: Published from 1981 to 1999, termed “information behaviour models”.
- **Core Idea**: Arises from user’s perceived need influenced by satisfaction with previously acquired information.
- **Threefold View**:
  - **Context of the Seeker**: Personal situations influencing information needs.
  - **System Employed**: Manual/computerised, personal/intermediary usage.
  - **Information Resources**: Selection based on perceived need.

- **Outcome**: Information-seeking behaviour effects (success/failure) allow for information transfer and sharing.

## 2.13.2 Wilson’s 1996 Model of Information Behaviour
- Expands 1981 model; incorporates literature from diverse fields (psychology, decision making, health communication).
- Focus: Individuals in context and their information needs.
- **Barriers:** Represented by intervening variables affecting information use.
  - **Motivators:** Based on Wilson’s activating mechanisms (Case 2012:155).
  - **Types of intervening variables:**
    1. Psychological predispositions (curiosity, risk aversion)
    2. Demographic background (age, education)
    3. Social role factors (manager vs. parent)
    4. Environmental variables (available resources)
    5. Source characteristics (accessibility, credibility)
- Information processing is crucial for satisfying information needs.

### Theoretical Ideas in the Model
1. **Stress/Coping Theory:** Explains variations in information-seeking triggers.
2. **Risk-Reward Theory:** Insights into source preference.
3. **Social Learning Theory:** Ability to carry out behaviours for desired outcomes.

- Recognized types of search behaviour:
  - Passive attention
  - Passive search
  - Active search
  - Ongoing search

## 2.14 Application of Information User Studies in Practice
1. **User Needs Studies:**
   - Clarifies information use in various circumstances; aids in understanding user needs for improving service.
2. **User Behaviour Patterns:**
   - Describes information usage; links biographical and social data to media usage.
   - Discoveries include:
      - Availability/accessibility for specific user groups.
      - Role of information in users' lives.
      - Characteristics and usage patterns of groups.
3. **User Interpretation of Media:**
   - Investigates readability and comprehension; outcomes impact educators and the publishing industry.
4. **Effects of Information Utilisation:**
   - Important for policymakers; relates to research application and implementation.

## 2.15 Concluding Remarks
- **Study Focus:** Information user studies focus on individuals as information users across various media.
- Historical surge in interest; demand for a conceptual framework led to emerging models.
- Models emphasized varying aspects of information usage, influenced by communication theories.
- Key issues identified in Information User Studies relate to user needs, characteristics, and behaviour.
- Existing models offer valuable insights, but a comprehensive framework is necessary for further research.

## 3.7 Categories of Information User
- **Definition of User**: Encompasses all readers of texts in paper-based or electronic formats, including independent, reluctant readers, and non-readers (dyslexics, illiterates).
- **User Types**:
  - **Independent Readers**: Read without difficulty.
  - **Reluctant Readers**: Read with hesitation.
  - **Non-Readers**: Includes illiterates and those unable to read due to socioeconomic environments.
  - **Users vs. Non-Users**: Users engage with various media; non-users are potential users and a key area in Information User Studies.
- **Sources of Information**: Formal (published) and informal (oral communication).

## 3.8 Information Users in a Social Context
- **Demographic Variables**:
  - **Age**: Children, youth, adults, seniors.
  - **Occupational Role**: Clerks, artisans, professionals.
  - **Non-Occupational Roles**: Homemakers, retired, unemployed.
  - **Geographic Location**: Rural vs. urban populations.
  - **Disadvantaged Groups**: Physically impaired, economically disadvantaged, illiterates.
  
### Focus on Illiterates and Developing Groups
- **Characteristics**: Often live in poverty, unemployed, and large families.
- **Educational Needs**: Require literacy programs and information for daily survival.
- **Information Access**: Limited for rural communities; subsistence farmers require practical agricultural information.

## 3.9 Information Users in an Occupational Context
- **Classification by Function/Environment** (Line, 1969):
  - **Research Conductors**
  - **Educators and Trainers**
  - **Managers and Administrators**
  - **Media Professionals**
  - **Politicians**
  - **Business and Commerce Professionals**
  - **Students**

### 3.10.1 Managers
- **Management Functions**: Planning, organizing, leading, coordinating.

### 3.11 Information Utilization Models

#### **3.11.1 Theoretical Framework: Paisley’s Model**
- Emphasizes the importance of the **scientist's personal system** as central in Information User Studies.
- Explores external systems that influence information usage behavior.
- **Ten Relevant Systems Identified by Paisley (1968):**
  1. **Cultural System**: Pervasive influence; largest circle in the model.
  2. **Political System**: Affects research focus, e.g., scientific nationalism.
  3. **Professional Membership Group**: Shapes access to discipline-specific information.
  4. **Reference Group**: Includes scientists with similar backgrounds, influences information management.
  5. **Scientific Fraternity (Invisible College)**: Small, personal network sharing direct information.
  6. **Formal Organisation**: Governs roles and policies affecting information access.
     - **6b. Umbrella Organisation**: E.g., university under educational department.
  7. **Work Team**: Subsystem focused on problem-solving and pertinent information.
  8. **Personality System**: Individual motivations, intelligence, and information relevance.
  9. **Legal and Economic System**: Impacts information flow (patents, copyrights).
  10. **Formal Information (Public) System**: Libraries/centers as competing information sources.
    - Wilkin (1977) emphasizes practical realization of this model.

#### **3.11.2 The Linkage Model: Havelock’s Model**
- Combines aspects of:
  - Research, Development, and Diffusion (RDD) model.
  - Problem-solving model.
  - Social interaction model.
- **Characteristics of Linkage Model**:
  - Views information transfer as two-way interaction.
  - Successful linkage needs understanding of user needs and problem-solving behaviors.
  - Requires shared trust and relationships for effective information transfer.
  
#### **3.11.3 The Task Performance Model: Blom’s Model**
- Shifts focus from user satisfaction to task performance.
- **Core Concept**: Information should serve scientific progress and effective decision-making.
- Context of information usage must align with specific tasks performed by scientists.
- **Model Structure**:
  - Central nucleus represents scientific task performance.
  - Surrounding segments represent factors influencing performance: individual, discipline, environment.
  
### 3.12 Dervin’s Sense-Making Model
- Conceptual tool for understanding communication, information, and meaning.
- Emphasizes active information seeking rather than passive reception.
- Focuses on situational contexts prompting information seeks, irrespective of user demographics.

- Focus on human information processing traits and contexts of information needs.

## 4.7 General Model of Information-Seeking Behaviour
### 4.7.1 Contextual Complexity
- Brown's (1991) model emphasizes barriers to information-seeking and cycles in search behaviour.
  
### 4.7.2 General Model Focusing on the Context of Information Seeking
#### 4.7.2.1 The Conditions
- **Exposure:** Continuous presentation of stimuli (visual, aural).
- **Evaluation:** Processing occurring at the sensory register; stimuli split into specific and general signals.
- **Cognitive Recognition:** Stimuli represented in memory as thoughts.

#### 4.7.2.2 The Context
- **Components:** Individual needs (physiological, affective, cognitive), role, environment (social/cultural/political/economic).
- Barriers arise at the intersection of self, role, and environment.

#### 4.7.2.3 The Process

  4. Diverse interpretations.

  - Studies indicate a correlation between gratification obtained, media choice, and content choice (Palmgreen, Wenner & Rosengren 1985:22).  

- **Motivation for Media Usage**  
  - Media usage motivated by gratifications associated with the medium.  
  - Constraints include availability and work schedule, but motivation is key (Palmgreen et al 1985:22).  

- **User Gratifications Related to Media Effects**  
  - Motivations can include a need for information, affecting learning from media (Palmgreen et al 1985:30–31).  

## 8.10.2 Functions of Communications Media

- **Functions Identified by Researchers**:  
  1. **Entertainment Function**  
     - Diversion, recreation, escapism.  
  2. **Information Function**  
     - Deliberate acquisition vs. incidental knowledge assimilation.  
  3. **Social Function**  
     - Media as a medium for social contact/interaction.  

- **Complexity of Media Functions**  
  - Realistic content may combine informative with recreational aspects (Bogart 1983:217).  
  - Function effectiveness varies across users and content formats (Von Feilitzen 1976:97–102).

## 8.10.3 Displacement Effects

- **Understanding Displacement Effects**  
  - Related to satisfaction of needs by media.  
  - New medium satisfaction may displace similar existing activities.  
  - Factors influencing media preference: age, gender, social status (Neuman 1988:436).  
  - Emphasis on understanding how media satisfy needs rather than mere displacement.

## 8.11 Effects of Information Utilisation

- **Forms of Information Usage**:  
  - Instrumental, conceptual, symbolic, communicative, directional, continued education. (Refer to Huberman’s model in figure 6.1)

### 8.11.1 Instrumental Use of Information
- Defined as direct application for decision making and problem solving.  
- Involves transforming knowledge into specific behaviours (Havelock 1986b:24–25).  
- Process of decision making includes problem identification, solution evaluation, and selection (Weiss 1980:381).

### 8.11.2 Conceptual Use of Information
- Involves influencing thought and policy indirectly through new insights.  
- Helps users understand and navigate complex social processes (Weiss 1980:390–397).  
- Policy development may occur without explicit research considerations.

### 8.11.3 Symbolic Use of Research
- Used to justify previous actions or support opinions in decision-making (Havelock 1986b:24).  
- Research assists in clarifying and defending positions within debates (Weiss 1980:388).  
- May involve selective or distorted usage to support specific activities.

### 8.11.4 Communicative Use of Information
- Knowledge conveyed to others, primarily through intermediaries (Havelock 1986b:23).  

### 8.11.5 Directional Use of Information
- Research provides guidance for policy formulation and decision-making.  
- Integrates new information into existing knowledge bases for planning (Weiss 1980:391).  

### 8.11.6 Use of Research for Continuing Education and Development
- High-level officials utilize research for professional development in knowledge fields.  
- Sociological research communicates needs and service challenges.

## 8.12 Effects of Information and Communication Technology

### 8.12.1 The Internet
- Enables information access anywhere with connectivity.  
- Transformative impact on communication and information dissemination in Africa (Chivhanga 2000:373).  

### 8.12.2 The World Wide Web
- Distinction between Internet (network) and Web (information system), spanning from Web 1.0 to Web 3.0 (semantic Web).

### 8.12.3 E-mail and Mailing Lists
- Simplifies global communication, facilitating rapid sharing of information and resources.

## 8.12 Mailing Lists
- **Definition**: Distribution of information via e-mail to many users (Hock 2010:137).
- **Uses**:
  - Communicates specialized topics globally.
  - Tracks new developments.
  - Shares common interests and ideas.
  - Provides searchable archives and FAQs.

## 8.12.4 Mobile Devices
- **Types**: Handheld devices, PDAs, tablets, smartphones.
- **Characteristics**:
  - Portable and personal nature.
  - Constant accessibility to information.
  - Facilitates instantaneous information sharing.
- **Impact**:
  - Enhances internet and social network access.
  - Broadens exposure and communication opportunities.

## 8.12.5 Media-Assisted Networking
- **Definition**: Active information sharing among groups for problem-solving.
- **Types of Networking**:
  - Individual exchanges (telephone, e-mail).
  - Online social networks (Facebook, Twitter).
- **Impact**: 
  - Technology enables global networking and information dissemination.

## 8.13 Concluding Remarks

- **Study Focus**: Functional use of media versus user cognition and attitudes.
- **Technology Impact**: Changing information transfer dynamics via internet and mobile technology.

## Glossary
- **Concept**: Generalized idea applicable to related phenomena.
- **Data**: Raw facts before organization.
- **Decision Making**: Evaluating and selecting among alternatives.
- **Effect**: Result of an action.
- **Information**: Processed data useful to individuals.
- **Information Behaviour**: Actions related to handling information.
- **Information Literacy**: Abilities to recognize, locate, evaluate, and use information effectively.
- **Information Overload**: Excessive information causing anxiety.
- **Information Seeking**: Act of searching for information.
