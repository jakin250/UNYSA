### 1.2 DEFINING INFORMATION AND KNOWLEDGE

#### 1.2.1 INFORMATION
- Information = structured data conveying meaning (Cambridge, 2015).
- Can represent: commodity, process, environmental state, or message.
- Related to knowledge as communication of facts.
- **Recorded information** informs and has the potential to change perceptions (Behrens 1994; Frankl 1990).
- **Artandi & Otten** see information as representing non-material interactions.
- Information is a public entity; persists beyond the individual's mind.

#### 1.2.2 KNOWLEDGE
- **Tacit knowledge**: unexpressed; **explicit knowledge**: expressible.
- Knowledge = applied information, structured perceptions.
- Knowledge is essential for cognition and influenced by social interactions.
- Personal knowledge feeds into social knowledge; subjective processes generate new understanding.
- Knowledge shifts with new experiences influencing individual reality.

#### 1.2.3 RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN INFORMATION AND KNOWLEDGE
- Information is external and public; knowledge is personal and private.
- **Information can exist without becoming knowledge** (e.g., unread books).
- Data is foundational to both; serve as components of a continuum.

---

### 1.3 PROFESSIONAL ETHICS

#### 1.3.1 DEMARCATION OF THE PROFESSION
- Definition of **information scientist**: focus on information gathering, organizing, and distribution on behalf of third parties.
- Activities: determining user needs, designing, implementing information systems, and ethical control over information.

#### 1.3.2 ETHICAL PERSPECTIVES ON THE PROFESSION
- Ethical perspectives center on the representation of reality through information.
- **Information scientists** serve as mediators due to their ethical obligation of managing user dependency and information integrity.
- Professionalism and self-regulation are essential components of ethical conduct within the information science career.

## 1.4 THE INDIVIDUAL AND SOCIETY
- Challenges in universal information access:
  - Cost of information services.
  - Limited funds can degrade service quality.
  - Purpose of information must be aligned with its use.
  - Confidentiality of private information vs. public access.
  - Economic feasibility limits media availability.
  - Nature of information restricts its presentation formats.
  - Translation and comprehension barriers complicate information dissemination.

## 1.5 ETHICAL GUIDELINES FOR THE HANDLING OF INFORMATION
- **Ethical Norms**: Four key variables impacting ethical actions:
  - Category of information.
  - Type of user and stakeholders.
  - Continuum level of information scientists.
  - Purpose and results of information use.
- Key ethical issues: privacy, access, intellectual property, accuracy.
- **Ethical Principles**: 
  - Integrity, confidentiality, business ethics, objectivity.
  - Importance of quality service and professional competence.
- Conflicts in ethical principles (e.g., privacy vs. societal safety).
- Tension between intellectual property rights and access to information.

### 1.5.1 APPLIED ETHICAL ASPECTS OF INFORMATION
- Emphasis on philosophical foundation and justice as an informational basis.
- Common ethical codes for various information services.
- Challenges from digitisation: intellectual property, privacy, access rights, censorship.
- Questions raised about centralized information control:
  1. Monopoly over quality and access.
  2. Cultural dominance from Western technologies.
  3. Impact of information on developing countries.
- Professional ethics: practical standards in specific environments.
- Information moral dimensions include accuracy, confidentiality, and commercialization tensions.

## 1.6 MORALS, ETHICS, JUSTICE
- **Definitions**:
  - Ethics: Study of morals; behavior principles.
  - Ethic: Moral system unique to groups.
  - Justice: Fairness and adherence to law.
  - Moral (adjective): Relating to good and evil, guided by conscience.
  - Moral (noun): Practical lesson on right behavior.
- Responsibilities: Employers, profession, society.
- Ethical mandates complicated by conflicting personal values.

## 1.7 DATA, COMPUTER AND INFORMATION ETHICS
- Branches of applied ethics focused on practical moral standards in ICTs (Floridi, 2007 & 2016).
- Data ethics: Guidelines on data handling; computer ethics: Standards for computer use.
- Evolution of computer ethics since the mid-1970s (Maner, 1996) highlighting new ethical dilemmas.
- **Approaches to Computer Ethics**:
  1. No-resolution: Ethical dilemmas are unsolvable.
  2. Professional: Similarity to other professional ethics, creating ethical professionals.
  3. Radical: Unique discipline needing new concepts.
  4. Conservative: Traditional ethics apply; new species of existing issues.
  5. Innovative: Information ethics as foundational, redefining ethical positions.
- Importance of applied ethics in ICT due to:
  - Creation of new entities (software, AI).
  - Large-scale operations in data handling.
  - Development of novel technologies (robotics).
  - New risk and liability considerations.
  - Pervasiveness in modern society.

# Study Notes: Information Ethics and Society

## 1.8 FOUNDATIONS OF INFORMATION ETHICS
- **Mason (1986) identifies four ethical issues:**
  - **Privacy:** Disclosure requirements about personal information.
  - **Accuracy:** Standards and policies to protect against misinformation.
  - **Property:** Ownership of data and knowledge; determination methods.
  - **Access:** Rights to information and balance of common good vs. restrictions.
  
## 1.9 INFORMATION POLICY
- **Definition:** Information policy establishes frameworks on organizational, national, and international levels to facilitate information flow.
- **Goal:** Remove obstacles to access socially valuable information while balancing individual/communal rights (privacy, security).
- **Global Context:**
  - Importance due to global information networks.
  - Challenges faced by information-poor cultures.
  - Commodification of information shifts it from a public good to a commercial resource (Dick 2002a).
- **Ethical Considerations:** Exploitation and uneven access due to economic and political discrepancies (Wilson & Al-Muhanna 1985).

## 1.10 SOCIOLOGICAL ASPECTS OF INFORMATION AND THE INFORMATION SOCIETY
- **Information Society Characteristics (Moore n.d.):**
  - **Economic Resource:** Information enhances innovation, competition, efficiency.
  - **Public Use:** Increased public reliance on information for consumer activities.
  - **Sector Development:** Establishment of infrastructure for information services (ICT, telecommunications).
- **Cultural Dynamics:**
  - Globalisation poses threats; grassroots movements counter such trends.
  - Advance of technology marks societal shift; prominence of information as a dynamic resource.
  - **Transformation Concepts:** Control revolution (Beniger 1986); shifts in societal operations via technology.
  
## Main Characteristics of an Information Society
- **Theoretical Knowledge:** Essential in problem-solving; promotes effective service through education.
- **Research Utilization:** Industry and government leverage information for decision-making.
- **Reliable Information:** Critical for problem-solving and scientific processes; dissemination speed is crucial.
- **Robust Infrastructure:** Essential for retrieving and using information efficiently; involves communication channels and publishing industries.

## 1.11 INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY
- **Control and Change:** The development of communication technologies influences societal control capabilities.
- **Impact Factors:**
  - Historical growth in scientific institutionalisation and supported research.
  - Changes in labor divisions due to technological integration.
  - Need for information literacy among the populace as a prerequisite for participation.
- **Social Implications of Technology:**
  - Influence across social dimensions including language, religion, and societal norms.
  - Ongoing awareness of information and technology is vital for societal engagement.

#### 2.2 WHAT DO WE MEAN BY THE CONCEPT “ETHICS”?

- Not all moral solutions are equally valid; criteria for assessment may include human rights and social good.

##### 2.2.1 VALUES VS ETHICS
- **Values**: Desirable convictions; may not align with societal norms.
- **Ethics**: Focus on what is "good" or "right" in interactions.

---

##### 2.2.2 WHAT IS THE PURPOSE OF ETHICS AND ETHICAL BEHAVIOUR?
- Functions of ethics:
  1. Maintain societal structure.
  2. Alleviate suffering.
  3. Promote flourishing.
  4. Address conflicts of interest.
  5. Assign moral responsibility (Pojman in Beekun, 1996).

- Normative theories guide actions, distinguishing right from wrong.

- Happiness derived from meaningful pursuits, not material possessions.
- Preconditions for eudaimonia:

  - Genuine friendships.

# Study Notes on Ethics

## Page 25

### 2.3.1 Virtue Ethics
- Focuses on **character** of individuals.
- Good character implies good choices.
- **Golden Mean**: Evaluate actions based on virtuous behavior.

#### 2.3.1.1 Weaknesses of Virtue Ethics
- Virtue variability across cultures.
- Lacks clarity in moral conflict resolution.
- Self-centered focus on character.
- Goodness tied to well-being, raising concerns of self-interest.
- Imprecise guidance on behavior.
- Moral maturity dependent on luck.
- Limited in right-action guidance.

#### 2.3.1.2 Strengths of Virtue Ethics
- Promotes habits for human excellence and happiness.
- Emphasizes social and personal dimensions of rational behavior.
- Actions based on moderation, not rigid principles.
- Provides moral motivation through excellence.
- Ideal exemplars: **Buddha, Moses, Jesus, Gandhi, Mother Teresa**.

## Page 26

### 2.3.2 Deontological Ethics
- **Immanuel Kant**: Moral laws must be universal and reason-based.
- Focus on **duty** and intention instead of outcomes.
- **Categorical Imperative**: Actions should be universalized (e.g., if lying is acceptable if common, it undermines promises).

#### 2.3.2.1 Limitations of Deontological Ethics
- Rigid application of laws; conflicts arise between duties.
- Consequences often necessary for rational decision-making.

#### 2.3.2.2 Advantages of Deontological Ethics
- Promotes rationality and clear moral standards.
- Treats all individuals as moral equals; framework against discrimination.

#### 2.3.2.3 Variations
- **William D Ross**: Exceptions possible; e.g., lying can be permissible to save life.
- Universal acceptability requires considering perspectives of all parties.

## Page 27

### 2.3.3 Utilitarian Ethics
- **Jeremy Bentham & John Stuart Mill**: Greatest good for the greatest number.
- Focus on outcomes; happiness defined as pleasure and absence of pain.
- Utilize a **cost-benefit analysis** for moral decisions.

#### 2.3.3.1 Limitations of Utilitarian Ethics
- Difficulty in measuring happiness and consequences.
- Lack of agreement on happiness definitions; subjective.
- Potential for unintended consequences.
- Incomparability of benefits; human life valuation complexities.
- Distribution of benefits may be unjust.
- Means of achieving ends may be morally questionable.
- Risk of selfish or unethical behavior in justification.

#### 2.3.3.2 Advantages of Utilitarian Ethics
- Emphasizes the importance of consequences in ethical reasoning.
- Requires consideration of interests of all parties.

## Page 28

### 2.3.4 Ethics of Care
- Feminist perspective emphasizing relational context in morality (Burton, n.d.).
- Developed by **Nel Noddings & Carol Gilligan**.
- Relationships and the inclination to care foundational to moral considerations.

#### Criteria for Ethical Care
1. Existence of a relationship.
2. Potential for mutual care development.

#### Levels of Caring Morality
1. Self-care to exclusion of others.
2. Care for others to exclusion of self.
3. Mutual understanding of needs.

#### Core Values
- Emphasis on **emotions**: sympathy, empathy, sensitivity.
- Critique of removal of emotion in ethical reasoning.

#### 2.3.4.1 Limitations of Ethics of Care
- Risk of devolving into tribalism; self-interest of social groups.

#### 2.3.4.2 Advantages of Ethics of Care
- Aligns with natural instincts to protect vulnerable.
- Humanizes ethics by incorporating emotions.
- Focuses on immediate human interactions and needs.

#### 2.3.5 UBUNTU ETHICS
- **Definition**: Ubuntu as a central concept in African philosophy; translates to "humanness".
- **Ethical Perspective**: Focuses on communal relationships and human excellence.
- **Key Ideals** (Ramose, 2002):
  - Affirming humanity through recognizing others’ humanity.
  - Prioritizing the preservation of life over wealth.
- **Goodness**: Distinction between animal life and a genuinely human life; societal perceptions reflect that goodness is linked to human nature, not individual pleasure.

##### 2.3.5.1 UBUNTU AND COMMUNITIES
- **Philosophical Expressions**: "To be is to belong" and "I am because we are."
- **Moral Obligation**: Emphasizes community involvement for personal fulfilment (Shutte, 2001).

##### 2.3.5.2 UNDERSTANDING COMMUNITY
- **Commonalities with Aristotle**: Both emphasize self-realization through virtue.
- **Community Focus**: 
  - Identify with and treat group members equally.
  - Engage in solidarity for shared benefits.
- **African Ethical Perspective**: Emphasizes harmony and community as fundamental.

##### 2.3.5.3 UBUNTU AND INFORMATION ETHICS
- **Emerging Field**: Information ethics in Africa underexplored; often assessed through Western philosophical lenses.
- **Olinger et al. (2005)**: Discussed Ubuntu's impact on:
  - Politics, Business, Corporate Governance, Restorative Justice, and Conflict Resolution.
- **Cultural Contrasts**: Ubuntu promotes community over individualism, unlike Western libertarianism.
- **Privacy Perspective**: Viewed as less critical in an Ubuntu-centric ethical framework.

---

### 2.3.6 RAWLS’ THEORY OF JUSTICE
- **Overview**: Developed by John Rawls (1971) as an alternative to utilitarianism; addresses distributive justice.
- **Fundamental Premise**: Society should balance freedom and equality.

##### 2.3.6.1 RAWLS’ THEORY OF JUSTICE – THE ORIGINAL POSITION
- **Concept**: A fair model where citizens are represented as equals.
- **Three Principles**:
  1. **Justice as Fairness**: Equal treatment in information access.
  2. **Contextual Justice**: Recognition of individual entitlements based on needs.
  3. **Addressing Inequality**: Inequalities should not disadvantage marginalized groups.

##### 2.3.6.2 THE VEIL OF IGNORANCE
- **Concept**: Representatives in the original position lack knowledge about social identifiers (race, class, etc.) to ensure unbiased principles.
- **Knowledge Within the Veil**:
  - Understanding of basic human interests and societal conditions.
  
##### 2.3.6.3 LIMITATIONS WITH THE CONCEPT OF JUSTICE
- **Ambiguity**: Justice has multiple interpretations leading to potential contradictions (Perelmann, 1967).
- **Common Justice Definitions**:
  1. Equal share.
  2. Based on merit.
  3. Based on actions.

# Study Notes on Ethical Theories and Decision-Making Tools

## 2.3.6.4 ADVANTAGES OF THE CONCEPT OF JUSTICE
- Justice promotes equal basic liberties and mutual respect, surpassing utilitarianism.
- **Rawls' First Principle**: "Justice as fairness" secures permanent equal liberties, enhancing social harmony.
- Supports liberal democracies striving for equality and resource distribution.
- Valuable in state building contexts.

## 2.4 COMPARISON OF THE THEORIES
- **Moral Motivation**:
   - **Kantianism**: Right action based on duty.
   - **Utilitarianism**: Consequential focus.
   - **Ubuntu**: Community impact orientation.

- **Criteria for Ethics**:
   - Universal rules utilized in Kantianism, universalism, and Ubuntu.

- **Focus**:
   - **Kantianism**: Individual-centric.
   - **Universalism**: All affected parties.
   - **Ubuntu**: Community vs individual care vs duty discussion is emphasized.

- Ethical theories applied to ICT problems amidst everyday moral decisions.
  
## 2.4.1 THE “MOVE” DECISION MAKING TOOL
- Developed by the **African Centre of Excellence for Information Ethics (ACEIE)** for ethical dilemmas.
- Provides structured reasoning but no definitive answers.
  
### ELEMENTS OF THE MOVE MODEL
- **M – Motivation**:
   - **Simple Approach**: Checklist for motivations based on integrity vs. negative emotions.
   - **Proper Approach**: Mean assessment via Virtue Ethics.

- **O – Opportunities and Risks**:
   - Identify stakeholders and analyze all opportunities/risks.
   - Perform a cost-benefit analysis.

- **V – Vortex**:
   - Consider irreversible consequences ('pulling the plug').
   - Assess societal impact of decisions from a utilitarian perspective.

- **E – Effects**:
   - Measure the broad impact on populations and resources.
   - Choose actions with positive outcomes on the greatest number.

### MOVE – Overview
- **M**: Honest testing of motivation.
- **O**: Qualitative evaluation of opportunity/risk distribution.
- **V**: Visualization of societal outcomes of similar decisions.
- **E**: Assessment of overall effects before proceeding with decisions.

## 2.4.2 RISE PRINCIPLES
- **R – Responsibility**: Use platforms for intended purposes; post cautiously.
- **I – Integrity**: Uphold ethical standards online.
- **S – Security**: Secure accounts and avoid phishing; protect personal data.
- **E – Equality**: Respect diverse viewpoints and help others access information.

  - Right to intellectual property: Ownership of data and knowledge.

  - **Human Rights**: Acknowledgment of basic rights; includes privacy and ownership.
  - **Freedom**: Formal (choice enabled) and material (content of choice) freedom.

#### 3.5.1 DEFINING PRIVACY
- Privacy involves access control (physical and informational).
- It creates tension between rights to restrict access and desires for access.
- Privacy is not absolute; exceptions apply (legal searches, etc.).

- **De George (2003:39–48)**: Privacy limits on access to physical, mental, and personal information.
- Philosophical views: 
  - Fundamental right vs. necessary condition for autonomy (Spinello 1994:114).

#### 3.5.2 FORMS OF PRIVACY
- **Bodily Privacy**: Protection against invasive procedures.
- **Privacy of Communications**: Security of messages (mail, email, phone).
- **Territorial Privacy**: Limits on intrusion into personal spaces (searches, surveillance).
- **Information/Data Privacy**: Rules for handling personal data (financial, medical).

- Electronic trails from transactions and interactions recorded in databases.
- Public information: Shared willingly (e.g., telephone directories).
- Personal information: Regarded as private (e.g., religion, medical details).
- Transitions between personal and public information:
  - **Voluntary**: Contest entries, surveys.
  - **Involuntary**: Statutory disclosures (births, marriages).

- **Rewards or Loyalty Programs**: Track purchases (e.g., Clicks, Edgars).

- **Merging Records**: Combining disparate databases to create composite profiles (Tavani 2004:127).
- **Data Mining**: Discovering patterns to form new classifications and insights (De George 2003:165).
