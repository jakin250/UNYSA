## 1. DEFINITION

- **Definition**: A thing is a legal object that is an independent part of the corporeal world, external to humans, subject to human control, and useful/valuable to humans.

### 1.1 Elements of definition

#### 1.1.1 Corporeality
- Law confined to corporeal things (e.g., land, car).
- Exception: Incorporeal rights (e.g., pledge of claims, **National Bank of SA Ltd v Cohen’s Trustee** 1911 AD 235, **Millman NO v Twiggs** 1995 (3) SA 674).
- Technological issues regarding definition (e.g., electricity, **Telkom SA Ltd v Xsinet (Pty) Ltd** 2003 (5) SA 309).

#### 1.1.2 External to humans
- Human body parts excluded as legal objects. 
- Future debates anticipated due to medical technology.
- **National Health Act 61 of 2003** regulates dealings with human body parts.

#### 1.1.3 Independence
- A thing must exist independently as a distinct entity.
- Issues arise with composite things (e.g., car).
- Definition adaptation in sectional titles and land demarcation (**Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986**, **Land Survey Act 8 of 1997**).

#### 1.1.4 Subject to human control
- Objects must have potential for human legal control.
- Non-controllable entities (e.g., Mars, air) cannot be legal objects.

#### 1.1.5 Useful and valuable to humans
- Legal relationships arise from value; trivial things don’t qualify (e.g., individual grains of sand not applicable, but a pile is).

---

## 2. CLASSIFICATION OF THINGS

### 2.1 Criteria for classification
- Classification by relation to humans and inherent nature.

#### 2.1.1 Relation to humans
- Focus on function in legal processes.
  
##### 2.1.1.1 Negotiability
- **Res in commercio**: Negotiable things.
- **Res extra commercium**: Non-negotiable (e.g., res communes omnium, res publicae, body parts).
- Negotiable things can be one’s property (**res alicuius**) or no one’s property (**res nullius**).

#### 2.1.2 Inherent nature
- Classification by characteristics.

##### 2.1.2.1 Singular and composite things
- Singular: Independent items (e.g., horse).
- Composite: Made of parts (e.g., car).
- Distinction: Collections treated as units but don’t lose individuality (e.g., flock of sheep as a legal unit).

- **Principal thing**: Independent entity in a composite.

### 2.1.2.1 Composite and Accessory Things
- **Composite Thing**: Land is the principal; buildings are not (SU 4 para 3.4.2).
- **Accessory Thing**: 
  - Independent existence forfeited via physical attachment to a principal (e.g., brick in a wall) (SU 4 para 3.4.2).
- **Auxiliary Thing**: 
  - Dependent economically but not physically (e.g., key: reliant on lock) (Senekal v Roodt 1983 (2) SA 602 (T)).

### Fruits
- **Definition**: Yield from the principal (e.g., apples, interest).
- **Types**: 
  - Accessory to principal until separated.
  - Natural Fruits (fructus naturales) vs. Civil Fruits (fructus civiles). 
    - Distinctions in natural fruits: 
      - Hanging (fructus pendentes)
      - Separated (fructus separati)
      - Gathered (fructus percepti).

### 2.1.2.2 Movable and Immovable Things
- **Immovable Things**: Land and permanently attached items (plants, buildings).
- **Movable Things**: Transferable without damage (e.g., chairs, cars).

- **Legal Implications**:
  - **Transfer of Ownership**: Movables (delivery); Immovables (registration) (SU 5 para 5).
  - **Statutes**: E.g., Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937; Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981.
  - **Private International Law**: 
    - Movables governed by owner’s domicile (lex loci domicilii).
    - Immovables governed by location (lex loci rei sitae) (Southern Tankers (Pty) Ltd t/a Unilog v Pescana D’’Oro Ltd 2003 (4) SA 566 (C)).
  - **Minor's Estate**: Court permission for alienation of immovables > R100,000.
  - **Judicial Execution**: Movable assets liquidated first for debts.
  - **Criminal Law**: Theft (movables); arson (immovables).
  - **Real Security**: Pledge for movables; mortgage for immovables (SU 11).

### 2.1.2.3 Fungible and Non-fungible Things
- **Fungible Things (res fungibiles)**: Replaceable (e.g., sugar).
- **Non-fungible Things (res non fungibiles)**: Unique (e.g., Picasso painting).

- **Legal Significance**:
  - **Law of Obligations**: Replaceability affects contractual performance.
  - **Pledge**: Basic rule excludes replaceable items.
  - **Transfer of Ownership**: Possible via commixtio or confusio (SU 4 para 4).
  - **Court Attitudes**: More likely to authorize repairs/replacements for fungibles (SU 9).

### 2.1.2.4 Consumable and Non-consumable Things
- **Consumable Things (res consumptibiles)**: Used up by regular use (e.g., food).
- **Non-consumable Things (res non consumptibiles)**: Retain value despite use (e.g., vehicle).

- **Legal Relevance**:
  - **Loan, Lease, Usufruct**: Duty to replace for consumables.
  - **Ownership Transfer**: Consumption confers ownership.
  - **Money**: Classed as consumable.

### 2.1.2.5 Divisible and Indivisible Things
- **Divisible Things**: Can be split without losing essential characteristics (e.g., fabric).
- **Indivisible Things**: Cannot be divided without changing nature (e.g., cars).

- Personal rights arise from obligations (contracts, delicts, unjust enrichment).
- Characterized as:
   - **Creditors**: Hold personal rights against **debtors**.

### 2. THEORETICAL DISTINCTION

#### 2.1 PERSONALIST THEORY

##### 2.1.1 Content
- Real rights enforceable against everyone (absolute).
- Personal rights enforceable only against specific individuals (relative).

##### 2.1.2 Criticism
- Overstates absoluteness of real rights; not applicable in all scenarios (e.g., estoppel).
- Exceptions where personal rights operate absolutely (e.g., service contracts recognized).

#### 2.2 CLASSICAL THEORY

##### 2.2.1 Content
- Real rights pertain to person-thing relationships (direct control).
- Personal rights pertain to person-person relationships (claim for performance).

##### 2.2.2 Criticism
- Difficulty defining “direct relationship with a thing.”
- Personal rights can also concern tangible objects.

### 3. DISTINGUISHING CHARACTERISTICS

- **Characteristics of Real Rights**:
  - Object: Corporeal thing.  
  - Content: Direct claim and disposal over the thing.  
  - Remedies: Enforced through real actions (e.g., **rei vindicatio**).

- **Characteristics of Personal Rights**:
  - Object: Performance (to give, do, or abstain from doing).
  - Content: Claim for performance from a specific person.
  - Remedies: Enforced through personal actions (e.g., **condictio furtiva**).

# Study Notes on Real Rights vs. Personal Rights

## 1. Origins
- **Real Rights**: Originates from legal facts (e.g., delivery of movable things, registration of land, accession, prescription).
- **Personal Rights**: Emerge through obligations (e.g., contracts, delicts).

## 2. Absoluteness
- **Real Rights**: Absolute in principle; vindication against any possessor.
- **Personal Rights**: Relative; enforced only against obligors.

## 3. Preference
- Real rights have preference in insolvency.
- "First in time is stronger in law" applies to competing real rights.
- This principle generally does not apply to personal rights.

## 4. Publicity
- Creation, transfer, or extinction of real rights requires publicity (e.g., delivery for movable property, registration for immovable property).
- Essential for acknowledgment by the public.

## 5. Distinctive Characteristics
- Characteristics assist in identifying real vs. personal rights but are not exclusive.
- **Real Right Definition**: Lawful relationship granting direct control over a thing, binding all legal subjects to respect this relationship.

## 6. Subtraction from Dominion (Ownership) Test
- **Legal Test**: Identify if an obligation is a burden on the land (real right) or merely on a person (personal right).
  - **Real Right**: Obligation burdens land, registrable.
  - **Personal Right**: Generic obligation, not registrable.
  
### Example: Ex parte Geldenhuys (1926 OPD 155)
- **Facts**: A mutual will concerning farm division upon death.
- **Legal Question**: Characterization of rights created by will provisions.
- **Ratio Decidendi**: Focus on the burden of obligation to determine real vs. personal rights.
- **Finding**: Provisions creating real rights were registrable; payments to specific heirs classified as personal rights.

## 7. Categories of Real Rights
- **Ownership**: Real right to one's own thing (ius in re propria).
- **Limited Real Rights**: Real rights to another's thing (iura in re aliena).
  - Ownership is comprehensive; limited rights are defined and restricted.
  
### Impact of Ownership vs. Limited Real Rights
- **Ownership**: Complete freedom of use.
- **Limited Rights (e.g., usufruct)**: Defined entitlements restricted from inception.

### Legislation Referenced
- **Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937**: Section 63 outlines registration criteria for real vs. personal rights.

# S T U D Y U N I T 3
**OWNERSHIP: DEFINITION AND LIMITATIONS ON OWNERSHIP**

## 1. Definition of ownership

### 1.1 Introduction
- Ownership is the most comprehensive real right regarding a thing.
- Definition reflects freedom to act on one's property, but limited by law and rights of others.
- Ownership varies across societies, affecting its nature and content.
- Distinction: 
  - **Ius in re propria**: ownership (right to one's own thing)
  - **Iura in re aliena**: limited real rights (rights to another's things).

### 1.2 Inviolability
- Inviolability principle: ownership cannot be lost without consent.
- Roman law maxim: *nemo plus iuris in alium transferre potest quam ipse haberet*.
- Example: If X sells Y's property to Z, Z does not acquire ownership; Y can reclaim through *rei vindicatio*.

### 1.3 Inherent nature
- Ownership defined as the most comprehensive real right.
- Comprehensive nature allows enforcement against the world; entitlements extend broadly.
- Limitations exist by law and rights of others.

### 1.4 Entitlements
- Entitlements arise from ownership: capacity to perform acts regarding the property.
- Typically classified as "entitlements" rather than "powers."
- Major entitlements include:
  - **Use and enjoy**: Entitlement to utilize the property.
  - **Fruits**: Right to benefit from the property (ex: collect rent).
  - **Control**: Physical possession of the thing.
  - **Consume or destroy**: Right to dispose of the property.
  - **Alienate**: Entitlement to transfer ownership.
  - **Burden**: Ability to grant limited rights over the property.
  - **Vindicate**: Right to reclaim property from unlawful possession.

### 1.4.1 Use and enjoy
- Primary entitlement is to use and benefit from one’s property (ex: driving a car).
- Burdening property (ex: servitudes) limits this entitlement.

### 1.4.2 Fruits
- Right to natural and civil fruits from ownership.

## 2.2 Elements

### 2.2.1 Control
- **Physical Control**: Essential for acquisition of ownership via *appropriation (occupatio)*; must be intentional.
- **Lawfulness of Control**: Control can be unlawful; e.g., illegal hunting may still result in ownership (e.g., *R v Mafohla 1958*).
- **Wounded Animals**: If not physically controlled post-wound, appropriation does not occur.

### 2.2.2 Thing which does not belong to anyone
- **Res Nullius**: Things that belong to no one, including wild creatures in a natural state or reverted state (e.g., fish, animals).
- **Exceptions**:
  - Tamed animals remain owned until they lose their domestic habits.
  - Domestic animals not considered res nullius (e.g., Game Theft Act 105 of 1991).
- **Abandoned Things (res derelictae)**: Rightful abandoned property becomes res nullius and can be appropriated (*Reck v Mills 1990*).
- **Lost Things (res deperditae)**: Remain the owner's property unless intention to retain is lost.

### 2.2.3 Thing within sphere of law
- Ownership not acquireable for body parts or corps, reflecting legal limitations.

### 2.2.4 Intention of becoming owner
- *Underwater Construction and Salvage Co (Pty) Ltd v Bell (1968)* - ownership acquired with seizure intending ownership; physical control serves as an indicator of intention.

## 3 ACCESSION (ACCESSIO)

### 3.1 Definition
- Accession defined as acquiring ownership when an accessory merges with a principal thing, forming one entity; owner of the principal owns the whole.

### 3.2 Introduction
- Owner of principal gains ownership of accessory, accessory loses independence.
- Determining principal vs. accessory can focus on weight or value; generally, land is principal.

### 3.3 Accession of immovables to immovables
- Distinction between natural boundary (e.g., water) and artificially fixed boundary.

#### 3.3.1 Invisible Accretion (Alluvio)
- **Definition**: Gradual addition of land through water's natural action.
- **Elements**:
  - Gradual, imperceptible addition.
  - Occurs via natural water action.
  - Only applicable where property boundary is a river or sea.

#### 3.3.2 Visible Accretion (Avulsio)
- **Definition**: Ownership acquired by sudden land addition from external violent forces.
- **Elements**:
  - Sudden substantial increase.
  - Results from violent natural causes (e.g., floods).
  - Dislodged from one property to another; mature plants indicate completion.

#### 3.3.3 Island Arising in Riverbed (Insula nata in flumine)
- **Definition**: Ownership of newly formed island in a river is proportionate among riparian owners.
- **Elements**: Formation parallels alluvio principles.

## Chapter 9: Acquisitive Prescription
### 9.1 Definition
- **Acquisitive Prescription**: Ownership acquired by open control of property for an uninterrupted period of thirty years.

### 9.2 Introduction
- Contrast with **Extinctive Prescription**: Both processes recognized by law; former in acquisition, latter in enforcement.

### 9.3 Requirements
- **Prescription Act 18 of 1943**: Defines acquisition of ownership via possession for thirty years, **nec vi, nec clam, nec precario**.
- **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**: Similar definition, with focus on open possession.

### 9.4 Elements
- **(i) Possession**: 
   - Physical (corpus) + Mental (animus) elements.
   - Objective control and intention of ownership validated by reasonable inference.
  
- **(ii) Openly**:
   - Corresponds with not by force (nec vi), not secretly (nec clam), not on sufferance (nec precario).
  
- **(iii) As if he/she were the owner**:
   - External conduct reflecting animus domini.

- **(iv) For an uninterrupted period of thirty years**:
   - **(a)** Calculation includes predecessors (i.e., **Stephenson v Lamsley**).
   - **(b)** Continuity not absolute (i.e., **Morkels Transport v Melrose Foods**).
   - **(c)** Continuity disturbance analyzed through interruption or suspension.

### A. Interruption
- Prescription period resets upon interruption, defined as:
  - **(i) Natural Interruption**: Loss of possession required; voluntary or involuntary.
  - **(ii) Judicial Interruption**: Serving process (per **Section 4 of the 1969 Prescription Act**) interrupts running until final judgment; temporary unless successful.

### B. Suspension
- Temporary halting of prescription without lapse:
   - Applies to individuals unable to assert rights (i.e., minors, insane individuals, etc.).
   - **Section 3 of the 1969 Act**: Completion of prescription postponed for three years following removal of impediment.

  - Concept confirmed in related case law.
  
- **Application in Case**: Groenewald claimed ownership of a threshing machine sold by D. D's involvement and Groenewald's threat to sue positioned him to exercise control.

### 4.2.2 Shorta Manu (Delivery with the Short Hand)
- **Definition**:
  - No physical transfer occurs; transferee already has control.
  
- **Case Reference**: **Info Plus v Scheelke (1998 (3) SA 184)**
  - Ownership remains with the seller until payment completed.
  - Clear indication of the intention to transfer ownership is necessary.
  
- Without further transfer of control needed as transferee was already in possession.

### 4.2.3 Applicability of Rei Vindicatio
- **Concept**:
  - Legal action to reclaim ownership (rei vindicatio) must demonstrate:
    - Proof of ownership.
    - Current possession of the item by another party.
    
- **Implications**:
  - If control requirements are unmet, rei vindicatio may fail.

### 4.2.4 Constitutium Possessorium
- **Definition**:
  - Transfer of possession where another party exercises physical control.
  
- **Example**: Sale of a dry-cleaning business which involved delayed ownership transfer until full payment.

  - Ownership (based on valid transfer).

# Study Notes on Constitutum Possessorium and Attornment 

## Chapter 4: Ownership Transfer

### 4.1 Constitutum Possessorium  
- **Definition**: Transfer of ownership where the transferor retains physical control but changes intention towards the thing.  
- **Key Case**: *Vasco Dry Cleaners v Twycross* (1979 (1) SA 603 (A)) establishes the nature of the transfer.
- **Legislation**: No explicit legislation mentioned, but principles derived from case law.
- **Fraud Risk**: 
  - Possibility of misleading creditors through claims of ownership transfer.
  - Courts scrutinize assertions of ownership based on intention change alone.
- **Safeguards from *Goldinger’s Trustee v Whitelaw and Son* (1917 AD 66)**:
  - Constitutum not presumed; must show clear supporting facts.
  - Distinct causa detentionis required; mere change of mind does not effect transfer.
- **Findings in *Vasco Dry Cleaners v Twycross***:
  - Ownership not transferred merely by mental change.
  - Court determined transaction primarily akin to a pledge, not a sale.
  - Required delivery of control for valid pledge; constitutum lacks this.
  - Twycross cannot reclaim machinery since ownership never passed.
  
### 4.2 Attornment  
- **Definition**: Transfer of ownership where transferor, transferee, and third party agree that the third party will control the property on behalf of the transferee.  
- **Requirements**:
  - **Tripartite Agreement**: Agreement among transferor, transferee, and holder.
  - **Physical Control**: Holder must exercise control at the moment of transfer.
- **Case Law**: 
  - *Air-Kel (Edms) Bpk h/a Merkel Motors v Bodenstein* (1980 (3) SA 917 (A)) illustrates requirements for attornment.
  - *Caledon en Suid-Westelike Distrikte Eksekuteurskamer Bpk v Wentzel* (1972 (1) SA 270 (A)) extended principles, allowing holder's prior declaration of intent for future ownership holding.
  
### 4.3 Conclusion  
- Ownership of movable things can only be transferred if the holder controls the item; loss of control nullifies transfer capability through attornment.

**Chapter 2: PROPERTY LAW REMEDIES**

### 2.1 Rei Vindicatio
- **Definition**: A real action for an owner to reclaim unlawfully possessed property, applicable to movable and immovable things.
  
- **Principles**:
  - Inviolability of ownership: Ownership cannot be lost without consent.
  - No one can transfer greater rights than they possess (nemo plus iuris).

- **Key Case**: *Chetty v Naidoo (1974 (3) SA 13 (A))*
  - Establishes the owner's right to reclaim possession of property.

### 2.1.1 Requirements for Rei Vindicatio
1. **Ownership**: Plaintiff must prove ownership.
2. **Existence & Identifiability of the Thing**:
   - Item must exist in identifiable form; changes (accession/mixing) may affect recovery.
3. **Defendant's Control**: Defendant must be in unlawful control of the item at the time of action.

### 2.1.2 Defences and Restrictions
- **Defence Types**:
  - **Requirement Non-Fulfillment**: Defendant may show lack of ownership or destruction/unidentifiability of the item.
  
#### 2.1.3 Sales in Execution
- **Legislation**: Section 70 of the *Magistrates Courts Act 32 of 1944*
  - Protects bona fide purchasers against claims by true owners post-sale.

#### 2.1.4 Statutory Limitations on Eviction
- **Constitution**: Section 26(3) protects against eviction without court order (Case: *Jaftha v Schoeman*).
- Certain statutes provide additional protections for vulnerable groups (e.g., *Land Reform Act*, *Extension of Security of Tenure Act*).

#### 2.1.5 Estoppel
- **Definition**: Defence against rei vindicatio where the owner’s conduct leads third parties to believe in another's ownership.
- **Requirements**:
  1. **Representation**: Must have been made by the owner.
  2. **Fault**: Owner must have acted with intent/negligence.
  3. **Detriment**: Third party must show detrimental reliance on the representation.
  4. **Causal Connection**: Proof that reliance caused detriment.

- **Key Cases**:
  - *Oakland Nominees (Pty) Ltd v Gelria Mining & Investment Co (Pty) Ltd (1976 (1) SA 441 (A))*
  - *Quenty’s Motors (Pty) Ltd v Standard Credit Corporation Ltd (1994 (3) SA 188 (A))*

### 2.2 Interdict
- **Definition**: Summary court order for urgent relief against infringement or imminent infringement of rights.

### 2.2.1 Requirements for an Interdict
- Clear right: The applicant must show ownership or rights.
- Actual or reasonably apprehended harm: Must demonstrate likelihood of rights violation.
- Absence of other remedies: No other effective legal protections available.

- **Key Case**: *Setlogelo v Setlogelo (1914 AD 221)*
  - Established requirements for claiming an interdict.

### 3.1 Condictio Furtiva
- **Definition**: Action for recovery of stolen property from the thief or their heirs.

  - Causal link between loss and conduct.

## POSSESSION AND HOLDERSHIP  

### 1. Introduction  
- Concept of "possession" is complex, comprising a physical element and a mental state.  
- Broad sense includes possession (unlawful) and holdership (lawful/unlawful).  
- Possession = physical control (corpus) + intention of owner (animus).  
- Lawful possession occurs when ownership exists; unlawful possession exemplified by a thief.  

### 1.1 Physical element (corpus)  
- Refers to physical/control by the possessor or holder. 
- Legal criteria determine the significance of physical control; circumstances and nature of the relationship are considered.  

#### 1.1.1 Direct physical control  
- Actual control judged by physical criteria; e.g., holding a pen or sitting on a chair (Scholtz v Faifer 1910 TS 243; Nienaber v Stuckey 1946 AD 1049).  

#### 1.1.2 Indirect physical control  
- Situations where direct control isn’t feasible; e.g., owning a chair while at work (Welgemoed v Coetzer 1946 TPD 701).  

#### 1.1.3 Acquisition of control  
- Stricter criteria apply for establishing physical control for ownership transfer versus maintenance of control.  
- Buyer must gain direct control for effective delivery, then may retain through indirect means (Nienaber v Stuckey 1946 AD 1049; R v Mafohla 1958 (2) SA 373).  

#### 1.1.4 Personal exercise of control  
- Control can be exercised by one for another (as a representative); distinction crucial between control for another vs personal interests (Strydom v De Lange 1970 (2) SA 6).  

#### 1.1.5 Continuous control  
- Continuous control not always required; ability to regain control is significant (Scholtz v Faifer 1910 TS 243; Nienaber v Stuckey 1946 AD 1049).  
- Nature of object and use matter in determining continuous control (Nienaber v Stuckey; Scholtz v Faifer).  

### 2. Possession – fact or right?  
- Distinguishing the nature of possession as either a factual state or a legal entitlement.  

### 3. Ius possessionis and ius possidendi  
- **Ius possessionis**: Right to possess (factual).  
- **Ius possidendi**: Right to hold (legal).  

### 4. Possession and presumption of ownership  
- Importance of possession in establishing presumptions regarding ownership rights.

## 1.1.6 Degree of contact
- Greater contact required for movable things than immovables.

## 1.2 Mental element (animus)
- Intention (animus) essential for establishing legal relationship.
- Requirements:
  - Capacity to form legal intention.
  - Awareness of relationship with the thing.
  - Formation and maintenance of intention regarding control.
- Forms of intention discussed:
  - **Animus domini** (intention of ownership).
  - **Animus ex re commodum acquirendi** (intention to derive benefit).

### 1.2.1 Intention of owner (animus domini)
- Control over a thing with intentions of an owner; signifies ownership assumption.
- Non-owners can possess animus domini under certain conditions.
- Groups:
  1. **Owners**: Legal owners perceive and assert ownership.
  2. **Bona fide possessors**: Incorrectly assume ownership (opinio domini); unaware of true ownership status. Examples include:
     - Purchaser from a non-owner.
     - Accidental possessor.
     - Neighbor encroachment.
  3. **Mala fide possessors**: Aware of non-ownership but assert control illegitimately; typical example: a thief. They recognize ownership exclusion.

### 1.2.2 Intention to derive a benefit (animus ex re commodum acquirendi)
- Control with the intent to benefit without ownership assumption; recognizes true ownership.
- Legal grounds for control (e.g., permissions, contracts).
- Groups:
  1. **Lawful holders**: Control with owner’s consent; examples include:
     - Tenants, borrowers, credit purchasers, pledgees.
  2. **Unlawful holders**:
     - **Bona fide unlawful holders**: Unaware of illegitimacy (e.g., invalid lease).
     - **Mala fide unlawful holders**: Aware of the absence of owner’s consent; e.g., tenant after lease expiration.

## 2 POSSESSION – FACT OR RIGHT?
- Debate whether possession is a fact or a real right.
- Roman-Dutch law: Possession recognized via actions/possession interdicts.
- Modern law: Provides protection, even to mala fide possessors (spoliation remedy).

## 3 IUS POSSESSIONIS AND IUS POSSIDENDI
- **Ius possessionis**: Right of possession, recognized legally (includes thieves).
- **Ius possidendi**: Legally recognized right to possess; not applicable to thieves. 
- Example: Buyer before delivery has ius possidendi; lessees have ius possidendi.

## 4 POSSESSION AND PRESUMPTION OF OWNERSHIP
- Assertion of possession (ius possessionis) does not assure ius possidendi.
- Burden on third parties to prove ownership.
- Rebuttable presumption: Controller presumed owner unless contrary evidence provided.

#### 2.2.1 Purpose
- Aims to protect the legal order; prevents self-help leading to breaches of peace.

#### 2.2.2 Definition

### 2.3 Defences Against Spoliation Remedy
1. Applicant lacked peaceful and undisturbed control.
2. Respondent did not disturb control.
3. Disturbance was lawful (e.g., court order).
4. Applicant delayed application excessively (over 1 year).
5. Impossibility to restore control (cases: *Fredericks v Stellenbosch Divisional Council*, *Ierse Trog CC v Sulra Trading CC*, *Rikhotso v Northcliff Ceramics (Pty) Ltd*).

  - Respondent’s prior control over the implements at destruction.
  
- **Example Context**
  - S leases farm equipment; X (father) destroys S’s rented equipment.
  - Remedy: Possessory action to reclaim control or seek damages.

## 2.5 Condictio Furtiva
- **Purpose:** Action for recovery against the thief or representative post-death.
- **Applicability:** Can be initiated by an owner or lawful interest holder (Clifford v Farinha).
  
- **Requirements to Succeed**
  1. Proof of ownership or lawful interest from date of theft to action date.
  2. Evidence of theft or deceitful removal.
  3. If against an heir, proof of status as heir of the thief.

## 2.6 Aquilian Action
- **Definition:** Delictual remedy to recover damages from unlawful impairment of ownership.
- **Eligibility:** Primarily for owners, but non-owners with patrimonial interest may also claim.
  
- **Extension of Remedy**
  - Available to bona fide possessors and lawful holders (Smit v Saipem).

- **Example Context**
  - Unauthorized use of S’s equipment causes irreparable damage.
  
- **Requirements to Succeed**
  - Demonstrated actual loss resulting from another's culpable damage.

---

## 2.7 Enrichment Action
- **Basis:** Recovery for unjustified enrichment when one improves another's property without legal cause.
  
- **Types of Improvements**
  1. **Essential Expenses (impensae necessariae):** Necessary for preservation.
  2. **Useful Expenses (impensae utiles):** Increase market value.
  3. **Luxurious Expenses (impensae voluptuariae):** No legal recovery availed.

- **Eligibility:** For possessors and holders; not applicable to luxurious expenses.
- **Recovery Basis:** Lesser of owner's enrichment or plaintiff's impoverishment; includes possible removal of improvements (ius tollendi).

## Termination of Possession and Holdership
- **Methods of Termination**
  1. Death of possessor or holder.
  2. Destruction of the thing.
  3. Termination due to:
     - Loss of physical control.
     - Loss of mental element (intention to control/benefit).

- Limited real right providing specific enjoyments of another’s property.

### 6.2.3 Dwelling (Habitatio)

### 1. Introduction  
- Real security rights = limited real rights; includes pledge, mortgage, security through claims, tacit hypothecs, liens, judicial pledge.  
- **Real security rights vs. Personal security**:  
  - Real security: limited real rights over property (iura in re aliena).  
  - Personal security: surety binds personally to creditor.  
  - Real security holders = limited right over security object; personal security holders = personal rights against surety.  

### 1.1 Real Security Rights and Personal Security  
- Real security replaces the need for suretyship in credit security.  
- Distinction between creditor's rights in personal security vs. real security: property law focuses on real security rights.

### 1.2 Real Security Rights  
#### 1.2.1 Limited Real Rights  
- Creditor obtains limited real right over a thing (security object) as security for principal debt.  

#### 1.2.2 Conventional and Tacit Real Security Rights  
- **Conventional**: Based on agreement; requires registration.  
- **Tacit**: Arises by law, without agreement; termed legal mortgages.  

#### 1.2.3 Preferential Claim  
- Strengthens creditor’s position amidst insolvency (concursus creditorum).  
- Preferential claim allows creditor to satisfy debt from security before other creditors.  

#### 1.2.4 Legal Acts Involved in Creation of Real Security Right  
- Typically involves:  
  - Loan contract: establishes principal debt.  
  - Security contract: stipulates nature of the security (e.g., pledge or mortgage).  
  - Constitution (vesting) of limited real right: may involve physical delivery with intent to pledge.  
- Reference: Millman NO v Twiggs 1995 (3) SA 674 (A).

#### 1.2.5 Accessory Nature  
- Real security rights cannot exist without a principal debt.   
- The discharge of principal debt extinguishes security right (ipso iure).  
- Reference: Thienhaus v Metje & Ziegler Ltd 1965 (3) SA 25 (A); Kilburn v Estate Kilburn 1931 AD 501 506.  

#### 1.2.6 Security Object  
- Movable or immovable things may serve as security.  
- Indivisible; secures entire claim (principal debt).  

#### 1.2.7 Different Meanings of ‘‘Mortgage’’  
- Mortgage refers generically to real security rights (pledge, mortgage, hypothec).  
- Mortgage bond: document embodying terms of mortgage.

### 2. Pledge  
#### 2.1 Definition  
- Pledge involves physical delivery of property as security.  

#### 2.2 Constitution/Vesting  
- Requires an agreement and physical delivery or transfer of possession.  

#### 2.3 Clauses in Pledge Agreement  
- **Summary Execution**: Allows creditor to enforce the pledge without court order (parate executie).  
- **Retention Clause**: Allows pledgee to keep the thing if the pledgor defaults (pactum commissorium).  
- **Purchase Clause**: Pledgee may buy the pledged item at a specified price.  
- **Redemption Clause**: Pledge cannot be redeemed under certain conditions.  
- **Pactum Antichresis**: Pledge for utility or profits derived from the property.  

#### 2.4 Rights and Duties of Parties  
- **Pledgee**: Rights include holding the property until debt satisfaction; duties include care of the property.  
- **Pledgor**: Rights include reclaiming property upon satisfaction of the debt; obligations to fulfill terms of the pledge.

- Pledge extinguished upon repayment of principal debt or mutual agreement.

## 2.4 Rights and Duties of Parties

### 2.4.1 Pledgee
- **Limited Real Right**: Pledgee has a right to keep the pledged article until the debt is paid.
- **Recovery of Control**: Can claim via **real actio quasi serviana** from anyone in control, including bona fide purchasers.
- **Spoliation Remedy**: Can use against unlawful removal of control.
- **Involuntary Loss**: If control is lost through chance, cannot recover the item (**mobilia non habent sequelam ex causa hypotheca**).
- **Judgment and Sale**: Pledgee can obtain judgment to sell the pledged item to cover debt, paying surplus to pledgor.
- **Preference in Insolvency**: Under **s83, Insolvency Act 24 of 1936**, enjoys preference from proceeds of sale.
- **Fruits and Offspring**: Pledge extends to fruits and offspring of pledged items.
- **Expense Recovery**: Can recover necessary maintenance expenses via **actio pigneraticia contraria** (Acton v Motau, 1909).
- **Care Obligation**: Must take reasonable care (bonus paterfamilias) and liable for fault damage but not for chance damage (Nyabele v Pieterse, 1914).

### 2.4.2 Pledgor
- **Security Requirement**: Can require pledgee to refrain from unauthorized use.
- **Action to Reclaim**: After debt payment, can reclaim pledged item via **actio pigneraticia directa** or **rei vindicatio**.
- **Expense Reimbursement**: Obliged to refund pledgee for necessary maintenance expenses incurred.

  5. Novation of principal debt
  6. Merger (pledgee becomes owner)
  7. Prescription
  8. Voluntary loss of control
  9. Court order

### 3.1 Definition
- A mortgage is a limited real right over mortgagor's property to secure debt repayment.

### 3.2 Kinds of Mortgages

### 3.3 Kinds of Conventional or Express Mortgages

#### 3.3.1 Notarial Bond
##### 3.3.1.1 Introduction
- Registered bond over movable property. 
- Governs differences across provinces pre-Security by Means of Movable Property Act 57 of 1993.

##### 3.3.1.2 Definition

##### 3.3.1.4 Legal Operation

- **Special Notarial Bond**: Carries real security right upon registration, secured creditor status, immediate realization rights under **s83, Insolvency Act 24 of 1936**.

- General and special notarial bonds do not create real security rights over movable property without a perfection clause (1).

- Control can be acquired if the mortgagor agrees (1) or via court order (1).

## Case Scenario Analysis
- Z contests the validity of S’s special notarial bond due to improper asset identification in the bond.
- Reference: Section 1(1) of the Security by Means of Movable Property Act 57 of 1993; **Ikea Trading Und Design v BOE Bank** (2005 (2) SA 7 (SCA)).

## 3.3.2 Special Mortgage Over Immovable Things
- A mortgage secures payment; the mortgagee can sell the property without court intervention if the debt is unpaid.

### Endorsement Practice
- A special mortgage is only valid on specific immovable property.
- **Standard Bank van SA v Breitenbach** (1977(1) SA 151 (T)): Endorsement on the title deed not required for registration as per Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937.

## 3.3.3 Kustingbrief
- Defined as a special mortgage over immovable property to secure a debt incurred for its purchase; registered simultaneously with the transfer deed.

## 3.3.4 Covering Bond
- A special mortgage securing future debt; must declare future debt and stipulate maximum amount.

## 3.3.5 Participation Bond

## 3.4 Operation of Special Mortgage Over Immovables
- Mortgagee acquires a limited real right; mortgagor retains control without mortgagee consent for further encumbrances, but may not establish additional real rights without consent.
- Mortgagee’s rights extend to attachments (Venter v Graham and Muller (1906) 23 SC 729).
- Mortgage extinguished upon debt discharge; foreclosure process if default occurs.

## Chapter 3: Liens (Rights of Retention)

### General Definition of Liens
- **Liens:** Limited real rights to secure claims from work/money spent on another's property.
- **Categories:**
  - **Debtor-and-Creditor Liens:** Arise from agreements; personal rights.
  - **Enrichment Liens:** Arise from unjust enrichment; enforceable against third parties.

### Types of Liens
1. **Salvage Liens:** Secured for essential expenses preserving property.
2. **Improvement Liens:** Secured for useful expenses enhancing market value.
3. **Luxurious Expenses:** Not covered by unjustified enrichment; require a contract.

### Key Principles from Case Law
- **Buzzard Electrical (Pty) Ltd v 158 Jan Smuts Avenue Investments (Pty) Ltd (1996):**
  - Enrichment lien requires proof of unjust enrichment and expense incurred by lienholder.
- Requirements re-emphasized:
  - Existence of a principal debt due to unjust enrichment essential for lien validity.
  - Enrichment liability must arise at the expense of the lienholder.

### Conclusion
- **Lan's Security:** Liens secure outstanding claims, distinguished by their basis (enrichment vs. contract).
- **Physical Control:** Loss of possession negates lien; restoration possible upon unlawful deprivation.

# MINERAL RIGHTS

## 3.1 Common law
- Owner of land is also owner of minerals until extraction or separation.  
- Mineral rights entitle holder to prospect and mine minerals unless reserved/transferred.  
- Can be severed via separate title or notarial cession.  
- Considered "quasi servitudes", a unique class of real rights (sui generis).  
- In **Agri South Africa v Minister of Minerals and Energy** (2013 (4) SA 1 (CC)), the right was defined as a limited real right allowing search and removal of minerals.

## 3.2 Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002
- Section 3 of the Act: all mineral and petroleum resources vested in the State as custodian for South African people.  
- Abolishes common law rule of landowner's ownership of unsevered minerals; interpretation of State ownership is open.  
- Act does not define mineral rights but regulates prospecting and mining rights.  
- Rights must be applied for; granted by State; subject to State control for maximum exploitation and utility.  
- "Old order" rights terminate if not converted within prescribed transitional period.   
- "Unused" rights expire if not applied for in transitional period; period has lapsed.  
- Prospecting/mining rights are limited real rights; transfer requires Minister's consent.  
- Rights and duties defined within the Act.  
- Applications must be lodged in prescribed manner at Regional Manager’s office.  
- Prospecting rights: max 5 years, renewable for 3 years; Mining rights: max 30 years, renewable for 30 years at a time.  
- Repeals Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937 regarding mineral rights registration; abolished registration in Deeds Office.  

### Ownership of minerals
- **MPRDA** abolishes cuius est solum principle.  
- Sec 3(1): minerals and petroleum as common heritage.  
- State as custodian, regulates access and ensures exploitation.  
- Prospecting/mining rights require DMR application, are limited real rights, require Minister's consent for transfer.  
- Right of access, to prospect, mine, and remove minerals.

# LESSEE’S (TENANT’S) RIGHTS

## 1. Introduction
- Legal principle: **"huur gaat voor koop"** (lease overrides sale).
- Developed from:
  - **Johannesburg Municipal Council v Rand Townships Registrar** (1910)
  - **Breytenbach v Frankel** (1913)
  - **Graham v Local and Overseas Investments** (1942)
  - **Genna-Wae Properties (Pty) Ltd v Medio-Tronics (Natal) (Pty) Ltd** (1995)
  - **Spearhead Property Holdings Ltd v E & D Motors (Pty) Ltd** (2010).

## 2. Applicable Only to Houses and Land
- Rule originated for land but applies to movable property.

## 3. Establishment of Lessee’s (Tenant’s) Real Right
- Personal rights for contractual enforcement inter partes.
- Control or registration required for limited real rights.

### 3.1 Long Leases
- Defined as leases for 10 years or more.
- Registration required for enforcement against successors.

**General Rules:**
1. Successors under lucrative title bound for full lease period.
2. Successors under onerous title bound if:
   - Registered, or
   - They had knowledge of the lease.
   - For the first 10 years if unregistered and unaware but lessee in control.

- **Kessoopersadh v Essop** (1970): Common law not amended; registration remains necessary under **Formalities in Respect of Leases of Land Act 18 of 1969**.

### 3.2 Short Leases
#### 3.2.1 General
- Short-term lessees without control hold personal rights only.
  
#### 3.2.2 Gratuitous and Onerous Successors
- Lessees may maintain leases against gratuitous successors irrespective of notice.
- Cannot maintain against onerous successors.

*Diagram Reference:* 
- **Diagram 28:** Lease overrides sale (‘‘huur gaat voor koop’’).

## 4. Effect of Lessee’s (Tenant’s) Real Right
- Lessee’s rights prioritized over subsequent vested rights, including mortgages.
- Transfers of property do not transfer contractual duties.
  - New owners cannot refuse to recognize lessee’s rights but do not automatically inherit obligations.

- Court stance: Upon property transfer, new owners assume rent obligations and lessor duties per **Genna-Wae Properties (Pty) Ltd v Medio-Tronics (Natal) (Pty) Ltd** (1995).

### 2. PROPERTY CLAUSE
- **Section 25 Summary:**
  - (1) No arbitrary deprivation of property; must be lawful.
  - (2) Expropriation only for public purpose, with compensation.
  - (3) Compensation aspects: equitable balance, market value, etc.
  - (4) “Property” includes more than just land; covers various rights.

### 2.1 MEANING OF “PROPERTY” IN TERMS OF SECTION 25
- Definition extends beyond traditional tangible assets.
- Includes:
  - Ownership and limited real rights.
  - Intangible and immaterial rights (e.g., copyright).
  - Customary land rights and new property rights.
  
- **Key Cases:**
  - **Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd v MEC for Economic Development**: A commercial trading license recognized as property.
  - **National Credit Regulator v Opperman**: Right to recover money identified under property.
  
- **Constitutional Interpretation:** Requires extensive view of "property."

### 2.2 DEPRIVATION
- Deprivation differs from expropriation; may not involve taking away property.
- Terms associated: regulation, limitation, restriction.
- **Section 25(1)**: No deprivation without law; must not be arbitrary.

- **Examples**: State regulations on land use, planning legislation.
  
- **Validity of Deprivation**:
  1. Must be lawful under general application.
  2. Must not be arbitrary as determined in **First National Bank case**.

### 2.3 EXPROPRIATION
- Expropriation defined under **Section 25(2)**:
  - Involves actual acquisition of property by the State.
  - Mandatory compensation payable, reflecting public purpose.
