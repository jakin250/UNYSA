### Necessity for Enrichment Liability
- **Justification for Enrichment**: Essential to address situations where assets are obtained without legal grounds.
- **Examples in South African Law**: 
    - **Accessio**: Improvements on property lead to unjust enrichment of the landowner at the occupant's expense.
    - **Transfer of Ownership**: Mistaken belief in valid sales leading to unjust transfers.

### Constitutional Framework
- **Section 25 of the Constitution**: Protects property from arbitrary deprivation unless justified by law with compensation. 
  - Includes broad interpretation of "property" to cover assets beyond land.
  - **Case Reference**: *National Credit Regulator v Opperman and Others 2013 2 BCLR 170 (CC)*.

### Concepts of Good Faith
- **Relation to Good Faith**: Essential in the context of Ubuntu, public policy, and fairness in contract and enrichment law.
- **Case Reference**: *Everfresh Market Virginia (Pty) Ltd v Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd 2012 1 SA 256 (CC)* highlights incorporation of Ubuntu into legal interpretations.

### Historical Development
- **Roman and Roman-Dutch Law**: Root of South African enrichment law with minimal influence from English Common Law.
- **Roman Law Sources**: 
  - *D 12 6 14*: Prohibition against unjust enrichment.
  - *D 50 17 206*: Natural law equity discouraging profiting at another’s expense.
  
- **Historical Shifts**: Evolution from specific enrichment actions to an emerging general enrichment action recognized under South African law.

### Judicial Recognition of Enrichment Actions
- **Distinct Judicial Opinions**: Some courts have highlighted an absence of general principle yet acknowledged principles of unjustified enrichment as sources for obligations.
- **Key Cases**: 
  - *McCarthy Retail Ltd v Shortdistance Carriers CC 2001 3 SA 482 (SCA)* recognizes piecemeal development of enrichment liability without unequivocal general action.

### Ongoing Developments

## STUDY UNIT 1: General overview (Page 9)

- **General Action**: 
  - Mediaeval writers and Grotius did not recognize an independent action for unjustified enrichment.
  - Notable influence from Spanish jurists noted; Grotius possibly viewed unjustified enrichment as a source of obligations akin to contract/delict.
  - Schrage (ed.) *Unjust Enrichment: The Comparative Legal History of the Law of Restitution* provides insights into Grotius’ stance.

- **18th Century Developments**:
  - By the 18th century, the **Hooge Raad** accepted the general enrichment action. 
  - Individual judges differed in description; significant impact of unreported decisions by Bynkershoek and Pauw noted.
  - The common-law basis for recognizing general enrichment action exists, although incorrect in **Nortjé’s case**.

- **Current South African Enrichment Law**:
  - No general enrichment action but general requirements applicable to specific enrichment acts.
  - Existing actions can extend to new situations; potential development of a general enrichment action alongside existing ones.
  - Tendency noted in case law to prioritize general principles over specific actions (Eiselen and Pienaar, Unjustified enrichment 3–4).

- **Principles vs. Actions**:
  - De Vos emphasized extraction of universally applicable principles, transforming liability focus from specific actions to broader principles.
  - This shift aligned enrichment law with the principles seen in contract and delict.
  
- **Shift in Judicial Perception**:
  - Recognition by the **Supreme Court of Appeal** (Kudu Granite Operations (Pty) Ltd v Caterna Ltd [2003] 3 All SA 1; Glenrand MIB Financial Services (Pty) Ltd and Others v Van den Heever NO [2013] 1 All SA 511) regarding general requirements.

- **Ongoing Developments**:
  - **Visser, McCarthy, Nissan South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Marnitz NO**, and **Glenrand** cases raised questions about enrichment liability's scope.
  - Suggestions of expanding enrichment liability in line with the Constitution and Ubuntu.

## Development of General Principles in South African Law (Page 11)

- **Key Requirements for Enrichment**:
  - Plaintiff must have been impoverished.
  - Defendant must have been enriched.
  - Enrichment must be sine causa (without legal cause).
  - Causality: enrichment at the impoverished party's expense.
  
- **Judicial Citations**:
  - **St Helena Primary School and Another v MEC: Department of Education** (2007) highlights the necessity of basic requirements under recognized actions.
  - Reference to four identified requirements established in various cases (Kudu Granite Operations; McCarthy Retail Ltd).

- **Distinction from Other Liabilities**:
  - Enrichment law distinct from contractual and delictual liability.
  - No overlap where contractual liability exists; enrichment liability excluded due to sine causa principle.
  
- **Extent of Liability**:
  - Plaintiff entitled to the lesser amount between their impoverishment or the defendant's enrichment.
  - Enrichment value determined at the action institute's time.
  - Defendant not liable for unutilized potential benefits from enrichment gains.

- **Reduction or Extinction of Liability**:
  - Liability can be reduced if enrichment is lost before action starts.
  - Instances where earlier dates for calculations apply:
    - Awareness of unjust enrichment.
    - Realization of potential unjust benefit.
    - Mora debitoris.
    - Bad faith (mala fide) actions.

- **Minor Exception**:
  - Enrichment of minors confined to amounts when an unauthorized contract is involved.
  
- **Purpose of Unjustified Enrichment Law**:
  - Functions as a corrective mechanism to prevent unfair enrichment aligns with constitutional values of fairness and Ubuntu.

## 2.1 INTRODUCTION
- **Liability Basis**: Asset movement must show:
  - Plaintiff's impoverishment.
  - Defendant's enrichment.
  - Legally relevant relationship exists.
- **Claim Recognition**: Requires enrichment to be unjustified (sine causa).
- **Claim Calculation**: Lesser of the two amounts (enrichment or impoverishment).
- **Property Transfer**: If property exists with enriched party, retransfer possible; otherwise claim value.
- Distinguish between:
  - Contractual liability arising from agreements.
  - Delictual liability from unlawful acts.
  - Enrichment liability from value transferred without legal basis.

- **Forms of Enrichment**:
  - Increase in assets: **Brooklyn House Furnishers Ltd v Knoetze & Sons**

  - Non-increase in liabilities.

- **Value in Claim**: Enrichment comprises either the thing or its value at claim time.

### 2.2.2 The plaintiff must be impoverished

- **Constitutes of Impoverishment**:
  - Decrease in assets.
  - Non-increase in assets.
  - Increase in liabilities.
  - Non-decrease in liabilities.
  
- **Side-effects**: Consideration of both beneficial and detrimental side-effects affecting claims.

## STUDY UNIT 2: General Requirements for Enrichment Liability

### 2.2.3 The Defendant’s Enrichment Must Have Been at the Expense of the Plaintiff
- Causal link required between the defendant's enrichment and the plaintiff's impoverishment.
- Indirect enrichment issues arise when benefit accrues to a third party (C) while performing for another (B).
  - **Examples**: 
    - A contracts with B to build for B, but C owns the property.
    - A repairs B's car, unknowingly belonging to C.
    - A subcontracts from B for work benefiting C.
- If B pays A, C is enriched at B's expense; if B becomes insolvent, A's claim may be complex.
- **De Vos's View**: C enriched at B’s expense, not A’s, negating A’s claim against C.
- **Gouws v Jester Pools (1968) 3 SA 653 (T)** endorsed De Vos's perspective, ruling no action from A against C.
- **Criticism**: 
  - Van der Walt, Scholtens, and Van Zyl argue A should succeed based on direct transfer.
  - Illustrations of scenarios reinforcing the direct transfer concept.
    - **Example**: A completes work for B; if B pays C directly, A can claim against C.
    - **Example**: Materials supplied move from A to B and then C, not satisfying the direct transfer concept.

### 2.2.4 The Enrichment Must Have Been Sine Causa (Unjustified)
- Enrichment liability must also ensure that retention of benefits is inequitable.
- **Van der Walt Definition**: Enrichment is sine causa if no obligation exists between the enriched and impoverished.
- **De Vos's Critique**: Broadens the understanding of unjustified enrichment to include situations lacking direct obligations, such as court orders or prescription claims.

### 2.2.5 Practical Examples from Case Law
- **Greenhills Producers (Pty) Ltd v Benjamin (1960) 4 SA 188 (E)**:
  - Facts: Contract for sheep between company and defendants fell away upon winding up.
  - Company sought enrichment action for sheep kept post-contract; critical focus on whether enrichment was sine causa.
  - **Decision**: Court held consideration of unjustified enrichment crucial, determining defendant's benefits were unjustified based on prior contract.

**Notes on Enrichment and Rights**:
- Enrichment lien (right of retention) indicates a strong interest in claims for necessary expenses incurred.
- **Brooklyn House case & McCarthy considerations** explore lien possessor rights where ownership issues surfaced.

## STUDY UNIT 2: General requirements for enrichment liability

### 2.1 Sine Causa Requirement
- **Principle**: Enrichment must be unjust; joint errors by parties negate unjustness.
   - Case: *Dugas v Kempster Sedgwick (Pty) Ltd* 1961 1 SA 784 (D): Buyer’s enrichment from invalid sale is not unjust.
   - Case: *Auby and Patellides (Pty) Ltd v Glen Anil Investments (Pty) Ltd* 1960 4 SA 865 (A): Causa for transfer can justify enrichment; no entitlement for improvements post-cancellation.
   - Case: *Buzzard Electrical*: Subcontractor claims against owner; enrichment justified by contract with the developer.

### 2.2 Nature and Extent of Enrichment Claims
- **Objective of Claim**: Transfer of specific items or property back to original owner from the enriched party.
   - **Ownership Transfers**: Automatic under South African law despite invalid contracts; restitution via value claims if third party involved.
   - Example: If a horse sold at R150,000 is resold at R140,000, original owner claims residual value of R140,000.
  
- **Calculation of Liability**: Generally at lodgment of claim (litis contestatio), but can be earlier under certain conditions:
   - **Knowledge Provisions**: Liability diminished if the enriched party can prove loss not their fault.
   - **Negligence**: Enriched party remains liable unless they demonstrate loss was unavoidable.
   - **Implied Knowledge**: Enrichment pegged to when a reasonable person should have recognized the enrichment.

- **Mora Debitoris**: Default does not relieve liability unless loss proved unavoidable if retransfer timely made.
   - Distinction in mora contexts; only recognized post-demand for retransfer or payment.

- **Mala Fide Conduct**: If bad faith in enrichment reduction, liability may fix to an earlier date than claim lodgment.

- **Minors Exception**: Liability of minors restricted to enrichment amount at claim time (referencing *Edelstein case*).

### 2.3 Practical Scenarios 
- **Key Considerations**:
   - Scenario 1: If deposit payment inconclusive due to void contract, B can make a full claim against A.
   - Scenario 2: Enrichment determination hinges on contractual relationships and performance fulfillment; assess potential implicit enrichment of E.
   - Scenario 3: Indirect enrichment needs application of principles from related cases (Gouws, Buzzard Electrical, McCarthy).
   - Scenario 4: Valid contracts elicit analysis of enrichment cause (sine causa).
   - Scenario 5: Distinguish enrichment claims from delictual or property claims (e.g., actio rei vindicatio).

## STUDY UNIT 3: Condictio indebiti: general requirements

**Overview**
- Examines condictio indebiti, focusing on the requirements and error element.

---

### 3.1 Introduction
- Performance under a mistaken belief may be reclaimed via condictio indebiti.
- Historical foundations in Roman law adapted into South African law.

---

### 3.2 Roman Law
- **Requirements for condictio indebiti**:
  - **Datio**: Transfer of money or things.
  - **Error**: Payment made under the impression it was owed.
  - **Undue Payment**: Payment made without a debt existing.

- **Key Principles**:
  - Knowledge of non-debt negates claim (animus donandi).
  - Only factual mistakes (error facti) recoverable; legal errors (error iuris) generally not.
  - No recovery under natural obligations.
  - Receiver restores benefits (including fruit) with allowances for necessary expenses.

---

### 3.3 Roman-Dutch Law
- Generally mirrors Roman law with equity considerations.
- Obligation to return valued items or equivalent if the item is lost/alienated.
- The receiver can defend against repayment if loss incurred after awareness (but before default).

---

### 3.4 Application in South African Law
- **Condictio indebiti** as an unjustified enrichment remedy.
- Can claim from parties other than actual recipients (e.g., agents).
  
**Requirements**:
1. **Transfer of Ownership**: Includes corporeal and incorporeal things.
2. **Mistake**: Must be a reasonable mistake (iustus error).
3. **Claim under Duress/Protest**: Presumes reclaimable undues.

- **Impoverished Party Must Prove**:
  - Payment not owed.
  - Payment made by mistake (excusable).

- **Contention** over retention of iustus error requirement; seen as outdated by some schools of thought.

---

### Additional Notes on Legal Implications
- **Defenses for Enriched Parties**: Non-enrichment claims; failure to retain separate accounting does not negate the defense.
- **Circumstances for Total Liability**:
  - Awareness of non-debt.
  - Later realization of non-debt.
  - Should have foreseen potential undue nature.

- **Interest of Enrichment in Non-retention Cases**:
  - Responsible for the total value if payment made knowingly or if no reasonable basis existed for returning partially.

---

**Pressure for Reform**: Potential for constitutional considerations (e.g., fairness and equality) to influence the removal or reform of the iustus error requirement in future legal interpretations.

## STUDY UNIT 4: Condictio indebiti: specific applications  

### 4.1 INTRODUCTION  
- Overview of specific applications of **condictio indebiti** in South African law, including:  
  - Statutory enrichment action under **s 28 of the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981**  
  - Applications in insolvency law and succession law  
  - Payments made under duress and protest  
  - Ultra vires payments  

### 4.2 STATUTORY ENRICHMENT ACTION: ALIENATION OF LAND ACT  
- Void contracts lack legal enforceability; parties can reclaim performance through **condictio** if all requirements are met.  
- If a party knows the contract is void yet expects counter-performance, they may invoke **condictio causa data causa non secuta**.  
- **Carlis v McCusker 1904 TS 917**: Entitlement to restitution exists if performance in a void contract is demonstrated.  
- **Wilken v Kohler 1913 AD 135**: Recovery barred where both parties perform; if the defendant is willing to perform, the plaintiff must plead this in their claim.  
- The **Cape Provincial Division** rejected the Transvaal view in **CD Development Co (East Rand) (Pty) Ltd v Novick 1979 2 SA 546 (C)**:  
  - Plaintiff must prove entitlement to condictio.  
  - Recovery is possible even if the defendant performed.  
- **s 28 of the Alienation of Land Act** establishes a statutory enrichment action:  
  - **Subsection (1)**: Allows recovery of performance due to void contracts.  
  - **Subsection (2)**: Validates transactions where the alienee has fully performed.  
  - Legislation accounts for all factors of enrichment and impoverishment.  

### 4.3 THE CONDICTIO INDEBITI AND THE LAW OF SUCCESSION  
- **Condictio indebiti** applicable to executors, creditors, and beneficiaries:  
  - Executors can reclaim incorrect payments (s 50(b) of the **Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965**) due to improper distribution.  
  - Creditors may pursue claims (ss 50(a), 31) against executors for late claims.  
  - Executors can reclaim from beneficiaries who received overpayments.  
  - Creditors cannot pursue those already paid (s 31(b)).  
- Liability of executors ceases upon discharge unless acted in bad faith (s 56(2)).  
- Unpaid creditors can utilize the **condictio indebiti** against overpaid beneficiaries.  
- **Prescription** for claims under **s 11(d) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969**: Claims prescribe after three years.  
- **Rapp v Ruflex Holdings (Pty) Ltd 1972 3 SA 835 (T)**: Clarified claims post-executor discharge: creditors can pursue beneficiaries for overpayments.  

### 4.4 THE CONDICTIO INDEBITI AND THE LAW OF INSOLVENCY  
- **Rapp case**: Court rejected use of **condictio indebiti** for unpaid creditors against shareholders post-liquidation.  
- Differing outcome in **Kommissaris van Binnelandse Inkomste v Willers 1994 3 SA 283 (A)**: Found condictio indebiti available for creditors.  
- Confirmed in **Bowman, De Wet and Du Plessis NNO and Others v Fidelity Bank 1997 2 SA 35 (A)**.

### 4.5 ULTRA VIRES PAYMENTS AND THE CONDICTIO INDEBITI  
- Uncertainty around reclaiming ultra vires payments resolved in **Bowman case**: Payments made beyond powers can recover through **condictio indebiti**.  
- Court stated such payments are not owed ("onverskuldig").  

### 4.6 PAYMENTS MADE UNDER DURESS AND PROTEST  
- **Condictio indebiti** requires:  
  - Payment should not be owing.  
  - Payment made under duress.  
  - Payment must be protested at the time.  
- Example: Dispute in **CIR** case led to tax duties paid under force; majority held tacit agreement for repayment, while minority preferred the presumption of intention to not donate due to duress.

- **Definition**: Legal principle governing recovery where one party is enriched at the expense of another due to an illegal or immoral agreement.  

- **Examples**:  
  - A payment for an unlawful act, e.g., murder.  
  - A verbal contract for land sale (not unlawful but void due to lack of writing under **s 2 of the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981**).  

- **Key Inference**: Noncompliance with formalities does not render a contract unlawful; it may simply be void.  

- **Requirements to Succeed with Condictio ob turpem vel iniustam causam**:  
  - Performance rendered under an illegal agreement.  
  - Enrichment at plaintiff's expense.
  - Tender of return of the performance has to be made.
  - Plaintiff is not a **turpis persona** or valid reasons exist to relax the par delictum rule.  

- **Requirements Explained**:    
  - **Performance and Enrichment**: Plaintiff must show that their performance met the requirements above.  
  - **Tender Requirement**: Stemming from **Albertyn v Kumalo (1946 WPA 529)**, where the plaintiff must return received performance, aiding the efficiency of enrichment (see **MCC Bazaar** case).

## STUDY UNIT 9: Enrichment by means of improvements and attachments (accessio)

### 9.1 INTRODUCTION
- Movable property may be attached to movable/immovable property of another.
- Ownership transfers depending on attachment type (accessio).
- Question arises whether the original owner can claim compensation for enrichment.
- Improvements and attachments are original means of acquiring ownership.

### 9.2 SOUTH AFRICAN LAW

#### 9.2.1 General
- Roman law: No general enrichment action; remedies limited to specific actions.
- 18th-century modifications recognized general enrichment action in Roman-Dutch law.
- South African law recognized new actions through analogy and public policy, allowing broader recourse for unjustified enrichment.
- Different categories of individuals can bring compensatory actions (discussed further in unit 10).

#### 9.2.2 Bona fide possessor
- Definition: A bona fide possessor believes, reasonably or not, they own a property.
- Reasonableness of mistake not a factor in establishing bona fide status; subjective belief determines status.
- Ownership of attached material passes to owner of immovable property.
- Right of recourse based on unjustified enrichment if the bona fide possessor has enriched the owner through improvements.
- Right to remove attachments (ius tollendi) exists as long as the possessor is still in possession.
- Compensation claims allowable for:
  - Necessary expenses (impensae necessariae) - full reimbursement if successful.
  - Useful expenses (impensae utiles) - lesser of total expense or value enhancement.
  - Luxurious expenses (impensae voluptuariae) - non-compensable unless there's an increase in property value for sale.
- Right of retention (ius retentionis) allows the bona fide possessor to withhold property until compensated.
- Voluntary relinquishment of control results in loss of retention right.

#### 9.2.3 Mala fide possessor
- Definition: Mala fide possessor knows they lack ownership but exercises owner's rights.
- Case law indicates mala fide possessors may be compensated, subject to restrictions:
  - Lord de Villiers’ view in **Bellingham v Bloommetje** permits compensation for enhancements.
  - **De Beers case** allows compensation only for necessary improvements unless the owner is silent.
- Proposed view: mala fide possessors should have similar rights as bona fide possessors, based on enrichment.
- Conflicting opinions on the right of retention:
  - **De Beers case** denies retention rights to mala fide possessors.
  - **JOT Motors case** upheld retention right, although legally termed as lawful.

### 10.2 OCCUPIERS AND HOLDERS (DETENTORES)
- **General Principle**: Occupiers/holders typically cannot claim compensation from owners for improvements.
  - Courts have extended remedies permitting claims for compensation.

#### 10.2.1 Legal occupiers/holders
- **Common Law Rights**: Certain legal occupiers (lessee, pledgee, usufructuary) entitled to recourse for expenses.
- **Case Law**:
  - **Brown v Brown (1929)**: Granted compensation to a buyer for improvements despite being a legal occupier with no right of recourse under common law (minus seller compensation).
  - **Eiselen and Pienaar** (at 312): No extra deduction from compensation for fruit gathered unless impossible to value.
  - **Actio negotiorum gestorum contraria**: Claims for compensation can be instituted under specific conditions (Nortjé en ’n Ander v Pool NO 1966).
  - **Wynland Construction (Pty) Ltd v Ashley-Smith en Andere (1985)**: Adjustments for owner’s enrichment or occupier’s impoverishment in compensation claims.
- **Enrichment Lien**: Lawful occupiers can retrieve expenses if owner is enriched at their expense, but improvements must still be attached to the property.
- **Example**: Loss of right of retention if maize crop is removed post-harvest (Peens case).
- **Standard Kredietkorporasie Case**: Established lien rights for lawful holders; referenced inaccurate terminology regarding lawful possession.
- **Placaats**: Applicable to farmland, not urban land; protect landlords from unconsented improvements.
- **Business Aviation Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Rand Airport Holdings (Pty) Ltd (2007)**: Confirmed Placaats’ application only to farmland.
- **Eiselen and Pienaar (at 333–334)**: Lessees can:
  - Remove non-necessary improvements during lease; essential improvements stay with the land.
  - Claim compensation for improvements (value of materials used, seed costs, and labor excluded).
  - No compensation rights if lease is prematurely terminated ("Kumalo v Piet Retief Village Council 1931").
  
- **Modern Critique**: Brand JA in **Business Aviation** questioned the fairness and relevance of the Placaats today, especially regarding inequalities between rural and urban leases.

### 10.2.2 Bona fide occupiers/holders
- **Definition**: Occupiers/holders who believe, albeit mistakenly, they have a legal right to the property.
- **Case Law**:
  - **Bellingham v Bloommetje (1874)**: Recognized a bona fide occupier's right to compensation and retention.
  - **Rubin v Botha (1911)**: Confirmed entitlement of bona fide occupiers to compensation based on improvements made.
    - Established two principles for calculating compensation: impoverishment vs. enrichment.
  - **Fletcher and Fletcher v Bulawayo Waterworks Co Ltd (1915)**: Equalized treatment of bona fide possessors and detentors in improvement claims, with adjustments for occupation value.
  
- **Compensation Calculation**: 
  - Bona fide detentor can have occupation value subtracted from improvement value.
  - **Eiselen and Pienaar (at 291)**: Advocated consideration of gathered fruit's value when occupation cannot be evaluated.

### Conclusion
- The distinction is important between legal, bona fide occupiers/holders, affecting their right to compensation for improvements made.
- Continuous legal evolution is necessary to address current societal and constitutional imperatives.

## STUDY UNIT 11: Rights of retention and liens

### 11.1 Rights of retention

- **Types of liens:**
  - **Enrichment liens:** Real rights for unpaid expenses incurred for preservation (impensae necessariae) or improvement (impensae utiles) of property.
  - **Debtor and creditor liens:** Not real rights; arise from an agreement regardless of necessity or utility.
  
- **Key case: Goudini Chrome (Pty) Ltd v MCC Contracts (Pty) Ltd 1993 1 SA 77 (A)**
  - Differentiates between enrichment liens and debtor-creditor liens.
  
- **Retention Rights:**
  - Enrichment lien allows possession retention until reimbursed for necessary/useful expenses, irrespective of contractual relationship.
  - Debtor-creditor lien secures full agreed remuneration against the other contracting party.

### 11.2 IMPENSAE NECESSARIAE

- **Definition**: Necessary expenses required for property preservation or protection.
- **Recovery**: Full recovery of expenses incurred if they are reasonable; retention until compensated.
  
### 11.3 IMPENSAE UTILES

- **Definition**: Useful expenses that enhance market value but are not necessary.
- **Recovery**: Lesser of market value enhancement or amount spent.
  
### 11.4 IMPENSAE VOLUPTUARIAE

- **Definition**: Luxurious expenses that do not preserve property or increase value.
- **Recovery**: No right of retention arises from these expenses.

### Control Requirement

- Right of retention depends on physical control; loss of control leads to loss of retention. Restoration of control can revive the right.

### 13.1 Contractual Claims and Enrichment
- If exceptio not allowed, plaintiff has contractual claim; entitled to contract price minus costs to eliminate defect.
- Onus to prove costs on skilled artisan (worker).
- If exceptio allowed, plaintiff's claim shifts to enrichment claim based on principles of enrichment liability (whichever is least: enrichment or impoverishment), not contract price minus defect elimination costs.
- Court retains discretion regarding substantial performance; must allege proper performance or grounds for discretion.
- **Case Reference**: *BK Tooling*.

### 13.2 Contracts for Services
- Employee (locator operarum) entitled to remuneration only upon full term completion.
- If not fully rendered, cannot claim full agreed remuneration unless contract states otherwise.
- May lead to unjust enrichment claim if contract claim defeated by exceptio non adimpleti contractus.
- **Leading Case**: *Spencer v Gostelow* (1920 AD 617).
  - Employee dismissed before service completion entitled to compensation based on enrichment liability (remuneration for period served).
  - Plaintiff's claim tied to employer's saved costs (pro rata remuneration).
  - Employee must establish impoverishment correlating to remuneration earned.
- Law treats desertion differently; deserting employees lose enrichment claim.
- Criticism exists regarding fairness between terminated contracts and those resulting from desertion.
