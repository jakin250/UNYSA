## 1.2 INTRODUCTION TO ESTOPPEL

### 1.2.1 Meaning of the Concept “Estoppel”
- **Estop**: Comes from Latin *stuppa*, means to bar or preclude; legal preclusion under specific circumstances.
- **Bower's Definition**: In English jurisprudence, “estop” and “estoppel” carry technical significance distinct from their general meanings.

### Definition:
- **Estoppel**: A doctrine preventing a person from asserting a fact different from what has been represented, which may have led another party to change their legal position to their detriment.

### Example:
- **Case**: *Thomas v Baumann & Gilfillan* 1914 TPD 197
  - Mrs. Thomas undertook to pay a debt, leading B & G to rely on her misrepresentation, which prevented her from claiming protection under the law against her brother.

### Summation:
- Doctrine holds a person to representations made through actions or words, preventing reliance on the actual state of affairs after detrimental reliance.

### 2.1 Introduction into South African Law
- The gradual introduction of “estoppel” into South African legal terminology through judicial decisions.

- **Lord de Villiers** (In re Reynolds Vehicle and Harness Factory Ltd 1906): Caution against the term "estoppel" had been issued, but it gained usage by then.

- Kotze CJ acknowledged estoppel's existence in South African law through references to Roman-Dutch legal principles.

### 2.4 Concept of Estoppel in Roman and Roman-Dutch Law
- Roman-Dutch law had maxims similar to English estoppel but lacked a comprehensive doctrine.
- **Waterval Estate & Gold Mining Co Ltd v New Bullion Gold Mining Co Ltd 1905**: Judicial endorsement of English doctrine.

### 2.5 Curlewis re the Doctrine of Estoppel in Pais
- **Curlewis J**: Estoppel in pais parallels the Roman law *exceptio doli mali*; legal outcomes are similar.
  
### 2.6 Meaning of Exceptio Doli
- Supported by **Oakland Nominees (Pty) Ltd v Gelria Mining & Investment Co (Pty) Ltd 1976** and others.
- **Bank of Lisbon and SA Ltd v De Ornelas 1988**: Declared *exceptio doli* not part of South African law, thus the prevailing legal interpretation.

### 2.7 Appellate Division re Estoppel by Representation
- **Baumann v Thomas 1920**: English estoppel by representation recognized as part of South African law.
- Judicial reception of English doctrine is established.

### 2.8 Naturalisation of the Doctrine of Estoppel by Representation
- **Connock’s (SA) Motor Co Ltd v Sentraal-Westelike Kooperatiewe Maatskappy Bpk 1964**: English doctrine regarded as part of South African law.

### 2.9 New Approach to the Law of Estoppel
- **Trust Bank van Afrika Bpk v Eksteen 1964**: Appellate Division emphasizes the need to focus on South African law before relying on English authorities.

## 3. Requirements of English Law for Estoppel by Representation
### 3.1 Proof Required in English Law
- **Bower** identified the necessary elements for estoppel by representation:
  - (a) Valid representation deemed legally recognized.
  - (b) Precise representation must have been made.
  - (c) Later assertion contradicts earlier representation.
  - (d) Original representation induced reliance to detriment.
  - (e) Representation made by the party to be estopped or their representative.

### 3.2 Requirement of Neither Intent Nor Negligence
- English law does not mandate intent or negligence from the estoppel-denier (Bower avoids referencing negligence in estoppel discussions).

#### 3.1 INTRODUCTION

- **Doctrine of Estoppel**: Prevents denial of established beliefs if one party induces another to act detrimentally based on those beliefs.
- **Requirements for Estoppel**:
  1. Creation of a false belief by the estoppel-denier.
  2. Fault (intent or negligence) of the estoppel-denier.
  3. Prejudice to the estoppel-asserter.
  4. Causation between the prejudice and the reliance on the misrepresentation.
  5. Applicability of estoppel in law to the specific case. 

#### 3.2 MISREPRESENTATION 

- **Creation of False Impression**: Misrepresentation is inherent in the doctrine of estoppel.
- **Requirements**: 
  - A representation must be made that creates a false impression; both aspects are encompassed in misrepresentation.
  
##### 3.2.1 Representation 

- Any word or conduct can constitute a representation, including silence.
- **Nature of Representation**:
  - Must pertain to facts, not future intentions. 
  - Here, silence or non-action can imply representation.

##### 3.2.2 Misrepresentation 

- **Requirement**: Representation must reflect an untruth for it to be a misrepresentation.
- **Determination Criteria**:
  - Misrepresentation assessed based on community legal views (eoni mores).
  - Tested against requirements like intention to mislead or reasonable expectations of misleading impacts.
  
---

##### 3.2.3 Misrepresentation by Omission (Omissio)

- An omission is misrepresentation if there’s a legal duty to act, failing which leads to detrimental reliance by another party.

### Duty to Act Positively
- English law refers to the "duty to act or speak."
- No unanimity on whether this duty is necessary for misrepresentation by omission leading to estoppel (See **De Wet 45-54; Van der Merwe 1964 THRHR 57-60**).
- De Wet: duty to act does not create or fail to rectify a misconception.
- Silence can induce reliance without a duty to speak (according to De Wet’s explanation).

### Misrepresentation Contrary to Legal Duty
- Establishing a misrepresentation demands assessing objective reasonableness.
- A misrepresentation emerges only if the omission contravenes a legal duty under prevailing circumstances.

### Duty to Act Positively
- No general duty to rectify misconceptions (**Parsons v Langemann and Others 1948 4 SA 258 (C); Fellner v Donges NO 1953 3 SA 517 (T)**).
- Legal duty may arise in specific contexts where community standards imply a duty to act.
- Estoppel may occur when parties are expected to act positively based on the relationship.

### Objective Reasonableness or Eoni Mores
- Duty to speak or act arises if reasonable for avoiding detriment to another.
- Assessment based on the relationship and surrounding circumstances (**LAWSA para 656**).

### Misrepresentation in Terms of Entrustment
- Misrepresentation can occur when property is entrusted to another.
- Questions of whether possession implies ownership arise frequently in court cases.
  
#### Example: Adams v Mocke 23 SC 722
- A's driver hired a mule, leading to misunderstandings about possession.
  
#### Example: Morum Bros Ltd v Nepgen 1916 CPD 392
- A's sale subject to a suspensive condition; possession before payment does not imply ownership.

### Separation of Ownership from Possession
- Without clear evidence, mere possession does not infer ownership.
- Misrepresentation is determined by how a reasonable person perceives the scenario.

### Handing Over of Title Documents
- Conduct that provides documents may imply a misrepresentation.
  
#### Example: Fawdon v Lelyveld 1937 TPD 339
- Owner's false receipt allowed a misrepresentation regarding ownership, leading to estoppel claim success.

### Person to Whom Misrepresentation Must Be Made
- Misrepresentation must be directed to the party pleading estoppel.
- It can be sufficient if made to a public group of which the claimant is a member (**LAWSA para 660; See also Fellner v Donges 1953 2 SA 517 (T)**).

# REPUDIATION OF OWNER’S REI VINDICATIO

- **Estoppel and Owner’s Representation**:  
  - Easier for an estoppel-asserter to repudiate owner’s claim with reasonable representation.  
  - Contrast with owner's awareness of truthfulness of statement.

## INCOMPATIBILITY OF MAJORITY AND MINORITY JUDGMENTS

- **Weighing Interests**:  
  - True owner's interests vs. bona fide acquirer of possession.
- **Roman Law Rule**:  
  - **Ubi meam rem invenio, ibi vindico**: Ownership protection in SA law.
  - Owner usually can reclaim possession except in exceptional cases like estoppel.
- **Germanic Law Rule**:  
  - **Mobilia non habent sequelam**: Favor bona fide acquirer of possession, not owner.

## EXTENSION OF EXCEPTIONS

- **Chief Justice’s Opinion (Grosvenor Motors)**:  
  - **Expansion of exceptions** to ownership rule is not readily adopted.  
  - Traditional ownership protection remains strong unless owner culpably misrepresents.

## RESTRICTIONS ON THE EFFECT OF REI VINDICATIO

- **Rumpff JA’s Minority Judgment**:  
  - Commercial requirements may restrict owner’s rei vindicatio due to estoppel.
  - Estoppel serves as a defence to limit owner's claim.

# LEARNING UNIT 5: REQUIREMENTS FOR A SUCCESSFUL RELIANCE ON ESTOPPEL: PREJUDICE

## Operation of the Principle of Estoppel
- Estoppel prevents prejudice; it operates only to the extent of the actual prejudice suffered.
- Example: If B represents to A that she owes R2,000 and A, relying on this, wastes R100, estoppel can be pleaded only for the amount wasted (R100).
- Reference Case: **Durban Corporation Superannuation Fund v Campbell 1949 3 1057 (N)**.

## Need for Normative Judgment
- Wider approach suggests that the requirement of prejudice for estoppel is non-patrimonial; any unreasonably unacceptable change in legal position constitutes sufficient prejudice (Van der Walt JC).

## Inclusion of Future Patrimonial Loss
- Views on prejudice requirement:
  - Wider approach includes possible or uncertain future patrimonial loss.
  - Must still be a patrimonial prejudice affecting the aggrieved’s position detrimentally (Van der Merwe and Van Huyssteen).

# LEARNING UNIT 6: REQUIREMENTS FOR A SUCCESSFUL RELIANCE ON ESTOPPEL: CAUSATION

## 6.1 Introduction: Factual and Legal Causation
- **Causal Link**: Required between misrepresentation by the estoppel-denier and eventual act leading to prejudice (Southern Life Association Ltd v Beyleveld NO 1989 1 SA 496 (A)).
- Distinction between factual (condition sine qua non) and legal causation is not expressly made in estoppel cases.

## 6.2 The “Proximate Cause” Test
- **Proximate Cause**: Misrepresentation must be the proximate or real and direct cause of the estoppel-asserter’s action resulting in prejudice.
- **Case Reference**: **Union Government v National Bank of South Africa Ltd 1921 AD 121**.
  
### Judgments of the Appellate Division
- **Innes CJ**: No negligence by P; government not estopped.
- **Solomon JA**: Negligence not proximate cause of the bank’s loss; intervening act by S was too remote.
- **Juta JA**: P’s negligence was not the immediate cause; intervening events nullified proximate causation.

### Practical Implications
- The proximate cause test shows that estoppel cannot be invoked when an intermediary’s actions cause misrepresentation unless those acts are independent of the owner’s conduct.
- Summary: The proximate cause test inherently includes considerations of both factual and legal causation, validating the extent of potential negligence’s remoteness to the loss.

### 7.3 ULTRA VIRES ACTS

**Powers and Duties of Statutory Bodies**
- Estoppel is unavailable to statutory bodies for ultra vires acts.
- Statutory bodies act within powers conferred by statute; cannot be estopped to do beyond their powers or prevented from fulfilling legal duties.
- **Case Reference**: Hoisain v Wynberg Municipality [1916 CPD 194], Maberley v Woodstock Municipality [1901 SC 443], Abrahamse v Connock’s Pension Fund [1963 2 SA 76 (W)].

**Compliance with Internal Formalities**
- Distinction between ultra vires acts and those valid upon internal formalities compliance.
- Third parties may plead estoppel if they assume formalities are met unless they know otherwise.
- **Case Reference**: City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v RPM Bricks (Pty) Ltd [2007 3 All SA 17 (SCA)].

**Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**
- The Constitution influences public law estoppel; emphasizes balancing interests and prejudice.
- Courts should assess individual vs. public interests when considering estoppel.
- **Case References**: Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of SA; Eastern Metropolitan Substructure v Peter Klein Investments (Pty) Ltd [2001 (4) SA 661 (W)].

**Possible Future Deviation from Established Approach**
- Statutory bodies may be bound by estoppel regarding ultra vires acts after interest consideration.

### 8.2 Estoppel and the Rei Vindicatio
- **Rei Vindicatio**: Owner claims property based on Roman principle.  
- **Germanic Principle**: Owner's claim restricted if possession was voluntarily surrendered.
- Limitations on owner’s rei vindicatio:
  - **Where a Factor Sells Goods**: Owner can reclaim only if buyer colluded with factor (e.g., *Pretorius v Loudon* 1985 3 SA 845 (A)).
  - **Culpable Misrepresentation**: If an owner misrepresents another as the owner, they may be estopped from reclaiming property (cases: *Adams v Mocke* 1906 23 SC 782, *Morum Bros Ltd v Nepgen* 1916 CPD 392, etc.).

##### 8.2.1 The Effect of a Successful Reliance on Estoppel Against the Rei Vindicatio
- **Implication of Estoppel**: An owner (A) may not reclaim property from a third party (C) if estoppel applies, despite C buying from a misrepresented source (B) — adheres to *nemo plus iuris rule*.
- **Legal Certainty**: To protect bona fide acquirers, acceptance that ownership may transfer through estoppel is essential (views of Van Heerden and Louw).

### 8.3 Estoppel and Representation in Terms of Mandate
- **Contract of Mandate**: Establishes the relationship and authority between mandans and mandatarius.
- **Estoppel Application within Mandate**:

  - Private instructions do not affect third-party dealings.

### 8.4 Estoppel and Partnership
- **Partnership Liability**: Partners can bind each other unless restricted by agreement.
- **Liability for Misrepresentation**: Misleading others into believing one is a partner can invoke estoppel.
- **Post-Resignation Liabilities**: Former partners may be held liable for debts incurred if third parties believed they were still partners.

### 8.5 Estoppel and the Conclusion of a Contract
- **Estoppel and Lack of Consensus**: Controversy exists regarding estoppel’s role when parties lack consensus due to mistake.
- **Views on Estoppel in Mistake**:

  - **Apparent Consent**: Positive law may bind parties despite true dissent; estoppel may play a role.

  - **Consent Theory**: Contractual liability based solely on mutual agreement.

## 9.1 Basing of Estoppel on Exceptio Doli
- **Estoppel and Exceptio Doli**: 
  - Basing estoppel on Roman law's *exceptio doli* was acknowledged in *Baumann* and *Oakland Nominees* cases.
  - In *Oceanair (Natal) (Pty) Ltd v Sher* 1980 (1) SA 317 (D), estoppel was linked to *exceptio doli*.
- **De Ome Case**:
  - Held that *exceptio doli* never became a part of Roman-Dutch law (De Ome 1988 (3) SA 580 (A)).

## 9.2 Exceptio Doli Specialis and Generalis
- **Exceptio Doli Specialis**:
  - Targets fraudulent past misrepresentation, not encompassing all estoppel liabilities (liabilities can arise from negligent misrepresentation).
- **Exceptio Doli Generalis**:
  - Concerns fraud in the present, lacking a substantive principle of liability; reflects a tool to adapt existing law.
  - Initially required bad faith, later abandoned; serves to introduce new law.
- **Principles from Jansen J in North Vaal**: Emphasizes the generality supports various legal developments.
- **Estoppel Comparison**:
  - Requires examination of equity; *exceptio doli* is solely a defense, unlike estoppel, which allows plaintiff invocation.
  - Estoppel's theoretical dependencies on *exceptio doli* have been critiqued.

## 9.3 The Maxim Nemo Contra Suum Factum Venire Debet
- **Nemo Contra Suum Factum**:
  - Principles of estoppel are reflected, however, qualification is necessary; mere ownership or intent does not establish estoppel.
- **Contractual Basis of Estoppel**:
  - Some propose estoppel arises from implied agreements regarding factual representations.
  - Often viewed as artificial; questions linger on presence of mutual consent in negligent misrepresentations.

## 9.4 Protection of Good Faith
- **Estoppel's Characteristic**:
  - Centered around protecting good faith via maintenance of impressions created.
- **Impression and Fault**:
  - Justification for creators losing rights not solely based on good faith; fault must be established.
  - Good faith must be linked to culpable misrepresentation for loss justification.

## 9.5 Misrepresentation
- **Reliance on Estoppel**:
  - Can arise from intentional/negligent misrepresentation leading to delictual liability or estoppel.
- **Actio de Dolo Malo and Actio Legis Aquiliae**:
  - Historical shift from relying on fraudulent misrepresentation toward integrating *actio* principles.
- **Fraudulent Misrepresentation Remedies**:
  - Options include: action in delict, estoppel, or resiling from contract where material error is absent.
- **Negligent Misrepresentation in English Law**:
  - Initially limited but expanded to include liability in misrepresentation cases (*Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd* 1964 AC 465).
- **Invocation of Estoppel**:
  - Possible under negligence, establishing reliance in replication rather than as an exception.
  
### Key Cases
- *Pandor’s Trustee v Veatley & Co* 1935 TPD 358: Interpretation of estoppel as defense.
- *Biloden Properties (Pty) Ltd v Wilson* 1946 NPD 736: Confirmed estoppel's limited actionable characteristics.
- *Mann v Sydney Hunt Motors (Pty) Ltd* 1958 2 SA 102 (G): Estoppel reaffirmed as a defense, not a cause of action.

### Conclusion
- Estoppel is primarily a defense rooted in negligent misrepresentation; it should not be conflated with delictual actions. The framework for its application is distinct and requires careful consideration of its principles and requirements.

## 9.1 Comparison of Estoppel and Delict
- Requirements for estoppel: 
  - Fault
  - Prejudice
  - Causation
  - Legally permissible reliance
- Delict differences:
  - No requirement for permissible reliance
  - Distinct nature and function of remedies

## 9.2 Comparison of Estoppel, Actio Legis Aquiliae, and Interdict
1. **Nature of Remedy**: 
   - Estoppel serves as defense; not in declaration.
2. **Scope of Application**: 
   - Estoppel applicable in case of misrepresentation; both interdict and action ex delicto apply to any unlawful conduct.
3. **Objectives**:
   - Action ex delicto: Recover damages for loss.
   - Interdict: Prohibit human conduct.
   - Estoppel: Uphold created impressions, does not focus on recovery or prohibition.
4. **Fault Requirement**:
   - Interdict: No fault needed.
   - Action ex delicto: Fault required.
   - Estoppel: Fault needed only in certain cases.
5. **Aim of Remedies**: 
   - Action ex delicto: Damages recovery.
   - Estoppel: Prevention of damage.

## 9.3 Estoppel as a Rule of Evidence
- Estoppel perceived as a rule of evidence:
  - Precludes denial of previously made statements.
  - Confusion between pleading and evidence.
- Misleading view:
  - Estoppel must be pleaded; it introduces facts at issue.
  - Not an exclusion but allows for additional admissible evidence.

## 9.4 Estoppel as a Legal Remedy Sui Generis
- Sui generis nature:
  - Distinct from exceptio doli and action ex delicto.
  - Introduced to address legal gaps in South African law influenced by English law.
- Importance of recognition:
  - Understanding its uniqueness avoids categorization confusion.
  - Principles should evolve without dogmatic constraints.

## 9.5 Definition of Estoppel by Representation
- **Core Definition**: 
  - A person is estopped from denying the truth of a representation if:
    - The other party relied on the representation.
    - The reliance resulted in prejudice.
