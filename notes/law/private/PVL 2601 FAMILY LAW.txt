# Study Notes: Marriage and Family Law

## SECTION 1: Marriage Law

### 1. The Legal Requirements for a Civil Marriage
- **Consent:** Parties must freely consent to marriage.
- **Capacity:** Legal age, sound mind.
- **Formalities:** Compliance with procedure as prescribed in the *Marriage Act*.

### 2. Void, Voidable and Putative Civil Marriages
- **Void Marriages:** No legal effect (e.g., non-consent, prohibited relationships).
- **Voidable Marriages:** Valid until annulled (e.g., misrepresentation).
- **Putative Marriages:** Valid for one party, unaware of the void status.

### 3. The Invariable Consequences of a Civil Marriage
- **Personal Status:** Affects legal identity.
- **Legal Obligations:** Duties towards each other, including fidelity and support.
  
## SECTION 2: Matrimonial Property Law

### 4. The Variable Consequences of a Civil Marriage – Marriage in Community of Property
- **Joint estate:** Equal ownership and responsibilities.
- **Management:** Both spouses manage jointly.

### 5. The Variable Consequences of a Civil Marriage – Marriage out of Community of Property
- **Separate estates:** Each party retains independent control.
- **Systems:** (1) *Ante-nuptial contract*; (2) with accrual.

### 6. Alteration of the Matrimonial Property System
- **Legal Process:** Requires court approval.
- **Factors:** Changed circumstances, needs of parties.

## SECTION 3: Dissolution of a Civil Marriage

### 7. Dissolution of a Civil Marriage – General and Dissolution by Death
- **Modes of Dissolution:** Death, divorce.
  
### 8. The Grounds for Divorce
- **No-fault Grounds:** Irretrievable breakdown.
- **Fault Grounds:** Adultery, mental illness, etc.

### 9. Patrimonial Consequences of Divorce – General
- **Asset division:** Fair distribution based on contribution and need.

### 10. Forfeiture of Patrimonial Benefits
- **Conduct Basis:** Serious misconduct leads to loss of benefits.

### 11. Redistribution of Assets
- **Court Discretion:** Based on fairness, needs, contributions.

### 12. Maintenance of a Spouse After Divorce
- **Spousal Support:** Based on financial needs and ability to pay.

### 13. The Interests of the Children of Divorcing Parents
- **Primary Concern:** Welfare of children.
- **Custodial Rights:** Determined by the best interests of the child.

## SECTION 4: Civil Unions

### 14. Civil Unions
- **Requirements:** Similar to civil marriages.
- **Consequences:** Equivalent legal status to marriage.
- **Dissolution:** Governed by similar rules to divorce.

## SECTION 5: Customary Marriages

### 15. Customary Marriages
- **Legal Requirements:** Compliance with customary laws.
- **Consequences:** Rights and obligations under customary law.

## SECTION 6: Muslim and Hindu Marriages

### 16. Muslim and Hindu Marriages
- **Recognition:** Governed by religious principles within legal framework.
- **Legal Rights:** Vary based on personal law.

## SECTION 7: Life Partnerships

# Study Notes on Civil Marriage

## Page Numbers: 13-16, 24-29, 31-32

### 1. INTRODUCTION
- Definition of **civil marriage**: Must understand both traditional and modern definitions; avoid describing it as merely a contract.

### 2. REQUIREMENTS FOR A CIVIL MARRIAGE
- Legal requirements summarized:
  1. Capacity to act
  2. Agreement between parties
  3. Lawfulness of the marriage
  4. Compliance with prescribed formalities

#### 2.1 Capacity to act
- **Persons with no/limited capacity**:
  - **Prodigality**: Can marry without curator’s consent.
  - **Mental illness**: Can marry only during a lucid interval.
  - **Curatorship**: Can marry without curator’s consent.
  - **Minors**: Require consent from parents/legal guardians.

#### 2.2 Agreement
- Factors affecting agreement:
  - **Mistake**: Material mistakes exclude agreement, e.g., identity or nature of the act.
  - **Misrepresentation**: Only serious misrepresentation excludes agreement (e.g., concealing pregnancy).
  - **Duress**: Consent forced, affecting validity (e.g., *Smith v Smith*).
  - **Undue influence**: Influenced choice affecting consent.
- **Effects on validity**:
  - Mistake: Unclear void/voidable.
  - Misrepresentation: Voidable (serious cases only).
  - Duress: Voidable.
  - Undue influence: Voidable.
  - Simulated civil marriages: Valid if intention to marry present (*Martens v Martens*).

#### 2.3 Lawfulness
- Instances of **unlawful marriages**: Must know various specific cases.
- **Degrees of relationship**:
  - **Consanguinity**: Blood relations.
  - **Affinity**: Relatives by marriage.
- **Basic rules**:
  1. Direct line relatives may not marry.
  2. Collateral relations prohibited if related within first degree.
  3. Affinity in direct line barred (e.g., ex-spouse's relatives).
  4. Affinity in collateral line allowed (e.g., marrying ex-spouse's siblings).
- **Calculation of degrees**: Use diagrams to visualize relationships.

### 1. VOID CIVIL MARRIAGES
#### 1.1 Definition  
- A void marriage means no legal existence.

#### 1.2 Grounds for Nullity  
- **Two Grounds**:  
  1. Non-compliance with formal requirements  
  2. Non-compliance with material requirements  
- Familiarity with relevant cases essential.

#### 1.3 Consequences  
- No legal recognition or effects of marriage:
  - Status remains unmarried
  - No property system
  - No duty of support
  - No inheritance rights
  - Children categorized as born to unmarried parents
  - Freedom to remarry

### 2. VOIDABLE CIVIL MARRIAGES
#### 2.1 Definition  
- A voidable marriage can be annulled under certain circumstances.

#### 2.2 Grounds for Setting Aside  
- **Seven Grounds**:  
  (a) **Minority**: Consent from parents/guardians required.  
  (b) **Material Mistake**: Identity or nature of the act.  
  (c) **Duress**: Coercion leading to marriage.  
  (d) **Undue Influence**: Manipulation affects consent.  
  (e) **Stuprum**: Pregnancy from another man undisclosed at marriage.  
  (f) **Impotence**: Inability to consummate; affects validity if undisclosed.  
  (g) **Sterility**: Concealment can lead to annulment.

### Comparison of Terms
- **Stuprum**: Extramarital relations pre-marriage; affects marriage under specific conditions.
- **Impotence**: Affects consummation; should be disclosed for validity.
- **Sterility**: Ability to have intercourse but unable to procreate; marital implications if concealed.

  5. Minors marry without parental consent against wishes.

- **Void Marriage** (1)
  - Never comes into existence (void ab initio).
  - Legal consequences:
    - Status of parties: unmarried.

- **General**: Void but carries some legal consequences of valid marriage.

# 4.2 Liabilities

- **(a) General**
  - All debts incurred before or during marriage in community of property form part of the joint estate.
  - **Nedbank v Van Zyl**: Spouses are co-debtors for debts incurred.

- **(b) Antenuptial debts**
  - Antenuptial debts are liabilities incurred before marriage.
  - Upon marriage in community of property, these become joint debts, including contractual and maintenance liabilities.
  - Uncertainty exists regarding antenuptial delictual debts becoming joint debts upon marriage.

- **(c) Debts incurred during the subsistence of the marriage**
  - Different rules apply to contractual, delictual, and other debts:
  - **(i) Contractual debts**
    - Recovery can be from joint estate or separate estate based on consent and capacity.
  - **(ii) Delictual debts**
    - Governed by **section 19 of the Matrimonial Property Act**; applies to delicts against third parties or each other.
  - **(iii) Other separate debts**
    - Current legal position on criminal fines and separate debts is uncertain; **section 19** likely applicable.

- **(d) Debts which are outstanding at the dissolution of the joint estate**
  - Differentiate regarding contractual, delictual, and separate debts:
  - **(i) Contractual debts**
    - Distinction between debts incurred before or during marriage.
  - **(ii) Delictual and other separate debts**
    - Only original debtor liable for outstanding debts upon dissolution.

# 5 ADMINISTRATION OF THE JOINT ESTATE

- **5.1 Abolition of the marital power**
  - **Section 11 of the Matrimonial Property Act** abolished marital power, establishing equal control over the joint estate.

- **5.2 The principle of equal administration of the joint estate**
  - **Section 14 of the Matrimonial Property Act**: Both spouses have equal capacity for juristic acts binding the joint estate with necessary consent.

- **5.3 Acts for which the consent of both spouses is required**
  - **Sections 15(2), 15(3), 17(1)**: Forms of consent required include:
    - Prior written consent (attested)
    - Written consent (attested)
    - Written consent (no additional requirements)
    - Oral or tacit consent 
    - Know specific juristic acts for each form.

- **5.4 Acts for which the other spouse’s consent is unnecessary**
  - Section 15(6): Acts in the ordinary course of profession, trade, or business; courts interpret this requirement.

- **5.5 Protective measures in respect of the administration of the joint estate**
  - **(a) Protection of third parties**
    - **Bona fide vs. mala fide** third parties: 
      - **Bona fide**: Transaction valid if unaware of consent requirement (section 15(9)).
      - **Mala fide**: Transaction may be void based on **case law** (e.g., *Amalgamated Bank of South Africa Bpk v Lydenburg Passassiersdienste*).
  - **(b) Protection of the spouses between themselves**
    - **(i) Statutory remedies**
      - Remedies include right to adjustment upon dissolution, suspension of powers regarding the joint estate.
    - **(ii) Common-law remedies**
      - **Interdict**: Requirements include:
        - Threat to alienate joint estate assets.
        - Intent to prejudice other spouse.
        - Risk of loss if interdict not granted.
      - **Actio Pauliana utilis**:
        - Alienated assets.
        - Fraudulent intent by the alienating spouse.
        - Knowledge of fraud by third party.
      - **Right of recourse**: Available post-dissolution under specific conditions; includes fraud intent.
      - **Prodigal spouse declaration**: Current constitutional doubts discussed.

### 5. THE ACCRUAL SYSTEM
#### 5.1 Introduction
- Reason for legislative introduction of the accrual system: Promote fairness in asset sharing at dissolution.

#### 5.2 Marriages to Which the Accrual System Applies
- Applicable to:
  - Marriages out of community without expressly excluding accrual.
  - Introduced accrual via documentation.

#### 5.3 When and How Accrual Sharing Occurs
- **General**:
  - Accrual is postponed community of profit.
  - Post-dissolution sharing of assets accumulated during marriage, owned separately during subsistence.

- **Accrual Claim**:
  - Difference between accruals determines claims at dissolution (Example: H: R50,000; W: R10,000 → W claims R20,000).

- **Claim Distinction**:
  - Claims arise only at marriage dissolution.
  - Spouses’ claims are not transferable or attachable during marriage.

- **Difference in Sharing**:
  - Accrual sharing does not grant rights to each other’s assets.
  - No pre-emptive claims on assets pending divorce.

#### 5.4 Calculating the Accrual
- Reference: Pages 94-99 in the textbook.
- Determine estate values at marriage dissolution as per **Brookstein v Brookstein [2016] ZASCA 40**.

1. Calculate **Net End Value** of each estate.
2. Determine **Net Commencement Value** and adjust using CPI.
3. Exclude assets not part of the accrual (Sections 4 & 5, Matrimonial Property Act).
   - Distinction: **Patrimonial vs Non-patrimonial losses**.
  
4. Calculate **Accrual**:
   - Assess smaller and larger accruals then derive claims.
   - Formula: Accrual Claim = (Larger Accrual - Smaller Accrual) / 2.

# Study Notes on Maintenance Orders and Child Interests in Divorce

## 2.3 The movement towards rehabilitative maintenance or no maintenance at all
- Women cannot rely on lifelong maintenance from marriage.
- Permanent maintenance orders rarely granted.
- Women in paid employment expected to self-maintain; non-employed women expected to find or increase paid work post-divorce.
- Rehabilitative maintenance defined; relevant cases include **Botha v Botha** and **Kooverjee v Kooverjee**.
- Clean-break principle often overlooks long-term impacts of domestic and childcare responsibilities.

## 2.4 Lump-sum maintenance
- Historically, courts hesitant to allow lump-sum maintenance.
- Current permissibility of lump-sum maintenance established through cases **Zwiegelaar v Zwiegelaar** and **Oshry v Feldman**.

## 2.5 Token or nominal maintenance
- Token maintenance may be awarded even if not needed immediately, to provide future support.
- Allowed under circumstances outlined in section 7(2) of the Divorce Act.
- Relevant cases: **Qoza v Qoza**, **Buttner v Buttner**.

- Generally indicates change in circumstances but specific statutory requirement absent.
- Courts reluctant to vary maintenance from settlement agreements.
- Examples:
  - Sufficient reasons may include fraudulent non-disclosure.

- Merges principles of customary law with those of civil law regarding marriage.

## 2. THE LEGAL REQUIREMENTS FOR A CUSTOMARY MARRIAGE  
- Differentiates between customary marriages before and after **15 November 2000**.  
- Validity of pre-Act marriages is determined by customary law; post-Act requires adherence to **Recognition of Customary Marriages Act**.  
- Additional requirements exist for minor’s customary marriages.

## 3. REGISTRATION OF THE MARRIAGE  
- Customary marriages must be registered, but non-registration does not invalidate the marriage.  
- Registration acts as **prima facie proof** of the marriage.

## 4. THE PATRIMONIAL CONSEQUENCES OF THE MARRIAGE AND CONTROL OF THE MATRIMONIAL PROPERTY  
- **Monogamous marriages**: Similar consequences regardless of the date of marriage; affirmed by **Gumede v The President of the Republic of South Africa**.
- **Polygynous marriages**: Different rules apply depending on before or after the Act.  
  - Pre-Act marriages follow traditional customary law.  
  - Post-Act requires a **court-approved contract** to regulate matrimonial property distribution.

## 5. THE WIFE’S STATUS  
- Wives in customary marriages have equal status to husbands, gaining full legal capacity.  
- Ambiguity exists about the equality among wives in polygynous marriages.

## 6. TERMINATION OF THE MARRIAGE BY DIVORCE  
### 6.1 Ground for Divorce  
- Sole ground: **Irretrievable breakdown of the marriage**.

### 6.2 The Consequences of Divorce  
- Patrimonial consequences align with civil marriage principles.  
- Courts empowered by **section 8(4)(a)** to redistribute assets for all customary marriages; **section 8(4)(b)** specific to polygynous marriages.

### 6.3 Joinder, Jurisdiction, Procedure and Interim Relief  
- Procedural aspects relevant to divorce under customary law outlined in relevant case laws and statutes.

## STUDY UNIT 17 - Life Partnerships

**DEFINED**: A life partnership is the relationship of couples living together without a legally recognized marriage. Lacks standard marriage consequences but has some recognition through legal mechanisms.

#### 2. LEGISLATIVE AND JUDICIAL RECOGNITION OF LIFE PARTNERSHIPS 
- Certain Acts treat life partners as spouses for specific purposes. 
- Key Acts confer benefits, including:
  - Medical scheme and job-related benefits (for heterosexual partnerships).
  - Limited court extensions of spousal benefits to heterosexual life partners (Volks v Robinson 2005 (5) BCLR 446 (CC)).

    - *Langemaat v Minister of Safety and Security*

    - *Du Toit v Minister for Welfare and Population Development*
    - *Centre for Child Law v Minister of Social Development*
    - *Gory v Kolver*

#### 3. THE CONSTITUTION AND LIFE PARTNERSHIPS 
- Most decisions regarding spousal benefits pre-date the **Civil Union Act**, which recognizes civil unions for both same-sex and heterosexual partners.
- Result: Same-sex life partners have more rights due to historical context.
- Assessment of constitutional acceptability of differential treatment between same-sex and heterosexual life partners.

# STUDY NOTES ON DUTY OF SUPPORT

### PURPOSE OF THIS STUDY UNIT
- Scope and calculation of the duty of support
- Duty to support: parents, grandparents, siblings; step-parent's duty unclear
- Enforcement of the duty of support
- Reciprocity of the duty of support
- Termination of the duty of support

### PRESCRIBED LEARNING MATERIAL
- Textbook pp. 303-310 (Maintenance)
- Textbook pp. 349-352 (Enforcement of parental responsibilities and rights)

### CONTENT OF THIS STUDY UNIT

#### 1. GENERAL
- Definition of maintenance: includes necessary expenses for child
- Factors influencing maintenance scope 
- Calculation steps for maintenance contributions: court aggregates usually specify total amount

#### 2. THE BEARERS OF THE DUTY TO SUPPORT A CHILD
- **State**: obligated only if parents/family cannot provide support
- **Parents**: support duty exists ex lege
- **Grandparents**: duty arises if neither parent can support; reforms on paternal grandparents' duties
- **Siblings**: responsible if parents/grandparents are unable to support
- **Others in care**: includes those under court orders or agreements
- **Step-parents**: traditionally not obligated under common law, but **Heystek v Heystek** and **MB v NB** challenge this, suggesting implied support obligation under constitutional rights.

#### 3. RECOVERY OF EXPENSES RELATING TO SUPPORT
- No right of recourse against children for maintenance provided
- One parent may recover excess expenses from the other parent

#### 4. THE RECIPROCITY OF THE DUTY OF SUPPORT
- Parents must support children; children reciprocate support
- Grandchildren must support grandparents likewise
- Conditions for support duty elaborated in the textbook

#### 5. ENFORCEMENT OF THE DUTY OF SUPPORT
- **Children’s Act, section 305(4)**: Legal obligation to maintain children; failing to do so is an offence

#### 6. TERMINATION OF THE DUTY OF SUPPORT
- Ends in the following instances:
  - Child’s death (not parent's)
  - Child's adoption
  - Child becoming self-supporting
  - Child entering marriage/civil union (still able to claim from parents/grandparents if unable to support)

- Distinction between termination of a maintenance order and duty of support detailed in **23.6.3 Termination of an order regarding a child’s maintenance** in textbook.

### SUMMARY
- Covered scope of duty of support
- Identified obligations of state, parents, grandparents, siblings
- Clarified care obligations
- Discussed step-parent obligations
- Reviewed reciprocity, enforcement, and termination of duty of support
