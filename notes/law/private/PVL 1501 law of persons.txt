# LEARNING UNIT 2
## Introduction to the law of persons
**Page 13 - 18**

### 2.1 INTRODUCTION
- Law of persons is a subsection of private law.
- Governs relationships between individuals in a community.
- Legal subjects are the core of law, vital for understanding other law branches.

**Key Concepts:**
- Legal subject: bearer of rights, duties, and capacities (e.g., contracts, ownership).

### 2.2 DEFINITION OF LAW OF PERSONS
- Studies the legal subject: identity, origin, legal status, and termination.
- Determines:
  - Different kinds of legal subjects.
  - How legal subjects originate and cease.
  - What legal status entails.
  - Effects of various factors on legal status.

### 2.3 DIFFERENT KINDS OF LEGAL SUBJECTS
- Categories: **Natural persons** and **Juristic persons**.
  - Natural persons: all human beings.
  - Juristic persons: entities recognized by law (e.g., corporations).
  
**Recognized Juristic Persons:**
- **General enabling legislation** (e.g., banks).
- **Specially created** entities (e.g., universities).
- **Common-law requirements**: continuous existence, rights and duties, non-commercial purpose (e.g., SPCA, TAC).
- **Partnerships**: not juristic persons.

### 2.4 LAW OF PERSONS, THE CONSTITUTION, AND TRANSFORMATIVE CONSTITUTIONALISM
- **Transformative constitutionalism** promotes social and legal culture transformation.
- **Sections of SA Constitution** (1996):
  - **Section 21**: Unmarried fathers' rights (Children’s Act 38 of 2005).
  - **Section 27(1)(c)**: Right to social security; child support grant example.
  - **Section 27(1)(a)**: Right to healthcare; ARV Treatment Programme as an example.

**Legal Culture Transformation:**
- Focus on values and policy in legal education; beyond formal interpretation.

### 2.5 LAW OF PERSONS AND CUSTOMARY LAW
- **Section 211(3)**: Courts apply customary law subject to Constitution.
- **Sections 30 & 31**: Cultural rights under the Bill of Rights.
  
**Definition and Characteristics of Customary Law:**
- Customs of indigenous African peoples as part of cultural identity.
  
**Key Characteristics:**
- Largely unwritten; exceptions exist (e.g., KwaZulu-Natal codification).
- Evolves over time.
- Underlying principles shared across cultures.

**Distinctions:**
- **Living customary law**: practices governing communities.
- **Official customary law**: formalized legal rules; can misrepresent actual customs.

**Relevant Legislation:**
- **Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998**: defines customary law.

### 3.1 INTRODUCTION  
- Legal personality begins at birth.
- Importance of understanding the **Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992** and its regulations.

### 3.2 THE BEGINNING OF LEGAL PERSONALITY  
- Legal personality starts only at birth; not considered a legal subject before being born alive.
- **Requirements for legal personality commencement:**
  - **Completion of birth:** Must be fully completed; severance of the umbilical cord is not required.
  - **Child's survival post-birth:** Must live after separation.
  - Viability, though mentioned, is deemed unnecessary by authority (Heaton p 7).

#### Key Legislation:
- **Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992**.

- **Determining whether a child lived after birth:** Medical evidence admissibility (Heaton p 7).
- **Viability definition implication:** Must be able to exist independently (Heaton p 7).
- **Objection to viability requirement:** It is a vague concept leading to evidentiary issues (Heaton p 7).

### 3.3 REGISTRATION OF BIRTHS  
- Notification of birth to the **Director-General of Home Affairs** is mandatory for every child born alive.
- **Registration Process:**
  - Both parents should register when possible; otherwise, the mother registers (if father is unavailable).
  - Procedures differ for abandoned children.
  
#### Important Aspects:  
- **Registration of births for married vs unmarried parents:** Procedures outlined (Heaton pp 8–12).
- Important sections of the **Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992** include:
  - Who needs to register births and by whom.
  - Registration name distinctions for children of married versus unmarried parents.
  - Conditions for changing registration details for children of unmarried parents.
  
#### Document Requirements:  
- **Documents accompanying the notice of birth:** Distinction based on timing:
  - Within 30 days.
  - After 30 days.
  - Child over one year old.
  - Procedure for abandoned children.

### CONCLUSION  
- Discussed requirements for legal personality commencement and viability's relevance.
- Explored implications of the **Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992**.

# Detailed Study Notes: The Interests of the Unborn Child (Nasciturus) (PVL1501/1)

### 4.1 INTRODUCTION
- Legal personality commences at birth; a fetus lacks rights, duties, and capacities.
- Law protects potential interests of unborn children via **nasciturus fiction**.

### 4.2 DEFINITION AND REQUIREMENTS
- **Definition**: Nasciturus is a conceived but unborn child. Also referred to as "fetus."
- **Fiction**: An assumption deemed true, treating the unborn child as a living person.
- **In Abeyance**: Legal position on interests held temporarily until the child is born or it's confirmed no person evolved from the nasciturus.
- **Requirement**: The nasciturus must benefit, implying joint benefits with a third person (e.g., a parent).

### 4.3 THE INTERESTS TAKEN INTO ACCOUNT
- The application of nasciturus fiction has expanded in South African law, protecting any conceivable interest.

#### 4.3.1 Patrimonial Interests
##### 4.3.1.1 Succession
- Distinction between **testate** (will present) and **intestate** (no will) succession is critical.
  
##### 4.3.1.2 Maintenance after Birth
- **Case**: **Shields v Shields 1946 CPD 242** - Court ruled that a mother cannot agree to waive maintenance responsibilities for her unborn child.

##### 4.3.1.3 A Dependant’s Action for Loss of Support
- **Case**: **Chisholm v East Rand Proprietary Mines Ltd 1909 TH 297** - Established that a child can claim loss of support if their parent dies before their birth due to delict.

#### 4.3.2 Prenatal Injury
- Law of **delict**: Concerned with unlawful acts causing loss.
- Prenatal injuries questioned – are actions for such injuries valid against the nasciturus?
- Resolved in **Road Accident Fund v Mtati 2005 (6) SA 215 (SCA)**: Future claims for prenatal injuries must operate under standard delict principles, not nasciturus fiction.

### 4.4 DOES THE PROTECTION OF THE INTERESTS OF THE NASCITURUS IMPLY THAT AN UNBORN CHILD IS SOMETIMES A LEGAL SUBJECT?
- Consideration of whether an unborn child may occasionally possess legal personality.

## 4.3 The Interests of the Unborn Child (Nasciturus)

### 4.3.1 Claim Against the Road Accident Fund
- Case: **Road Accident Fund appeal** concerning a claim for prenatal injuries.
- Legal question:
  - Claim for damages due to disabilities.
  - Use of nasciturus fiction vs. ordinary delict principles.
- Supreme Court of Appeal ruling:
  - Action for prenatal injuries is permitted under delict.
  - Nasciturus fiction not applicable in future claims; apply delict principles instead.

### 4.3.2 Guardianship and Care
- **Children’s Act 38 of 2005** replaces:
  - "Parental power" → "Parental responsibilities and rights."
  - "Custody" → "Care."
- Guardianship and care remain components of parental responsibilities and rights.
- Courts may address guardianship and care orders in divorce if the woman is pregnant.
- Parental responsibilities and rights cannot arise before birth; contracts for unborn child benefits necessary.

### 4.3.3 Termination of Pregnancy
#### 4.3.3.1 General
- Nasciturus fiction not applicable for termination of pregnancy; aborted fetus cannot be born alive.

#### 4.3.3.2 Circumstances for Termination
- **Choice on Termination of Pregnancy Act 92 of 1996** regulates abortion:
  - First 12 weeks: Woman's choice.
  - 13th-20th week: Additional requirements.
  - After 20 weeks: Strict circumstances.

#### 4.3.3.3 Consent
- Consent provisions under the Choice on Termination of Pregnancy Act:
  - Mentally able women can consent independently.
  - Mentally disabled or unconscious women need guardian, spouse, or partner for consent if <21 weeks pregnant.
- Case: **Christian Lawyers’ Association v Minister of Health 2005**:
  - Challenged minor's right to terminate without parental consent. Court held legislative framework ensures informed consent.

#### 4.3.3.4 Foetus’s Right to Life
- Courts historically viewed the foetus as lacking legal subjectivity and right to life before Bill of Rights.
- Case: **Christian Lawyers Association of South Africa v Minister of Health 1998** rejected the argument that the Act violated the right to life (s. 11, Constitution).
- Heaton indicates no conflict between rights of the unborn and pregnant woman; unborn has no rights pre-birth.

### 4.3.4 Sterilisation
- Study provisions in **Sterilisation Act 44 of 1998** (Heaton pp. 25–26):
  - Different provisions for majors, minors, and incapable persons.
  - Voluntary sterilisation permitted from 18 years of age.

### 4.3.5 Legal Subjectivity of the Unborn
- **Nasciturus Rule vs. Nasciturus Fiction Debate:**
  - Some scholars advocate the unborn as a legal subject from conception for interests.
  - Others argue nasciturus fiction protects interests without granting legal subjectivity.

## 5. The end of legal personality
### 5.1 Introduction
- Legal personality ends at death.
- **National Health Act 61 of 2003**: Provides legal definition of "death".
  
### 5.2 The End of Legal Personality
- Legal personality ceases at death (Heaton, pp. 27-28).

### 5.3 Proof of Death
- **Common-law vs. Statutory Procedure**: Differences in application for presumption of death.
- For established medical death, estate administration and marriage dissolution can proceed.
- Uncertainty about living status necessitates presumption of death to resolve legal issues.

### 5.4 Presumption of Death
#### 5.4.1 Common-law Procedure
- **Requirements**: 
  - Interested parties (spouse, child, creditor) can apply.
  - Facts for court consideration:
    - Age when missing.
    - Duration of absence.
    - Dangerous occupation status (Heaton p. 29).
- Court procedure includes setting a return date and publishing notice for interested parties (Heaton p. 29).

#### 5.4.2 Statutory Procedure
- **Inquests Act 58 of 1959**: 
  - Governs presumption of death when unnatural causes suspected.
  - Magistrate inquest findings ratified by High Court as presumption of death order (Heaton pp. 29-30).

### 5.5 The Effect of an Order of Presumption of Death
- The order creates a rebuttable presumption—it does not conclusively prove death, and can be contested (Heaton p. 32).
- Effects on estate administration and marital status detailed (Heaton, pp. 30-32).

### 5.5 Presumption of Death
- **Status of Person Presumed Dead**
  - Estate administered as if proven dead (**Heaton, p. 31**).
  - Rebuttable presumption: court can set aside order if evidence shows the person did not die.
  - Application can be made by interested parties or the missing person themselves (**Heaton, p. 32**).

### Mrs. Jonas Case
- **Dissolution of Marriage**
  - Under **Dissolution of Marriages on Presumption of Death Act 23 of 1979**:
    - Court may dissolve marriage upon request.
    - Separate application required unless at the time of presumption.
  - **Effect**: Marriage deemed dissolved by death legally, similar to actual dissolution (**Heaton, p. 31**).

### Inquests Act Consideration
- **Marriage Dissolution without Separate Application**
  - Section 2 of the **Dissolution of Marriages on Presumption of Death Act**: presumption expressed under **Inquests Act 58 of 1959** automatically dissolves marriage.
  - No separate application required; effective from court’s death date finding (**Heaton, p. 31**).

### Estate Division Upon Refusal of Presumption
- Court may divide estate among heirs even if presumption order is denied.
- Heirs may need to provide security for property return if missing person reappears (**Heaton, p. 31**).

## Page 44
### 5.6 Summary of Differences
#### Common-Law vs. Statutory Presumption of Death
- **Common Law**:
  - **When**: Missing person, uncertain status.
  - **Who Can Apply**: Interested parties.
  - **Burden of Proof**: Preponderance of probabilities.
  - **Court Authority**: High Court of domicile.
  - **Applicable Law**: Common law.
  - **Marriage Effect**: Requires separate application for dissolution.

- **Statutory**:
  - **When**: Death certainty not due to natural causes.
  - **Who Can Apply**: Judicial officer (Inquests Act).
  - **Burden of Proof**: Certainty beyond reasonable doubt.
  - **Court Authority**: Judicial officer following investigation.
  - **Applicable Law**: **Inquests Act 58 of 1959**.
  - **Marriage Effect**: Automatically dissolved if confirmed by the High Court.

### 5.9 Duty to Bury the Deceased
- Legal obligation for burial order prior to cremation or burial; adherence to wishes for funeral arrangements highlighted (**Heaton, p. 33**).

## CONTENTS
### 8.1 INTRODUCTION 
- Examines the status of children based on marital status of parents.
  
### 8.2 DEFINITION OF “CHILDREN BORN OF MARRIED PARENTS” AND “CHILDREN BORN OF UNMARRIED PARENTS”
- **Married Parents**: Child born of parents legally married at child's conception or birth. Defined by **Children’s Act 38 of 2005** and **Civil Union Act 17 of 2006**.
- **Unmarried Parents**: Child born to parents not in a valid marriage or civil union at the relevant times (Heaton p. 47).

### 8.3 CATEGORIES OF CHILDREN BORN OF UNMARRIED PARENTS
- Categories: Natural children, adulterine children, incestuous children. The differences are largely irrelevant in contemporary law (Heaton p. 48).

### 8.4 ARTIFICIAL FERTILISATION
#### 8.4.1 Meaning of “artificial fertilisation”
- Defined as introduction of male gamete into a woman's reproductive organs via artificial means for reproduction (Heaton pp. 48-49).

#### 8.4.2 Status of a child born from artificial fertilisation
- Child status determined by consent of both spouses/civil partners. If the mother is married and uses husband's sperm, the child is considered born of married parents, regardless of consent (Heaton p. 49).

#### 8.4.3 Relationship implications
- Discusses parental rights and responsibilities related to artificial fertilisation (Heaton pp. 49-52).

### 8.5 PROOF OF PARENTAGE
#### 8.5.1 Introduction
- **Children's Act** excludes certain individuals from qualifying as parents (Heaton pp. 52-53).

#### 8.5.2 Presumption of paternity
##### 8.5.2.1 Married Parents

## The minor’s contractual capacity – unjustified enrichment

### 15.1 INTRODUCTION
- Minors cannot be held liable in contracts without guardian assistance (refer to Learning Units 12 and 13).
- Adults contracting with minors have remedies, including unjustified enrichment.

### 15.2 UNJUSTIFIED ENRICHMENT
- Discussed in Heaton, pages 96-97.
- Understanding this remedy is crucial for mastering problem-type questions.

#### 15.2.1 What is unjustified enrichment?
- **General Rule**: A person (A) cannot enrich their estate at another's (B) expense without legal justification.
- Definition: A is unjustifiedly enriched at B's expense if A benefits financially without a recognized legal ground (Heaton p. 97).
- Minor (A) can be liable for unjustified enrichment even without a valid contract, as per **Edelstein v Edelstein 1952 (3) SA 1 (A)**. 
- **Key Concept**: Liability differs between valid contracts and unjustified enrichment claims. 

#### 15.2.2 How is the extent of the minor’s enrichment calculated?
- Reference: Heaton p. 97.
1. **Moment of Calculation**
   - Calculate enrichment at the time the claim is instituted, not when the contract was made.
   
2. **Amount of Enrichment**
   - Two calculations are necessary:
     - a. Value increase of the minor's estate due to other party's performance (actual value, not contract price).
     - b. Value decrease of the other party's estate due to performance (also actual value).

3. **Handling Circumstantial Changes**
   - If minor loses the performance or its value decreases:
     - a. No recovery if performance is gone.
     - b. Liability only for decreased value.
     - c. If sold, liability based on use of proceeds:
       - i. Proceeds remaining = liable for that amount.
       - ii. Proceeds used for necessaries = liable for the purchase price.
       - iii. Proceeds for luxury items = liable for value remaining of those items.

### 15.3 IN A NUTSHELL
- Be familiar with the distinctions between unjustified enrichment and contractual liability for minors.
- Understand how to calculate the extent of enrichment in various situations.

  - **Curator bonis**: Manages estate.  
  - **Curator ad litem**: Represents interests in legal matters.  
  - **Curator personae**: Cares for the individual.  
- Appointment of a curator does not eliminate all capacity to act. Reference: **Pienaar v Pienaar’s Curator 1930 OPD 171**.  

#### 20.2.6 Care and administration of the property of mentally ill persons  
- Heaton pages 124-125 explain property management for mentally ill individuals.  

### 20.3 INABILITY TO MANAGE ONE’S OWN AFFAIRS  
- Appointment of **curator bonis** for those incapable due to physical/mental disabilities.  
- Appointment does not terminate capacity to act; capability at transaction time determines validity.  

### 20.4 THE INFLUENCE OF ALCOHOL AND DRUGS  
- Intoxication includes both alcohol and drugs.  
- Actions void if individual is unaware of effects/consequences.  
- Burden of proof on claimant regarding intoxication; impacts capacity only during intoxication duration.
