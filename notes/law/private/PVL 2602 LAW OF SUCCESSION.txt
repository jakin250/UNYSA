## Chapter 2: Intestate Succession

### 2.7 Applicable Intestate Succession Laws
- **Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987, Section 1(1)(c)**: If intestate and survived by spouse & descendant, spouse inherits:
  - Either child’s share or statutory amount (currently R250,000) — whichever is greater.
- **Child's Share Calculation**:
  - Total estate value divided by (number of surviving children + number of spouses).
  
### 2.8 Order of Succession Under the Intestate Succession Act Read with the RCLSA
- Importance of understanding order in which intestate succession occurs.
- Spouse's inheritance based on comparison between child’s share and statutory amount.

#### Examination Guidance
- Ability to divide intestate estate based on established rules is crucial.
  
#### Case Example
- **Xavier's Estate**:
  - Joint estate of R800,000 divided by 2 for community property, spouse Wendy entitled to R400,000.
  - Child’s portion: R800,000 ÷ 2 = R400,000; statutory amount is R250,000.
  - Wendy inherits R250,000; Sam inherits remaining R150,000.
  
### 2.9 Disqualification and Repudiation
- Discusses consequences when an intestate heir is disqualified or repudiates their inheritance.
- Reference: **Intestate Succession Act Sections 1(6) & 1(7)**; correspond with **Wills Act Sections 2C(1) & (2)**.

### 2.10 Customary Law of Succession
- Customary law largely abolished by **Bhe case & RCLSA** (effective Sept 20, 2010).
- Testators may still dictate estate devolution per customary law in wills.

##### 7.1 INTRODUCTION – PERSONS CAPABLE OF INHERITING: JURISTIC PERSONS
- Any person (natural or juristic), born or unborn, may be a beneficiary under a will.
- Common law disqualifies certain individuals, e.g., murderers (**Harris v Assumed Administrator Estate MacGregor 1987 (3) SA 563 (A)**) from inheriting.
- The Wills Act 7 of 1953 excludes individuals involved in execution processes from succession (see par 7.4.3).
- Discussion includes adopted, illegitimate, unborn children, and persons of unsound mind.

##### 7.4 PERSONS DISQUALIFIED FROM INHERITING
- A murderer cannot inherit under the maxim "De bloedige hand er neemt geen erfenis." (The bloody hand does not inherit) (**Ex parte Steenkamp and Steenkamp 1952 (1) SA 744 (T)**).
- The principle applies specifically to inherit from the victim; murderers can inherit from others.
  
###### 7.4.1 General Unworthiness
- Distinction: Only unworthy to inherit from specific victims, not universally unworthy.
- Inheriting from a victim's heir is permissible.

###### 7.4.2 Intestate and Testate Succession
- In intestate succession, a grandchild cannot inherit from a grandfather if the child (father) was predeceased and the grandchild murdered the grandfather.
- **Steenkamp case:** excludes murderers of closer relatives (parent, child, spouse). No exclusion for distant relatives (e.g., grandparent).

###### 7.4.3 Causal Relationship
- Exclusion applies if the murder results in direct benefit; assessed under delictual liability.
- **Ex parte Steenkamp:** test of a usual, natural, or reasonably foreseeable result.

###### 7.4.4 Negligent Causes of Death
- Negligently causing death also disqualifies, but moral reprehensibility is a consideration (**L. Taylor v A.E. Pim (1903) 24 NLR 484; Casey v The Master 1992 (4) SA 505 (N)**).

###### 7.4.5 Wills Act Provisions
- **Section 4A:** Disqualifies individuals involved in will execution; exceptions apply under (**Wills Act 7 of 1953**).

##### 7.5 CUSTOMARY LAW
- Customary rules apply similarly to exclusion under testate and intestate succession, with specific additional principles influencing inheritance.

### SUMMARY
- Beneficiaries can be disqualified from inheriting; differentiation is critical between intestate and testate succession.
- Important to understand circumstances leading to disqualification.

## CHAPTER 9: Modus and Estate Massing

### Par 9.5: Modus or Obligation
- **Definition:** A testator may obligate a beneficiary to use a bequest for a specific purpose, known as a modus.
- **Key Point:** Compliance with the modus does not affect the vesting of the beneficiary's rights; it imposes a personal obligation.
- **Reasons for Imposing a Modus:**
  - Varying motivations, detailed in par 9.5.1.
  - **Enforceability:** Not all objects of a modus are enforceable.
- **Distinctions:**
  - Modus vs. suspensive condition.
  - Modus vs. terminative condition discussed in par 9.5.2.

### Par 9.6: Estate Massing
- **Definition:** Estate massing is commonly found in joint wills, particularly for couples married in community of property, but can exist in other contexts.
- **Examples:** Joint wills that mass estates (e.g., husband and wife leave property to a son with usufruct for the surviving spouse).
- **Acceptance:** Surviving spouse must accept the usufruct for massing to take effect.
- **Requirements for Massing:**
  - Discussed in par 9.6; involves legal institutions like usufruct, fideicommissum, and trust.
- **Common Law Position:** Survivors had only personal rights against the property contributed, leading to issues for ultimate beneficiaries.
- **Legislative Change:** Section 37 of the Administration of Estates Act regulates massing (discussed in par 9.6.2).
- **Court Case: Rhode v Stubbs 2005 (5) SA 104 (SCA)** emphasizes the complexities and presumption against massing.

#### Rhode v Stubbs Case Summary
- **Facts:** Attie and Lettie Williams' joint will contained a clause about their property rights after the first-dying spouse's death.
- **Legal Question:** Did the mutual will mass their estates?
- **Court Findings:**
  - Acceptance of benefits under a mutual will does not automatically result in massing.
  - Requires clear intention indicating massing.
  - Presumption against massing in ambiguous cases.
- **Legal Principles Established:**
  - Interpretation of joint wills defaults to two separate wills until proven otherwise.
  - Freedom of testation is a fundamental right, reinforced by the presumption against massing.

### Summary of Important Concepts
- **Modus:** Personal obligation without affecting vesting.
- **Estate Massing:** Effected through mutual wills, requiring acceptance of benefits and specific intent for massing.
- **Presumption Against Massing:** Uncertainty in wills interpreted to favor individual testamentary freedom.
- **Case Significance:** Rhode v Stubbs highlights the importance of clarity in joint wills and the interpretation rules ensuring testators' freedom. 

### Key Takeaways
- Understand the distinction and implications of a **modus** versus conditions.
- Recognize the legal framework and requirements for **estate massing**.
- Analyze the precedent set by **Rhode v Stubbs** for future cases regarding joint wills and estates.

### CONTENT OF THIS CHAPTER
#### Par 10.1 INTRODUCTION
- Study of legal concepts in wills, including direct substitution, fi deicommissary substitution, usufruct, and common law right to accrual.
- Reference to figure 10.1 illustrating types of substitution.

---

#### Par 10.2 SUBSTITUTION
- Substitution occurs when a testator appoints a secondary beneficiary to inherit if the first is unable.
- Two types of substitution:
  - **Direct Substitution**: One beneficiary inherits; e.g., John inherits unless he predeceases the testator, then Peter takes.
  - **Fi deicommissary Substitution**: One beneficiary inherits first, then passes to another after certain conditions; e.g., a farm to son, then to grandson.

---

#### Par 10.3 DIRECT SUBSTITUTION
- **Types of Direct Substitution**:
  - **Express Direct Substitution**: Clearly stated in the will.
  - **Direct Substitution Ex Lege**: Implied by law; occurs under **section 2C of the Wills Act**.
  
- **Section 2C Details**:
  - 2C(1): Allows a child to renounce inheritance for the surviving spouse to inherit.
    - Applies only if the child renounces.
  - 2C(2): Permits representation of deceased beneficiaries' descendants.
    - Excludes cases of prior disqualification.
    
- **Direct Substitution Excludes Accrual**: Both express and implied direct substitutions negate the application of accrual rights.

---

#### Par 10.4 FIDEICOMMISSARY SUBSTITUTION (FIDEICOMMISSUM)
- **Case Study**: *Du Plessis v Strauss 1988 (2) SA 105 (A)*.
- A fi deicommissum is characterized by a succession of owners.
- Types include:
  - Conditional fi deicommissa: Coupled with conditions.
  - Fi deicommissum residui: Allows part alienation during a fi duciary's lifetime.
  
- **Tacit fi deicommissum**: Implied by clauses like *si sine liberis decesserit*.

---

#### Par 10.5 STATUTORY RESTRICTION ON THE FIDEICOMMISSUM
- The **Immovable Property (Removal or Modification of Restrictions) Act 94 of 1965** limits fi deicommissa of immovables to two substitutions post-original fi duciary.

---

#### Par 10.6 LEGAL POSITIONS OF THE PARTIES TO A FIDEICOMMISSUM
- **Table 10.2** outlines legal positions:
  - **Fi duciary**: Cannot alienate or destroy property.
  - **Fi deicommissary**: No vested right to inherit until survival of the fi duciary.

**Par 10.7 PRESUMPTION AGAINST FIDEICOMMISSUM**
- **Case**: Van Zyl v Van Zyl 1951 (3) SA 288 (A)
- Common law's stance against *fideicommissa*: Viewed as burdensome due to limitations on fiduciaries and *fideicommissaries*.
- **Presumption against substitution**: Develops only when testator’s intention is ambiguous regarding direct vs. *fideicommissary* substitution; not applicable to ambiguity between *fideicommissum* and usufruct.

**Par 10.8 USUFRUCT**
- Usufruct: Personal servitude where one party holds ownership (*dominus*) while another (*usufructuary*) has rights to use and benefit from the property.
- Example wording: "I leave my farm to my son. However, my wife is to have the lifelong usufruct of the farm."
  - **Son**: Vested right (dies cedit) in ownership; enjoyment rights (dies venit) deferred.
  - **Wife**: Vested right in usufruct upon the death of the testator.
- Contrast with *fideicommissum*: Usufructuary never gains ownership.

**Par 10.9 COMMON LAW ACCRUAL**
- **Case**: Lello v Dales 1971 (2) SA 330 (A)
- Right of accrual (*ius accrescendi*): Heirs/legatees inherit benefits from those who forfeit or are unable to inherit.
- Example: "I leave my farm to A and B." If A predeceases, B inherits.
- **Substitution exclusion**: If the testator indicates a substitute, accrual does not occur between co-beneficiaries.
- **Testator's intention** is pivotal for determining whether accrual happens; reliance on guidelines from will's language and circumstances.
- Three types of joinders influencing accrual: *joinder re*, *joinder re et verbis*, and *joinder verbis tantum*.

**Par 10.10 CUSTOMARY LAW**
- Emphasizes constitutional considerations in the context of wills.

  3. **Formality**: Must comply with will formalities.
  4. **Identification**: Trust property must be clear.
  5. **Purpose**: Trust's object or purpose must be clear.
  6. **Lawfulness**: Trust object must be lawful.

## Chapter 1: Administration of Deceased Estates

### Learning Unit 1: Initial Steps Post-Death

- **Executor’s Responsibilities:**
  - Identify and secure estate assets.
  - Notify creditors and heirs.
  - Prepare and submit inventory of assets.
  - Manage estate disputes and claims.
  - Distribute the estate according to the will or intestacy laws.

### Learning Unit 3: Liquidation of Estates

- **Methods of Liquidation:**
  - **Sale of Assets:** Selling physical property or assets to raise funds.
  - **Distribution in Kind:** Allocating specific assets to heirs rather than liquidating them.
  - **Payment of Debts:** Settling debts using estate assets.
  - **Court-Ordered Liquidation:** Involvement of a court if disputes arise.
  - **Voluntary Liquidation:** Executor’s decision to liquidate based on circumstances.
