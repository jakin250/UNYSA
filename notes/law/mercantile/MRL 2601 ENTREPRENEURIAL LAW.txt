# MRL2601/1

## Transformative Constitutionalism
- Long-term project involving constitutional enactment, interpretation, and enforcement.
- Aims to transform political, legal, social institutions, and power relations democratically.

## Ubuntu
- Based on African idiom "Motho ke motho kabatho" (I am because you are).
- Emphasizes community and sound relationships, allowing opposing views and interests.
- Advocates for a horizontal decision-making structure (not top-down).
- Impacts juridical concepts; recognized in **S v Makwanyane 1995 (6) BCLR 665 (CC)**.
- Harms JA in **Pharmaceutical Society of South Africa v Tshabalala-Msimang 2005 3 SA 238 (SCA)** defines ubuntu as mutual respect.
- Associated with treating people with dignity in **Koyabe v Minister for Home Affairs 2010 4 SA 327 (CC)**.

## Africanisation of Business Values
- **King Reports (2002 & 2016)** introduce African business values, notably ubuntu, emphasizing human service.
- In **South African Broadcasting Corporation Ltd v Mpofu [2009] 4 All SA 169 (GSJ)**, the court highlighted corporate governance importance, particularly in state-owned entities guided by section 195 of the Constitution.

## Corporate Governance and Ubuntu
- Directors must promote ethical standards, integrity, and leadership consistent with ubuntu.
- **Dikoko v Mokhatla 2006 (6) SA 235 (CC)** cites ubuntu’s foundation in social reconciliation.
- Ubuntu merges individual rights with community values, shaping corporate decision-making.

## Natural Justice Principle
- **Audi alteram partem rule**: Each party must be heard before a decision is made, reflected in ubuntu.
- Fairness in relationships mandates compassionate listening and the right to voice interests.

## Values of Ubuntu in Business
- Compassion, social justice, harmony, connectedness, integrity, transparency, due process.

## Constitutional Principles/Values
- **Section 8(2)**: Bill of Rights binds natural/juristic persons where applicable.
- Courts may develop the common law to uphold Bill of Rights' values per **Section 36(1)**.

### Examples of Ubuntu in Business Law
- Company names must respect dignity (non-offensive).
- Directors’ decisions must promote social cohesion, ensuring inclusivity (referred in **Mpofu**).
- Companies Act promotes compliance with the Bill of Rights; underscores human dignity and rights ( **Section 7(a)**).
- Directors are accountable for community and environmental impacts (Companies Act).
- Removal of directors requires adherence to procedural fairness ( **Section 71**).
- Protection for minority shareholders emphasizes fairness.
- Collective decision-making mirrors ubuntu's principles.
- Informal dispute resolution aligns with traditional practices.
- Contracts, including shareholders agreements, should honor humaneness and fairness.
- Disclosure principles enhance transparency.
- Consequences for misconduct reflect ubuntu justice.

## Legislative Context
- **Broad-based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003** aims to address racial ownership disparities.
- **Protected Disclosures Act 26 of 2000** provides whistleblower protections.
- Companies Act facilitates stakeholder rights in business rescues ( **Section 7(k)**).

## Constitutional Interpretation and Development of Common Law
- Constitutional values guide legislative interpretation; courts prefer broader protective constructions.
- **Section 39** mandates common law development in light of Bill of Rights principles.
- Courts adapt common law reflecting contemporary community needs.
- **Everfresh Market Virginia (Pty) Ltd v Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd 2012 (1) SA 256 (CC)** illustrates the requirement for good faith in contracts amidst traditional legal structures.

## 1. Legal Precedents and Common Law Development

- **Solutions t/a Orlando Service Station v Engen Petroleum Ltd & another 2016 (1) SA 621 (CC)**:
  - Pre-democratic legal precedents can guide current law; age does not justify change.
  - Common law must align with **section 39(2)** objectives; courts must assess necessary developments.
  - Example of common law development in case law: companies possess constitutional rights akin to natural persons:
    - **Right to Equality** - *Manong & Associates (Pty) Ltd v City Manager, City of Cape Town & another*
    - **Right to Reputation** - *Dhlomo v Natal Newspapers (Pty) Ltd*
    - **Right to Privacy** - *Financial Mail (Pty) Ltd v Sage Holdings Ltd*
    - **Right to Identity** - *University of Pretoria v Tommie Meyer Films*

## 2. Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

### 2.1 Definition and Purpose
- **Corporate Governance**: Regulates corporate conduct for stakeholder balance; ensures efficiency and profitability.
- **CSR**: Integration of economic, social, environmental imperatives into company activities, addressing diverse stakeholder expectations.

### 2.2 Stakeholders
- Stakeholders include shareholders, employees, communities, governments, regulatory bodies, suppliers, customers, NGOs, and the public.

### 2.3 Arguments Regarding CSR
#### 2.3.1 Against CSR
- Critics claim no direct link between CSR and competitive advantage; argue businesses should focus on profit, leaving social issues to the State.

#### 2.3.2 In Favor of CSR
- Benefits include:
  - Sustainable value generation through stakeholder relationships.
  - Preferential procurement and government support (e.g., BBEE).
  - Enhanced reputation, potentially leading to increased business opportunities.
  - Attraction and retention of quality workforce leading to better performance.

### 2.4 CSR Reflection in the Companies Act
- The Companies Act reflects CSR through:
  - Recognition of social and ethical concerns in corporate law reforms.
  - Promotion of economic and social benefits (section 7(d)).
  - Non-profit companies for social activities (section 1).
  - Extended locus standi for stakeholders to enforce provisions (section 165).
  - Appointment of a Social and Ethics Committee required for certain companies (section 72).

## 3. Globalisation

### 3.1 Definition and Characteristics
- **Globalisation**: Integration of nations through goods, services, information, and capital flows.
- Characteristics include increased globalisation, electronic communication, social and ethical sensitivities, evolving markets, and heightened competition.

### 3.2 Legal Responses to Globalisation
- **Companies Act** Objectives:
  - Enhance economic welfare globally (section 7(e)).
  - Consideration of foreign law for interpretation (section 5(2)).
- Influences of common law principles and statutes from jurisdictions like the USA and UK present (e.g., appraisal remedy - section 164).
- Regulations for electronic communication and proxy appointments point to increased sensitivity to globalisation.

## 4. Entrepreneurial Law and the South African Legal System

### 4.1 Key Legislation
- Main legislation includes:
  - **Companies Act 71 of 2008**
  - **Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984**
  - **Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988**
- Important related legislation affects companies:
  - **Insolvency Act 24 of 1936**
  - **Income Tax Act 58 of 1962**
  - **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995**
  - **Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003**
  - Constitutional requirement for compliance with Bill of Rights within company law. 

### 4.2 Conclusion
- The evolving legal framework is necessary to support corporate development in alignment with constitutional mandates and international trends.

### 1. Introduction
#### Important Terms
- **Legal Personality**: Juristic personality recognized by law, holding rights and liabilities.
- **Incorporation**: Formation of a company via registration with the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission.
- **Lifting/Piercing the Corporate Veil**: Process to disregard a company's separate personality to hold individuals liable.

#### Company Benefits
- Legal personality allows limitation of liability of company participants.
- **Companies Act**: Section 8(3) - No association can claim legal personality without registration as a company.
- Legal personality acquired by:
  1. Companies Act registration.
  2. Registration under other legislation.
  3. **Conduct** - excluded by Companies Act.

### 2. Legal Personality of Companies
- **Salomon v Salomon & Co Ltd**: Established separate legal personality implications:
  - Company is assessed separate from shareholders.
  - Company debts are not shareholder debts.
  - Shares grant interest but not proprietary rights in assets.
  - Only appointed representatives can act on behalf of the company.
  - **Ahmadiyya Ishaati-Islam Lahore (South Africa) v Muslim Judicial Council (Cape)** confirms company must seek redress.
  - Companies bear rights/duties under the Constitution.

- **Ngcwase v Terblanche**: Corporation as a statutory juristic entity without physical existence.

- **Dhlomo v Natal Newspapers**: Juristic persons hold reputation rights.

- **Financial Mail (Pty) Ltd v Sage Holdings Ltd**: Companies have rights to privacy and identity.

- **Manong & Associates (Pty) Ltd v City Manager, City of Cape Town**: Corporations possess rights as natural persons, including equality.

- Company personality ceases upon dissolution and deregistration.

### 3. Legal Personality of Branches and Divisions
- Divisions/branches do not have separate legal identities unless registered.
- **ABSA Bank Ltd v Blignaut and another** clarifies divisions are part of the company.

### 4. Trusts and Partnerships
- Trusts recognized as juristic persons per the Companies Act for limited liability, but not fully acknowledged as separate legal entities.
- Partnerships generally lack legal personality benefits; only occasionally seen as separate from members.

### 5. Disregarding Separate Juristic Personality
#### Introduction
- Exclusion from liability is not absolute; circumstances allow piercing corporate veil.
- Methods:
  - **Common Law**: Piercing the corporate veil.
  - **Statutory Provisions**: 
    - **Companies Act**: Section 20(9).
    - **Close Corporations Act**: Section 65.

# Chapter 3 of the Companies Act: Types of Company

## 1. Introduction

- A company acquires legal personality upon incorporation, which allows it to hold rights/duties separate from members.
- The principle of separate personality can be lifted in specific instances to prevent abuse.

## 2. Profit Companies

### Important Terms

- **Domesticated Company**: Foreign company registered in South Africa.
- **External Company**: Foreign company operating in South Africa.
- **Non-Profit Company**: Incorporated for public benefit, not for profit.
- **Profit Company**: Incorporated for shareholders' financial gain.
- **Public Company**: 
  - Issues shares to the public.
  - Can be listed on the JSE.
  - Must have minimum three directors, hold AGMs, and appoint auditor and secretary.
- **Private Company**: 
  - Restricts share issuance and transferability within its Memorandum of Incorporation.
- **Personal Liability Company**:
  - Directors and past directors liable for company debts.
  - Mainly for professional associations.
  - Requires statement in Memorandum of Incorporation.
  
### Key Points

- **Public Company (Ltd)**: 
  - Shares can be publicly offered and traded.
  - Required directors and compliance obligations (AGMs, audits).
  
- **State-owned Company (SOC Ltd)**:
  - Registered under the Companies Act, with majority provisions of public companies applicable, unless exempted.
  - Examples include Airports Company South Africa (ACSA).

- **Personal Liability Company**: 
  - Critically used by professions (e.g., attorneys).
  - Directors liable for debts "contracted" during tenure (as per **Fundtrust (Pty) Ltd v Van Deventer**).

### Legislation and Case Law

- **Companies Act, section 19(3)**: Limits directors’ liability to contractual debts, excluding delictual/statutory responsibilities.
- **Case Reference**: **Dadoo Ltd v Krugersdorp Municipal Council**, recognized distinct legal persons and necessity to lift corporate veil in fraudulent instances.
- **Cape Pacific v Lubner** and **Botha v Van Niekerk**: Established tests and guidelines for piercing corporate veil based on abuse or unjust enrichment.

### Reflection and Learning Outcomes

- Understand types of companies under Companies Act and responsibilities.
- Identify when legal personality may be disregarded.
- Be aware of the implications of separate legal identity upon company insolvency or misconduct.

### Types of Companies

#### Private Company (“(Pty) Ltd”)
- Prohibits public securities offering; transferability restrictions apply.  
- Unlimited shareholders (previously limited to 50 under Companies Act 1973).  
- Section 8(2)(b) of the Companies Act mandates the incorporation restrictions.  
- Can be formed by one person, requiring at least one director.  

#### Non-Profit Companies (“NPC”)
- Formed for social, public benefit, cultural activities; not profit-driven.  
- Minimum formation of three persons as directors.  
- Directors prohibited from financial gain, except remuneration.  
- Income/property non-distributable; upon liquidation, assets must transfer to similar-purpose entities.  

#### External and Domesticated Companies
- External company: a foreign entity operating in South Africa; must maintain an office in the Republic.  
- Registration as an external company due within 20 days of conducting business (form CoR 20.1).  
- Activities that do not constitute “conducting business” listed in section 23(2A) of the Companies Act.  
- Foreign companies can transfer registration to South Africa, gaining the same status as domesticated companies.  

### Reflection

- Distinct features: private companies restrict share issuance; public companies may list shares.  
- Registration through the Commission required for all companies, including foreign entities.  
- Ability to identify company types and advise on formation requirements.

## 1. Introduction
- **Common law vs. Companies Act** 
  - Common law restricts agency for non-existent entities.
  - **Section 21 of the Companies Act** permits pre-incorporation contracts.

## 2. Common law methods of concluding pre-incorporation contracts
- **General Principle**: Agents cannot act for principals that do not exist.

### 2.1 Cession and delegation
- **Definitions**: 
  - **Cession**: Transfer of rights.
  - **Delegation**: Transfer of duties.
- **Process**: Contract in the individual's name; rights transferred to the company post-incorporation.
- **Risk**: Requires consent from all parties for delegation; residual liabilities may remain with the original party.

### 2.2 Nomination
- **Process**: Includes a term for nominating the company upon incorporation.
- **Risk**: Company might decline the nomination or inability to fulfill obligations.

### 2.3 Option
- **Process**: An offer remains open for a specified time; can be ceded to the company post-incorporation.
- **Liability**: Original party remains liable unless specified otherwise in the option agreement.

### 2.4 Contract for the benefit of a third party (stipulatio alteri)
- **Process**: Contract includes provisions benefiting the future company.
- **Risk**: Company may not materialize or accept benefits, original party liable only if specified.

## 3. Statutory method of conclusion of a pre-incorporation contract (section 21)
- **Advantages of Common Law**: Avoids automatic personal liability for promoters if company fails to incorporate or ratify the contract.
- **Definition (Companies Act, Section 1)**: Pre-incorporation contract is a written agreement made before incorporation with intent for the proposed company to be bound.
- **Binding Conditions under Section 21**:
  - Contract must be in writing and executed by/for a future company.
  - Must be ratified or unopposed by the board within three months post-incorporation.
- **Liability**: Joint and several liabilities arise if:
  - Company not incorporated or contract rejected.

## 4. Registration of company names
- **Importance**: Name provides corporate identity and influences public perception.

### 4.1 Reservation process
- **Procedure**: Complete form CoR 9.1 and pay filing fee.
- **Criteria for Acceptance**:
  - Protects public from misleading names.

### 2. The Definition of a Share
- **Legal Definition (Companies Act, §1)**: Share as a unit of proprietary interest in a profit company.
- **Characteristics**:
  - Movable and transferable property, without nominal value.
  - Contributes to company's share capital.
- **Memorandum of Incorporation**: Authorizes share classes and numbers; Board may adjust authorized capital.
- **Case Reference**: *Standard Bank of SA Ltd v Ocean Commodities Inc* - Rights attached to shares include voting, dividends, and residual assets.

### 3. Classes of Shares
- Shares categorized by rights conferred: 
  - Voting rights
  - Information rights
  - Dividend entitlements
  - Asset claims on winding-up

#### 3.1 Preference Shares
- **Description**: Holders receive preferential treatment for dividends and capital upon winding-up.
- **Voting Rights**: Often limited; however, Companies Act grants voting rights for preferences amendments.
- **Types**:
  - *Cumulative*: Rights carried over if dividends are not declared.
  - *Participating*: May receive additional dividends after preference dividends.
  - *Preferential Right to Capital*: Right to repayment of invested capital upon winding-up.
  - *Convertible*: Convertible to another class of shares post-maturity.

#### 3.2 Ordinary Shares
- **Description**: Represent equity; dividends fluctuate based on company profits.
- **Rights**: 
  - Receive dividends after preference shareholders.
  - Right to vote, though Companies Act allows curtailment.
  
#### 3.3 Deferred Shares
- **Description**: Issue to founders; dividends paid only after ordinary shareholders, and exceeding specific thresholds.

### 4. Issue of Shares
- **Board Authority**: Can issue shares within the limits of the Memorandum of Incorporation.
- **Special Resolution Required**: 
  - Issuance to current/future directors or prescribed officers.
  - Issuance to related parties or nominees, exceeding 30% voting power.
- **Exempt from Special Resolution**: 
  - Underwriting agreements
  - Pre-emptive rights exercise
  - Proportional shareholding offers
  - Employee share schemes
  - Public offers
- **Restriction**: A company may not issue shares to itself.

## Chapter 14: Partnerships

### 1. Introduction
- Partnerships are one of the oldest business forms in South Africa.
- Easy and inexpensive to form; characterized by flexibility.
- Created via a partnership agreement, focused on profit generation.
- No separate legal personality; partners must maintain good faith for business continuity.
- Regulated primarily by common law; no specific Partnerships Act.

### 2. Important Sections of Legislation
- **Section 13(1)**: Sequestration of partnership estate dealt with separately from individual partners' estates.
- **Section 8(3)**: Non-registered associations not recognized as legal persons for profit.
- **Section 24H**: Partners are deemed participants in the partnership business, allowing legal actions in the partnership's name.
- References: **Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, Companies Act 71 of 2008, Income Tax Act 58 of 1962, High Court and Magistrate Court Rules**.

### 3. Important Terms
- **Co-owners**: Partners sharing ownership of partnership assets.
- **Joint Liability**: Shared responsibility for a debt/claim.
- **Joint and Several Liability**: Group members individually or mutually liable for obligations.
- **Partnership Agreement**: Contract outlining terms between partners.
- **Universal Partnership**: All property shared; includes current and future assets/obligations.
- **Particular Partnership**: Formed for a specific transaction.
- **Anonymous Partnership**: Limited liability for undisclosed partners not involved in operations.
- **Partnership en Commandite**: Limited liability up to fixed contribution amount, in exchange for a share of profits.

### 4. Definition of a Partnership
- Defined in **Pezzutto v Dreyer and others**: Legal relationship created by contract for mutual benefit and profit among partners making contributions.

### 5. Types of Partnership
#### 5.1 Universal and Particular Partnerships
- **Universal Partnerships**: Non-restricted to specific transactions (e.g., societas universorum bonorum; societas universorum quae ex quastu veniunt).
- **Particular Partnerships**: Specific project-focused (e.g., construction).

#### 5.2 Ordinary and Extraordinary Partnerships
- **Extraordinary Partnerships**: Limited liability for certain partners; includes:
  - **Anonymous Partnership**: One partner operates publicly; undisclosed partners share risks.
  - **Partnership en Commandite**: Liable only to the amount of their capital contribution.
  - **Special Partnerships**: Registered under repealed legislation; limited liability hampered by partner involvement in transactions.

### 6. Differences Between Partnerships and Companies
- **Legal Personality**: Companies possess separate legal identity; partnerships do not.
- **Formation**: Companies require incorporation under the Companies Act; partnerships can form by agreement.
- **Objectives**: Partnerships primarily aim for profit; companies can be non-profit.

### 7. Responsible Cases
- **Pezzutto v Dreyer and others (1992)**: Establishes partnership definition.
- **Bester v Van Niekerk (1960)**: Affirms conditions under which specific partnerships arise.

### 1. Company vs. Partnership

- **Impact of membership change:** 
  - Company: No effect on existence.
  - Partnership: Leads to dissolution.

- **Ownership of assets:** 
  - Company: Owned by it; no member property rights.
  - Partnership: Partners are joint co-owners.

- **Profit entitlement:** 
  - Company: Profits only via declared dividends.
  - Partnership: Partners share net profits directly.

- **Authority to bind:** 
  - Partners bind the partnership (mutual mandate); shareholders do not bind the company.

- **Membership requirements:** 
  - Partnership: Minimum two partners; 
  - Company: Some companies (e.g., private) can have one shareholder.

- **Identity of members:** 
  - Partnership: Partners.
  - Company: Shareholders (profit) or members (non-profit).

### 5. Advantages and Disadvantages of Partnerships

**Advantages:**
- Ease of formation.
- Diversification of partner skills/abilities.
- Increased capital accumulation.
- Minimal legal formalities.

**Disadvantages:**
- Personal liability for partners.
- Difficulty in disposing of interests.
- Potential for partner conflicts.
- Lack of continuity (can be mitigated by agreement).

---

### 6. Essentialia of a Partnership

- **Definition:** Elements necessary for a partnership agreement.
  
#### 6.1 Contribution by Partners
- Economic input required from each partner (money, property, skills) exposed to business risks.

#### 6.2 Joint Benefit
- Partnerships must aim for mutual net profit sharing. Unequal profit shares are permissible.

#### 6.3 Profit Motive
- Refers to material gains (as per **Ally v Dinath**). Non-profit entities (e.g., sports clubs) aren't partnerships.

#### 6.4 Valid Contract
- Requires intention of forming a partnership (e.g., **Harrington v Fester**). Validity hinges on essentialia.

##### 6.4.1 Other Legal Formalities
- No formal partnership registration needed. Any agreement (written/oral/implied) is valid unless specific formalities are agreed upon.

#### 6.5 Essentialia as an Aid
- All essentialia must be present to form a valid partnership. Intent of partners is crucial (refer **Pezzutto v Dreyer**, **Purdon v Muller**).

---

### 7. Legal Nature of a Partnership

- Partnerships lack separate legal personality; rights and obligations are those of individual partners.
  
#### 7.1 Insolvency
- **Insolvency Act:** Section 13(1) mandates separate treatment of partnership and individual member estates (refer **Michalow NO v Premier Milling Co Ltd**).

#### 7.2 Litigation
- Partners generally must sue and be sued jointly. Partnerships may also engage in litigation under their business name (see **Uniform Rules of the High Court: Rule 14**, **Magistrates’ Courts Act: Rule 54**).

---

### 8. Relationships in Partnerships

#### Important Terms
- **Essentialia:** Key contract terms distinguishing contract types.
- **Naturalia:** Implied terms by law.
- **Incidentalia:** Terms agreed upon by parties.
- **Actio pro socio:** General action enforcing partner compliance.
- **Actio communi dividundo:** Right for partners to demand fair asset division.
- **Fiduciary duty:** Duty to act in the best interest of the partnership.
- **Joint liability:** Collective responsibility for debts.
- **Joint and several liability:** Individual or mutual responsibility for debts.
- **Ratification:** Acceptance of unauthorized actions, validating them retroactively.

#### 8.1 Natural Consequences of Partnership Agreement (Naturalia)
- Profit sharing: Generally proportional to contributions; losses shared similarly.
- Representation: Each partner can represent the partnership.
- No compensation for contributions unless altered by agreement.
- Joint ownership of partnership assets.

#### 8.2 Relationship Between Partners
- Fiduciary relationship exists; utmost good faith required, per **Purdon v Muller**.
- Broad categories of duties:
  - Adherence to agreement.
  - Promote partnership interests; avoid personal conflicts.

### 8. Acquisition of Member’s Interest by the Corporation

  - Written consent from all members required before payment
  - Corporation must be solvent and liquid before and after payment

- Financial assistance for acquiring member’s interest also possible:
  - Written consent from every member
  - Corporation must be solvent and liquid before and after providing assistance

### 9. Internal Relations and Association Agreements
- **Key Terms**
  - **Association Agreement:** Non-compulsory internal affairs arrangement
  - **Internal Relationships:** Members inter se and with the corporation

- **Association Agreements:**
  - Not compulsory but advisable
  - Can regulate members’ business carry-on, decision-making, and payments

- **Regulatory Framework:**
  - Must be written, signed by all members, and kept at registered business offices
  - Not public documents; only accessible to members
  - Cannot contravene the Close Corporations Act; clauses contrary to the Act are void
   
- **Prescribed Case Law:**
  - J&K Timbers (Pty) Ltd v GL&S Furniture Enterprises CC 2005 (3) SA 223 (N)

### 10. Representation of a Close Corporation
- **Authority of Members:** 
  - Each member can conclude contracts for the corporation with outsiders (as per **Section 54**).

- **Doctrine of Constructive Notice:**
  - Does not apply; members act as agents regardless of specified authority.

- **Key Case:**
  - J&K Timbers (Pty) Ltd v GL&S Furniture Enterprises CC: Members act as agents of the corporation unless third parties know of any lack of authority.

### 11. Payments by Corporation to Members
- **Rules on Payment (Section 51):**
  - Payments to members require compliance with solvency and liquidity and written consent from all members.
  
- **Liability on Creditor Claims:**
  - If payments are due to creditors, standard obligations apply independent of Section 51.

### 12. Prohibition of Loans to and Security on Behalf of Members
- Loans/security to members require written consent from all other members (Section 52).
- Noncompliance renders loans/security invalid; consenting members assume liability.

### 13. Accounting Officer, Records, and Financial Statements
- **Financial Reporting Requirements:**
  - Annual financial statements required.
  - Appointment of an accounting officer instead of an auditor.
  
- **Accounting Officer Role:**
  - Report irregularities to members; notify Registrar of significant changes (removal, inactivity, exceeding liabilities).
  
- **Record Approval:**
  - Accounting records must be approved by members holding at least 51% interest; not submitted to Registrar.

- A close corporation is a separate legal entity, but courts may “pierce the corporate veil” for liability under certain conditions (Sections 23, 26, 42, 43, 52, 64, and 65).
- Liability arises from:
  - Omission of "CC" in name (Section 63).
  - Reckless trading harming creditors (Section 64), requiring proof of negative impact on creditor claims (L&P Plant Hire CC v Bosch).

  - Greater capital acquisition potential
  - Continuity
    
- **Disadvantages:**
  - No new close corporations under Companies Act
