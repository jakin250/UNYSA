# STUDY UNIT 2: THE CONTRACTS OF EMPLOYMENT AND ITS RELATIONSHIP WITH LABOUR LEGISLATION

## 2.1 PRESCRIBED MATERIAL
- Chapter 2 of Essential Labour Law

## 2.2 GENERAL INTRODUCTION
- Regulated by **common law**.
- If not covered by **LRA** or other **labour legislation**, common law applies.
- Validity requires satisfaction of common law contract requirements.
- Common law duties must align with principles of fairness and humaneness.

## 2.3 IMPORTANT MATTERS TO UNDERSTAND

### 2.3.1 Introduction
- Describes the employment contract's link to labour legislation and the significance of the **employment relationship**.

#### 2.3.1.1 Development of the employment contract
- Roman law distinction between:
  - **Locatio conductio rei**: hiring physical objects.
  - **Locatio conductio operis**: hiring specific work.
  - **Locatio conductio operarum**: hiring personal services (contract of employment).

#### 2.3.1.2 The continued importance of the employment contract
- Confirms employee status for labour legislation purposes.
- Serves as a source for terms and conditions of employment.
- Employees may choose to enforce either statutory or contractual rights.

### 2.3.2 Definition of the employment contract

#### 2.3.2.1 Essential elements
- Defined as an agreement where employee renders work for employer in exchange for remuneration.
- Key elements: **voluntary agreement, work, remuneration**.
- Reciprocal nature: employee provides labour; employer provides payment.

#### 2.3.2.2 The element of control
- Control does not require actual day-to-day oversight.
- Employer's right to control is sufficient.

#### 2.3.2.3 Concluding employment contracts

##### 2.3.2.3.1 Requirements for a valid employment contract
- Must comply with:
  - **Consensus** between parties.
  - Parties must have **legal capacity**.
  - **Performance must be legally possible**.
  - **Performance must be physically possible**.
  - Adherence to any necessary **formalities**.

##### 2.3.2.3.2 Negotiation and formalities
- Common law lacks recognition of power imbalance.
- Contracts typically presented as predetermined by employer.
- Written contracts not always mandatory, except where specified by statute (e.g., merchant seamen, learnerships).

#### 2.3.2.4 The common law contractual duties of the parties

##### 2.3.2.4.1 The common law contractual duties of the employee
- Duties include:
  - Tender services.
  - Work competently and diligently.
  - Obey lawful instructions.
  - Act in good faith.

##### 2.3.2.4.2 The contractual duties of the employer
- Duties include:
  - Remunerate the employee.
  - Provide work.
  - Ensure safe working conditions.
  - Fair dealings with employees.

# Study Notes on Employment Law

## 2.3.2.5 Other Terms and Conditions of Employment

### 2.3.2.5.1 Freedom to Contract and Basic Conditions of Employment
- **Legislation**:
  - **National Minimum Wage Act 9 of 2018 (NMWA)** - Establishes minimum wage entitlements.
  - **Basic Conditions of Employment Act (BCEA)** - Sets minimum standards for employee protection.
- Freedom to negotiate terms affected by NMWA and BCEA.
- Collective bargaining can modify individual employment contracts.

### 2.3.2.5.2 What Are Terms and Conditions of Employment?
- Employment terms derived from:
  - Agreement (verbal/written/conduct).
  - Common law implied terms.
  - Statutes.
  - Collective agreements.
- Key aspects include:
  - Job description.
  - Working hours and leave entitlements.
  - Termination notice period.
  - Remuneration details.
- Modification methods:
  - Individual agreements.
  - Collective agreements.
  - Legislative changes (e.g., Ministerial sectoral determinations under BCEA).

### 2.3.2.5.3 Restraint of Trade
- A clause restricting an employee from practicing their trade post-employment within a specific timeframe and area.
- Evaluated against public policy:
  - Factors: nature, extent, duration of restraint, and parties' circumstances.
- Protection interests include confidential information and trade secrets.
- Restraints continue after contract termination; raises questions of fairness.

### 2.3.2.5.4 Vicarious Liability
- Employers can be liable for employees' wrongful acts during their duties.
- Requirements:
  - Employment relationship.
  - Act within scope of employment.
  - Act is a delict.
- Links to societal norms; employers have control over employees and bear financial responsibility.
- Fairness of employer liability considered in context.

### 2.3.2.6 Breach of the Employment Contract
#### 2.3.2.6.1 Forms of Breach
- Breach can be serious (fundamental) or less serious.
- Distinction is crucial for determining remedies.

#### 2.3.2.6.2 Contractual Remedies
- Options for innocent parties:
  - Accept repudiation (terminate contract).
  - Seek specific performance (enforce contract).
- May also claim damages regardless of the initial choice.

## 2.3.2.7 Termination of the Employment Contract

### 2.3.2.7.1 Forms of Termination
- Lawful termination methods include:
  - Completion of contract.
  - Mutual agreement to terminate.
  - Termination due to insolvency.
  - Breach of contract.
  - Termination with notice.

### 2.3.2.7.2 Lawful Termination and Unfair Dismissal
- Lawful termination follows agreed contractual notice provisions or is executed summarily.
- Common law allows termination without justification if notice requirements met.
- **Labour Relations Act (LRA)** emphasizes fairness in dismissals:
  - Must comply with substantive and procedural fairness requirements.

### STUDY UNIT 3: THE MEANING OF EMPLOYEE

#### 3.1 PRESCRIBED MATERIAL
- Chapter 3 of Essential Labour Law

#### 3.2 GENERAL INTRODUCTION
- Importance of distinguishing between employees and independent contractors.
- Employment relationships are protected under labour laws; independent contractors are not but are protected by the Constitution and contract law.

#### 3.3 IMPORTANT MATTERS TO UNDERSTAND
- Focus on defining 'employee' and differentiation from independent contractors.
  
##### 3.3.1 Introduction
- Labour rights apply only to 'employees.'
- Distinction between employees and independent contractors can be challenging.

##### 3.3.3.2 The definition of 'employee'
- **Statutory Definition:**
  - Defined by **LRA** and **BCEA** as a person working for another or the State, receiving any remuneration, excluding independent contractors.
  - EEA provides a slightly different definition.
  - NMWA defines a 'worker' rather than 'employee.'
  - Wide coverage includes domestic and farm workers.
  - Excludes independent contractors.
  
- **Exclusions:**
  - LRA excludes members of the National Defence Force and State Security Agency.
  - BCEA excludes unpaid volunteers.
  - EEA excludes specific intelligence and security agency members.

- **Inclusions:**
  - BCEA's section 83(1) allows the Minister to classify persons as employees.
  - EEA includes applicants for employment against unfair discrimination.

##### 3.3.3.2.2 Distinction between Employees and Independent Contractors
- **Tests Developed by Courts:**
  - **Control Test:** Identifies extent of employer's control over work.
  - **Organisation (Integration) Test:** Determines if a person is integral to the organization.
  - **Multiple/Dominant Impression Test:** Considers various factors to form an overall impression of employment status.

- **Key Factors:**
  - Employer's supervisory rights.
  - Worker’s dependence on the employer.
  - Restrictions on worker's ability to seek additional employment.
  - Obligation to work specific hours or personally perform duties.
  - Payment structure (fixed rate vs. commission).
  - Provision of tools by the employer.
  - Employer's disciplinary rights.

- **Legislative Presumptions (LRA section 200A and BCEA section 83A):**
  - Control/direction over work or hours.
  - Participation in an organization.
  - Average of 40 hours of work/month for 3 months.
  - Economic dependence on the employer.
  - Provision of tools or equipment.
  - Exclusivity in working for one person.

#### 3.3.3.3 Non-standard Employment
##### 3.3.3.3.1 Introduction
- Permanent employees work under an indefinite contract; non-standard employment includes fixed-term and part-time contracts.
  
##### 3.3.3.3.2 Fixed-term Employees
- Employed for a specified duration, project completion, or occurrence of a specified event (e.g., seasonal or replacement workers).

## Chapter 3: Types of Employees

### 3.3 Categories of Employees

- **3.3.3.3.3 Part-time Employees**
  - Work fewer days or shorter hours than full-time employees.
  - Maintains 'employee' status under labour legislation; entitled to protections against unfair dismissal.
  - Often prone to exploitation.

- **3.3.3.3.4 Temporary Employment Services (TES)**
  - Defined in **Section 198 of the LRA**.
  - Refers to entities that procure or provide workers to clients on a fee basis.
  - Creates a **triangular relationship** between TES, client, and employee.
  - **Subsections 198(2)-(4)** outline obligations:
    - Employees provided by TES are deemed employees of the TES.
    - Independent contractors are not classified as employees of TES.
    - Joint and several liabilities for TES and client if TES violates:
      - Collective agreements.
      - Binding arbitration awards.
      - **Basic Conditions of Employment Act (BCEA)**.
      - Sectoral determinations as per BCEA.

- **3.3.3.5 Other Employee Categories**
  - Includes probationary, senior, junior, public servant, and private employee categories.
  - **Probationary Employees:** 
    - Skills assessed; three outcomes post-probation: permanent employment, extended probation, or termination.
  - **Senior Employees:** 
    - Retain 'employee' status despite managerial powers.
    - Protected under labour legislation for those below BCEA threshold.
  - **Public vs. Private Employees:** 
    - Public servants protected by both specific public service legislation and general labour laws.

### 3.5 Identifying the Employer
- Critical to identify the correct legal employer if an employee asserts rights violations.
- The **State** is the largest single employer.

# Study Notes on Labour Legislation

## 4.3 Overview of Labour Legislation
- Promotes fair employment terms through minimum standards legislation.
- Key legislation: 
  - **National Minimum Wage Act 9 of 2018 (NMWA)** - regulates minimum pay.
  - **Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 (BCEA)** - regulates other employment conditions.
- Additional provisions via ministerial and sectoral determinations for specific employee categories.
- **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA)** - promotes collective bargaining.

## 4.3.2 The National Minimum Wage Act 9 of 2018 and Employee Pay
### 4.3.2.1 Goals of the NMWA
- Came into effect on 1 January 2019.
- Objectives include:
  - Improve wages of lowest-paid workers.
  - Protect against unreasonably low wages (**Section 2**).
  - Establish a **National Minimum Wage Commission** for annual reviews.

### 4.3.2.2 Scope of the NMWA
- Applies to various worker types, specifics detailed in the prescribed book.

### 4.3.2.3 Provisions of the NMWA
- **Section 4(1)** establishes NMW at R20/hour.
- Key rights and obligations:
  - Entitled wage cannot be below NMW (Sections 4(4)-4(7)).
  - Employers cannot waive NMW; all agreements must comply with NMW.
  - NMW prevails over other agreements unless more favorable.
  
### 4.3.2.4 Interaction Between NMWA and Other Sources of Employment Terms
- State interactions detailed in the prescribed book.

### 4.3.2.5 Enforcement of the NMW
- Enforcement outlined in the BCEA.
- **Section 73A** allows workers to claim unpaid NMW through CCMA, process follows conciliation then arbitration.

## 4.3.3 The Basic Conditions of Employment Act and Other Employment Terms
### 4.3.3.1 Goals of the BCEA
- Aims to enforce the right to fair labor practices per **Section 23(1) of the Constitution**.

### 4.3.3.2 Application of the BCEA
- Further details are in the prescribed book.
- **Exclusions** from provisions include:
  - Senior managerial employees.
  - Employees earning above statutory thresholds set by the Minister (**Section 6(3)**).
  - Employees working below 24 hours/month.

### 4.3.3.3 Regulation of Working Time
- **Chapter 2** regulates working hours.
- Maximum ordinary hours: 45/week; 9/day for a 5-day work week.
- Overtime regulations (**Section 10**):
  - Requires an agreement; capped at 10 hours/week, 15 hours via collective agreement.
  - Paid at 1.5x normal wage or equivalent paid time off.

#### Meal Intervals and Rest Periods
- **Section 14**: Meal interval of at least 1 hour after 5 continuous hours.
- **Section 15**: Daily rest of 12 consecutive hours and a weekly rest of 36 hours, typically including Sunday.

#### Sundays, Public Holidays, and Night Work
- **Section 16**: Double pay for Sunday work if not regularly scheduled; otherwise 1.5x for normal hours.
- **Section 18**: Public holiday work paid at least double.
- **Night work** defined in **Section 17**: requires agreement; must ensure safe transportation.

# Study Notes on the BCEA

## 4.3.3.4 Leave
- **Annual Leave:** 
  - **Entitlement:** At least 21 consecutive days (calendar days) on full remuneration per 12-month cycle.
  - **Conditions:** Must be granted within six months after the cycle; payable upon termination (s40).
  
- **Sick Leave:**
  - **Cycle:** 36 months with entitlement to six weeks’ paid sick leave.
  - **First Six Months:** One day paid sick leave for every 26 days worked.
  - **Conditions:** Employer not liable for payment absent medical certificate for >2 consecutive days or >2 occasions in 8 weeks (s23(1)).

- **Maternity Leave:**
  - **Entitlement:** At least four consecutive months; not mandated as paid leave by BCEA, but regulated by **Unemployment Insurance Act**.
  - **Notification:** Written notice required; must commence no later than four weeks before expected birth (s25).
  - **Post-Birth:** Cannot return until six weeks after birth unless certified fit (s25(3)).
  - **Miscarriage:** Six weeks’ leave granted if miscarriage/stillbirth occurs during third trimester.

- **Family Responsibility Leave:**
  - **Entitlement:** Three days’ paid leave per annual cycle (s27); applicable during childbirth, illness, or death in immediate family.

- **Parental, Adoption, and Commissioning Parental Leave:**
  - **Unpaid Parental Leave (s25A):** At least 10 consecutive days for parents; effective from birth/adoption date.
  - **Adoption Leave (s25B):** Adoptive parents of children ≤2 years have similar entitlement.
  - **Commissioning Parental Leave (s25C):** Commissioning parent in a surrogate agreement entitled to at least 10 weeks.

## 4.3.3.5 Particulars of Employment and Remuneration
- **Section 29:** Employers must provide employees with certain particulars in writing; minimum wage set by **NMWA**.
- **Section 34A(1):** Deduction from remuneration for benefit funds must be paid within seven days.
- **Section 35:** Wage calculation based on normal hours worked.

## 4.3.3.6 Termination of Employment
- **Section 37:** Minimum notice periods:
  - One week for ≤6 months service.
  - Two weeks for >6 months <1 year.
  - Four weeks for >1 year.

- **Section 38:** Payments in lieu of notice allowed; notice must be in writing.
- **Section 40:** At termination, payment for unpaid leave and overtime due required.
- **Section 41:** Severance pay equal to at least one week’s remuneration per year of service if dismissed for operational reasons.
- **Section 42:** Entitlement to a certificate of service upon termination.

## 4.3.3.7 Variation of the Provisions of the BCEA
- **Variations:** Possible through collective agreements (in or out of bargaining councils), employer-employee agreements, or Ministerial orders.

## 4.3.3.8 Ministerial and Sectoral Determinations
- **Ministerial Power (s50):** Can replace/exclude BCEA minimum standards; can establish sector-specific conditions.
- **Sectoral Determinations:** Set basic employment conditions and remuneration rates within defined sectors.

## 4.3.3.9 The BCEA, Contracts, and Collective Agreements
- **Interactions:** Clarification needed to determine governance of employment terms:
  - Identify BCEA applicability for the employee.
  - Consider whether specific issues are regulated by BCEA or employment contracts.
- **Legal Standards:** Minimum standards cannot be contracted out; variation possible through contracts or collective agreements (LRA s23(3)).

# Chapter 5: The Meaning of Dismissal

## 5.1 Prescribed Material
- Focus on Chapter 5 of Essential Labour Law.

## 5.2 General Introduction
- Employment relationships end via planned/unplanned terminations.
- Types of termination: voluntary vs. involuntary.
- Dismissal as a drastic termination mode.
- Different dismissal types outlined.
- Fair dismissal requirements:
  - **Procedural Fairness**
  - **Substantive Fairness**
- Emphasizes fairness principles, aligning with Ubuntu values.

## 5.3 Important Matters to Understand

### 5.3.1 The Importance of Unfair Dismissal Law

#### 5.3.1.1 Protection of Development of Employment Law
- Focus on employee protection against unfair dismissal.
- Common law traditionally did not protect against unfair dismissal; lawful reasons sufficed for dismissal without a hearing.

#### 5.3.1.2 The Development of Current Law on Unfair Dismissal
- Only employers have the dismissal right.
- International law influence mandates fair exercise of this right (e.g., **ILO's Termination of Employment Convention 158 of 1982**).
- Grounds for fair dismissal:
  - Misconduct
  - Incapacity
  - Operational requirements
- **Audi alteram partem** principle: Employees must have an opportunity to be heard before dismissal.

#### 5.3.1.3 Unfair Dismissal in Terms of the LRA
- Section 185 of the LRA: Right to not be unfairly dismissed.
- Flowchart for assessing dismissal:
  - **Was there a dismissal?**
    - If Yes:
      - **What was the reason?**
        - Discrimination → Automatically unfair dismissal
        - Misconduct/Incapacity/Operational reasons → Fair dismissal if substantiated and procedures followed
    - If No → Unfair dismissal.

# Chapter 5: Dismissal and Onus

## 5.3.2 The Statutory Definition of Dismissal and Onus
- **Establishing Dismissal**
  - Difficulty in establishing dismissal.
  - Employer's burden to prove fairness: valid reason + compliance with pre-dismissal procedure.

## 5.3.2.1 Definition of Dismissal in the LRA
- **LRA Definition (§186)**
  - Termination with/without notice.
  - Non-renewal of fixed-term contract when renewal was expected on similar terms.
  - Denial of return to work post-maternity leave.
  - Unequal re-employment offers post-dismissal.
  - Constructive dismissal.
  - Less favorable transfer terms under §197 of the LRA.

## Chapter 6: Automatically Unfair Dismissal

### 6.1 Prescribed Material
- **Chapter 6 of Essential Labour Law**

### 6.2 General Introduction
- **Automatically Unfair Dismissal**
  - Defined as dismissal that is inherently unfair with limited defenses (inherent job requirements, retirement age).
  - Protects fundamental rights per the Constitution, including dignity and fairness.

### 6.3 Important Matters to Understand
#### 6.3.1 The Concept of Automatically Unfair Dismissals
##### 6.3.1.1 International Labour Standards
- Recognizes ILO Convention 158 (1982), which protects employees from dismissal for exercising rights.

##### 6.3.1.2 The Constitution
- Guarantees fair labor practices and prohibits discrimination (§9) while allowing for justifiable affirmative action.

#### 6.3.2 Automatically Unfair Dismissal
- **Defined in §187 of the LRA**:
  - Dismissals related to strike participation, refusal to perform work during strikes, refusals on mutual matters, exercising rights under the Act, pregnancy-related issues, arbitrary discrimination, transfers under §197, and breaches of the Protected Disclosures Act.
  - Exceptions for inherent job requirements and legitimate retirement age.

#### 6.3.2.1 Onus in an Automatically Unfair Dismissal
- Employer can rebut claims of automatic unfair dismissal by proving the dismissal was for misconduct or other fair reasons, except in specific exemption scenarios.

#### 6.3.2.2 Dominant or Proximate Cause
- Courts use a twofold inquiry (factual and legal cause) to determine dominant reasons behind dismissal when multiple reasons exist.

# Study Notes on Infringement of Freedom of Association

## 6.3.3 Infringement of Freedom of Association
- **Freedom of Association**: Protected by the **LRA**, Constitution, and international law (refer to par 6.2.3).

### 6.3.3.1 Protection of Freedom of Association
- Protections extend to job seekers; not limited to employment relationships.
- Employers cannot penalize or dismiss for exercising this right; such dismissals are automatically unfair. 

### 6.3.3.2 Managers and Freedom of Association
- Managers possess the right to freedom of association but must balance this with managerial responsibilities.
- Managers can hold trade union positions; however, failure to balance activities may result in disciplinary actions.

## 6.3.4 Participation in Protected Strike or Protest Action
- Employees are protected against dismissal during **protected strikes**.
- No protection for participation in **unprotected strikes** or criminal acts.
- Exceptions allowing dismissal during a protected strike: 
  - Operational reasons
  - Misconduct

## 6.3.5 Replacement Labour
- Employers sometimes employ replacement labour to mitigate production loss during strikes.
- Defined under **Section 76 of the LRA**; co-employees cannot be dismissed for refusing to perform work of striking employees, or it constitutes an automatically unfair dismissal.

## 6.3.6 Employees’ Refusal to Accept a Demand
- Employment contracts are based on mutual agreement; unilateral changes are not allowed.
- **LRA allows strikes or lockouts** to pressure demands; dismissals during lawful strikes are automatically unfair.

## 6.3.7 Exercise of Rights
- Referrals to the **CCMA** or courts cannot be grounds for dismissal; such actions lead to automatically unfair dismissal.

## 6.3.8 Pregnancy
- Female employees are protected regarding maternity leave and workspace adjustments.
- Dismissal due to pregnancy or intent to become pregnant is an automatically unfair dismissal.

## 6.3.9 Discrimination

### 6.3.9.1 Unfair Discrimination and Onus
- Protections are in place against unfair discrimination under:
  - **Section 9 of the Constitution**
  - **Section 6 of the Employment Equity Act**
  - **Section 187(1)(f) of the LRA**
- Dismissals based on listed arbitrary grounds (unless exceptions apply) are automatically unfair.

### 6.3.9.2 Specific Instances of Discrimination
- Categories of discrimination include:
  - Racial
  - Gender
  - Sex
  - Religious belief
  - Age
  - Disability

### 6.3.9.3 Arbitrary Grounds
- The LRA specifies 16 grounds for discrimination, not exhaustive; other grounds may qualify if they impair dignity comparably.

### 6.3.9.4 Exceptions – The Escape Clause
- Exceptions noted include inherent job requirements and normal retirement age.

## 6.3.10 Transfer of Employment Contracts
- Employment terms must remain at least equal upon transfer; adverse changes can lead to claims of automatic unfair dismissal or constructive dismissal.

## 6.3.11 Protected Disclosure
- Governed by the **Protected Disclosure Act 26 of 2000 (PDA)**; encourages reporting of criminal activity with protections against victimisation or dismissal.
- Whistle-blowers protected under automatic unfair dismissal provisions; disclosures must be made to specified individuals to ensure protection.

### Case Reference: Ngwanyana v Saab Bank
- Case illustrates the implications of dismissal due to pregnancy; highlights the problematic nature of policies requiring prior notice for pregnancy.

# STUDY UNIT 7
## DISCIPLINE IN THE WORKPLACE AND DISMISSAL FOR MISCONDUCT

### 7.1 PRESCRIBED MATERIAL
* Refer to Chapter 7 of Essential Law.

### 7.2 GENERAL INTRODUCTION
- **Constitutional Right**: Fair labour practices per **section 23** of the Constitution protect against unfair treatment and dismissal.
- **LRA**: Recognizes misconduct as a valid ground for dismissal; requires both substantive and procedural fairness.
- **Ubuntu Principle**: Emphasizes fairness and the opportunity for all parties to be heard in the disciplinary process, aligning with the **audi alteram partem** rule.
  
### 7.3 IMPORTANT MATTERS TO UNDERSTAND
- Focus on discipline in the workplace, dismissal for misconduct, and fairness.

### 7.3.1 Discipline in the Workplace
#### 7.3.1.1 Common Law and Employer's Right to Discipline
- Employment is based on mutual respect; breach may lead to disciplinary action.
- Employer may dismiss or impose other disciplinary measures; discipline is employer's prerogative.

#### 7.3.1.2 LRA and Code of Good Practice - Dismissal
- Employers must develop and share a disciplinary code outlining expected standards.
- Deviation from the code does not inherently render discipline unfair; fairness of the process is critical.
- **Progressive Discipline Approach**:
  - Options include: suspension without pay, verbal/written warnings, demotion, transfer.
  - Dismissal reserved for gross misconduct (e.g., dishonesty, violence).

### 7.3.2 Overview of the Disciplinary Process
- A clear reason for discipline must exist; must note contraventions of the code or common law.
- Two requirements for fairness: a rule breach and adherence to procedural fairness.
- Employers may deviate from the code under exceptional circumstances; corrective discipline is supported by courts.

### 7.3.3 Common Types of Misconduct

# Study Notes on Misconduct and Dismissal

## 7.3.3.4 Dishonesty and related types of misconduct
- Employees act deceitfully for undue benefits.
- Violates common law duty of good faith; damages employer-employee trust.
- Serious offence—may warrant dismissal.
- Examples:
  - Fraudulent qualifications.
  - Misuse of property.
  - Non-disclosure of criminal records.
  - Bribery and corruption.

## 7.3.3.5 Other types of misconduct constituting a breach of the duty to act in good faith and promote the interests of the employer
- Failure to report troublesome conduct breaches good faith duty.
- Employees must act in employer's interest; non-reporting may lead to derivative misconduct.

## 7.3.3.6 Violence and related types of misconduct
- Workplace safety is paramount—no employee should endanger others.
- Threatening safety leads to disciplinary actions, possibly dismissal.
- Violence may be justified in self-defense contexts.

## 7.3.3.7 Misconduct infringing on the dignity of other employees/persons
- Employees are expected to maintain mutual respect; avoid foul language.
- Consequences include disciplinary measures for offenders and loss of dignity for victims.

## 7.3.3.8 The abuse of alcohol and drugs
- Use or possession of drugs/alcohol is prohibited in workplaces.
- Breaches common law duty of diligence.
- Impairs physical/mental faculties; risks workplace safety.

## 7.3.4 Substantive fairness of dismissal for misconduct
- Valid reason must support dismissal; breaches existing rules or common law.
- Arbitrary dismissal leads to substantively unfair outcomes.
- Employers should create a code of good practice that outlines dismissal procedures.

### Guidelines for assessing dismissal fairness:
- Did the employee violate a workplace rule?
- Was the rule reasonable and known to the employee?
- Was the rule applied consistently?
- Was dismissal suitable for the rule's violation?

## 7.3.5 Procedural fairness of dismissal for misconduct
- Valid dismissal requires fair hearing process.
- Audi alteram partem rule: dismissal must be substantively and procedurally fair.

### 7.3.5.1 The elements of procedural fairness
- Conduct an investigation.
- Notify the employee of charges and investigation.
- Allow reasonable preparation time for the employee.
- Provide an opportunity for the employee to present their case.
- Allow representation from a co-worker or union member.
- Inform the employee of the verdict.
- Advise on rights to appeal to the CCMA or court if unsatisfied with the outcome.

### 7.3.5.2 Agreement to an enquiry by an arbitrator
- Employers and employees can agree to refer disputes to an arbitrator instead of conducting internal hearings.

## 8.2 GENERAL INTRODUCTION
- Constitutional right to fair labour practices: Section 23 of the Constitution.
- Employee’s right against unfair dismissal.
- LRA stipulates that dismissal must meet substantive and procedural requirements to ensure fairness and respect for human dignity, reflecting the ethos of ubuntu.

## 8.3 IMPORTANT MATTERS TO UNDERSTAND
- Focus: Incapacity as a ground for dismissal.

### 8.3.1 Incapacity as a ground of dismissal
- **LRA grounds for dismissal**: Misconduct, operational requirements, incapacity.
- **ILO Convention 158 (1982)**: Recognizes incapacity as inability, not fault of the employee.
- Requires both substantive and procedural fairness, including counseling and support for affected employees.

#### 8.3.1.1 Distinction among incapacity, misconduct, and operational requirements
- **Misconduct**: Involves rule violation by employee.
- **Operational Requirements and Incapacity**: No rule violation; dismissal based on business focus or employee capacity.
- Procedures differ; incapacity requires counseling, misconduct requires disciplinary hearings.

#### 8.3.1.2 Overlap: Substantive and procedural fairness
- Must have valid reason (incapacity) and follow correct procedures (counseling, guidance).

### 8.3.2 Poor Work Performance
- **Definition**: Employee lacks necessary skills or ability; not the employee's fault.
- Grounds for dismissal fall under incapacity.

#### 8.3.2.1 Substantive Fairness
- Valid accusations of poor performance can only be made against set performance standards.
- Guidelines to consider:
  - Failure to meet performance standards.
  - Employee's awareness and opportunity to meet standards.
  - Appropriateness of dismissal as a sanction.

#### 8.3.2.2 Procedural Fairness
- Dismissal for poor performance requires:
  - Appropriate evaluation and guidance.
  - Reasonable time for improvement.
  - Investigation of unsatisfactory performance.
  - Employee's right to representation.

#### 8.3.2.3 Employees on Probation
- Probation allows evaluation of performance before confirming employment.
- Employer must provide guidance; abuse may lead to unfair practice.

#### 8.3.2.4 Senior Managers and Highly Skilled Employees
- Relaxed rules for senior or highly skilled employees regarding counseling and guidance.

### 8.3.3 Ill Health or Injury
- Illness or injury as a valid reason for dismissal due to incapacity.
- Guidelines:
  - Investigation into extent of incapacity.
  - Employee representation during process.
  - Special consideration for work-related illnesses.

#### 8.3.3.1 Substantive Fairness
- Assessments of illness/injury necessary; implement supportive measures.

#### 8.3.3.2 Procedural Fairness
- Must investigate and consult with affected employee.

#### 8.3.3.3 Interaction between incapacity and disability
- Distinction: Disability leads to automatically unfair dismissal.
- Overlaps where incapacity results from disability after reasonable accommodations fail.

#### 8.3.3.4 Medical Boarding/Application for Ill Health Retirement
- Regulated by retirement fund rules or insurance benefits.

#### 8.3.3.5 Medical Certificate
- Under BCEA, employees absent > 2 consecutive days must provide a doctor’s note.

#### 8.3.3.6 Enquiry into conduct or capacity
- Legal requirement for employers to investigate incapacity due to illness/injury.

# Study Notes on Unfair Labour Practices

## 11.2 GENERAL INTRODUCTION
- An employee cannot commit unfair labour practices against an employer.
- Unfair labour practices occur during employment, distinct from dismissals.
- Legal protection for employees against unfair practices (promotion, demotion, training, etc.) under the **Constitution** and **Labour Relations Act (LRA)**.
- Constitutional protection against unfair practices is broad, while LRA provides a limited definition.
- Fairness as a principle rooted in ubuntu and the Constitution; emphasizes respect and human dignity in employer-employee relations.

---

## 11.3 IMPORTANT MATTERS TO UNDERSTAND

### 11.3.1 Relevance of Unfair Labour Practice
- Section 23(1) of the **Constitution, 1996**: broad right to fair labour practices.
- Employees need protection against unfair employer conduct during employment.

### 11.3.2 Definition of Unfair Labour Practices in the LRA
- **Section 185(b) of the LRA**: right to not face unfair labour practices.
- **Definition**: Any unfair act or omission involving:
  - (a) Unfair employer conduct related to promotion, demotion, probation, training, benefits.
  - (b) Unfair suspension or disciplinary action short of dismissal.
  - (c) Failure to reinstate/re-employ former employees as per agreement.
  - (d) Occupational detriments due to protected disclosures under the **Protected Disclosures Act, 2000** (Act No. 26 of 2000).
  
#### 11.3.2.1 Who is Protected?
- Only employees, as defined under **LRA**, have protection against unfair labour practices.
- Coverage may extend to ex-employees regarding re-employment refusals.

#### 11.3.2.2 Closed List of Disputes of Right
- **Section 186(2)**: exhaustive list of unfair labour practices; conduct must align with specified actions to be deemed unfair.

### 11.3.3 Unfair Conduct: Promotion and Demotion

#### 11.3.3.1 Basic Principles
- **Section 186(2)(a)**: protects against unfair employer conduct in promotions and demotions.

#### 11.3.3.2 Meaning of ‘Promotion’
- Requires an employment relationship and comparison between current and aspired positions based on remuneration, benefits, authority, job security.
- Legitimate expectation must be considered before denying promotion.

#### 11.3.3.3 Meaning of ‘Demotion’
- Definition includes reduction in remuneration, benefits, status, or job security.

#### 11.3.3.4 Unfairness of Conduct
- Requires both substantive and procedural fairness.
- Procedural issues may arise from non-compliance with internal promotion criteria or fair procedures.

#### 11.3.3.5 Remedies
- Range of remedies from **CCMA**: declaratory orders, protective promotions, compensation (max 12 months), and re-instatement.

### 11.3.4 Unfair Conduct: Probation
- **Section 186(2)(a)** encompasses unfair practices related to probation, excluding dismissals.
- Key elements include reasonable probation period, performance standards, and guidance requirements per the **Code of Good Practice: Dismissal**.

### 11.3.5 Unfair Conduct Related to Training
- Employer obligations devolve from contracts or collective agreements; failure to provide training could reflect unfair conduct, especially if contrary to employees' expectations or essential for advancement.

### 11.3.6 Unfair Conduct: Provision of Benefits
- Unfair conduct may arise from discrepancies in benefits (transport, salary reductions, etc.). 

### 11.3.7 Unfair Suspension
- **Section 186(2)(b)**: protects employees against unfair suspension.
  - Distinction between precautionary and punitive suspension.
  
#### 11.3.7.1 Suspensions and Unfair Labour Practice
- Both precautionary and punitive suspensions are captured under this section.

#### 11.3.7.2 Fair Suspensions
- Justifiable reasons required for substantive fairness; procedural requirements may stem from applicable regulations and agreements. Employers typically must continue remuneration during suspension.

# Section 6 of the EEA
- Protects employees against unfair discrimination.
- Section 9 extends protection to employment applicants.
- Applies broadly to 'no person' preventing unfair discrimination (employees, contractors).

## 12.3.3 Meaning of ‘Employment Policy or Practice’
- **Section 6** prohibits unfair discrimination in:
  - Recruitment procedures
  - Advertising and selection criteria
  - Appointments and appointment processes
  - Job classification and grading
  - Remuneration, benefits, and terms of employment
  - Job assignments
  - Working environment and facilities
  - Training and development
  - Performance evaluations
  - Promotions, transfers, demotions, and disciplinary measures (excluding dismissal)

## 12.3.4 Meaning of 'Unfair Discrimination'
- EEA does not define 'unfair discrimination'; courts interpret it.
- Differentiate between:
  - **Differentiation** (not prohibited by EEA) – acceptable if based on legitimate reasons.
  - **Discrimination** – occurs when differentiation is linked to unacceptable grounds.
- Not all discrimination is unfair as EEA addresses only 'unfair' discrimination.
- **Section 6(2)** provides defenses: affirmative action and inherent job requirements.

## 12.3.5 Direct and Indirect Discrimination
- **Direct Discrimination**: Clear treatment based on prohibited grounds (Section 6).
- **Indirect Discrimination**: Neutral policies causing disproportionate negative effects on specific groups.

## 12.3.6 Justifying Discrimination
- Employers may justify discrimination based on inherent job requirements.
- Affirmative action is considered ‘not unfair discrimination’ under **Section 6(2)**, typically based on race/gender.
  
# 12.3.3 Harassment as Discrimination
- **Section 6(3)** states harassment is a form of unfair discrimination, including:
  - Sexual harassment
  - Racial harassment
  - Sexual orientation harassment
  - Religious harassment

## 12.3.3.1 Definitions of Sexual Harassment
- Unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature including:
  - Physical
  - Verbal
  - Non-verbal
- Types:
  - Quid pro quo harassment
  - Sexual favoritism
  - Hostile work environment harassment

## 12.3.3.2 Conduct Constituting Harassment
- Conduct must reach a level of unacceptability.
- **Tests for Harassment**:
  - **Subjective Test**: Based on victim's perception.
  - **Objective Test**: Considers reasonable person standard; risks ignoring victim’s experience.
  - **Reasonable Victim Test**: Balances victim's experience with context.

## 12.3.3.3 Employer Liability for Sexual Harassment
- **Requirements for liability under Section 60**:
  - Conduct committed by another employee.
  - Constituted unfair discrimination.
  - Occurred in the workplace.
  - Reported immediately to the employer.
  - Employer awareness of incident.
  - Failure to take necessary steps to eliminate conduct.
  - Lack of reasonable measures to prevent harassment.

- Employees may choose to rely on Section 60 or common law principles (vicarious liability).

# Study Notes on Employment Equity Act (EEA)

## Chapter 12: Discrimination and Employment Equity

### 12.3.3 Sexual Harassment Policy
- Employers must adopt and effectively communicate a sexual harassment policy.
- Policy should include:
  - Clear procedures for handling complaints.
  - Sensitive and confidential handling of cases.
- Employer responsibilities:
  - Consult with relevant parties.

  - Provide advice and counselling to complainants.

  - Informal resolution.
  - Formal procedures.
- Grievances must be kept confidential.

### 12.3.4 Other Remedies for Sexual Harassment
- Victims may resign due to harassment and claim constructive dismissal.
- If proven, it may lead to automatically unfair dismissal (see **case law**).
- Compensation may be ordered.
- Victims can sue perpetrators for delict or employer for vicarious liability.

### 12.3.5 Medical and Psychological Testing
- **Section 7** of the EEA prohibits medical testing unless legislatively required or justified.
- Justifiable reasons include medical facts and job requirements.
- **Section 8** prohibits psychological assessments unless:
  - Scientifically valid and reliable.
  - Fair application to all employees.
  - Non-bias and certified by a professional body (this requirement struck down in **Association of Test Publishers of SA v President of the Republic of SA**).

- **Regulation 7:**
  - Defenses available to employers against equal pay claims.

### 12.3.7 Disputes About Discrimination
- **Section 10** mandates filing a dispute with the CCMA within six months.
- Referral must show reasonable attempt to resolve the issue.
- Jurisdictional considerations:
  - Sexual harassment claims go to the CCMA.
  - Other discrimination cases depend on employee earnings relative to the BCEA threshold.

### 12.3.7.1 The Concept of Affirmative Action
- **Section 9(2)** of the Constitution allows measures for advancement of disadvantaged groups.

  - Promote equality.

### 12.3.7.2 Goals and Key Concepts of Affirmative Action
- **Section 2(b)** describes goals:
  - Ensure equitable representation of designated groups (black people, women, people with disabilities).
- Definitions:

### 12.3.7.3 Scheme of the Employment Equity Act
- Designated employers must implement affirmative action measures.
- Requirements for rational implementation and compliance reporting.

### 12.3.7.4 Employment Equity Plan
- Consultation with workforce required.
- Topics for consultation:
  - Employment policies analysis.
  - Preparation and implementation of the plan.
  - Reporting to Labour Department.

### 12.3.7.5 Enforcement

### Chapter 16: Collective Bargaining

#### 16.3.1 Collective bargaining: an introduction
- Importance of freedom of association and organizational rights for collective bargaining.

#### 16.3.2 A duty to bargain?
- No legal duty to bargain in South Africa; unions cannot compel bargaining through Labour Court.
- The Labour Relations Act (LRA) encourages collective bargaining by:
  - Protecting employees' rights to form and join unions.
  - Granting unions organizational rights to strengthen bargaining power.
  - Allowing strikes to compel employer participation in bargaining.
  - Regulating collective agreements to enhance effectiveness of collective bargaining.

#### 16.3.3 Collective bargaining and the Constitution
- Constitution grants rights to engage in collective bargaining.
- No enforceable obligation on employers to bargain.

#### 16.3.4 What is collective bargaining?
- Defined as a voluntary negotiation process between trade unions and employers to reach collective agreements on wages, terms, and conditions of employment.
- Single employees cannot bargain collectively; only trade unions can represent them.
- Outcomes: successful collective agreements or industrial actions (strikes/lock-outs).

#### 16.3.5 Levels of collective bargaining
- **Plant level**: Negotiations at a specific factory.
- **Enterprise level**: Negotiations across all facilities of a single employer.
- **Industry/Sector level**: Negotiations within a sector in a geographic area.
- **More than one level**: Issue-specific negotiations at both sectoral and enterprise levels.

#### 16.3.6 Collective agreements and the law
##### 16.3.6.1 Definition of a collective agreement
- **Section 213 of the LRA**: Collective agreement is a written agreement between registered trade unions and employers regarding employment terms or mutual interests.
  - Valid without signatures; must be in writing.
  - Only registered unions can be parties; must address employment conditions or mutual interests.

##### 16.3.6.2 Recognition agreements
- Agreements acknowledging a union as a representative of employees for various purposes, enabling organizational rights.

#### 16.3.7 Bargaining councils
- Established by registered trade unions and employers' organizations for specific sectors.
- Can create binding collective agreements for entire sectors.

#### 16.3.8 The binding effect of collective agreements
- **Section 23 of the LRA**: Binds parties to the agreement, their union members, and can be extended to non-members.
- Collective agreements amend employment contracts, incorporating their terms.

#### 16.3.9 Disputes about collective agreements
- Must include a resolution procedure for disputes; conciliation followed by arbitration if unresolved.
- Disputes may go to the CCMA if no internal procedure exists.

#### 16.3.10 Agency shop and closed shop agreements
##### 16.3.10.1 Nature of agency shop and closed shop agreements
- **Agency shop agreements**: Require employers to deduct agency fees from non-member employees.
- **Closed shop agreements**: Require all employees to join the union.
- Addresses free-rider issues.

##### 16.3.10.2 Agency shops, closed shops and freedom of association
- Agreements potentially limit freedom of association; must comply with **Section 23(6)** of the Constitution.
- Valid agreements require adherence to limitations in **Sections 25 and 26 of the LRA**.

##### 16.3.10.3 Requirements for validity
- **Agency shop agreements**:
  - Must be concluded by registered representative trade unions.
  - Must include conditions addressing membership eligibility and fees (less than or equal to union dues).

# Study Notes on Strikes and Lock-Outs

## Chapter 18: Strikes and Lock-Outs

### 18.3 Legal Framework for Strikes and Lock-Outs

- **Protected Strikes and Lock-Outs**
  - Require compliance with prescribed requirements.
  - Employers cannot claim damages or interdict strikes; cannot dismiss employees for participating.
  
- **Unprotected Strikes**
  - If requirements are not met, employers can claim damages, obtain interdicts, and dismiss employees for misconduct.

### 18.3.4 Statutory Definitions

#### 18.3.4.1 Definition of a Strike
- Defined in **LRA Section 213**: 
  - Partial/complete concerted refusal to work or obstruction by employees to remedy grievances or resolve disputes.
  - Includes overtime work.

#### 18.3.4.2 Definition of a Lock-Out
- Defined in **LRA**: 
  - Exclusion of employees by employers to compel acceptance of demands regarding mutual interests.

#### 18.3.4.3 Prohibitions/Limitations on Strikes and Lock-Outs
- The right to strike is not absolute; can be limited.
- **Section 65 Limitations**:
  - Prohibition by collective agreement.
  - Mandatory arbitration stipulated.
  - Must refer disputes to arbitration or Labour Court.
  - Essential and maintenance services included.
  - Issues regulated by awards or agreements.
  - Existence of a dispute.

### 18.3.4.4 Procedural Requirements
- Must comply with two core requirements:
  - Dispute referred to conciliation.
  - Notice of intended action given.

#### 18.3.4.4.1 Referral of the Dispute to Conciliation
- Disputes referred to relevant bargaining/statutory council or **CCMA**.
- Conciliation occurs within 30 days.

#### 18.3.4.4.2 Prior Notice
- After conciliation failure, 48 hours' notice for strikes; 7 days if the State is the employer.
- Notice must be in writing; precise content not mandated.

#### 18.3.4.4.3 Refusal to Bargain Disputes
- Covers:
  - Refusal to recognize trade unions.
  - Withdrawal of recognition.
  - Must first be referred to conciliation and then advisory arbitration if unsuccessful.

#### 18.3.4.4.4 Strikes and Operational Requirements Dismissals
- Dismissal disputes not appropriate for strikes; should go to arbitration or Labour Court.
- **Section 189A** allows strikes for operational requirement dismissals in specific circumstances.

#### 18.3.4.4.5 Non-Compliance Scenarios
- No need for prescribed procedures if:
  - Dispute dealt with by council per its constitution.
  - Collective agreement mandates specific procedures.
  - Employer acts unilaterally on employment conditions.

### 18.3.5 Secondary Strikes

#### 18.3.5.1 Definition of a Secondary Strike
- Defined in **LRA**: Support strike not pursued if directly related to primary demand within the council's registered scope.

#### 18.3.5.2 Requirements for a Protected Secondary Strike
- Primary strike must be protected.
- Seven days’ notice given to secondary employer.
- Reasonable nature and extent of the secondary strike in relation to primary employer impact.

### 18.3.6 Legal Consequences of Protected Strikes and Lock-Outs

#### 18.3.6.1 Protection Against Civil Liability
- Participants in protected actions are not liable for unlawful acts or breach of contract.
- No civil proceedings against strikers or employers in protected strikes/lock-outs.

#### 18.3.6.2 Protection Against Dismissal
- Employers cannot dismiss employees for participating in protected strikes; such dismissals are automatically unfair.
- Dismissal for misconduct during strike is permissible.

# Study Notes on Employment Law

## Chapter 18: Strikes and Lock-outs

### 18.3.6 Payment of Remuneration
- **Common Law Rule**: “No work, no pay” applies during strikes/lock-outs.
- **Exception**: Employers must continue providing benefits in kind (e.g., accommodation, food) during protected strikes.
- **Recovery Post-Strike**: Employers may recover monetary value of benefits from employees through Labour Court.

### 18.3.6.4 Protection Against Discrimination
- **LRA Provisions**: Prohibits discrimination against employees exercising rights under the LRA.
- **Discriminatory Practices**: Payment/benefits for non-strikers seen as discrimination against strikers.

### 18.3.7 Legal Consequences of Unprotected Strikes or Lock-outs
#### 18.3.7.1 Interdicts and Compensation
- **Unprotected Strikes**: Non-compliance with sections 64 & 65 of the LRA leads to legal consequences.
- **Labour Court Jurisdiction**: Can grant interdicts against participation in unprotected strikes or lock-outs.
- **Compensation**: Court may order “just and equitable” compensation for losses, not necessarily full reparation.

#### 18.3.7.2 The Dismissal of Strikers
- **Fair Dismissal**: Participation in an unprotected strike can justify dismissal.
- **Substantive Fairness Factors**:
  - Seriousness of contravention.
  - Compliance attempts.
  - Employer’s unjust conduct.
- **Procedural Fairness Factors**:
  - Early contact with trade union.
  - Clear ultimatum with compliance timeframe; exceptions allowed if not reasonably feasible.

### 18.3.8 The Use of Replacement Labour
- **Replacement Labour**: Permissible during protected strikes unless maintenance service is designated.
- **Lock-outs**: Replacement labour allowed only if in response to a strike.
- **Prohibition on Other Services**: Prevents use of temporary employment services or contractors.

### 18.3.9 Picketing
#### 18.3.9.1 What is a Picket?
- **Definition**: Conduct supporting a strike or in opposition to a lock-out.
- **Regulated by**: Section 69 of the LRA and the Code of Good Practice.

#### 18.3.9.3 Protected Pickets
- **Requirements**:
  - Authorised by a registered trade union.
  - Must be peaceful and lawful.
- **Conduct Restrictions**:
  - No intimidation or coercion of non-strikers or public.
  - Prohibits unlawful acts (e.g., violence, threats).
- **Location**: Allowed in public access areas; on employer premises only with permission.

### 18.3.10 Strike Violence
- **Issues of Violence**: Recognized as a significant concern in South African strikes.
- **NEDLAC Agreement**: Initiatives aimed at reducing violence during strikes.

#### 19.1 Prescribed Material

#### 19.2 General Introduction  
- Protest action: A form of industrial action distinct from strike action.  
- Trade unions pursue socio-economic interests beyond workplace issues.  
- The LRA supports employees' rights to protest action as a method to advance these interests.  
- Trade unions' involvement in socio-economic issues aligns with the principle of **ubuntu**.  

#### 19.3 Important Matters to Understand  

##### 19.3.1 The Right to Protest  
- **LRA** aims: Economic development, social justice, labour peace, workplace democratization.  
- **Section 77 LRA**: Grants right to protest for workers (excluding essential service providers) to promote socio-economic interests if procedural requirements are met.  
- Protected protest action equates to rights conferred in **Section 67 LRA** for protected strikes.  

##### 19.3.2 The Nature of Protest Action  
- Defined in **Section 213 LRA**: "Partial or complete concerted refusal to work to promote or defend socio-economic interests, not for mutual interest matters (i.e., strikes)."  

##### 19.3.3 Procedural Requirements for Protected Protest Action  
- **Section 77 LRA** outlines procedural steps for protection:  
  - Protest initiated by a registered trade union or federation.  
  - Notice served on **NEDLAC** including:  
    - Reasons for the protest.  
    - Nature of the protest.  
  - The matter must have been considered by **NEDLAC** or an appropriate forum.  
  - A notice of at least 14 days to be served prior to commencement.  
- Adherence to these requirements grants protections similar to those for strikes.  
- **Section 77(2) LRA**: The Labour Court can issue an order restraining participation in protected protests.  
  - Considerations for declaring an order: Nature and duration of protest, union efforts to minimize harm, participant conduct.  
- Breaching an order forfeits protection against dismissal.

### 21.2 GENERAL INTRODUCTION

- **Dispute Resolution Objectives**: Key objective of the LRA; includes institutions like the CCMA, Labour Court, Labour Appeal Court.
- **Ubuntu Principle**: Emphasizes resolution of disputes for societal peace, akin to traditional Kgoros.
- **Methods of Resolution**: Conciliation, mediation, and arbitration; promote cost-effective methods over court proceedings.

### 21.3 IMPORTANT MATTERS TO UNDERSTAND

#### 21.3.1 Introductory matters

- **Disputes of Interest vs. Disputes of Right**:
  - **Disputes of Interest**: Aim to create new rights (e.g., higher wages, improved conditions); resolved by collective bargaining and right to strike/lock-out.
  - **Disputes of Right**: Enforce existing rights (e.g., unfair dismissals, unfair labor practices under the LRA, unfair discrimination under the EEA).

#### 21.3.2 Statutory dispute resolution institutions

- **Sources of Rights**:
  - **Legislation**: LRA, BCEA, EEA, Public Service Act 1994, Employment of Educators Act 76 of 1998.
  - **Constitutional Rights**: Section 9 (equality) and Section 23 (labour relations).
  - **Contracts**: Collective agreements and employment contracts.

#### 21.3.3 The resolution of rights disputes in terms of the LRA

- **Objectives**: Provide institutions and simplified procedures for labour dispute resolution (conciliation, arbitration, adjudication).

### 21.4 Statutory Dispute Resolution Institutions

#### 21.4.1 Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA)

- **Jurisdiction**: Established under section 115 of the LRA with functions to conciliate and arbitrate disputes.
- **Procedure**: Must attempt conciliation; if unresolved, arbitrate if requested.
- **Regulatory Authority**: Establish rules for dispute resolution processes (CCMA Rules - Gov. Notice R223, Gazette 38572).

#### 21.4.2 Bargaining Councils

- **Dispute Resolution**: Constitutions must outline procedures for resolving disputes among council parties; may include non-members within sector and area.

#### 21.4.3 Accredited Agencies

- **Accreditation**: Section 127 allows private institutions to perform dispute resolution if standards are met.

#### 21.4.4 Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court

- **Labour Court**: Established under section 151; functions include granting urgent relief, review powers, declaratory orders (Section 157 & 158).
- **Labour Appeal Court**: Appeals from Labour Court judgments; established under section 167, with jurisdiction similar to Supreme Court of Appeal (Section 173).

#### 21.4.5 Constitutional Court

- **Appeals**: Dissatisfied parties from Labour Appeal Court may seek appeal; options for direct access are limited.

### 21.5 Statutory Dispute Resolution Processes - Conciliation

#### 21.5.1 Principles of Conciliation

- **Initial Step**: Conciliation as the first step, aiming to resolve disputes without proceeding to arbitration.
- **Conciliator's Role**: Neutral third party assists parties to reach a mutually acceptable settlement without imposing a decision.
- **Representation**: Limited to directors, employees, or union representatives; independent legal representation is excluded.

#### 21.5.2 Difference between Conciliation and Facilitation

- **Conciliation**: Focused on resolving disputes; involves negotiations led by a conciliator.
- **Facilitation**: Primarily in retrenchment consultations; facilitator guides through agreed processes (Regulations issued Oct 2003 - Gov. Notice R1445, Gazette 25515).

# Legal Study Notes - Statutory Dispute Resolution Processes

## 21.4 Statutory dispute resolution processes - Arbitration

### 21.4.1 General principles of arbitration
- Referral to arbitration occurs post unsuccessful conciliation (CCMA certificate issued).
- Arbitration involves a neutral third party (e.g., CCMA commissioner).
- Arbitration awards are final, binding, and enforceable as Labour Court orders.
- **Legislation:** LRA (Labour Relations Act) regulates arbitration processes.

### 21.4.2 Special forms of arbitration
- **Advisory arbitration**:
  - Not binding; persuasive effect.
  - Required prior to strike action (s64 LRA).
  - Applicable in protracted strikes (ss150A-D LRA) and for relationship clarification (s200A(3) LRA).
- **Con-arb procedure**: 
  - Immediate arbitration post failed conciliation for unfair dismissals/unfair labour practices.
- **Inquiry by an arbitrator** (pre-dismissal arbitration): 
  - Employers can request an investigation by an arbitrator instead of holding internal inquiries.

## 21.5 Court adjudication
- Labour Court function akin to ordinary legal proceedings.
- Judges decide cases; appeals possible to Labour Appeal Court and Constitutional Court.

## 21.6 Jurisdictional issues

### 21.6.1 The technical nature of jurisdiction
- Jurisdiction determined by whether an institution can hear a matter.
- No inherent jurisdiction; statutory framework (LRA) dictates power.

### 21.6.2 Jurisdictional issues in unfair dismissal and unfair labour practice disputes
- Main issues: 
  - Employee status.
  - Time periods for referrals.
  - Jurisdiction delineated by s191 LRA based on dismissal reasons, with exhaustive unfair labour practices defined in s186(2) LRA.

### 21.6.3 Overlapping jurisdiction: bargaining councils and the CCMA
- Disputes referred to bargaining councils first; CCMA only if no jurisdictional council exists.

### 21.6.4 Overlapping jurisdiction: Labour Court and High Court
- Section 77(3) of BCEA allows choice of court based on reliance on legislation or contract.

## 21.7 Variation, rescission, review and enforcement of arbitration awards
- **s143 LRA**: Arbitration awards (except advisory) are final and certified for enforcement as Labour Court orders.
- **s144 LRA**: Awards may be varied or rescinded for ambiguities, errors, or absence of parties.
- **s145 LRA**: Reviews are distinct from appeals; focus on outcome acceptability rather than correctness.

## 21.8 The resolution of unfair dismissal and unfair labour practice disputes
- Primary disputes from the LRA concern unfair dismissals and labour practices (s191 LRA).

### 21.8.1 Automatically unfair dismissals
- Conciliation referral within 30 days post-dismissal.
- Labour Court referral within 90 days after failed conciliation.

### 21.8.2 Misconduct and incapacity dismissals
- Compulsory conciliation referral within 30 days for misconduct/90 days for labour practices.
- Defaults to con-arb process post-failed conciliation.

### 21.8.3 Constructive dismissal
- Same resolution process as misconduct unless it constitutes automatically unfair dismissal.

### 21.8.4 Dismissal after a transfer of employment contract
- Same as misconduct/ incapacity dismissals, differentiated when s197 or s197A LRA applies.

### 21.8.5 Unknown reason for dismissal
- Follow the usual procedure for misconduct/inability dismissals.

### 21.8.6 Dismissals for operational requirements
- Referral for fairness disputes must go to Labour Court post-failed conciliation (s191(5)(b)(ii) LRA).

### 21.8.7 Remedies
- Remedies for unfair dismissals/unfair labour practices include:
  - **Reinstatement**: Restores former relationship.
  - **Re-employment**: New relationship without previous rights.
  - **Compensation**: Awarded if reinstatement impractical (s193(2) LRA).
