## 1 UNIT 1
### Overview of the South African Land Registration System

#### 1. Introduction
- **Conventional Land Registration**: A meticulous government process recording ownership and real rights through conveyancing. Publicity through delivery or registration is essential for ownership transfer.

#### 2. Types of Land Registration Systems
- **Positive System**:
  - State guarantees accuracy of deeds register.
  - Legality examination of documentation and transactions.
  - Maintains a register of title linked to cadastral maps.
  - Transfers occur through annotated register rather than new deeds.
  - Liability for inaccuracies resides with the State.
  - Example: **Torrens System (New Zealand, New South Wales)**.

- **Negative System**:
  - No State guarantees on deeds register accuracy.
  - Records deeds face value; no prior legal examination.
  - New deeds required for each transfer.
  - Minimal State intervention; parties may insure rights.
  - Example: **Dutch Land Registration System**.

#### 3. South African Land Registration System
- **Statutory Basis**: Governed by **Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981** and **Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937**. 
- **Hybrid System**: Nominally negative, incorporates positive elements.

- **Components**:
  - **Deeds Registries**: Government offices registering real rights. Locations include Pretoria, Johannesburg, and others.
  - **Conveyancers**: Attorneys specialized in conveyancing, requisite qualifications include passing the Law Society exam.
  - **Land Surveyors**: Professionals with qualifications to identify and diagram land precisely for registration.
  - **Surveyor-General’s Offices**: Maintain cadastral systems (maps and diagrams).
  - **Local Authorities**: Require consent for land developments; must ensure payments of levies and rates prior to transfer registration.
  - **State Departments**: May need to consent for registrations, especially under laws like the **Subdivision of Agricultural Land Act 70 of 1970**.

## Overview of the South African Land Registration System

- Participants:
  - **South African Revenue Service (SARS)**: Proof of tax payment required for property transfer registration.
  - **Property Developers**: Involved in township establishment and land subdivision.
  - **Financial Institutions**: Finance land purchases, secure repayments via mortgage bonds.

- **State Involvement**: Significant oversight in land registration.

## Characteristics of Land Registration Systems

### Positive System Characteristics
- State examines documentation & transactions for legality.
- Maintains a register of title.
- Transfers executed via new deed of transfer (s16).
- Linked to a cadastral system of maps.
- Preventive State liability for shortcomings in specific situations.

### Negative System Characteristics
- No guarantees on the accuracy of the State register.
- Liability from the State limited to extraordinary circumstances.
- Over-reliance on deeds registry data despite lack of State guarantee.

- **Hybrid System**: South Africa's system reflects attributes of both positive and negative systems, securing title without guaranteeing correctness.

### Key Cases
- **Barclays Nasionale Bank Bpk v Registrateur van Aktes**: Transfer did not terminate mortgagee’s rights.
- **Standard Bank v Breitenbach**: A missing bond endorsement was still valid once registered.
- **Legator McKenna Inc v Shea**: Abstract theory of ownership applies; real agreements take precedence over defective original causes.

## Title Validity Issues 
- Registration does not ensure valid title. Example:
  - Expropriated land not reflecting current ownership until registered.
  
### Legal Interpretations
- Incorrect or incomplete titles do not negate bona fide purchasers' rights.
- State liability under **section 99 Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937**: Claimants must demonstrate loss from negligence or intentional misconduct by Registry officials.

## Public-Private Partnership in Land Registration
- **Conveyancer's Role**: Manages financial transactions, ensures legal compliance, coordinates with multiple parties (sellers, buyers, banks, authorities).
- **Registrar of Deeds**: Ensures compliance and registrability of deeds.

## Example Transaction Dynamics
- **Scenario**: Sale of mortgaged property with simultaneous loan and bond registration from multiple banks.
- Important Elements:
  - Valid deed of sale (s2(A) Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981).
  - Cancellation of existing mortgage bonds before transfer.
  - Coordination among financial institutions and local authorities.

## Conclusion
- The South African land registration system, despite being nominally negative, is reliable and widely used by third parties.
- Emphasis on thorough verification processes and the importance of a well-coordinated conveyancing process.

## UNIT 2: The Conveyancer

### 1. Introduction
- Understand conveyancer qualifications and duties.
- Integration of knowledge from previous unit on financial management and obligations.

  - Requirements to become a conveyancer.
  - Who can act as a conveyancer, prepare deeds, and lodge them?

  - General format requirements for deeds in deeds registry.

### 2. Origin of the Conveyancing Profession in South Africa
- **Late 17th Century**: Transferors engaged State Secretary's office for land registration.
- **19th Century**: Establishment of deeds registries across regions (e.g., Pietermaritzburg, King William’s Town).
  - Transition from provisional titles to official deeds due to legislative developments.
- **Cape Ordinance No. 14 of 1844**: Shifted deed preparation to legal professionals, leading to specialised conveyancers.

### 3. The Contemporary Conveyancer

#### 3.1 What Does It Take to Be a Conveyancer in the Republic of South Africa?
- **Section 102, Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937**: Defines conveyancer as practicing in the Republic, including certain Transkeian attorneys.
- **Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014**: Defines conveyancer; requires attorney admission and passing of national exams by the Legal Practice Council.
- Requirements for a conveyancer:
  - Specialisation in deed preparation.
  - Pass additional exams.
  - High Court admission and electronic enrollment in the deeds registry as per **Regulation 16 of the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937**.

#### 3.2 Duties of the Conveyancer
- **Diagram Reference**: Conveyancer's duties overview.

##### 3.2.1 Ensure Valid Agreement of Sale of Land (Deed of Alienation)
- **Section 2(1) Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981**: Requires agreements in a deed of alienation.
- Case references: 
  - **Thorpe NNO v Trittenwein (2006)**: Validity of sales and trustee actions.
  - **Legator McKenna Inc v Shea (2010)**: 
  - **Section 28(2)**: Validates alienations despite non-compliance if transfer is registered.

##### 3.2.2 Manage Financial Matters and Transaction Process
- Responsibilities include:
  - Ensure adequate funds/guarantees cover purchase costs.
  - Ensure purchase price sufficient to settle existing bonds.
  - Verify transfer duty and related fees are paid.
  - Provide undertakings for seller's obligations upon registration.
  - Present guarantees for collection on registration date.

##### 3.2.3 Prepare Deeds and Documents and Take Responsibility for Correctness of Facts
- Preparation involves thorough checking of contents and certifying correctness.
  - **Section 15A(1)** and **Regulation 44(A)**: Place responsibility on conveyancer, relieving Registrar from fact-checking.
  - **Section 15, Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937**: Mandates conveyancer preparation for various deed types.

### Conclusion
- Conveyancers play a critical role in managing property transactions, ensuring compliance with legal provisions, and facilitating effective conveyancing practices.

## 3.2.3 Conveyancer Responsibilities and Deeds Preparation

- **Initialing Requirements (reg 43(2))**
  - Conveyancer must initial all alterations and every page of non-signature deeds.
  - Responsibility for correctness necessitates initialing to verify information.
  - Minor errors may allow the executing conveyancer to initial amendments (per Registrar’s discretion).

- **Material Alterations (reg 44(2))**
  - Must be initialed by the preparer:
    - Changes to names, identity numbers, marital status of transferor/mortgagor
    - Date of sale, property description, purchase price, mortgage amount
  - Registrar determines if changes are minor or material.

- **Documents for Registration**
  - Must be prepared by either:
    - Attorney, Notary, or Conveyancer
    - Includes power of attorney, applications, consent to registration, partition agreements (Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937).
  - Preparer accepts accountability for correctness (reg 44A, section 15A(1)(2)).

## 3.2.4 Types of Deeds

- **Deeds Prepared by Conveyancer**
  - Deed of transfer, partition transfer deed, cession of mortgage bond, mortgage bond, application for correction.

- **Deeds Prepared by Attorney/Notary/Conveyancer**
  - Partition agreement, power of attorney, consent to cancellation of a bond, cession of notarial bond.

- **Preparation Clause Signing**
  - Only conveyancers generally sign preparation clauses.
  - Attorney/notary signatures must be countersigned by a conveyancer.

## 3.2.5 Liability and Responsibility in Deeds

- **Section 15A and Regulation 44A Responsibilities**  
  - Upon signing preparation certificate, conveyancer is responsible for:
    - Accuracy of document facts.
    - Identical copies on lodgement date.
    - Correctly carrying forward township conditions from previous deeds.
    - Confirming representative signatories are authorized.
    - Ensuring correct reflective identities and marital statuses.

- **Registrar of Deeds Responsibilities (Section 15A(3))**
  - Assumes facts signed by preparer are conclusively proved unless prima facie incorrect.
  - Duties align with conveyancers’ to create an effective registration system.

## 3.2.6 Deed Format and Compliance (reg 20)

- **Lodgement Requirements**
  - Use of good quality A4 paper.
  - Specific blank spaces and margin rules.
  - Dark ink for clarity and minimum font size of 2 mm.
  - Initials required on changes.
  - Originals lodged in government offices must be certified.

## 3.2.7 Linkage of Deeds for Registration
- Separate lodging covers for each transaction, can be linked for simultaneous registration (reg 63(3)).
- Commonly used codes for transactions:
  - T: Deeds of transfer, titles
  - B: Mortgage bonds
  - PA: Powers of attorney
  - H: Antenuptial contracts
  - VA: Copies of lost deeds

- **Example of Linked Transactions**
  - Mortgaging property for sale linked to mortgage bond registration, indicated on lodgement covers.

### Chapter 3: Conveyancing Process

#### 3.2.5 Lodge deeds
- **Simultaneous Lodgement**: 
  - Required when conveyancers finalize financial arrangements and transaction processes.
  - Involves communication among conveyancers for transactions such as bond cancellations or new mortgages.
  - All deeds must be ready to lodge simultaneously; if one is unready, the whole batch is rejected.
  
- **Legislation**:
  - Regulation 45(1) stipulates that deeds, bonds, and powers of attorney must be lodged by conveyancers at the deeds registry.
  - Exceptions: Government department representatives may lodge documents without being conveyancers (subject to Registrar's approval).

- **Tracking System**:
  - DOTS (Deeds Office Tracking System) implemented for monitoring lodged deeds.
  - Involves bar-coded stickers on lodgement covers, allowing tracking through the registration process.

#### 3.2.6 Execute and register deeds
- **Registration Process**:
  - After 5-6 working days (reg 45(3)), deeds are reviewed by conveyancers for amendments based on deeds examiner's notes.
  - Corrections must be finalized within three days before registration.
  
- **Execution**:
  - Conveyancers sign deeds in the presence of the Registrar of Deeds.
  - Registrar's signature signifies registration (s 13(1) of the Act).

#### 3.2.7 After registration
- **Post-Registration Duties**:
  - Conveyancers inform clients of registration.
  - Payment guarantees and undertakings are processed.
  - Registered deeds are returned after sealing and recording.
  - Title deeds provided to clients or relevant parties (e.g., bondholders).

### Summary of Conveyancer's Role
- **Definition**: An admitted attorney specialized in conveyancing after passing national exams and being registered.
  
- **Duties**:
  - Ensure validity of alienation.
  - Manage overall process, especially finances.
  - Prepare deeds and guarantee correctness of records in compliance with section 15A and regulation 44A.

### Important Case Law and Legislation References
- **Act**: Refer to specific sections mentioned (e.g., s 5, s 13(1)).
- **Regulations**: 
  - Regulation 45(1) regarding lodgement by qualified individuals.
  - Regulation 45(3) regarding timelines for registration activities.

#### 2. Appointment of Registrars of Deeds
- Diagram Reference: Management structure of the Deeds Registries.
- Required qualifications (per *s 2 of Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937*):
  - Diploma Juris or equivalent.
  - Proven expertise or capacity to perform duties.
  
#### 3. Duties of a Registrar of Deeds
- *Section 3 Duties*:
  - **Take charge of records** (*s 3(1)(a)*).
  - **Examine deeds** (*s 3(1)(b)*): 
    - Verify legal compliance and endorse relevant documents.
    - Update records.
  - **Record interdicts** (*s 3(1)(w)*): 
    - Maintain court notices and related documents.
  - **Keep registers**: 
    - Ensure compliance with the Act via efficient land registration systems.
  - **Access to public registers** (*s 7(1)*): 
    - Allow inspections and copies under prescribed conditions.
  - Additional responsibilities: 
    - Register government grants, mortgages, cessions, and comply with directives from the Chief Registrar (*s 3(1)(j)-(p)*).

#### 4. Powers of the Registrar
- *Section 4 Powers*:
  - **Proof requirements** (*s 4(1)(a)*): May demand specific proof for registration.
  - **Rectification powers** (*s 4(1)(b)*): Correct errors in registered documents.
  - **Certified copies** (*s 4(1)(c)*): Issue certified versions of documents.
  - **Obtain replacements** (*s 4(1)(d)*): For illegible or unserviceable documents.
  - **Court reports** (*s 97(1)*): Submit reports concerning Deeds Registry acts.
- The Registrar operates as a semi-judicial officer, focusing on civil law, and is not liable for damages unless actions are mala fide or negligent (*Section 99*).

## Chapter 2: Analysis of the Deed of Transfer

### 2.1 Introduction
- **Legislation**: Section 20 of the Act mandates a deed of transfer is to be drawn in prescribed forms; minor deviations allowed (reg 82).
- **Forms of Deed**:
  - **Form E**: Sales, donations, inheritance (most common).
  - **Form F**: Partition transfers.
  - **Form G**: Expropriation transfers.
  - **Form H**: Transfers by court order.
  - **Form DDD**: Transfers under specific Acts.

### 2.2 Prescribed Form: FORM E

### 2.3 Discussion of Component Clauses
- **Preparation Clause (Certificate)**:
  - Indicates the conveyancer's responsibility (reg 43).
  - **Legislation**: Section 15 prohibits registration unless prepared by a conveyancer.
  - Exceptions exist for entities like SANRAL.

- **The Heading**: Title indicating the nature of the deed (e.g., “Deed of Transfer” or “Partition Transfer”).

- **The Preamble**:
  - Identifies conveyancer's details, transferor's name, identity number, and power of attorney specifics.
  - **Legislation**: Section 20 states deeds must be executed by the landowner or authorized conveyancer.

- **Definition of Owner**:
  - **Registered Owner**: Includes owners with specific legal roles such as trustees, liquidators, and executors (§ 102).
  - **Marriage Implications**: Joint ownership in community property scenarios affects ownership designation.

- **Case Reference**: *Gumede v President of the Republic of South Africa* 2009 (3) BCLR 243 (CC) - implications for customary marriages' property regimes.

### 2.3.4 The Recital
- Purpose: Provides the cause of the transfer to assess registrability.
- Examples of causes:
  - Sale
  - Donation
  - Succession
  - Exchange
  - Rectification
- Recitals must be concise and specific to the nature of the transaction.

#### 2.3.4.1 Sale Definition
- Recital must reflect clear intent of sale and include transaction dates for legal clarity regarding ownership and insolvency implications.

### 2.3.6 Notes on Multiple Owners
- **Ownership Transfer**:
  - Co-owners may transfer shares in a single deed (s 24(2)).
  - Separate deeds required for distinct properties owned separately by different parties.
- **Sequential Transactions**: Section 96 allows continuous transactions by a recognized party, simplifying registration processes.

### 2.3.4 Transfer of Land

#### 2.3.4.1 Sale and Deceased

- Mention if the buyer died before registration.
- Recital example:
  - “The deceased Ben Claasen did... purchase the property...”

#### 2.3.4.2 Donation

- Pre-1982 donations require acceptance reference in the deed.
- Post-1982: No acceptance reference needed as per **section 2(1) Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981**.

#### 2.3.4.3 Succession

- Recital facts:
  - Date of testator's death.
  - Mode of inheritance (testate/intestate).
  - Statement of entitlement (e.g., redistribution agreement).
  - Handling of testamentary conditions.

#### 2.3.4.4 Exchange

- Recital must disclose land exchanged and title deed reference.
- Simultaneous registration of both deeds typically expected.

#### 2.3.4.5 Rectification

- **S 4(1)(b)** provides simple procedure for minor deed errors.
- Major errors require a rectification transfer with full recital disclosure.
- Example of recital format in **Bester v Schmidt Bou Ontwikkelings CC**.

### 2.3.5 The Vesting Clause

## 2. Deeds of Transfer and Special Power of Attorney

### 2.1 Execution of Deeds of Transfer
- **Deeds of Transfer** may only be executed by:
  - Owner in person
  - Conveyancer authorized via power of attorney
- **Practicality**: Most transfers utilize a special power of attorney due to owner in-person attendance challenges

### 2.2 Analysis of a Special Power of Attorney
- **Legislation**: 
  - No prescribed form per Regulation 65(3) 
  - Must include:
    - Clear description of land
    - Registered number of property (if applicable)
    - Serial number and year of holding deed
    - Disposal date for land transfer

- **Power of Attorney Elements**:
  - **Preparation Clause**: Required, signed by practicing attorney or notary and countersigned by a conveyancer (Reg 44).
  - **Preamble**: Identifies transferor’s details (name, ID, marital status) and must accurately describe entities involved.
  - **Causa/Recital**: Background and intention for the transfer.
  - **Transferee Identification**: Name and description.
  - **New Registrable Conditions**: Relevant conditions attached to the transfer.
  - **Execution Clause**: Finalizing the document.

### 2.2.1 Preparation Clause or Certificate
- Must include a signed certificate from a practicing attorney/notary.
- **Responsibility**: Preparer ensures accuracy regarding facts (Reg 44A).

### 2.2.2 Preamble Requirements
- **Natural Person**: Full names, identity number, date of birth, marital status.
- **Entity/Other**: Full name and registration number.
- **Executor of Deceased Estate**: Must cite evidence from the letter of executorship (Legator McKenna Inc v Shea 2010 (1) SA 35 (SCA)).
- **Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984, Section 15(2)**: Consent required from spouse for transfers involving immovable property.
- **Error Correction**: Requires consent and initial from grantors for minor amendments.

### 2.2.2.1 Transferor Categories
- **Married/Civil Union Partners**:
  - **Out of Community**: Full contractual capacity (Matrimonial Property Act).
  - **In Community**: Limited capacity; consent from both spouses required for immovable property transactions.

- **Foreign Marriages**: 
  - Governed by husband’s domicile law (Frankel’s Estate v The Master).
  - Spousal assistance may be required unless waived by Registrar (Section 17(6) of the Deeds Registries Act).

- **Customary Marriages**:
  - **Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998**: Ensures equal status, rights, and obligations.
  - **Prior to Act**: Distinction made until **Gumede v President** found unconstitutional, deeming polygamous unions in community property postact.

### 2.2.2.2 Additional Notes on Customary Marriages
- **Further Marriages Post-Act**: Requires court approval and compliance with established property agreements.
- **Polygamous Relationships**: Must comply with requisite court-sanctioned agreements; described accordingly in deeds and documentation.

## 2.2.2.9 Transfer of Land and Personal Servitude  
- **Section 69(1)**: Transfer of land must accompany personal servitude; cession of rights without land transfer is prohibited.  
- **Section 66**: Cession of usufruct, usus, or habitatio allowed only to the land owner.  
- **Merger**: Transfer of both land and servitude to one person results in termination through merger.  
- **Deed of Transfer**: Describes transferor as owner of bare dominium and holder of servitude (*s 69(2)*).  

## 2.2.2.10 Fideicommissary Transferors  
- **Fideicommissary Substitution**: Property first vests in fiduciary; upon condition, it vests in fideicommissary (e.g., X to Y).  
- **Section 69bis(1)**: Both fiduciary and fideicommissary can transfer property together.  
- **Minor Fideicommissaries**: Court consent required for minor rights transfer (Ex parte de Winnaar 1959; Rogers NO v Erasmus 1975).  
- **Deed of Transfer**: Describes fiduciary and holder of fideicommissary rights (*s 69bis(2)*).  

## 2.2.2.11 Sheriff of the Court as Transferor  
- **Attachment**: Sheriff attaches property only with a court order; notice must be served on the Registrar of Deeds.  
- **Transfer of Attached Property**: Only debtor's rights transferred; e.g., sold subject to servitude.  
- **Bondholder Preferences**: Bondholders can acquire property free from servitudes if servitude holder waives preference (United Building Society v Du Plessis 1990).  
- **Sequestration**: If debtor's estate is sequestrated, sheriff cannot deal with assets; caution required.  
- **Installment Sales**: Notification required for purchasers on claims against the attached property (*s 21(3) of Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981*).  

## 2.2.2.12 Trustee in an Insolvent Estate as Transferor  
- **Proof of Insolvency**: Required before property transfer (*s 17 of Insolvency Act 24 of 1936*).  
- **Sequestration Order**: Must be lodged with the Registrar of Deeds.  
- **Provisional vs. Final Trustees**: Only a final trustee can sell property (*s 20*).  
- **Marriages**: Impact of marital property systems on asset sequestration (*s 21 of Act 24 of 1936*).  
- **Exceptions to General Rule**: Independence of transaction during insolvency; automatic rehabilitation under *s 127A*.  

## 2.2.2.13 Liquidator as Transferor  
- **Liquidation Process**: Initiated by court or voluntary decision (*s 79-81 of Companies Act 71 of 2008*).  
- **Deregistration**: Conditions under which companies may be deregistered (*s 82*).  
- **Liquidator’s Authority**: Transfer must be authorized by creditors or Master; no specific registrations required (*regulation 44A*).  
- **Effect of Liquidation on Attachments**: Attachments after commencement of liquidation are void.  

## 2.2.2.14 Business Rescue Manager as Transferor  
- **Business Rescue Definition**: Court-ordered process; copy of the order must be noted at the Registrar of Deeds.  
- **Asset Disposal**: Allowed if in ordinary course of business and approved at business rescue meeting (*s 152 of Companies Act 71 of 2008*).  

## 2.2.2.15 Juristic Person as Transferor  
- **Contractual Capacity Considerations**: Check memorandum of incorporation and relevant resolutions.  

## 2.2.2.16 Municipality as Transferor  
- **Varied Capacities**: Dependent on provincial ordinance; consent from the premier may be required for alienation.  

## 2.2.2.17 Statutory Body as Transferor  
- **Powers Under Statute**: Verify contractual authority as per authorising statute; administrator may transfer land if appointed.

## 1. Transfer Duty Receipt

### 1.1 Antenuptial Contract Donations
- Section 92(2) prohibits certain transactions (transfer, cession, mortgage) unless transfer duty paid on property donated in antenuptial contract.

### 1.2 Transfer Duty Receipt Specifics
- Full transfer duty amount receipt mandatory; “Deposito”/“Deposit” receipts not accepted unless signed by Receiver per s 11(3) of Transfer Duty Act 40 of 1949.
- Minor errors can be corrected with a conveyancer's certificate.

## 2. Inheritance and Transfer Duty
- X and Y's joint will and the inheritance to Z requires both a transfer duty receipt for the surviving spouse’s share and an exemption for the deceased's share. 
- Recent SARS policy requires lodging a transfer duty exemption for inheritance transfers.

## 3. Rates Clearance Certificates
- Required under section 118(1) & (1A) of Local Government Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 prior to property transfer.
- Must certify payment of municipal fees, valid for 60 days, cannot be reused.

### 3.1 Charges on Property
- Municipal fees due become a charge on the property, taking precedence over mortgage bonds.

## 4. Consents Required 
### 4.1 Title Conditions
- Title may include conditions prohibiting transactions or requiring consent from other parties.

#### 4.1.1 Pre-emptive Rights
- Holder has a right to buy before others; transfer possible only post pre-emptor's refusal.

#### 4.1.2 Reversionary Rights
- Ownership reverts to former owner upon specific events; conditions must persist in new deed.

### 4.2 Statutory Provisions
- Various Acts dictate consent requirements unrelated to title deed conditions.

#### 4.2.1 Subdivision of Agricultural Land Act 70 of 1970
- Actions concerning agricultural land require written consent from the Minister of Agriculture.

#### 4.2.2 Advertising on Roads and Ribbon Development Act 21 of 1940
- Consent required for transferring undivided shares near major roads to ensure traffic safety.

#### 4.2.3 Agricultural Holdings (Transvaal) Registration Act 22 of 1919
- Consent required from town council for subdivisions and portions less than 8,565 square meters.

#### 4.2.4 Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984
- Spousal consent required for juristic acts; specific conditions regarding business acts detailed.

#### 4.2.5 Further Education and Training Colleges Act 16 of 2006
- Disposal of college property requires concurrence from the MEC.

#### 4.2.6 Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937
- Requires lodgment of open mortgage bonds and consents for disposal.

#### 4.2.7 Insolvency
- Certain acquisitions by an insolvent permitted with a disclaimer from the trustee.

### 4.3 General Consent Guidelines
- Clear, unambiguous drafting of consent; full identification details necessary.

## 3 Partition Transfers

### 3.1 Introduction
- **Purpose**: Divide co-owned land ensuring each owner receives demarcated land.
- **Co-ownership**: Land held collectively by multiple owners, each with undivided share.

### 3.1.1 Undivided Share
- Cannot force co-owner to stay as co-owner if they prefer full ownership.
- Agreement to partition is required if co-owners wish to divide land.

### 3.1.2 Agreement Terms
- Written agreements required (Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981, Section 2(1)).
- Court can enforce partition if co-owners disagree (Deeds Registries Act, Section 26(6)).

### 3.1.3 Effects of Partitioning
- **Equal share**: Each owner acquires specific portions equal to their original share.
- **Surveyor's diagram approval**: Required for partitioned portions.

### 3.1.4 Difference Between Partition and Subdivision
- **Subdivision**: Owner splits land into separate titles, remaining in ownership of the land.
- **Partition**: Entire property divided between co-owners; cannot be partial.

### 3.2 Analyzing a Partition Transfer (Form F)
- Similarities to normal deed of transfer but includes uniqueness detailed below:

#### 3.2.1 Preamble
- Describes all co-owners as transferors to facilitate partition.

#### 3.2.2 Recital
- Describes original property and references title(s) and partition agreement/ court order.

#### 3.2.3 Vesting Clause
- Identifies new co-owners (transferees) for awarded shares.

#### 3.2.4 Property Clause
- Full description of awarded portions and their respective extents.

#### 3.2.5 Extending Clause
- Refers to original title deeds following the property description.

#### 3.2.6 Conditional Clause
- New shares subject to existing title conditions and any new ones.

#### 3.2.7 Divesting Clause
- Divests former co-owners from property, transferring ownership to new co-owners.

#### 3.2.8 Consideration Clause
- Specifies acceptable consideration for awarded portions:
  - If no consideration exists, acknowledges total partitioning as fair exchange.
  - If parties compensating for value, this must be disclosed.

### 11.2 Prerequisites

- **Section 40(1) of the Act** outlines prerequisites for issuing a certificate of consolidated title.

#### 11.2.1 Common Boundary
- Land must be contiguous or adjacent with a common boundary (s 40(1)(a)).
- If subdivided, the remainder is not viewed as separate (reg 32bis).

#### 11.2.2 Owners
- Land must be owned by the same person or co-owners holding equal undivided shares in each component.

#### 11.2.3 Consolidation Diagram
- Must be prepared per **Land Survey Act 9 of 1997**; approved by Surveyor-General.
- Serves as a basis for the certificate of consolidated title.
- Requires a certificate from the Surveyor-General if components are not described.

#### 11.2.4 Requirements for Certificate
- For registered deeds (e.g., lease, servitude) affecting the land:
  - Attach diagrams reflecting affected portions.
  - The consolidated diagram must highlight affected areas.

#### 11.2.5 Recording
- Land must be recorded in the same property register (s 40(1)(d)).
- Properties in separate townships cannot be consolidated (Registrars’ Conference Resolution 22 of 1972).

#### 11.2.6 Administrative District
- Land must be in the same administrative district (s 40(1)(e)) and province (s 40(1)(f)).
- Cannot consolidate land in different provinces, even under the same register (Registrars’ Conference Resolution 12 of 1996).

#### 11.2.7 Process of Consolidation
- Cannot consolidate erven directly from township title; a registered title per section 43 of the **Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937** is required first (Registrars’ Conference Resolution 10 of 1994).
- All prerequisites must be cumulatively met.

### 11.3 Prescribed Form
- Certificate of consolidated title must follow prescribed form (s 40(1)).
  
#### 11.3.1 Preamble
- Describes owner(s) applying for the certificate.

#### 11.3.2 Recital
- Refers to components and associated title deed(s) without disclosing component areas.

#### 11.3.3 Vesting Clause
- Identical to the vesting clause in a certificate of registered title.

#### 11.3.4 Description of Property
- Must match the diagram precisely.

#### 11.3.5 Extending Clause
- Must reflect a specific format without the title reference.

#### 11.3.6 Conditional Clause
- All title conditions must be accurately described; eliminate redundant references.
- Different scenarios of qualifications based on conditions attached to components outlined as follows:

1. **Components subject to same conditions**: No qualification necessary, subject to those conditions.
2. **Some components subject to same conditions**: Qualifications required; specify the contiguous components.
3. **Different components subject to different conditions**: Conditions must be qualified for each respective component.
4. **General and individual conditions**: General conditions apply to all but need specific qualifications for particular components. 

> **Note**: Ensure adherence to presentation and requirements in consolidation for each land component to avoid discrepancies.

## 2 Mortgage Bond
- Defined under **s 102 of the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937**; provides hypothecation of immovable property.
- Formal agreement to secure repayment of a debt by creating a real right (Estate Chin v National Bank 1925 AD 353).
- Benefits for registered landowners seeking credit.

## 2.1 Requirements for Existence
- Agreement to create a debt, which must not contravene public policy.
- Immovable property must be capable of being mortgaged.
- Existence of a registered mortgage bond.

## 2.2 Structure of a Bond
- No fixed form prescribed, but standard structure evolved from practice.
- Preparation by a conveyancer required, identifying mortgagor, mortgagee, and details of the obligation.

### 2.2.1 Heading
- Usually titled “Mortgage Bond” with specific type mentioned (e.g., collateral, surety, etc.).

### 2.2.2 Preparation Clause
- Similar requirements as discussed in previous units.

### 2.2.3 Preamble
- Identification of conveyancer and mortgagor details.

### 2.2.4 Acknowledgment Clause
- Mortgagor acknowledges the debt to the mortgagee.

### 2.2.5 Mortgagee (Bondholder)
- Identified as the creditor; can have multiple mortgagees but different obligations require separate bonds (s 50(5)).

## 2.2 Mortgage Bonds

### 2.2.5 Preference
- A mortgage bond may not prioritize one mortgagee over another (s 55(2) of the Act).
- No transaction yielding preferential claims among bondholders is permissible.

### 2.2.6 Amount
- Bond amount disclosure is not mandated by the Act, but is standard practice.
- Registration fees calculated based on debt amount.
- For future debts, a maximum amount must be specified (s 51(1) of the Act).
- Foreign currency bonds require an exchange rate certificate upon registration.
- If co-mortgagees exist, the total bond amount and individual shares must be stated (Registrars’ Conference Resolution 21 of 1954).

### 2.2.7 Cause of Debt (Causa)
- A bond secures a debt; without debt, no bond exists (Kirburn v Estate Kilburn 1931 AD 501).
- Common causes include money lent, goods sold/delivered, etc.
- Future debts can be secured via covering bonds (s 50(2) of the Act).
- Section 50(5) restricts multiple creditors for different causes to be secured by a single bond without legal or court authorization.
- Illegal debts cannot be secured.

### 2.2.8 Waiver of Legal Exceptions
- Prior to the National Credit Act 34 of 2005, mortgagors and mortgagees could contractually exclude defenses. Such waivers are now void (s 90).
  
#### 2.2.8.1 Non Causa Debiti
- Debt lacks cause; waived in bonds securing non-monetary claims (Conradie v Rossouw 1919 AD 279).

#### 2.2.8.2 Non Numeratae Pecuniae
- Claims that the acknowledged debt was not paid; waived in monetary loan bonds (Cohen v Louis Blumberg 1949 (2) SA 849).

#### 2.2.8.3 Revision of Accounts, Errore Calculi, No Value Received
- Collective exceptions resulting in the burden of proof on mortgagor for debt amounts claimed by creditor.

#### 2.2.8.4 De Duobus Vel Pluribus Reis Debendi
- Remedies for multiple co-debtors’ liability; waiver creates joint liability.

#### 2.2.8.5 Beneficium Ordinis Seu Excussiones
- Surety must be pursued post-debtor; renunciation allows creditor to pursue surety first.

#### 2.2.8.6 Beneficium Divisionis
- Multiple sureties typically liable proportionately; waiver permits creditor to sue any surety for full amount.

### 2.2.9 Interest and Repayment Clauses
- No statutory requirement to disclose interest rates; common practice to disclose.
- Repayment terms depend on mutual agreement, typically in installments.

### 2.2.10 Cost Clause
- Secures payment for costs incurred by creditor in addition to debt.
- Covers expenses like legal costs, taxes, and maintenance.
- Often includes an additional security amount representing 20% of the principal loan.

### 2.2.11 Ranking
- No mandatory disclosure of bond ranking in Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937.
- Ranking based on registration date (qui prior est tempore potior est iure).
- Established practice to disclose ranking (1st, 2nd, 3rd).
- Same day registration requires time stamp for bonds lacking explicit ranking.
- Bonds registered pari passu do not require waiver for simultaneous registrations.

## 3 Various Types of Mortgage Bonds

### Collateral bond
- Additional security bond for existing debt.
- Must be between the same mortgagor and mortgagee.
- Example: Additional security for a loan might involve hypothecating another property.
- Registered simultaneously or separately (reg 48).
- **Form KK** prescribed by Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937.

### Surety bond
- Two types: 
  1. General surety bond for existing secured debt.
  2. Surety bond for unsecured debt.
- Example: Co-debtor (Y) hypothecates property as security for another's (X's) debt.
- Renunciation of **beneficium ordinis** and **beneficium divisionis** may be required (Union Government v Van der Merwe 1921 TPD 318).
- **Form LL** prescribed for surety bonds.

### Covering bond
- Special mortgage securing a future debt.
- Must explicitly state intent to secure future debts with a stipulated maximum amount.
- Example: Future loans under an overdraft facility secured by property loan already registered.
- Constraints under **Section 51(1)** of Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937.

### Debenture bond
- Created and issued by companies under **Companies Act 71 of 2008**.
- Secured by notarial or mortgage bonds in favor of debenture holders/trustee.
- Certified copies of debentures must accompany the bond.

### Kinderbewys mortgage bond
- Surviving spouse acts as guardian for minor’s inheritance; must secure money with a mortgage bond.
- Must be approved by the Master of the High Court (s 43(2) Act 66 of 1965).
- If insufficient property exists, a notarial bond can be passed over movable property with additional sureties.
- Requires Master’s approval; forms available with approval clause from the Master.

# Study Notes on Various Types of Mortgage Bonds

## Chapter Overview
- Types of Mortgage Bonds
- Associated Legislation
- Practical Examples

### 1. Kinderbewys Bond (Section 38, Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965)
- Surviving spouse may secure the inheritance of minor beneficiaries via mortgage bond.
- Bond must be approved by the Master of the High Court.
- Structure:
  - Approval required for the estate to avoid insolvency.
  - Registered at deeds registry; contingent on separate certificate from Master.

### 2. Substituted Bond
- Definition: Replaces existing mortgage bond; same creditor and debtor.
- Causa (cause of debt) required; acknowledgment of debt included.
- Old bond cancels upon registration of new bond.
  
### 3. Judicial Mortgage
- Not a traditional bond, but a court attachment order.
- Process:
  - Creditor takes legal action for unpaid debts; court issues warrant.
  - Registrar notes attachment as interdict against property.
- Key Outcome: Prohibits property transfer and offers preference in insolvency cases.

### 4. Indemnity Bond (Vrywaringsverband)
- Purpose: Ensures compliance with obligations of one party on behalf of another, secured by a mortgage.
- Types:
  1. Indemnity by principal debtor for suretyship without existing mortgage.
  2. Indemnity by mortgagor to a surety with existing mortgage bond.
- Structure: Bond details obligations and waivers of legal exceptions by the indemnifier.

### 5. Kustingsbrief
- Definition: Special mortgage to secure unpaid purchase price upon land transfer.
- Characteristics:
  - Registered simultaneously with property transfer.
  - Provides better security for creditors over ordinary mortgage bonds.

### 6. Charges
- Defined: Not a mortgage bond; attaches to the land.
- Statutes require noting charges against land for debts owed.
- Example Legislation: 
  - **Land Bank Act 13 of 1963** (now replaced by **Land and Agricultural Development Bank Act of 2002**).
  - **Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000**, Section 118(3): Allows municipality charge against property for unpaid claims.

### Section 57: Substitution of Debtor Under a Bond

#### 5.5.1 General
- A debtor may be substituted as mortgagor when a purchaser takes over a mortgage bond (s 57(1) of the Act).
- **Example:** 
  - X sells Erf 1 to Y. If Z agrees, Y is substituted for X as debtor.
  - If joint owners X and Y transfer mortgaged land to X, Y, and Z (partnership), Y and Z can be substituted for X and Y.

- New debtors must waive **exception de duobus vel pluribus reis debendi (s 57(4)(b))** for joint liability.

- A **surviving spouse** can be substituted as mortgagor for existing mortgage, irrespective of the devolution method, requiring a formal deed of transfer (Registrars’ Conference Resolution 2/2005).

#### 5.5.2 When Substitution May Not Occur
- **5.5.2.1 Status of Transferor:**
  - No substitution if transferor is:
    - a trustee in an insolvent estate
    - an executor under the Estates Act 66 of 1965
    - a company liquidator (s 57(1) with s 56(1)(b)).

- **5.5.2.2 If Property Not Fully Transferred:**
  - New debtor must receive entire property to be substituted (s 57(1)).
  - Example: Transfer of part of property disallows substitution due to security issue. 
  - **Case Ruling:** Section 57 can apply if a co-owner transfers his/her entire share to another co-debtor (Registrar's Conference Resolutions 7/1994, 19/2005).
  
- **5.5.2.3 If Transferor Reserves a Real Right:**
  - Substitution not allowed if transferor reserves rights (e.g., usufruct), affecting bond security (s 57(1)).
  - Example: Usufruct reservation prohibits substitution unless waived.

- **5.5.2.4 If New Owner Incapable of Mortgaging:**
  - Minors cannot substitute unless guardians act with High Court consent if bond > R250,000 (Ex parte Ross 1940 CPD 6).

- **5.5.2.5 If Bond Secures Surety Obligations:**
  - No substitution if bond secures a surety’s obligations (s 57(1)).
  
- **5.5.2.6 Two Mortgagors May Not Substitute for One:**
  - A single mortgagor cannot transfer multiple properties to different entities as mortgagors (Registrar's Conference Resolution 18(a) of 1996).

#### 5.5.3 Substitution in Covering Bonds
- Substitution possible with consent from all parties in form W (based on regulations).
- Non-fulfillment of bond amount necessitates registration of part payment/reduction (reg 47).

## 11 UNIT 11: Sequence of deeds and miscellaneous applications

### 1 Introduction
- Transfers and cessions must follow prescribed sequence under **Section 14 of the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937**.

### 2 Significance of Section 14
- Cornerstone of land registration system; requires registration for ownership and rights.  
  - **Purpose**: Ensures comprehensive records and protects titles; transfer duty applicable even in exemptions.

### 3 The general rules
- **Sequence Required**: 
  - Transactions must follow the order of ownership rights.
  - Unlawful to alter the sequence in deeds registry.
- **Exceptions**: Insolvency law not subject to Section 14.

- **Transfer Process Examples**: 
  - X → Y → Q → Z (no direct transfer to Z). 
  - Bequeathal requires passage to estate before transfer to heirs or executors.

## 3.1 Exceptions to the rules contained in section 14

### 3.1.1 Exceptions contained in section 14 of the Deeds Registries Act itself

#### # Section 14(1)(b)(i)
- Transfer of land or real rights via executor to descendant's heir ab intestatos if:
  - Land/right devolved via will or intestate succession.
  - Descendant died intestate, minor, before deceased.
  - No executor appointed for minor’s estate.
- **Cumulative requirements**.
  
#### # Section 14(1)(b)(ii)
- Executor may transfer property directly to purchaser if:
  - Property devolved to heir or legatee.
  - Costs of transfer exceed property value.
  - Heir/legatee sold property and consented in writing.
- **Written valuation by sworn appraiser required (Reg 55)**.

#### # Section 14(1)(b)(iii)
- Executor or trustee can transfer land directly to entitled persons during asset redistribution, reflected in the liquidation and distribution account.
- Redistribution can include movable assets for equality in division.

#### # Section 14(1)(b)(iv)
- **Applicable mutatis mutandis** to:
  - Redistribution of joint estate assets post-divorce.
  - Redistribution on partnership dissolution without intermediary transfer.

#### # Section 14(1)(b)(v)
- Similar conditions apply in certain situations post-divorce or partnership dissolution allowing direct transfer without intermediary steps.

#### # Section 14(1)(b)(vi)
- Direct transfer to fideicommissary if fiduciary interest expires before transfer registration.
- Applicable to testamentary and inter vivos fideicommissa.

#### # Section 14(1)(b)(vii)
- Transfer may directly pass to third person under court order or sale in execution, avoiding intermediary transfers.

### 3.1.2 Other exceptions contained in the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937

#### 3.1.2.1 Section 33(1)
- Court can authorise direct registration of property when usual process fails due to circumstances such as disappearance of intermediary.

#### 3.1.2.2 Section 92(2)
- Property settled/donated in antenuptial contract can't be transferred to another by donor without required payments.

#### 3.1.2.3 Section 24bis
- Change in partnership shareholding does not require registration for property, though transfer duty applies.

### 3.1.3 Instances where the court directs otherwise
- Court rulings can permit registration bypassing standard transaction sequences.

### 3.1.4 Exceptions contained in other Acts
- Other Acts, e.g., **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, allow deviations from section 14 general rule.

#### 6.1.4 Effect of the Endorsement  
- Endorsement is a caveat, not a transfer of ownership.

---

### 6.2 Applications under Section 39(3) of the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965

#### 6.2.1 General  
- If transfer costs hardship for heirs, Master can authorize title deed endorsement indicating bequeathal or inheritance.

#### 6.2.2 Application  
- Prepared by a conveyancer, needs executor's reference (Reg 44 compliance).

#### 6.2.3 Supporting Documents  
- **Conveyancer certificate**: Section 42(1) and endorsement by the Master (Registrars’ Conference Resolution 20 of 1968).
- **Title deeds**: Required for endorsement.

#### 6.2.4 Effect of the Endorsement  
- Endorsement does not transfer ownership; it acts as a caveat.

---

### 6.3 Application under Section 40(1)(b) of the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965

#### 6.3.1 General  
- Will provisions for asset administration by a trustee require an endorsement on immovable property title deeds to reflect this.

#### 6.3.2 Application  
- Executor lodges application for endorsement of relevant deeds (Reg 44 compliance).

#### 6.3.3 Supporting Documents  
- **Will copy**: Certified by the Master.
- **Conveyancer’s certificate**: Section 42(1) to confirm that the account is clear.
- **Marriage proof**: If a joint estate is involved.

#### 6.3.4 Effect of the Endorsement  
- Trustee gains authority to administer property but does not acquire ownership title until duties are fulfilled per will provisions.

# LPL4804/1 Study Notes

## Legal Concepts

- **Interdict**: 
  - Court orders preventing transactions or restraining individuals.

- **Intestate Estate**: 
  - Estate of a deceased without a valid will or a will not accepted by the Master.

- **Joint Will**: 
  - Document containing wills of multiple individuals, typically spouses.

- **Legal Capacity**: 
  - Ability to bear rights and duties.

- **Legatee**: 
  - Person bequeathed specific items or sums by a testator.

### Property Registration

- **Linking**: 
  - Registering multiple deeds simultaneously via a code for financial reasons.

- **Lodgement**: 
  - Formal submission of documents for registration.

### Financial Management

- **Liquidation**: 
  - Process of winding up a company’s affairs.

- **Liquidation and Distribution Account**: 
  - Account detailing estate assets, liabilities, and distribution methods.

### Estate Management and Distribution

- **Massing**: 
  - Merging of estates under a joint will for distribution after death.

- **Redistribution Agreement**: 
  - Agreement among heirs to adjust estate bequests during administration.

- **Repudiation**: 
  - Rejection of a joint will by a surviving spouse.

### Real Rights

- **Mortgage**: 
  - Not synonymous with "bond"; a secured debt against property.

- **Mortgagee**: 
  - Lender holding the mortgage.

- **Mortgagor**: 
  - Borrower providing property as security.

- **Notarial Bond**: 
  - Mortgage over movable property executed under notary.

- **Praedial Servitude**: 
  - Property benefiting from a servitude over another property.

- **Personal Servitude**: 
  - Limited rights over another's property for personal use.

### Legal Instruments

- **Power of Attorney**: 
  - Authorization for an agent to act on behalf of a principal.

- **Special Power of Attorney to Transfer**: 
  - Specific authorization for conveyancers to execute transfers.

- **Title Deed**: 
  - Document affirming ownership or rights over property.

### Insolvency and Financial Status

- **Sequestration**: 
  - Court declaration of insolvency.

- **Unrehabilitated Insolvent**: 
  - Individual with limited capacity post-insolvency.

### Special Terms

- **Usus**: 
  - Right to use property for personal needs without major alterations.

- **Usufruct**: 
  - Right to use and enjoy property and its benefits.

- **Waiver**: 
  - Document renouncing an estate benefit.
