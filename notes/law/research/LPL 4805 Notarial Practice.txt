### General Functions of the Notary
1. **Definition of Notary**: Public officer admitted by the High Court to prepare, execute, and authenticate documents. Must pass the specialized examination of the Law Society.

   - Held in high esteem, serving as an officer of the court under High Court supervision.
   - Appointed by the court; removable by the court.
   - Must exhibit responsibility, credibility, impartiality, and professional integrity.

## STUDY UNIT 1: Office and general functions of the notary

### 1.1 **Requirements and Duties of a Notary**

- **Qualifications**
  - Must hold a prescribed university degree.
  - Complete a prescribed period of articles.
  - Pass practical examination for notaries.
  - Must be a practising attorney, an officer of the High Court (SA Notary 1987:31).

- **Protocol and Protocol Register**
  - Original deeds kept by notary in safe custody.
  - Deeds certified by notaries do not need to be retained.
  - Notary must maintain a chronological register (protocol register) of original deeds.

- **Duty of Care**
  - Exercise caution and expertise in work; stay updated on legal developments.
  - Verify identities and capacities of parties and witnesses.
  - Ensure parties understand documents and act freely.
  - Identify and refuse to notarize if a conflict of interest arises (Ratanee v Mahara 1950 2 SA 552 (N)).
  - Personal interest in a document invalidates it (Van den Heever v Kahn 1961 (3) SA 17 (T)).

### 1.2 **Drafting of Documents**

#### 1.2.1 **General Considerations**
- Notarial documents must reflect information clearly and accurately.

#### 1.2.2 **Guidelines for Document Drafting**
- **Professionalism**
  - Avoid personal interest in documents.
  - Confirm identities, capacities, and verify authorizations in foundational documents (s 15A of the Deeds Act).
  - Maintain confidentiality and respect client information.
  - Ensure understanding of instructions and provide unambiguous language.
  - Explain legal principles to clients and confirm comprehension (Ex parte Kock NO 1952 (2) SA 502 CPD).
  - Final signatures must have no blank spaces.

### 1.3 **Legislative Framework**

- **Deeds Act**
  - Notarial execution required for registration of specific legal documents (ss 87(1), 102, 65(1), 75(1), 77(1), 3(1)(p)).
  - Powers of attorney and agreements must be prepared and signed by authorized legal practitioners (s 26 of the Deeds Act).
  - Notary certifies the accuracy of documents signed (s 15A(1) and (2), r 44A).

- **Admission and Removal**
  - High Court admits notaries under the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979.
  - Basic admission requirements apply to both attorneys and notaries (s 18(1)).
  - Notaries follow the same ethical and procedural standards as attorneys. 

### 1.4 **Code of Conduct**

- Must adhere to ethical standards as prescribed by section 57 of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1973.
- Responsibilities include maintaining high standards of honesty, integrity, professionalism, and respect for clients and peers.

### 1.5 **Conclusion**
- A notary has a fundamental role in ensuring legal documents are prepared competently and ethically, bearing significance in their execution for public assurance. Proper adherence to legislative frameworks and codes of conduct ensures the integrity of notarial acts.

### 1.2.2.2 Formal requirements
- **Deeds Act compliance**: Notarial deeds must follow the formal appearance regulations found in the Deeds Act (rr 20, 22-25, r 18, r 26).
- **Power of attorney requirements**:
  - Must be witnessed by two witnesses or authorized individual (e.g., magistrate, notary).
  - Includes date and place of execution.
  - Property descriptions must comply with specific regulations (r 65(2)-(3)).
  - Non-compliance leads to rejection, but not invalidity.
- **Document correctness**: Notaries assume responsibility for the accuracy of prepared documents akin to a conveyancer.
- **Standard form and structure**: Notarial deeds developed a custom standard form for uniformity, drawing from precedents.

### 1.2.2.3 Content
- **Clarity and structure**: Notarial deeds must reflect parties' intentions and be unambiguous.
- **Drafting principles**:
  - Use clear, brief sentences; address separate matters in distinct paragraphs.
  - Adapt existing drafts or precedents to specific needs.

### 1.2.2.4 Interpretation
- **Intention of author**: Interpretation focuses on the author’s intent as expressed in the document language.
- **Heading and preamble usage**: Aids in interpretation but not dominant if inconsistent with the deed's governing part.
- **Marginal notes**: Generally excluded from contract interpretation.
- **Ambiguity**: Courts favor interpretations against the drafter; clear obligations are enforceable; vagueness renders terms void.
- **Expressio unius est exclusio alterius**: Established rule that specifying one thing excludes others.
- **Punctuation**: Crucial in document interpretation.

### 1.3 AUTHENTICATION OF DOCUMENTS

#### 1.3.1 Introduction
- **Authentication vs. certification**: Authentication verifies a signatory's signature; certification confirms document content duplication.
- **Notary's role**: Must verify signatory identity and their acknowledgment of the document.

#### 1.3.2 Rules for documents executed within South Africa
- **Government Notice R 277 of 3 March 1967**:
  1. Notary authenticates by signing a certificate and adding a seal, if applicable; signatory must acknowledge in person.
  2. Notary signature requires authentication by a magistrate/registrar.
  3. Further authentication by the Director-General of Justice is necessary.
  4. Direct authentication by the Director-General is allowed for expediency.

#### 1.3.3 Rules for documents executed outside South Africa
- **1.3.3.1 Introduction**: Documents must be authenticated for use in South Africa via USC Rule 63 or the Hague Convention.
  
- **1.3.3.2 Rule 63 Summary**:
  1. Broad definition of document types for authentication.
  2. Authentication criteria include signatures from authorized figures (e.g., South African diplomatic entities, certain foreign officials).
  3. No authentication required for powers of attorney from specified neighboring countries if properly signed.

#### 1.3.3.3 Hague Convention
- **Context**: Simplifies international document authentication.
- **Applicability**: Only for public documents, excluding diplomatic or commercial administrative documents.
  
- **Procedure requirements**:
  - Signature authentication via an apostille (certificate).
  - Must fulfill form requirements as defined in specific articles of the convention.
  - Need not involve witness signatures for deeds registry acceptance.

### 1.4 POWERS OF ATTORNEY

#### 1.4.1 Introduction
- **Definition**: Power of attorney document detailing a representative's authority on behalf of the grantor.
- **Types**:
  - **General power of attorney**: Grants broad authority (e.g., managing interests).
  - **Special power of attorney**: Limited to specific acts (e.g., transferring property).

### 1.4.2 Requirements

- **Terminates on:**
  - Execution of mandate.
  - Completion of granted period.
  - Death of the representative.
  - Impossibility of execution.
  - Principal's incapacity (death, insanity, sequestration).
  - Representative's denouncement.

### 1.4.5 Amendment of Power of Attorney
- **Alterations:** Must be clearly marked; all material changes initialed by grantor and witnesses.

### 1.4.6 Irrevocable Power of Attorney
- **Definition:** Cannot be revoked unilaterally but may still be withdrawn by grantor.
- **Types:**
  1. Binding agreement between grantor and representative; revocable if not yet acted upon.
  2. Secured performance obligation; terminates on death or insolvency.
  3. Related to representative's interest; terminates on death or insolvency.

### 1.4.7 Power of Attorney Intended for Acts of Registration in the Deeds Registry

#### 1.4.7.1 General Structure
- **Content Requirements:**
  - Personal details of grantor (ID number, status).
  - Representative's details (ID or birth date, optional for registry).
  - Specific acts authorized with explicit powers.
  - Place and date of execution.
  - Signatures of grantor and witnesses.

#### 1.4.7.2 Deeds Registry Requirements
1. **Paper and Writing:** Must be legibly prepared on A4 paper; specific font requirements.
2. **Preparation Certificate:** Must be signed by an attorney, conveyancer, or notary. Co-signing by a practicing conveyancer required.
3. **Accuracy Responsibility:** Preparer accepts responsibility for the accuracy of facts (Section 15A of the Deeds Act).
4. **Foreign Execution:** Must follow authentication rules; two-witness requirement waived for authenticated documents.

##### 1.4.7.2.4 Foreign Execution and Language
- **Authentication:** Required per Supreme Court Rules or Hague Convention.
- **Witnessing:** Not required for foreign documents authenticated.

##### 1.4.7.2.5 Witnesses
- **Witnessing Requirements:** Minimum of two witnesses; specific exclusions (e.g., representatives cannot witness).
- **Foreign Documents:** Can be exempt from witness requirements if duly authenticated.

##### 1.4.7.2.6 Specific Provisions Regarding Content
- **Execution Details:** Must include date and clear location.
- **Property Description:** Necessary for transfers; must include serial numbers and details.
- **General Powers:** Must specifically authorize transactions on immovable property.
- **Alteration Initials:** Material changes require initials by grantor and witness, all alterations by notary.

## STUDY UNIT 2: Notarial Bonds

- **Legislation:**
  - **Security by Means of Movable Property Act 57 of 1993:** Entire Act.
  - **Deeds Act:** Sections 3(1)(j), 3(1)(j)bis, 3(1)(h) and others.
  - **Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984:** Section 15(2).

- **Important Cases:**
  - **Boland Bank v Vermeulen 1993 (2) SA 241 (E)**
  - **Contract Forwarding (Pty) Ltd v Chesterfin (Pty) Ltd 2003 (2) SA 253 (SCA)**.

- **Nature and Contents of a Notarial Bond:**
  - Acts as a unilateral deed executed before a notary.
  - Secures specific or all movable property of the mortgagor.

- **Jurisdictional Restrictions:**
  - Deeds Act prohibits notarial bonds over immovable property.

- **Creation of Real Security:**
  - Notarial bond alone does not confer real security; requires additional control measures.

##### 3.3.1 Execution

### 3.5 MATRIMONIAL PROPERTY ACT 88 OF 1984: ACCRUAL SYSTEM

- Marriages in South Africa: community of property unless specified otherwise in an antenuptial contract.
- Antenuptial contract must be executed before marriage to exclude community of property.
- **Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984**: Introduces accrual system for marriages out of community of property.
- **Section 2**: Marriages out of community of property subject to accrual unless excluded.
- At dissolution:
  - Spouse with lesser or no accrual can claim half the difference in accruals from the other spouse's estate (**Section 3(1)**).
  - Right to share in accrual arises only at marriage dissolution, not transferable or attachable during marriage (**Section 3(2)**).
  - Application for immediate accrual division allowed if spouse's rights are prejudiced (**Section 8(1)**).
- Accrual calculation:
  - Difference between net estate values at marriage commencement and dissolution (**Section 4(1)**).
  - Assets pre-marriage excluded unless stated otherwise in antenuptial contract (**Section 21(c)**).
  - Non-inclusive assets: damages (non-patrimonial), excluded assets, third-party donations (**Sections 4(1)(b)(i)-(ii), 5(1)**).

### 3.6 AMENDMENT AND CANCELLATION OF ANTENUPTIAL CONTRACT

- Amendable prior to marriage by mutual agreement; must register amendments to be enforceable against third parties.
- Post-solemnisation amendment/cancellation is exceptional, requiring court order.
- Possible cancellations: if marriage does not proceed or prior agreement changes pre-solemnisation (e.g., **Cross v Garner 1914 CPD 355**).

### 3.7 POSTNUPTIAL REGISTRATION AND POSTNUPTIAL SIGNING OF ANTENUPTIAL CONTRACT

- Unregistered antenuptial contracts: enforceable between parties but not against third parties (**Section 86, Deeds Act**).
- **Postnuptial registration**: High Court allows registration when:
   - Contract executed pre-marriage but not registered on time.
   - Agreement to marry out of community but contract not executed.
- **Ex parte Oxford 1920 CPD 367**:
  - Requirements include affidavits, binding pre-marital agreements, good reasons for delays, prompt applications.

- Joint application to modify matrimonial property requires:
  - Justifiable reason for modification (e.g., undue influence).
  - Notification to creditors.
  - No prejudice to third parties (**Ex parte Coetzee et Uxor 1984 (2) SA 363 (WLD)**).

## STUDY UNIT 4: Servitudes
- Definition: A **servitude** is a right of one person in the property of another (ius in re aliena), diminishing the owner's dominion over a thing.
- Upon termination, rights are not transferred back to the owner; ownership is restored fully.

### 4.2 VESTING
- Mere servitude agreements do not establish servitudes; rights are personal until registered.
- **Innes CJ** in *Willoughby’s Consolidated Co Ltd v Copthall Stores Ltd 1918 AD 1*: Registration is required for servitude rights to vest legally.
- **Claasen J** in *Janse van Rensburg v Koekemoer 2011 (1) SA 118 (GSJ)*: Oral agreements for habitation require registration.

#### Characteristics of Servitudes:
1. No servitude over one’s own property (nulla res sua servit).
2. No servitude can exist over another servitude (servitus servitutis esse non potest).
3. Right must be exercised least prejudicially (civiliter modo).
4. No positive obligation on the servient landowner (principle of passivity).
5. Right is enforceable against the entire world (actio confessoria).
6. Holder acquires a limited real right.

- **Court Ruling**: Ownership similarity affects servitude validity (e.g., *Mocke v Beaufort West Municipality 1939 TPD 135*).

### 4.2.1 Doctrine of Notice
- Issues with unregistered servitudes: Parties are liable until registration; new owners (Z) are not bound unless they have knowledge of the agreement (actual or constructive).
- **Dhayanundh v Nairan 1983 (1) SA 565 (N)**: Knowledge at the time of registration is critical.

### 4.3 TYPES OF SERVITUDE
1. **Praedial Servitudes (Real Servitudes)**
   - Defined as limited real rights benefiting one piece of land over another.
   
2. **Personal Servitudes**
   - Limited real rights granted to a person in their capacity.

3. **Public Servitudes**
   - Limited real rights created for public benefit, does not conform to typical servitude rules.

4. **Statutory Servitudes**
   - Created by legislation, such as under the **National Water Act 36 of 1998**.

#### 4.3.1 Praedial Servitudes
- Always involves dominant and servient tenements.

#### 4.3.2 Characteristics of Praedial Servitudes
1. Granted only for immovable property.
2. Requires two distinct pieces of land.
3. Created for owners of the dominant tenement.
4. Perpetual unless stated otherwise.
5. Attached to land, transferable only with it.
6. Indivisible and cannot be subdivided.
7. Adjacent or closely situated tenements.
8. Must enhance the value and use of the dominant tenement.

#### 4.3.3 Personal Servitudes
- Defined as rights to use another's property for specified durations.

##### Characteristics of Personal Servitudes
1. Can be over movable or immovable things.
2. Granted in favor of a specific person.
3. Non-transferable, except certain entitlements of use.
4. Limited duration or lifetime of holder.

##### Types of Personal Servitudes
1. **Usufruct**: Use and benefit from another's property with the obligation of return.
2. **Use**: Limited use for personal benefit without sale rights.
3. **Habitation**: Right to occupy another's property without fruit entitlements.

### 4.3.4 Public Servitudes
- Created for the general public, distinct and not reliant on two tenements (examples include roads).

### 4.5 SERVITUDES UNDER SECTIONAL TITLES ACT 95 OF 1986
- **Implied Servitudes**: Rights of access and maintenance among sectional properties, outlined in sections **28(1)** and **29**.

### 4.5.1 Creation of Servitudes
- Praedial and personal servitudes can be created by registration of a notarial deed or through a power of attorney during property transfers.

### 4.5.2 Reservation in Power of Attorney
- Personal servitudes can be reserved in transfer powers, e.g., for the transferor or spouse under section **67 of the Deeds Act**.

### Key Legislation and Cases
- **National Water Act 36 of 1998**: Highlights statutory servitudes.
- **Deeds Act**: Governs registration and characteristics of servitudes.
- **Mocke v Beaufort West Municipality 1939 TPD 135**: Key case on servitude rights validity.

## STUDY UNIT 6: Trusts

### 6.1 Definition and Nature of Trusts
- Trust = arrangement where one person (trustee) holds property for the benefit of another (beneficiary) (Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988, s 1).
- Not a legal person (CIR v Mac Nellies Estate 1961 3 SA 833 (A); Kohlberg v Burnett 1986 (3) SA 12 (A)).
- Distinction between:
  - Property trust: trustee owns trust assets for beneficiaries.
  - Bewind trust: beneficiary owns assets; trustee acts as administrator.

### 6.2 Creating a Trust
- Creation by agreement + transfer of assets (inter vivos) or will (mortis causa).
- Reasons: education, estate planning, tax purposes, etc.
- Must lodge the deed of trust with the Master (ss 3 & 4 of Act 57 of 1988).
  
### 6.3 Registration of a Trust
- Trust deeds must be registered with the Master (IT number for inter vivos; MT number for mortis causa).
- Covering letter must include:
  - Beneficiaries' names + relationships.
  - Trustees’ occupations + experience.
  - Auditor information + annual audit indication.
  - Instructions for recording and banking.

### 6.4 Key Elements of a Trust
- Must be autonomous.
- Intent by the founder to create a trust.
- Separation of founder’s estate and trust assets.
- Clearly defined trust assets.
- Binding obligation in the trust deed.
- Clear objective/aim of the trust.
- Legal purpose.
- Acceptance by trustees.

### 6.5 Setting Up a Trust
- Three parties: founders, trustees, beneficiaries.
- Creation methods:
  1. Inter vivos deed of trust.
  2. Will (mortis causa trust).

### 6.6 Trustees
- Must be competent, accept the nomination, and be appointed by the Master (Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988, s 4).
- Incompetent individuals include:
  - Those without contractual capacity.
  - Excluded persons by trust document.
  - Witnesses of the will as trustees.
  - The Master in an official capacity (Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965, s 99).

### 6.7 Duties of a Trustee
- **Deed of Trust**: Submit to Master if not already submitted.
- **Security**: Must provide to Master to receive authorization (s 6(2)).
- **Address**: Notify Master of address changes within 14 days (s 5).
- **Care**: Act with reasonable diligence (s 9(1)).
- **Trust Account**: Open a separate bank account for trust funds.
- **Identification of Property**: Clearly identify trust property.
- **Custody of Documents**: Do not destroy documents without consent (s 17).
- **Administration**: Comply with trust objectives and inform beneficiaries.

### 6.8 Termination of Trustee Office
- **Resignation**: Written notice to Master and beneficiaries (s 20(3); s 21).
- **Removal**:
  - Court order if removal is in the interest of trust/beneficiaries.
  - Master’s removal for incompetence or failure to comply with duties.

### 6.9 Remuneration of a Trustee
- Generally outlined in the deed of trust or reasonable remuneration fixed by the Master if unspecified (s 22).

### 6.10 Amendment and Termination of a Trust
- Trust can be amended/terminated if:
  - Allowed in the trust document.
  - Unanimous agreement from founding, trustees, and beneficiaries.
  - Court finds provisions contrary to public interest or prejudicial to beneficiaries.

### 6.11 Drafting a Deed of Trust
- Not standardized, tailored to specific circumstances.
- Must include:
  1. Clear parties: founder(s), trustee(s), beneficiary(ies).
  2. Intent to create trust and specific purpose.
  3. Separation of founder’s estate from trust assets; invalid if founder is sole beneficiary.

### 7.3.2 Requirements for Presentment for Acceptance

  - (c) If drawee is deceased, present to executor.
  - (d) If drawee is insolvent, present to drawee or trustee.
  - (e) Presentment by post is sufficient.

### 7.3.3 Exceptions
- Presentment excused if:
  - (a) Drawee is dead, insolvent, fictitious, or has no capacity to contract.
  - (b) Reasonable diligence fails to effect presentment.
  - (c) Irregular presentment results in refusal for other grounds.

### 7.4 Non-Acceptance
- If bill not accepted within usual time after due presentment, treat as dishonoured.
- Right of recourse against drawer and endorsers arises immediately.
- **Usual time for acceptance**: 24 hours (excluding non-business days).

### 7.5 Bills: Presentment for Payment
- **Compulsory presentment** to hold drawer and endorser liable (s 43(1)).
- **Duly presented for payment**:
  - (a) If not payable on demand, present on due date.
  - (b) If payable on demand, present within reasonable time post-issue/endorsement.
  - (c) Present at reasonable hour on business day, specified place, to designated payer or authorized person.
  - (d) If multiple drawees, present to all.
  - (e) If drawee deceased, present to executor if located.

### 7.6 Promissory Notes: Presentment for Payment
- Present at specific place to render maker/endorser liable (s 91).
- Presentment necessary for endorser liability (s 91(2)).
- **Dishonour** of promissory note grants immediate recourse right (s 35(2), s 93).

### 7.7 Notice of Dishonour
- **Requirement**: Notice must be given to drawer and endorsers upon dishonour (s 46).
- **Rules for notice**:
  - (1) Must be by holder/endorser liable on bill.
  - (2) Notice can be through agent.
  - (3) Valid notice via written or personal communication.
  - (4) Return of dishonoured bill acts as sufficient notice.
  - (5) Written notice need not be signed; may be supplemented by verbal.
  - (6) Notice to be sent to authorized person if not given directly.
  - (7) If drawer/endorser is deceased or insolvent, notify executor/trustee.
  - (8) Must notify each drawer/endorser if multiple (unless one has authority).
  - (9) Must be given within reasonable time.

### 7.7.1 Notice Not Required
- No protest/notice required for acceptor of bill/maker of promissory note (s 0(3), s 93).

### 7.7.2 Dispensing With Notice
- Notice may be dispensed if:
  - (a) Reasonable diligence fails to deliver notice.
  - (b) Express/implied waiver by drawer/endorser.
  - (c) Specific cases regarding drawer (e.g., drawee fictitious).
  - (d) Endorsers aware of drawee’s capacity issues.

### 7.8 Noting and Protest
#### 7.8.1 General
- Notarial noting/protest provides authentic evidence of presentment and dishonour.
- Notation serves as prima facie evidence and cannot be contested preliminarily.

#### 7.8.2 Noting
- Notary presents bill again if dishonoured; records in noting slip.
- Noting slip shows details of presentment and dishonour.

#### 7.8.3 Protest
- Formal report by notary stating dishonour intent to claim amount and costs.
- If notary unavailable, local owner can issue certificate of refusal (equal operation).

### Section 49 of the Act: Protest Regulations
- (1A) Bills may be protested; no necessity to protest for holding drawer/endorser liable.
- (2) Protesting for non-acceptance allows for subsequent protest for non-payment.
- (3) Must protest by next business day post-dishonour; provisions for Saturdays.
- (4) Protest if acceptor becomes insolvent prior to maturity for better security.
- (5) Must protest where bill dishonoured or at place specified.
- (6) Protest must include copy of bill, demand made, response, etc.
- (7) If lost, protest on copy or particulars.
- (9) Delay in protesting is excused under certain circumstances (s 48).
