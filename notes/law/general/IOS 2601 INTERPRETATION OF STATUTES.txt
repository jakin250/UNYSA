## Chapter 2

### 2.1 The Nature of Legislation
- Courts during apartheid could declare subordinate legislation invalid but not original legislation.
- Post-1994, courts can declare any legislation invalid.
- Distinction between original and subordinate legislation remains relevant:
  - Delegated legislation must align with enabling legislation.
  - Authority for delegated legislation is limited to the framework set by enabling legislation.
  - Delegated legislation does not affect the interpretation of enabling legislation.

### 2.2 What is Legislation?
- **Legislation** is defined broadly and includes both original and subordinate acts.
- Section 1 of the **Interpretation Act** states rules of interpretation apply to all laws and regulations.

### 2.3 What is Not Legislation?
- Not everything published in the Gazette is legislation.
  - Common law rules and unwritten customary laws are not classified as legislation.

### 2.4 Structure of Legislation
- Understanding the structure is essential but detailed exploration is reserved for **Chapter 6**.

### 2.5 Relationship Between Legislation and Common Law
- Pre-1994, common law rules were used for interpreting legislation.
- Common law presumptions must align with constitutional principles post-1994.

## Chapter 3: Commencement of Legislation

### 3.1 Adoption and Promulgation
- Distinct processes:
  - **Adoption**: stages of acceptance by the legislative body.
  - **Promulgation**: formal enactment that puts legislation into operation.

### 3.2 Requirement of Publication
- Legislation generally comes into operation upon publication in the Gazette (**Section 13(1) of the Interpretation Act 33 of 1957**).
- May specify another commencement date or may indicate future enactment.

### 3.3 Commencement of Legislation
- Three possible commencement times:
  1. Upon publication in the Gazette.
  2. On a specified day in the statute.
  3. On an unspecified date to be proclaimed.
- **Section 13(1)** and **Section 13(2)** of the Interpretation Act outline these rules, including the immediate effect post-publication.

### 3.4 The Presumption That Legislation Only Applies to the Future
- Common law presumption that laws apply only to future matters is supported by constitutional values.
- **Constitution, Section 35(3)(l)** grants rights to not be convicted retroactively.
- Legislation may apply retrospectively if justified constitutionally.

## 4.3.2(b) Effect of Repeal
- **Legislation Repeal**: Governed by the **Interpretation Act, 1957**.
- **Section 12**: Previous actions (achievements or proceedings) remain effective despite repeal; must be completed as if the Act were in force.
- **Case Reference**: **Nourse v Van Heerden 1999 (2) SACR 198 (W)** – crucial for understanding implications of repeal.

- **Temporary Suspension**: Explained by Botha, includes examples from textbook (see page 78).

## 4.5 Presumption Against Unnecessary Law Change
- **Legislative Clarity**: New laws that repeal or amend existing laws must do so clearly.
- **Presumption**: New legislation does not change existing law beyond necessity if ambiguity exists.

### 4.5.1 Common Law
- **Legislative Power**: Legislature can modify common law as needed, provided clarity indicates replacement of old law.
- **Presumption Applicability**: If unclear, interpret legislation in light of common law.

### 4.5.2 Legislation
- **Express Repeal**: Typically, new Acts expressly repeal existing laws (e.g., **Constitution, Schedule 7**).
- **Implicit Change**: Absence of explicit repeal means the presumption is in favor of maintaining existing law, ensuring reconciliation of old and new laws.
- **Case Reference**: **Government of the Republic of South Africa v Government of KwaZulu 1983 (1) SA 164 (A)** – illustrates presumption in action.

## 5.3 THEORIES OF INTERPRETATION
- Understand differences between textual and contextual approaches.
- Study historical context and present constitutional application.

### 5.3.1 The orthodox text-based (literal) approach
- Primary rule: apply clear, ordinary meaning of legislative text; derive meaning from dictionary.
- Golden rule: depart from ordinary meaning only if ambiguous or leads to absurd result.
- Internal aids: rest of legislative text (e.g., title, preamble).
- External aids: outside legislative text (e.g., reports, debates).
- Common law presumptions as last resort when ambiguity persists.

### 5.3.2 The purposive (text-in-context) approach
- Historically applied, but overshadowed by textual approach.
- Contextual interpretation focuses on legislative purpose and scope.
- Advocated due to relevance in post-apartheid South Africa.

### 5.3.3 Criticism of the textual approach
- Points of criticism include:
  - Limited judicial creativity.
  - Reliance on mechanical application of plain meaning.
  - Obscures legislative intent and broader societal context.
- Debate on the evolution from textuality to contextuality.

## 5.3.4 Illustrative Cases
- **Public Carriers Association v Toll Road Concessionaries Pty Ltd (1990)**
  - Court favored the toll operators using textual interpretation.
  - Recognized ambiguity in “alternative road”, shifted to legislative purpose when textual aids failed.

- **Jaga v Dönges**
  - Majority applied textual approach, asserting "sentenced to imprisonment" contained a suspended sentence.
  - **Minority view** by Schreiner JA adopted contextual approach emphasizing legislative purpose, noting suspension allowed for rehabilitation contrary to deportation intent.

### 5.3.5 Conclusion on Approaches
- Emphasis on contextual understanding to align legislative interpretation with underlying principles and societal values.

## 7.1 WHAT IS CONCRETISATION?
- Concretisation involves interpreting legislation through the application of its text, purpose, and constitutional values to specific facts.
- Characterized by creative discretion in interpretation by the court.

## 7.2 THE LAW-MAKING FUNCTION OF THE COURTS
- Misleading to label this discretion as law-making; it involves realizing existing law in new contexts.
- Debate between textual and contextual approaches:
  - **Textual viewpoint**: Meaning fixed at promulgation; no addition through application.
  - **Purposive viewpoint**: Meaning develops upon application; courts clarify meaning in context.

### 7.2.1 The orthodox viewpoint
- Assumes meaning is fixed; interpretation does not alter this.

### 7.2.2 The purposive viewpoint
- Courts clarify meaning through practical application.
- Distinction between structural (abstract) and functional (concrete) statute.

### 7.2.3 The myth that courts merely interpret the law
- False assumptions by textualists:
  1. Confuse modification of meaning with alteration of text.
  2. Accept literal interpretations that exceed legislative purpose.
  3. Rely on parliamentary supremacy, overridden by the Constitution.

### 7.2.4 Factors that support and limit judicial law-making during statutory interpretation
- **Restrictions**: Six factors limit court discretion.
- **Supportive factors**: Six factors enhance judicial law-making considerations.

## 7.3 POSSIBILITIES DURING CONCRETISATION

## Chapter 7: Judicial Law-Making During Concretisation

### 7.1 Interpretation Methods
- **Modificative Interpretation**:
  - Restrictive Interpretation
  - Extensive Interpretation

#### (a) Restrictive Interpretation
- **Cessante Ratione Rule**:
  - Focus: Purpose of legislation ceasing.
  - Court’s action: Must suspend operation of legislation if purpose is non-existent.

- **Eiusdem Generis Rule**:
  - Challenges of broad legislative terms.
  - Court limits wide phrases to specific categories to maintain legislative intent.

#### (b) Extensive Interpretation
- **Interpretation by Implication**:
  - Expands initial text meaning through implied understanding.
  
- **Examples**:
  - **Ex Contrariis Rule**: Opposites imply added meanings (e.g., prohibitions encompass all means leading to results).
  
  - **Inclusio Unius Rule**: Express mention of one thing implies exclusion of another.

## Chapter 8: Noncompliance with Legislation

### 8.1 General Introduction
- Legislative provisions often state consequences for noncompliance.
- **Peremptory Provisions**: Noncompliance leads to nullity of actions.
- **Directory Provisions**: Allows for substantial compliance to be valid. 

### 8.2 Some Guidelines
#### 8.2.1 Semantic Guidelines
- **Peremptory Provision**: Indicative words (e.g., must, shall) imply obligation; negative terms indicate mandatoriness.
- **Directory Provision**: Permissive language (e.g., may) suggests discretion; vague terms indicate flexibility.

#### 8.2.2 Jurisprudential Guidelines
- **Peremptory Provision**:
  - Presence of penal provisions indicates stringent requirements.
  - Non-compliance that defeats legislative purpose results in nullity.

- **Directory Provision**:
  - Positive terms without penalties imply non-compliance does not invalidate actions.
  - Consideration of justice and prevention of fraud may influence directory classification.

# Chapter 8

## Study Unit 8: Noncompliance with Legislation

### 8.2 Peremptory and Directory Provisions

- **Historical Context**: Indicates whether a provision is peremptory (mandatory) or directory (permissive).

#### 8.2.1 Definitions
- **Peremptory Provision**:
  - Requirements must be fully complied with to validly obtain rights, privileges, or immunities.
  - Emphasis on individual freedom leads courts to stress a peremptory nature.
  
- **Directory Provision**:
  - Non-compliance does not invalidate the act.
  - Presumption exists against nullity if legislation protects public revenue.

#### 8.2.2 Guidelines for Interpretation
- If a provision mandates action within a specific time without the possibility of extension, it is presumed peremptory.
- Other provisions becoming superfluous based on non-compliance suggest directory status.
- Application of these guidelines is context-dependent; fairness, justice, and practicality are crucial considerations.

### 8.3 Case Studies

- **Weenen Transitional Local Council v Van Dyk 2000 (3) SA 435 (NPD)**:
  - Dispute on tax assessment procedure under Local Authorities Ordinance 25 of 1974.
  - **Court’s Judgment**: In favor of Van Dyk; strict adherence to publication requirements was necessary for due taxes. The imperative wording (“shall publish”) supported this.

- **Commercial Union Assurance v Clarke 1972 (3) SA 508 (AD)**:
  - Issue of compliance with mail requirement in Motor Vehicle Insurance Act 29 of 1942.
  - **Court's Judgment**: Held that compliance was substantial; the technical failure (ordinary post instead of registered) did not prejudice the insurer. A purposive approach was adopted.

- **African Christian Democratic Party v Electoral Commission 2006 (3) SA 305 (CC)**:
  - ACDP's deposit compliance for election participation.
  - **Court's Judgment**: ACDP substantially complied despite technicalities. The decision emphasized a trend towards purposive interpretation to uphold voting rights.

### 8.4 Key Principles

- **Peremptory vs. Directory**:
  - Peremptory requires exact compliance; results in nullity if not complied with.
  - Directory allows for substantial compliance; does not invalidate the act upon partial compliance.

- **Purposive Interpretation**:
  - Legislative compliance is viewed within the context of fair and just outcomes.
  - Courts must assess adherence based on the central purpose of the legislation and align it with the Bill of Rights (Section 39(2) of the Constitution).

### 8.5 Steps for Determining Compliance

1. Establish the central purpose of the provision.
2. Assess if a literal interpretation obstructs its purpose.
3. Consider an interpretation that furthers the central purpose.
4. Ensure the interpretation aligns with the spirit and objects of the Bill of Rights.

### 9.1 INTRODUCTION 
- Constitutional interpretation differs from statutory interpretation.
- Constitution is crafted by a Constituent Assembly, not a legislative body.
- Bill of Rights has broad value statements; legislation focuses on technical details.
- Section 39(2) mandates consideration of the Bill of Rights in legislative interpretation.

### 9.2 WHY IS A SUPREME CONSTITUTION DIFFERENT? 
- Constitution is the supreme legal text setting state structure and foundational values.
- Contains ethical and symbolic elements lacking in ordinary legislation.
- Quasi-constitutional legislation (e.g., **Employment Equity Act**) aligns with ethical aspirations.

### 9.3 HOW TO INTERPRET THE CONSTITUTION 
#### 9.3.1 Constitutional guidelines
- Two sets: as per the Constitution (section 39(1)) and judicial interpretations.
  
(i) **Section 39(1) and foundational values**  
- Values in section 39(1) found in the Preamble and section 1; establishes the Bill of Rights’ purpose.
  
(ii) **Principles from the courts**  
- Courts affirm principles, e.g.:
  - Generous and purposeful Constitution interpretation (**Shabalala**).
  - Liberal and flexible interpretation.
  - Consideration of underlying values during interpretation (**Acheson**).
  
#### A comprehensive methodology 
- Various principles = a smaller set of methods/approaches, reference **S v Makwanyane**. 
- Weigh various considerations judiciously among five methodologies.

### 9.4 AVOIDING UNCONSTITUTIONAL LEGISLATION 
- Courts assess legislation against constitutional standards to prevent unconstitutionality.
