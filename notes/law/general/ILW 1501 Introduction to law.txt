## Law and Justice - Are they synonyms?
- **Definitions**:
  - **Law**: A set of norms distinguishing right from wrong; defines rights and duties; influenced by societal values and morals.
  - **Justice**: Achieved through comprehensive consideration of individual differences.
- **Critical Questions**:
  - What is the law?
  - Where do we find the law?
  - What is justice?
  - How do we achieve justice?
  - Are law and justice the same?

## 1.2 What is the Law?
- **Concept**: A body of rules that orders society fairly and equitably.
- **Application**: Governs relationships (private vs. State).
- **Rule of Law**: Everyone is equal before the law; adherence is essential.
- **Evolution**: Law evolves with societal changes (e.g., smoking regulations).
- **Sanctions**: Imposed for non-adherence to laws.

## 1.3 Where is our Law Situated?
- **Sources of Law**:
  - **Legislation**: Primary source; written laws passed by Parliament. Must align with the Constitution.
  - **Court Decisions (Case Law)**: Binding judgments; precedent affects future cases.
  - **Common Law**: Derived from Roman-Dutch law; includes unwritten principles (e.g., murder).
  - **Customary Law**: Unwritten community practices; indigenous law recognized since 1994 and protected by the Constitution.

## 1.4 Different Branches of our Law
- **Public Law**: Regulates relationships between State and individual (e.g., criminal cases).
- **Private Law**: Governs relationships between individuals (e.g., contracts, property).

**Criminal Cases**: State prosecutes offenders for violating the law, impacting victims and community.

**Civil Cases**: Disputes between two parties without state intervention, often settled in court.

## 3.2 When is a Dispute a Legal Dispute?
- Society’s diversity leads to differing views and potential conflicts.
- Resolution of disputes typically occurs through dialogue (social disputes).
- Legal dispute arises when resolution fails or laws are broken.

### Types of Disputes:
- **Social Dispute**: 
  - Example: Family disagreement over selling property without consent (no legal implications).
  - Example: Partners reaching agreement on purchases (negotiated resolution).

### 3.2.1 Dispute with Legal Implications
- **Example**: Child damages neighbor's property (television). 

### Resolution Methods:
- **Negotiation**: Discuss directly with the other party.
- **Mediation**: Involves a neutral third party to facilitate a resolution.
- **Arbitration**: Binding decision made by an appointed third party. 

### Informal Dispute Resolution:
- **Negotiation**: Attempt to reach an agreement on damage costs directly.
- **Mediation**: Use of a mutual friend/community leader for resolution guidance.
- **Arbitration**: Third-party adjudication deciding responsibility for damages.

### 3.2.2 Formal Dispute Resolution
- If a dispute remains unresolved, court intervention is necessary.
  
### 3.3 How Do These Examples Differ?
- Interpersonal disputes on social level vs. disputes involving legal implications.
- Various resolution methods exist, including court involvement.
- Criminal actions result in legal charges, distinguishing them from informal disputes.

### 3.4 Legal Disputes: The Difference Between Criminal and Civil Disputes
- Legal disputes can be resolved informally (Alternative Dispute Resolution) or formally (litigation).
- Litigation involves formal court procedures where judges or magistrates preside.
- Distinction between criminal and civil matters based on purpose and procedure.

### 3.5 Role Players in the Court Process
#### 3.5.1 Criminal Disputes
- Criminal disputes pertain to public law, held under criminal procedure.
  - Presiding officer: Magistrate or Judge.
  - Roles involved:
    - Police: Investigates crime.
    - National Prosecuting Authority: Institutes proceedings.
    - State Prosecutor: Represents the State, presents evidence.
    - Legal Practitioner: Assists the accused.
   
#### 3.5.2 Legal Process
- A crime is defined by unlawful conduct, requiring:
  - Conduct (act or omission).
  - Compliance with definitional elements of crime.
  - Unlawfulness.
  - Culpability (blameworthy state of mind).
- State prosecutes the accused aiding justice and community protection.
- Standard of proof: Beyond a reasonable doubt; outcomes are acquittal or conviction.

### 3.5.2 Civil Disputes
- Civil disputes involve private law, without State prosecution.
- Parties: Plaintiff (wronged party) vs. Defendant (accused).
- Aim: Determine liability, leading to potential damages or compensation.
- Standard of proof: Balance of probabilities; success results in awarded damages.
- Examples include:
  - Defamation
  - Breach of Contract
  - Medical Costs after accidents. 

### Conclusion
- Criminal cases involve the State and standard of proof is high; whereas civil cases are voluntary, aiming to resolve disputes without State intervention.
- A single event can lead to both civil and criminal proceedings, highlighting the interplay between various legal frameworks.

# Study Notes 

## 3.6: A Less Adversarial Process  
- Law assists in legal disputes, and court is often lengthy and expensive.  
- Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) can offer quicker and cheaper solutions than court.  
- Main forms of ADR in civil disputes:  
  - **Negotiation**: Parties communicate to resolve differences.  
  - **Mediation**: Impartial third party (mediator) facilitates negotiation; parties control outcome.  
  - **Arbitration**: Independent third party (arbitrator) makes a binding decision.  

## 3.7: A Few Tips About the Court Process  
- Civil disputes follow specific procedures aimed at settling agreements.  
- **Prescription**: Time limits exist for pursuing claims; if not pursued in time, the claim prescribes.  
- **Legal Aid**: Section 34 of the Constitution provides access to courts; legal aid available for indigent persons.  
  - Eligibility determined by a means test considering income and assets.  

## 3.8: Different Cases, Different Courts  
- Different courts for different disputes, governed by jurisdiction.  
- **Magistrates' Courts**:  
  - Deal with less serious criminal and civil matters.  
  - **District Courts**:  
    - Max 3 years prison or fines up to R120,000; civil claims < R200,000.  
  - **Regional Courts**:  
    - Hears serious crimes (e.g., murder); max fine R600,000; civil claims between R200,000 and R400,000.  

- **High Courts**:  
  - Hear both civil and criminal matters; no monetary limits.  
  - Handle appeals from Magistrates’ Courts.  

- **Supreme Court of Appeal**:  
  - Only one Supreme Court of Appeal in Bloemfontein, hears appeals from High Courts, with cases heard by at least three judges.

## **Chapter 3: Understanding the Court System**

### **3.1 Constitutional Court**
- Highest court in South Africa, located in Braamfontein, Johannesburg.
- Judges preside over cases regarding constitutional issues, focusing on interpretation, protection, and enforcement of the Constitution.

### **3.2 Other (Speciality) Courts**
- Various courts address specific legal issues.
- Notable speciality courts include:
  - **Small Claims Courts**
  - **Equality Courts**
  - **Children’s Courts**
  - **Child Justice Courts**

### **3.3 Small Claims Courts**
- Established in 1985 for minor civil disputes (< R20,000).
- Aims to make justice accessible and affordable.
- Commissioner (presiding officer) is usually a practicing advocate or attorney.
- No legal representation allowed; parties represent themselves.

### **3.4 Equality Courts**
- Established under the **Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000**.
- Address unfair discrimination, hate speech, and harassment.
- No attorney required for cases.

### **3.5 Children’s Courts**
- Governed by **Children’s Act 38 of 2005**; child defined as under 18 years.
- Focus on child protection, rights, and welfare.
- Deals with issues like paternity determination, adoption, and child protection.

### **3.6 Child Justice Courts**
- Operate under the **Child Justice Act 75 of 2008**.
- Establish a child justice system distinct from adult criminal procedures.

## 4.12 The Public Prosecutor (Page 1)
- Role: Key functionary in the criminal justice process.

### Pre-Trial Phase
- Decision to prosecute based on reasonable evidence assessment of the suspect:

### During Trial
- Prosecutor presents evidence and manages witness testimonies.
  
### Fit in Criminal Justice System
- Works for the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA).
- Mandate: Institute/discontinue criminal proceedings (Constitution of the Republic of South Africa).

### NPA Overview
- Powers sourced from the *National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998*:
  - Institute criminal proceedings on behalf of the State.
  - Discontinue criminal proceedings.
  - Conduct functions incidental to prosecutions.

## The Role of the Case Docket (Page 2)
- Case docket initiated by SAPS post-crime:
  - Registration, transfer to detective unit, investigation.
- Docket contents for prosecution include:
  - Objective evidence (e.g., physical, forensic).
  - Subjective evidence (witness testimony).
  
### Evidence Collection
- Witnesses must be interviewed to strengthen the case.

### Docket Preparation
- Steps:
  - Secure evidence, interview witnesses, gather data, identify suspects.
- Could lead to arrest and bail considerations.

## The Role of the Prosecutor during the Trial (Page 2)
- Presents State's evidence; proves guilt “beyond reasonable doubt.”
- Ensures victim and witness rights; keeps them informed.

## Diversion of Procedures (Page 3)

### The Public Prosecutor
- Roles include:
  - Assisting SAPS in evidence gathering.
  - Deciding on prosecution merits (prima facie cases).
  - Presenting evidence for State's conviction.
  - Advocating for justice and fair sentencing.
- **Inefficiency Consequences:** 
  - Potential wrongful acquittals.
  - Overcrowding in correctional facilities.
  - Perception that "crime pays".

### The National Prosecuting Authority (NPA)
- Determines whether there is sufficient evidence to warrant prosecution.
- Key Example: NPA's decision on President Zuma's prosecution, judged illegal by the North Gauteng High Court.

### Witnesses
- Types:
  - Victims, eyewitnesses, police, forensic experts.
- Their testimony can be challenged by the accused's legal representative.

### Defence Lawyer
- Tasks include:
  - Countering prosecutor's evidence.
  - Cross-examining witnesses.
  - Presenting the accused’s case.

### The Presiding Officer
- Role includes:
  - Evaluating evidence and arguments.
  - Making a determination of guilt or innocence based on "beyond reasonable doubt".
- Acquittal occurs if reasonable doubt exists.

## 5.4 Bail
- **Legislation:** Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (detention not exceeding 48 hours without court order).
- **Factors Assessed for Bail:**
  - Risk to individuals/society.
  - Likelihood of flight.
  - Potential for witness interference or evidence destruction.
  - Impact on justice system objectives.
  - Public order disturbance.

- **Section 35 of the Constitution:** Rights of arrested, detained, and accused persons.
- Rights including:
  - Right to know the charge.
  - Right to a prompt and public trial.
  - Right to appear in court within 48 hours post-arrest.
