## 1 LEARNING UNIT 1: Setting the scene

### 1.1 EXTERNAL AND INTERNAL LEGAL HISTORY
- **External Legal History**: Traces sources/factors influencing legal system development (political, constitutional, economic, sociological, religious).
- **Internal Legal History**: Covers origins/development of legal rules/principles influenced by external events.
- **Examples**:
  - **Apartheid Policy**: External event; **Group Areas Act** as internal development.
  - **Trade Unions**: Economic event leading to recognition of employee rights.

### 1.2 THE DIFFERENT COMPONENTS OF SOUTH AFRICAN LAW
- **Legal Pluralism**: Existence of multiple legal systems in South Africa (Hindu, Muslim, indigenous African, Western).
- **Mixed/Hybrid Legal System**: Combination of civil (Roman) law and English common law; indigenous law recognized post-1990s.
- **Constitutional Recognition**: Indigenous law now part of the legal system.
- **Case Reference**: *Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of South Africa v President of the Republic of South Africa* 2000 (2) SA 674 (CC) para [44].

### 1.3 THE SOURCES OF LAW: THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN THE DIFFERENT COMPONENTS
- **Sources of Law**:
  - **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**: Supreme law.
  - **Legislation**: Primary authoritative source.
  - **Court Decisions**: Judicial precedents.
  - **Common Law**: Historical component, primarily Roman-Dutch law influenced by English law.
  - **Custom**: Includes indigenous African law.
- **Indigenous Law**: Previously secondary; now recognized as a source of law post-1996 Constitution.
- **Common Law Adaptation**: Courts adapt common law to modern values (e.g., *Paulsen v Slip Knot Investments* 2015 (3) SA 479 (CC)).
- **Interrelation of Legal Components**: Indigenous African law increasingly influences legal development (e.g., *Le Roux v Dey* 2011 (3) SA 274 (CC)).

## LEARNING UNIT 1: Setting the scene

- **Constitution as Supreme Law**  
  - Bill of Rights is the supreme law.  
  - All laws must conform to the Constitution.  
  - **Section 8**: Bill of Rights binds all state organs.  
  - **Section 39(2)**: Courts must interpret laws in line with the Bill of Rights.  
  - Laws conflicting with the Constitution can be developed or abolished.

### 1.4 THE RECEPTION PHENOMENON

- **Legal System Integration**  
  - Legal systems can become applicable through:
    - **Reception**: Adoption of rules into an existing system.
    - **Transplantation**: Introduction into a territory without a legal system.
    - **Imposition**: Forced introduction into an existing system.

- **Historical Context**  
  - **Roman-Dutch Law**: Initially thought to be transplanted; however, indigenous legal systems existed.
  - **Imposition**: Roman-Dutch law was imposed on indigenous communities.
  - **Reception Examples**: 
    - English law was willingly received by the Cape settler community.
    - Human-rights law was accepted through the Constitution.

- **Types of Reception**  
  - **Practical Reception**: Adoption of actual rules.
  - **Scientific Reception**: Adoption of concepts and structures.

## LEARNING UNIT 2: The African component and Islamic law

### 2.1 Indigenous Law

- **Indigenous Law Overview**
  - Recorded through legislation, codiﬁcation, and restatement; primarily oral law.
  - Continues to develop within indigenous communities; field research necessary for understanding.

#### 2.1.1 The Cape 1652–1795
- **Jan van Riebeeck's Arrival (1652)**
  - Established a trading post for the Dutch East India Company.
  - Judicial administration was poorly organized; indigenous law application was neglected.
- **British Control (1795)**
  - Marked the end of the pre-colonial era; indigenous law began to be regulated.

#### 2.1.2 The Colonial Period
- **Civilizing Policies**
  - Aimed to eliminate indigenous laws and customs; indigenous law often unrecognized.
  - Recognition subject to a repugnancy clause; only applicable if not contrary to Western public policy.
- **Legislation**
  - 1878: Codiﬁcation of Zulu law in Natal.
  - 1883: Code for Transkeian Territories.
  - 1899: Orange Free State recognized indigenous law.
- **Political Transitions**
  - Little change for indigenous populations post-Union (1910) and Republic (1961); unofficial law gained importance.

#### 2.1.3 The Post-Colonial Era
- **External Legal History**
  - Apartheid policies (post-1948) aimed at segregation; influenced indigenous law development.
  - **Black Administration Act 38 of 1927**
    - Provided limited recognition of indigenous law; established a system of black administration.
    - Created separate court systems for blacks, leading to loss of credibility over time.

- **Special Courts for Blacks Abolition Act 34 of 1986**
  - Abolished commissioners’ courts; repealed section 11 of the Black Administration Act.
  - Repugnancy clause retained in subsequent legislation.

#### 2.1.4 The Constitutional Era
- **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**
  - Recognizes indigenous law as a source of South African law (Section 211(3)).
  - Indigenous law subject to the Constitution; must align with equality standards.
- **Bhe Case (2005)**
  - Constitutional Court ruled male primogeniture unconstitutional; led to the Reform of Customary Law of Succession and Regulation of Related Matters Act 11 of 2009.
- **Law Reform**
  - Repeal of the Black Administration Act and Amendment of Certain Laws Act 28 of 2005; aimed to eliminate remnants of discriminatory laws.
  - Increasing importance of indigenous law in legal education and practice.

### 2.2 Islamic Law

- **Historical Context**
  - Muslims arrived at the Cape in the 1650s; personal laws not recognized, causing hardships.
  - Conflicts between Islamic law and Western common law, particularly in marriage and succession.

#### 2.2.1 Before the Constitutional Era
- **Court Attitudes**
  - Non-recognition of Muslim marriages due to potential polygyny deemed against public policy (Ismail v Ismail, 1983).
  
#### 2.2.2 The Constitutional Era
- **Changing Court Perspectives**
  - 1990s: Courts began recognizing Muslim marriages (Ryland v Edros, Amod v Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund).
  - **Daniels v Campbell NO (2004)**
    - Recognized monogamous Muslim marriages under the Intestate Succession Act.
  - **Hassam v Jacobs NO (2009)**
    - Declared section 1 of the Intestate Succession Act unconstitutional for excluding polygynous marriages.
  
- **Law Reform Commission**
  - Proposed Bill for recognition of Islamic marriages; not yet approved by Parliament.

### Key Legislation and Cases
- **Black Administration Act 38 of 1927**
- **Special Courts for Blacks Abolition Act 34 of 1986**
- **Reform of Customary Law of Succession and Regulation of Related Matters Act 11 of 2009**
- **Repeal of the Black Administration Act and Amendment of Certain Laws Act 28 of 2005**

### Conclusion
- Indigenous law and Islamic law have evolved under significant historical and legislative influences.
- Current legal frameworks reflect ongoing changes and recognition efforts, particularly in the context of constitutional rights.

## Chapter 3: The Western Component: Roman Legal History until the 11th Century

### Learning Outcomes
- Importance of Roman law for South African jurists.
- Influence of ancient Greek philosophical thought on Western legal tradition.
- Development of Roman law through four political eras.
- Significance of the **Corpus Iuris Civilis** for modern jurists.

### Overview of Roman Law
- Roman law is part of Roman-Dutch law; relevant today despite the fall of the Western Roman Empire.
- **Case Reference**: *Hendricks v Hendricks and others* (2016) - Supreme Court of Appeal cited the **Institutes of Justinian**.
- **Constitutional Adaptation**: Courts adapt Roman law to societal needs per **Section 39(2)** of the Constitution.
- South Africa has a hybrid legal system influenced by Roman law and English common law.

### Historical Context
- **Roman Empire Division**: Western Empire (capital Rome) fell in AD 476; Eastern Empire (Byzantium) lasted until AD 1453.
- **Government Types**: Monarchy (753 BC–509 BC), Republic (509 BC–27 BC), Principate (27 BC–AD 284), Dominate (AD 284–AD 476).
- **Roman Law Development Periods**:
  - Early Roman Law (753 BC–250 BC)
  - Pre-Classical Period (250 BC–27 BC)
  - Classical Period (27 BC–AD 284)
  - Post-Classical Period (AD 284–AD 565)

### 3.1 The Monarchy and Early Roman Law (753 BC–509 BC)
- **Founding of Rome**: Legend of Romulus; kings held autocratic power.
- **Legal System**: Rigid ius civile applied only to Roman citizens.
- **Social Classes**: Division into patricians and plebeians led to class struggles.

### 3.2 The Republic: Early Roman Law and Pre-Classical Roman Law (509 BC–27 BC)
- Transition from monarchy to republic; growth of Rome into an empire.
- **Key Influences**:
  - **Law of the Twelve Tables** (450 BC): Ended patrician control, provided legal access, created legal certainty.
  - **Praetor's Role**: Administered justice, developed civil procedure, introduced **ius gentium** for foreigners.
  - **Jurists**: Initially laymen, later became legal advisors.

### 3.3 The Principate and Classical Roman Law (27 BC–AD 284)
- Governed by emperors; Augustus as the first emperor.
- **Legal Development**: Classical Roman law emerged, superior to other ancient legal systems.
- **Contributors**:
  - **Emperor**: Central authority in law-making.
  - **Praetor**: Continued to refine legal procedures.
  - **Jurists**: Provided legal interpretations and advice.

### Important Notes
- **Critical Thinking Roots**: Ancient Greek thought influenced Western legal ideals.
- **Legal Evolution**: Roman law adapted to societal changes; not the sole foundation of South African law.

## LEARNING UNIT 3: The Western component: Roman legal history until the 11th century

### 3.1 The Emperor
- Roman Empire governed by emperor and Senate; actual power with emperor.
- Emperor had law-making authority; jurists influenced law administration.
- Imperial legislation became significant; by 2nd century AD, most laws from emperor/officials.
- Quality of legislation often low.

### 3.2 The Praetor
- Praetor elected by emperor, acted on emperor's instructions.
- Functions of praetor absorbed by emperor; Edictum Perpetuum (AD 130) codified praetorian edicts.
- Praetor bound by Edict; limited contribution to legal development.

### 3.3 The Jurists
- Elite group of legal experts; provided legal advice, teaching, court assistance, and interpretation.
- Writings crucial for Roman law development.
- **Five Great Roman Jurists**:
  - **Gaius**: Author of *Institutes*, influential textbook.
  - **Papinian**
  - **Ulpian**
  - **Paul**
  - **Modestinus**
- Their works comprised two-thirds of writings codified by Justinian (6th century AD); Statute of Citation (AD 426) recognized them as authoritative.

### 3.4 The Dominate and Post-Classical Roman Law (AD 284–AD 476)
- Post-classical era marked by autocratic emperors (beginning with Diocletian).
- Jurists absorbed into imperial law office; Senate reduced to announcing imperial legislation.
- Legal simplification efforts; rise of "vulgar law" (Roman law influenced by Germanic law).
- Renewed interest in classical law led to Justinian's codification.

### 3.5 Collections of Imperial Laws
- Codex Theodosianus (AD 438): first official collection of imperial legislation; influenced later codifications (e.g., Lex Romana Visigothorum, Corpus Iuris Civilis).

### 3.6 Collections and Simplifications of Classical Writings
- Post-classical jurists produced simplified works based on classical writings (e.g., Epitome Gai, Sententiae Pauli).

### 3.7 The Statute of Citation, AD 426
- Simplified legal authority; only works of Five Great jurists considered authoritative.

### 3.8 The Fall of the Western Roman Empire
- Fall in AD 476 due to Germanic invasions; personality principle applied (individuals followed tribal laws).
- **Leges Romanae barbarorum**: Germanic codifications of Roman law for Roman subjects (e.g., Lex Romana Visigothorum, AD 506).

### 3.9 Emperor Justinian’s Codification: The Corpus Iuris Civilis
- Justinian (AD 527-565) aimed to codify law for clarity and accessibility.
- Codification process:
  - **Codex**: Systematized imperial legislation.
  - **Digest**: Updated juristic law; majority from Five Great jurists.
  - **Institutes**: Textbook for students, based on Gaius.
  - **Novellae**: Later imperial legislation.
- Importance: Corpus Iuris Civilis became a key source of Roman law; influenced legal systems, including South Africa.

### 3.10 Conclusion
- Roman law's development over 2,000 years; Corpus Iuris Civilis remains relevant.
- Rediscovered in the 12th century; influenced modern legal systems.

### References
- **Legislation**: Codex Theodosianus, Lex Romana Visigothorum, Corpus Iuris Civilis.
- **Cases**: Hendricks, Paulsen.

## LEARNING UNIT 4: The Western component: Legal development in Europe from the 12th to the 19th centuries

### 4.1 THE MEDIEVAL LAW SCHOOLS
- **The glossators**
  - First jurists studying Justinian’s codification of Roman law (12th century, Bologna).
  - Aimed to rediscover and restore Roman law using the *Corpus Iuris Civilis*.
  - Employed interpretative methods, writing "glosses" on the *Corpus Iuris*.
  - Notable figure: **Accursius**; his *Glossa Ordinaria* became authoritative.
  - Their work ensured the survival and spread of Roman law across Europe.

- **The ultramontani**
  - Jurists at the French law school of Orléans (13th-14th centuries).
  - Focused on practical applications of Roman law for 14th-century courts.
  - Investigated town law, canon law, and Germanic customary law.
  - Key figures: **Revigny** and **Bellaperche**; established rules for canon law reception.

- **The post-glossators or commentators**
  - Emerged post-12th century, addressing practical legal challenges due to economic growth.
  - Wrote commentaries on the *Corpus Iuris Civilis*, integrating various legal sources.
  - Notable figures: **Bartolus** and **Baldus**; foundational in private international law.
  - Achievements:
    - Laid groundwork for 17th-century natural law.
    - Influenced modern private international law.
    - Facilitated Roman law's integration into justice systems across Europe.

### 4.2 CANON LAW
- **Historical backdrop**
  - Canon law integrated into Germanic customary legal systems, influencing Roman-Dutch law in South Africa.
  - Canon law less formal than Roman law, softening its severity.
  - Principle of *pacta servanda sunt* (agreements must be honored) derived from canon law.

- **The Corpus Iuris Canonici**
  - Compiled by **Gratianus** in the 12th century; foundational for canon law studies.
  - Studied alongside the *Corpus Iuris Civilis* in medieval law schools.

- **Reception of canon law into secular law**
  - Initially random; later structured by ultramontani rules.
  - Canon law applied to church matters; Roman law to secular matters.
  - Canon law's fairness tempered Roman law's severity.

### 4.3 THE EUROPEAN IUS COMMUNE
- Common law developed from Roman law, canon law, and Germanic customary law (12th-15th centuries).
- Key components:
  - Adaptation of Roman and canon law to local needs.
- Influenced legal systems in Western Europe; facilitated by commentators and printing.
- **Relevance to South African law**:
  - Roman-Dutch law's development linked to European legal history.
  - Shared legal heritage among Southern African countries (e.g., South Africa, Namibia).

### 4.4 LEGAL DEVELOPMENT IN EUROPE FROM THE 16TH TO THE EARLY 19TH CENTURIES
- Shift of legal scholarship center from Italy to France (16th century).
  
- **France and legal humanism**
  - Humanists aimed to rediscover classical Roman law pre-Justinian.
  - Focused on systematic study rather than fragmented analysis.
  - Contributions:
    - Spread of Roman law.
    - High-standard work in pure Roman law.
    - Systematization of legal material.
  - Notable jurist: **Pothier**; influential treatises on civil law.
  - **Code Civil** (1804) codified French civil law, influencing many European legal systems.

- **Germany**
  - Roman law reception more complete than in France or the Netherlands.
  - German legal literature followed humanist approaches, with greater practical influence.

## LEARNING UNIT 4: The Western component: Legal development in Europe from the 12th to the 19th centuries

- **17th and 18th Centuries: Usus Modernus Pandectarum**
  - Emergence in Germany; practical application of law.
  - Leading proponent: **Carpzovius II**.

- **19th Century German Jurisprudence**
  - Influential on European legal systems.
  - Rise of the **historical school**; reaction against natural law.
  - Key figure: **Savigny**; significant contributions to German law.
  - Codification movement; introduction of **Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch** in 1900.

### 4.5 THE NETHERLANDS

- **Roman-Dutch Law in South Africa**
  - Introduced in 1652; significant influence on South African law.
  - Law of Holland was predominant; other provinces influenced by it.

- **Reception of Roman Law**
  - Occurred from the late 15th to late 16th centuries.
  - Influenced by political, economic, and cultural factors.
  - Foundation of **University of Louvain**; dual faculties of canon and Roman law.

- **Codification**
  - **Napoleon's Conquest**: Introduction of French civil code by Louis Bonaparte in 1809.
  - Dutch civil code promulgated in 1838; modeled on **Code Civil**.
  - Similarities among French, German, and Dutch law during this period.

- **Dutch Jurists**
  - Roman-Dutch law as an independent system for nearly three centuries.
  - Key jurists:
    - **Hugo de Groot (Grotius)**: 
      - *Inleidinge tot de Hollandsche Rechtsgeleerdheid*; first treatise on Roman-Dutch law.
      - *De Jure Belli ac Pacis*; foundational work in public international law.
    - **Simon van Leeuwen**: 
      - First to term existing Dutch law as “Roman-Dutch law”.
      - *Het Roomsch-Hollandsche Recht*; comprehensive review of Roman-Dutch law.
    - **Johannes Voet**: 
      - Influential in South African practice.
      - *Commentarius ad Pandectas*; extensive commentary on Roman-Dutch law.
    - **Dionysius van der Keessel**: 
      - *Theses Selectae Juris Hollandici et Zeelandici*; significant work before South Africa's separation.
    - **Johannes van der Linden**: 
      - Prolific writer; *Regtsgeleerd, Practicaal en Koopmans Handboek*; last treatise before codification.

- **Dutch Humanist School**
  - Influenced by humanism; focused on practical needs of existing law.

## 4.6 CONCLUSION
- Revival of Roman law and its reception in Europe.
- Development of a European ius commune with Roman law and canon law as foundations.
- Importance of the law of Holland in South African legal history.

## LEARNING UNIT 5: The development of the Western component in South African law before the 1990s

- Focus on the integration of Roman-Dutch and English law in South Africa (1652-1990s).

  - Existing justice system maintained; temporary Dutch control in 1803.

- **Second British Occupation (1806)**

  - Commission established to recommend legal system changes; led to **Charter of Justice** in 1828.

## LEARNING UNIT 5: The development of the Western component in South African law before the 1990s

### 5.3 THE RECEPTION OF ENGLISH LAW AT THE CAPE (1828–1910)
- **Charter of Justice**: Initiated changes in court structure and formal law, leading to English law influence.
- **Court Changes**:
  - **Supreme Court of the Colony of the Cape of Good Hope** replaced the Raad van Justitie.
  - **Privy Council** appeal established.
  - **Jury system** introduced (discontinued in 1969).
  - Judges recruited from England, Ireland, Scotland.
  - Advocates required to have qualifications from specific universities or prior experience in the Raad van Justitie.
- **Second Charter of Justice (1834)**: Re-enacted first Charter with amendments; Roman-Dutch law remained applicable, but gradual English law assimilation was encouraged.

### 5.4 THE MECHANICS OF THE RECEPTION PROCESS
- **English Institutions**: Strong influence in education, language, and commerce; English customs dominated business practices.
- **Judiciary**: English judges promoted assimilation of English law into Roman-Dutch law; advocates trained in English law preferred English authorities.
- **Legislation**: Cape statutes from 1828 mirrored British legislation, enhancing English law emphasis.
- **Extent of Reception**: Included comprehensive reception of English Companies Act, negotiable instruments law, and parliamentary conventions; represented both practical and scientific reception of English law.

### 5.5 LEGAL DEVELOPMENT OUTSIDE THE CAPE (1838–1910)
- **Boer Migration**: Non-British population (Boers/Voortrekkers) moved inland to form independent states (Transvaal and Free State).
- **Legal Foundations**:
  - **Zuid-Afrikaansche Republiek**: Law based on Van der Linden’s *Koopmans Handboek*, supplemented by Leeuwen and Grotius.
  - **Orange Free State**: Roman-Dutch law declared basic law, with Voet, Leeuwen, Grotius, Van der Linden, and Van der Keessel as authoritative sources.
- **Anglo-Boer War (1899-1902)**: Strained relations led to British annexation and subsequent assimilation of English law into Roman-Dutch law.
- **Natal**: Initially Roman-Dutch law; modified by English procedural laws post-1845 British control.

### 5.6 LEGAL DEVELOPMENT SINCE THE UNIFICATION OF SOUTH AFRICA IN 1910
- **Unification (1910)**: Merged four colonies; established Appellate Division of the Supreme Court, pivotal for legal development.
- **Scientific Legal Practice**: Emergence of law faculties at independent universities fostered scientific study of Roman-Dutch law.
- **Appellate Division Role**: Unified legal system; independent legal development; rejected outdated Roman-Dutch principles while not deviating from established principles.
- **Influence of English Law**: Continued but diminished post-1910; courts adapted Roman-Dutch rules to modern needs (e.g., *Linvestment CC v Hammersley*).

### 5.7 THE SOUTH AFRICAN LAW REFORM COMMISSION
- **Established (1973)**: Investigates legal matters referred by the Minister of Justice; proposes law changes based on societal needs.
- **Draft Bills**: Often incorporate comparative legal material, harmonizing common law and indigenous law.

### 5.8 CONCLUSION
- Overview of the Western component of South African law until the early 20th century; next unit will address the liberation movement's impact on law development.

## LEARNING UNIT 6: The South African liberation movement
- Influenced the development of a constitutional democracy and created institutional constraints on the exercise of power.

### 6.1 What is a national liberation movement?
- Political movement aimed at national independence and freedom from domination.
- In South Africa, sought a just society and legitimate legal order through:
  - Passive resistance (non-violent protest)
  - Civil disobedience (deliberate law-breaking)
- Gained international support from the Organisation of African Unity.

### 6.2 The national liberation movement and change in South Africa
- **Background**
  - Racial discrimination and political repression existed throughout South Africa's history.

## LEARNING UNIT 7: The universal component: The history of human rights in South Africa

### 7.1 Natural-law philosophy as the foundation of human rights
- Human rights traditionally viewed as Western origin, linked to natural-law theory.
- Natural-law: higher, eternal norms not created by humans; all states subject to these norms.
- Key philosophers:
  - **St Augustine** (4th century AD)
  - **Ibn Rushd** (12th century AD)
  - **Thomas Aquinas** (13th century AD)
  - **Hugo de Groot**: modern natural law; derived from human rationality, not divine.
- **John Locke**: inalienable rights to life, liberty, property; state exists to protect these rights; foundation of constitutional democracy.
- Global movements: 
  - **American Declaration of Independence** (1776)
  - **French Constitution** (1789)
  - **Universal Declaration of Human Rights** (1948)
- **African Charter on Human and People’s Rights**: adopted 1986; African Court established 2004.

### 7.2 The African philosophy of ubuntu as the foundation of human rights
- **S v Makwanyane**: Constitutional Court emphasized the Bill of Rights rooted in South African values, particularly ubuntu.
- **Ubuntu**: spirit of humaneness, social justice, fairness; virtues include tolerance, compassion, forgiveness.
- Emphasizes human dignity and community over individualism; includes rights and duties.

### 7.3 Human rights in South Africa before April 1994
- Apartheid legislation violated human rights; common law restricted.
- **Zuid-Afrikaansche Republiek (ZAR)**: Constitutional crisis; courts invalidated Volksraad decisions; Chief Justice Kotzé dismissed.
- **Orange Free State Constitution (1854)**: early recognition of human rights; influenced by American Constitution; procedural rules for legislation.
- **Union of South Africa (1910)**: formal testing powers of courts; Harris v Minister of the Interior: courts ruled on procedural validity, not moral content.

### 7.4 Human rights in South Africa since April 1994
- Bill of Rights in 1993 and 1996 Constitutions marked a shift towards human rights recognition.
- **Truth and Reconciliation Commission (1996)**: addressed past human rights violations; identified reasons for judicial inaction during apartheid:
  - Parliamentary sovereignty doctrine.

  - Legal positivism: separation of law and morality.
- **Le Roux and others v Dey**: Roman-Dutch law as a fair system; equitable principles present.
- 1993 and 1996 Constitutions established constitutional supremacy, replacing parliamentary sovereignty.

### 7.5 Conclusion
- South African law characterized as a "mixed" legal system; integration of African, Western, and universal components.
- Constitution and human rights culture fundamentally transformed South African law.

## Chapter 2: The Law of Property

### 2.1 Introduction
- **Law of Property**: Governs legal relationships regarding specific legal objects (things).
- **Roman Law Influence**: Basic principles remain relevant; ownership concepts differ in African customary law vs. Western law.
- **Constitutional Impact**: Sections 25 and 26 influence property law; identify gaps for economic transformation.

### 2.2 Scope and Function
- **Definition**: Regulates relationships between legal subjects and legal objects (things).
- **Function**: Harmonizes individual rights concerning specific things.

### 2.3 Definition and Classification of Things
- **Corporeal Objects**: Things that can be perceived by senses (e.g., land, clothing).
  
#### 2.3.1 Things in Commerce vs. Outside Commerce
- **In Commerce**: Can be owned and possessed.
- **Outside Commerce**: Beyond human control; includes:
  - Divine law subjects (religious objects).
  - Public property (air, roads).
  - State/community property.

#### 2.3.2 Single and Composite Things
- **Single Things**: Unit objects (e.g., a tomato).
- **Composite Things**: Composed of multiple components (e.g., a house).

#### 2.3.3 Consumable vs. Non-consumable Things
- **Consumables**: Used up (e.g., food).
- **Non-consumables**: Retain existence after use (e.g., tools).

#### 2.3.4 Movable vs. Immovable Things
- **Immovable**: Land and attachments (e.g., buildings).
- **Movable**: Not fixed in location (e.g., furniture).

#### 2.3.5 Fruits
- **Economic Benefits**: Derived from the use of a principal thing.
  - **Fructus naturales**: Natural products (e.g., crops).
  - **Fructus civiles**: Contractual benefits (e.g., rent).

#### 2.3.6 Res Mancipi vs. Res Nec Mancipi
- **Res Mancipi**: Valuable property requiring formal transfer.
- **Res Nec Mancipi**: Less formal transfer methods.

### 2.4 Slavery
- **Historical Context**: Slavery as property; significant in Roman society.
- **South African Context**: Dutch importation of slaves; gradual legal improvements; abolition in 1834.
- **Modern Implications**: Human trafficking addressed by the **Prevention and Combating of Trafficking in Persons Act**; **Section 13** of the Constitution protects against slavery.

### 2.5 Relationships Between Legal Subjects and Legal Objects
- **Real Rights vs. Personal Rights**:
  - **Real Rights**: Rights enforceable against any person regarding a thing.
  - **Personal Rights**: Rights enforceable against specific individuals.

## 2.6 POSSESSION (POSSESSIO)
- **Definition**: Legal term indicating physical and factual control over a corporeal thing.
- **Distinction from Ownership**: 
  - Possession does not equate to ownership; a non-owner can possess a thing (e.g., lessee).
  - Legal consequences differ between possession and ownership.
- **Protection of Possession**: 
  - Law protects possession via interdicts.
  - Important for limited real rights.
- **Complexity**: Law of possession is intricate; further study in PVL3701.

## 2.7 OWNERSHIP
### 2.7.1 What is Ownership?
- **Definition**: Most significant real right; fullest right over a corporeal thing.
- **Rights of Ownership**:
  - **ius utendi**: Right to use the thing.
  - **ius fruendi**: Right to the fruits produced.
  - **ius abutendi**: Right to alienate or destroy.
- **Constitutional Protection**: Section 25 protects ownership rights against arbitrary deprivation.

### 2.7.2 Restrictions on Ownership
- **Personal Rights**: Limitations arise from agreements (e.g., lease).
- **Legal Provisions**: Restrictions based on public interest (e.g., neighbour law).
- **Case Reference**: *Gien v Gien* - Ownership is not absolute; legal restrictions apply.

### 2.7.3 Indigenous Property Rights
- **Categories**:
  - **Family Property**: Controlled by the head of the family for collective benefit.
  - **Household Property**: Includes gifts and household items, managed for the household's advantage.
  - **Personal Property**: Individual use, minimal group value; rights may be limited by family structure.
- **Constitutional Relevance**: Indigenous rights protected under section 25.

### 2.7.4 Comparison of Ownership in Roman Law and Indigenous Law
- **Contextual Differences**: Indigenous ownership is community-oriented, contrasting with Roman individual ownership.
- **Key Questions**: Control and use of property depend on familial and marital relationships in indigenous law.

## 2.8 THE ACQUISITION OF OWNERSHIP: DERIVATIVE ACQUISITION
- **Modes of Acquisition**: Distinction between original and derivative acquisition.
- **Derivative Acquisition**: Ownership transferred with predecessor's cooperation (e.g., sale).
- **Original Acquisition**: Independent acquisition without predecessor's assistance.

### 2.8.1 The Nemo Plus Iuris Rule
- **Principle**: No one can transfer more rights than they possess.

### 2.8.2 Traditio ex Iusta Causa (Delivery on a Lawful Ground)
- **Definition**: Transfer of ownership through delivery; requires intention from both parties.
- **Legal Basis**: Ownership transfer necessitates both delivery and agreement (case reference: *Air-Kel (Edms) Bpk h/a Merkel Motors v Bodenstein*).

### 2.8.3 Simple Delivery (Traditio de Manu in Manum)
- **Definition**: Physical handing over of the item.

### 2.8.4 Delivery with the Long Hand (Traditio Longa Manu)
- **Definition**: Pointing out the item within sight for immediate control.

### 2.8.5 Delivery with the Short Hand (Traditio Brevi Manu)
- **Definition**: No physical handover needed if transferee already possesses the item.

### 2.8.6 Constitutum Possessorium
- **Definition**: Possession remains with the transferor under a lease agreement post-sale.

### 2.8.7 Symbolic Delivery (Traditio Symbolica)
- **Definition**: Transfer of possession through symbolic acts (e.g., documentation).

## 2.9 THE ACQUISITION OF OWNERSHIP: ORIGINAL ACQUISITION

- **Original Acquisition**: Ownership can be acquired independently from another person.
- **Forms of Original Acquisition**:
  - **Appropriation (occupatio)**: Taking possession of a res nullius (unowned thing) with the intention of ownership.
    - Types of res nullius:
      - Never owned (wild animals, found objects).
      - Abandoned (res derelictae).
      - Lost (res deperditae) not considered res nullius.
    - **Case Reference**: *Reck v Mills* - Ownership lost when abandoned; occupatio grants ownership to the new possessor.
  
  - **Acquisition of Fruits (acquisitio fructuum)**: Ownership of fruits acquired upon separation from the principal thing.
    - Civil fruits (e.g., rent) transfer ownership on delivery.
  
  - **Creation of a New Thing (speciﬁ catio)**: New thing created from existing materials.
    - Ownership depends on material ownership and ability to revert to original state.
    - **Justinian's View**: Maker of new thing from own and others' materials becomes owner unless revertible.
  
  - **Prescription (usucapio)**: Acquiring ownership through possession over time.
    - Governed by the **Prescription Act**.
    - Requirements:
      1. Property must be capable of ownership (res in commercium).
      2. Actual control of property during the required period.
      3. Valid iusta causa (e.g., contract).
      4. Good faith possession.
      5. Prescribed time period must be uninterrupted.

## 2.10 PROTECTION OF OWNERSHIP

- **Legal Remedies**: Available for ownership infringement, including actions, interdicts, and exceptions.
- **Rei Vindicatio**: Key remedy for reclaiming ownership.
  - Owner claims property from current possessor, regardless of good or bad faith.
  - Requires proof of ownership.

## 2.11 LIMITED REAL RIGHTS

- **Definition**: Rights over another's property (ius in re aliena).
- **Categories**:
  - Real rights of enjoyment (e.g., servitudes).
  - Real rights of security (e.g., hypothec).
- **Protection**: Limited real rights enforceable against third parties.

## 2.12 SERVITUDES

- **Definition**: Real right exercised over another's property.
- **Types**: Personal servitudes and praedial servitudes (focus on personal servitudes).
- **Characteristics**:
  - Specific to one person; cannot be inherited or transferred.
  - Ceases upon the holder's death.
  - Must be exercised reasonably, without causing inconvenience to the owner.
  - Does not require the owner to act but to tolerate or refrain from actions.
  - Indivisible; persists across subdivided properties.

### 2.12.3 Usufruct (ususfructus)

- **Definition**: Right to use and enjoy another's property without altering it.
- **Origin**: Ensures maintenance for surviving spouses while preserving inheritance for children.
- **Rights**: Usufructuary has the right to use (ius utendi) and take fruits from the property.

## 2.12 Usufruct and Servitudes

### 2.12.1 Usufruct
- **Definition**: Right to use and enjoy property (ius fruendi) while preserving its substance.
- **Bare Ownership**: Owner can alienate property (ius abutendi) but cannot use it.
- **Fruits**: 
  - Natural fruits (e.g., crops, livestock)
  - Civil fruits (e.g., rental income)
- **Maintenance**: Usufructuary must maintain property and not alter its nature (salva rerum substantia).
  - No conversion of property type (e.g., wine farm to cattle farm).
  - Must maintain condition and stock levels.
  - Must exercise reasonable care (diligens paterfamilias).

### 2.12.4 Protection of Servitudes
- **Actio Confessoria**: Enforces servitude holder's rights against interference.
- **Actio Negatoria**: Available to landowners against unlawful servitude claims.

## 2.13 Real Securities

### 2.13.1 Hypothec (Hypotheca)
- **Definition**: Security for debt using property, allowing creditor to sell property if debt is unpaid.
- **Types of Real Security**: 
  - **Fiducia**: Not discussed in detail.

  - **Hypothec**: 
    - Originated from leasing practices.
    - Tenant's movables and farm fruits serve as security for rent.
    - Praetor granted actio Serviana for landlords against tenants.
- **Advantages for Debtor**:
  - Broad range of property can be used as security.
  - Retention of ownership and possession.
  - Ability to offer property as security to multiple creditors.

### 3.2 OBLIGATIONS
- **Definition**: Legal bond between parties; creditor has a right to claim performance from debtor.
- **Personal vs. Real Rights**: Obligations involve personal rights to claim performance, unlike property rights.

#### 3.2.1 What is an obligation?
- Obligation creates a right and corresponding duty; creditor claims performance, debtor delivers.
- **Roman Definition**: Obligation binds another to perform for the creditor.

#### 3.2.2 Sources of obligations
- **Gaius**: Sources include contracts, delicts, and other causes.
- **Justinian's Four Sources**:
  - Contracts
  - Delicts
  - Quasi-contracts
  - Quasi-delicts

**Contracts**:
- Types:
  - Consensual contracts (agreement only)
  - Verbal contracts (agreement + formal words)
  - Real contracts (agreement + delivery)
  - Written contracts (agreement + writing)

**Delicts**:
- Roman law recognized theft, robbery, unlawful damage, and insults.
- Modern law classifies theft and robbery as crimes, not delicts.

### 3.3 GENERAL PRINCIPLES OF THE LAW OF CONTRACT
- Principles apply universally across Roman and modern South African contracts.
- Key questions:
  - How is a contract concluded?
  - What must be performed?
  - Who is liable?
  - When must performance occur?

## 3.3.1 Agreement (consensus)

  - **Fraud (dolus)**: Deceitful conduct leading to voidable contracts.
  - **Duress (metus)**: Intimidation affecting free will, resulting in voidable contracts.
  - **Mistake (error)**: Incorrect understanding affecting contract validity.

- **Contract Validity**:
  - **Void Contracts**: No legal effect from inception (void ab initio).
  - **Voidable Contracts**: Valid initially but can be declared void by the injured party.

- **Important Note**: Avoid using "invalid"; use "valid," "void," or "voidable."

### Fraud (dolus)
- Definition: Deceptive conduct intended to persuade.
- Result: Contract voidable; remedies available for the wronged party.

### Duress (metus)
- Definition: Coercion affecting decision-making.
- Result: Contract voidable at the victim's request.

### Mistake (error)
- Definition: Bona fide misunderstanding not due to fraud.
- Types of Mistakes:
  - **Error in negotio**: Mistake about the nature of the contract (void).
  - **Error in corpore**: Mistake about the object (void).
  - **Error in nomine**: Mistake in naming (valid).
  - **Error in persona**: Mistake about identity (voidable if material).
  - **Error in substantia**: Mistake about quality (potentially void).

## 3.3.2 Performance in Terms of a Contract
- **Performance**: Required actions or omissions under a contract.
- **Requirements for Valid Performance**:
  - Not conflict with morals/legal principles.
  - Determinable in monetary terms.
  - Due to the other party.
  - Physically and legally possible.

- **Impossibility of Performance**:
  - **Initial Impossibility**: Contract void if impossible at inception.
  - **Supervening Impossibility**: Becomes impossible post-contract; liability depends on cause (act of God vs. debtor's fault).

## 3.3.3 Contractual Liability
- **Contractual Liability**: Obligation to perform as per contract terms.
- **Malperformance**: Failure to perform adequately.
  - Can result from fraud or negligence.
  - Standard of care: **Bonus et diligens paterfamilias** (utmost care expected).

### 4.1 Introduction

### 4.2 Definition of a Delict
- Wrongful, culpable conduct causing damage to property, personality, or body.

### 4.3 Difference Between Delict and Crime
- A single act can constitute both a delict and a crime.

### 4.4 Elements of a Delict

   - Direct action or omission causing damage.
   - Indirect causation recognized.

2. **Wrongfulness**
   - Conduct must be against the law (e.g., murder, theft).

3. **Fault (Mens Rea)**
   - Blameworthy attitude; can be intent or negligence.
   - Intent: subjective frame of mind.
   - Negligence: objective test; three degrees in Roman law (culpa levis in abstracto, culpa levis in concreto, culpa lata).

- Understanding delictual elements is crucial for establishing liability.
- The evolution of delict law reflects societal changes and justice interests.

## Chapter 4: Obligations: The Law of Delict

### 4.4 Elements of a Delict

- **4.4.1 Fault**
  - Negligence defined by the standard of a diligent bonus paterfamilias.

- **4.4.4 Damage**
  - Must cause damage to another's property, body, or personality.
  - No claims for self-inflicted damage.
  - Types of damage:
    - Property (e.g., vehicle damage)
    - Bodily harm (e.g., shooting)
    - Personality infringement (e.g., defamation)

- **4.4.5 Causation**
  - Causal connection required between wrongdoer's conduct and victim's damage.
  - Example: Direct act leading to damage (e.g., throwing a bottle).

### 4.5 Damage to Property (Damnum Iniuria Datum)

- **4.5.1 Origins and Early Development**
  - Early Roman law: limited delicts in the Twelve Tables.
  - Lex Aquilia (287 BC) established a uniform delict for wrongful damage to property.
    - Chapter 1: Wrongful killing of slaves or livestock.
    - Chapter 3: Wrongful destruction of property.

- **4.5.2 Elements of a Delict under Lex Aquilia**
  - **Conduct**: Direct or indirect acts recognized as conduct.
  - **Unlawfulness**: Grounds of justification can exclude unlawfulness.
  - **Fault**: Intentional or negligent damage; standard of culpa levis in abstracto.
  - **Damage**: Claims for damage to body recognized; full damages include consequential damage and loss of profit.
  - **Causation**: Damage must result from the wrongdoer's action.

- **4.5.3 Actio Legis Aquiliae**
  - Action instituted against wrongdoer for delict of damnum iniuria datum.

### 4.6 Insulting Behaviour (Iniuria)

- **4.6.1 Development of the Delict**
  - Originated from physical attacks; evolved to include personality infringements.
  - Actio iniuriarum created for claims against insulting behavior.

- **4.6.2 Forms of Iniuria**
  - **Physical Attack (Corpus)**: Any unlawful physical attack or threat.
  - **Dignity Attack (Dignitas)**: Actions undermining self-worth (e.g., public humiliation).
  - **Reputation Damage (Fama)**: False rumors damaging a person's reputation.

- **4.6.3 Elements of Iniuria**
  - **Conduct**: Any act infringing physical integrity, dignity, or reputation.
  - **Wrongfulness**: Action must be unjustifiable; justifications exclude delict.

- **Actio iniuriarum**: Legal action for claims of iniuria.
