## PART 1: THEORETICAL AND STRUCTURAL OVERVIEW OF AFRICAN CUSTOMARY LAW

### Learning Unit 1: Historical Overview of Customary Law

- **Introduction**
  - Customary law evolved through pre-colonial, colonial, union, apartheid, and democratic eras, shaped by political contexts and state power.

#### Lecture 1: The Pre-Colonial Era (Before 1652)
- Customary law was supreme, founded on an indigenous framework and the philosophy of **ubuntu**.
- **Ubuntu Characteristics**:
  - Communal living, accountability, cooperation, and reciprocity.
- Key Institutions:
  - **Ukwakha ukulobola**, **isondlo**, **ukwethula**, **ukwenza**: Central to indigenous obligations, reflecting the human element.

#### Lecture 2: The Colonial Era (1652–1909)
- **Textbook Reference**: Pages 4–9
- Colonialism began with the Dutch settlers and led to the marginalization of indigenous legal systems.
- The **Khoisan** experienced significant loss of legal systems, leading to **obsolescence**.
- British occupation (1806) retained **Roman-Dutch law**, initially excluding indigenous law, promoting the principle of equality.
- **amaXhosa** retained some social structures post-colonialism but lost land sovereignty across South Africa.

#### Lecture 3: The Union Era (1910–1947)
- **Textbook Reference**: Pages 9–13
- **Status of Africans**: Established by the **Native Administration Act 1927**, granting the Governor-General immense powers over Africans.
- **Judicial Views**:
  - **Ngcobo J** in *Bhe v The Magistrate Khayelitsha* commented on forced removals.
  - **Schreiner JA** in *Ex parte: Minister of Native Affairs – In re Yako v Beyi* held that indigenous law was not favored, favoring common law instead.

#### Lecture 4: The Apartheid Era (1948–1990)
- **Textbook Reference**: Pages 13–17
- The apartheid period perpetuated colonial restrictions on customary law via the **repugnancy clause**.
- Key References:
  - **Black Authorities Act 1951**
  - *Tongoane v National Minister for Agriculture and Land Affairs* (2010)

#### Lecture 5: The Transitional Era (1990–1996)
- **Textbook Reference**: Pages 17–22
- Transition marked by **Constitution of 1993** sections on traditional leadership and customary law recognition.
- Emerged issues: patriarchy, racism, sexism, and inequality re-examined.

#### Lecture 6: The Democratic Era (1994 to Date)
- Recognition of customary and traditional institutions enshrined in the **Constitution of 1996**:
  - Chapter 12: Recognizes traditional institutions.
  - Chapter 1: Constitutional values.
  - Chapter 2: Fundamental rights.
  - Section 39(2): Equates customary law with common law under constitutional principles.

#### Lecture 7: Transformation of Customary Law
- **Indigenous Values**: Acknowledgment of traditional leaders, law, and culture.
- **Constitutional Values**: Customary law applies universally, adhering to equality, dignity, and freedom.

## Legislation and Cases Highlighted
- **Native Administration Act 1927**
- **Black Authorities Act 1951**
- *Bhe v The Magistrate Khayelitsha* (2005)
- *Yako v Beyi* (1948)
- *Tongoane v National Minister for Agriculture and Land Affairs* (2010)

## LCP4804/1 Study Notes

### 1. Introduction to Customary Law
- Traditional institutions (ukufakwa ukulobola, isondlo, ukwethula, ukwenzelela) enhance human condition.
- Colonial intervention (1652-1830) led to dispossession, sovereignty destruction, and legal invalidation of African values.

### 2. Relationship Between Customary Law and Common Law
- **Schreiner JA Interpretation** (Black Administration Act - BAA):
  - Customary law applied at discretion of Commissioners’ Courts for African customs, not opposed to public policy or natural justice.
  - BAA does not grant customary law its own status; common law prevails.
  
### 3. Impact of BAA on Poverty
- BAA functions as a tool for separate administration, institutionalizing black impoverishment.
- Established segregation and false geographic divisions, leading to displacement of black populations.

### 4. Transitional Period in South Africa (Post-1994)
- Participation of all races in governance marks significant shift in legal landscape.
- **Interim Constitution (27 April 1994)**: Customary law recognized as distinct, aligned with constitutional values.

### 5. Legislation Impacting Customary Law
- **Recognition of Customary Marriages Act, 1998**:
  - Definition of customary law as indigenous customs observed by African peoples.
  
- **Reform of Customary Law of Succession Act, 2009**:
  - Reinforces and regulates aspects of customary law.

### 6. Nature and Characteristics of Customary Law
- Customary law as distinct foundational legal system.
- Should not be viewed through the common law lens; consists of unique normative values.

### 7. Key Case Law
- **Alexkor v The Richtersveld Community & Others (2004)**:
  - Indigenous law defines rights to land; must not derive from common law.
  - Indigenous law seen as integral to South African law, valid under the Constitution.
  - Courts must apply customary law as per section 211(3) of the Constitution; consistent with Bill of Rights.

### 8. Attributes of Indigenous Law
- Not fixed; evolves with societal changes.
- Unwritten and orally transmitted; reflects community norms and values.
- Subject to Constitution and complementary legislation.

### 9. Conclusion
- Customary law has undergone significant transformation from colonial distortions to its current recognized status under democratic governance.
- Its integration into South African law underscores the need for continuous evolution within its cultural context.

## Lecture 3: The Difference Between Living Customary Law and Official Customary Law

- **Nature and Characteristics of Customary Law**
  - *Pilane v Pilane 2013 (4) BCLR 431 (CC)*:
    - Customary law is recognized and protected by the Constitution.
    - It is a **living body of law**, active and dynamic, capable of evolving.
    - Historical imposition led to the fossilization of customary law through codification.
    - The Constitution aims to preserve and evolve customary law, ensuring compliance with constitutional norms.

## Lecture 4: Transformation of Customary Law

- **Indigenous Values**
  - Living customary law reflects dynamic social practices.
  
- **Constitutional Values**
  - Established a relationship between living customary law and the Constitution.
  - Overcame objections regarding contradictions between customary law and constitutional provisions.

### Case Analysis
- **Recognition of Customary Marriages Act, Act 120 of 1998**
  - Definition highlights cultural specificity of customary law, asserting its connection to indigenous peoples' cultures.
  
- **Mayelane v Ngwenyama and Another 2013 (8) BCLR 918 (CC)**
  - Confirmed requirement of main wife's consent for subsequent marriages within Xitsonga custom.
  - Established nationwide applicability of consent due to constitutional equality and dignity principles.

- **Alexkor Ltd and Another v Richtersveld Community and Others 2003 (12) BCLR 1301 (CC)**
  - Affirmed equality of customary law and common law.
  - Jettisoned repugnancy clause; recognized customary law on its own normative values.
  - Critiqued previous assessment of customary law through Western lenses, emphasizing its own epistemology.

## 1. Case Law Overview

- **Focus**: Abolition of patriarchy, racism, and sexism in colonial and apartheid administrations.
  
- **Key Rules Targeted**:
  - Male primogeniture and unfair discrimination.
  
### **Mthembu Cases**
- Technical survival of rules due to:
  - Death before 27 April 1994; pre-constitutional law applied.
  - Validity of marriage not proved; no widow’s protection.
  - Lack of legitimacy of descendants prevented claims against deceased's father.
  - Court upheld male primogeniture for intestate succession.
  - **Legislation**:
    - Intestate Succession Act, 81 of 1987, §1(4) excluded black people from benefits.
    - BAA §23 regulated black estates; survival of rules.

- Outcome: Estate reverted to deceased’s father (senior male relative).

### **Bhe Case**
- Claim involved deceased’s father vs. two minor daughters.
- Court invalidated primogeniture rule, along with:
  - BAA §23.
  - Intestate Succession Act exclusions, enabling equality in customary law estates.

### **Nwamitwa/Shilubana Cases**
- Nwamitwa v Philia (2005) & Shilubana v Nwamitwa (2007): Primogeniture rule upheld as not unconstitutional.
- Shilubana v Nwamitwa (2008): Declared primogeniture unconstitutional; affirmed gender equality in leadership appointments.

## 2. Transformation of Customary Law

### **Indigenous Values**
- Recognition of lineage; Philia Shilubana's appointment as traditional leader restored rights.
- Amendment of Valoyi customary law permitted due to constitutional provisions.

### **Constitutional Values**
- Abolition of male primogeniture and legislative exclusions led to equality in distribution of estate rights.
- Affirmation of human dignity, equality, and freedom.

## 3. Relevant Cases and Commentary

### **Shilubana/Nwamitwa Case Overview**
- Philia Shilubana denied leadership in 1968; appointed by community in 2001.
- **Legal Questions**:
  - Allowed female appointment as traditional leader?
  - Authority to amend customary law?

### **Court Decisions**:
- Early rulings favored traditional past practices (High Court & SCA).
- **Constitutional Court Decision**: Philia Shilubana’s appointment as valid; customary law recognized as living and evolving.
- Emphasized contemporary practices over historical precedent.

### **Commentary on Customary Law Values**
- Court supported community law development but conflicted with lineage considerations for succession.
- Should have ensured future appointments honored lineage continuity.

## 4. Succession vs. Inheritance in Customary Law

### **Critical Distinctions**
- **Succession**: Rights to inherit according to customary practices.
- **Inheritance**: Actual distribution of property upon death.
  
### **Living Law Development**
- Customary law retains capacity for evolution post-Bhe case and RCLSRRMA.
- Family/community rights to equitable distribution upheld.

## Conclusion
- Explored differences between inheritance and succession in customary law.
- Emphasis on decolonization aligning with constitutional ideals of dignity, equality, and freedom.

## Learning unit 9
### Customary law of obligations (contracts)

**Study**  
Study chapter 10 of the textbook.

### Learning outcomes
- Appreciate the communitarian nature of the customary law of contracts and its philosophical foundations.
- Describe the community's role in creating binding commitments in traditional societies.
- Conceptualize individual participation as representatives of families or communities.

### Introduction
- Exploration of indigenous African obligations (contracts) and unique values regulating binding agreements.
- Individuals undertake customary commitments as family heads, with binding obligations underwritten by family heads.
- The traditional economy relies heavily on cattle as currency.
- Indigenous contracts illustrate communal contexts: *ukulobola*, *ukwenzelela*, *ukwethula*, *ukufakwa*, *isondlo*, and *inqoma* reveal the collective nature of agreements.

### Lecture 1: Capacity to enter into binding customary law commitments
- Understand the Constitution and legislation's role in enabling individuals to act in customary law (equality rights & age of majority).

### Lecture 2: Customary law contracts: 
*ukwenzelela, ukwethula, isondlo/dikotlo, inqoma/mafi sa/sisa*
- Distinguish between contracts reflecting communal living; oral tradition emphasizes community presence during transactions.
- Contracts embody *ubuntu* principles.

### Lecture 3: Ukufakwa as a specific customary law contract
- *Ubuntu*: Fundamental measure for human conduct; promotes communal living, solidarity, responsibility, generosity, co-operation, and reciprocity.
- *Ukufakwa* allows male relatives to assume paternal responsibilities, entitled to lobolo goods and liable for the daughter’s delicts.
- Rights to lobolo portion arise immediately upon identification of goods; no claims against the father allowed if goods are not delivered.

### Lecture 4: The attributes of ubuntu as found in ukufakwa
- **Communal Living**: Collective family unit; shared wealth and burdens.
- **Shared Sense of Belonging**: Interdependence among community members.
- **Group Solidarity**: Collective strength; shared shame and disgrace.
- **Reciprocity**: Mutual assistance and benefits; good deeds are investments.
- **Collective Ownership of Assets**: Family driving shared goals; individual contributions enhance collective wealth.
- Attributes for a good life include generosity, respect, responsibility, and accountability.

### Lecture 5: Transformation of customary law
- **Indigenous values**: Contracts reflect *ubuntu*, focusing on individual well-being.
- **Constitutional values**: *Ubuntu* harmonizes with constitutional principles (human dignity, equality, freedom), validated in *Mabena* case and highlighted in *Makwanyane* and *Dikoko* cases for transformative impact.

### Conclusion
- The learning unit emphasizes the centrality of *umuntu/motho* in forming binding commitments, demonstrating *ubuntu* in practice.
