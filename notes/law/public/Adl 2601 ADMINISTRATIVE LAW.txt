- **Common Law**  
  - Unwritten laws not codified in legislation.  

- **Constitution**  
  - Both a broad body of rules governing state authority and a specific written document (South Africa's Constitution, 1996).  

- **Constitutionalism**  
  - Governance limited by the Constitution; supremacy of law and authority.  

- **Constitutional State**  
  - A state where constitutionalism prevails.  

- **Delegated (Subordinate) Legislation**

- **Duty**

- **Executive (Authority)**  
  - Political officials and their functions.  

- **Fons et Origo**  
  - Latin term for "source and origin."  

- **Function**  
  - Task performed by an authority or administrator.  

- **Government**  
  - Ambiguous term encompassing legislative, executive, and judicial functions; primarily refers to executive actions.  

- **Inter Se**  
  - Latin term for "between themselves."  

- **Legal Subject**  
  - Entities capable of rights and duties within the legal framework.  

- **Judicial Precedent (Stare Decisis)**  
  - Binding nature of higher court decisions on lower courts.  

- **Judicial Authority**  
  - Definition includes all courts in the Republic (s 165(1) of the Constitution).  

- **Judicial Review**  
  - Courts examining excess of power and procedure in administrative action.  

- **Just Administrative Action**  
  - Lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair actions (s 33 of the Constitution).  

- **Law**  
  - Encompasses Constitution, statutes, and common/customary law.  

- **Legality**  
  - Lawfulness of government action under legal principles.  

- **Legislature**  
  - Elected body responsible for law-making (e.g., Parliament).  

- **Limitation Clause**

### Characteristics of Administrative Law

- **Legal Relationships:**
  - Govern relationships between legal subjects, who hold rights and duties (e.g., John Learner vs. school principal; Theodor Refugee vs. reception officer; Thami Educator vs. Department of Education; Maria vs. municipality).

- **Power Dynamics:**
  - Entities exercise authority over subordinates, creating vertical relationships:
    - Principal, refugee reception officer, Department of Education, municipality (exercise public power).
  - Subordinate parties are compelled to act under this authority (e.g., inability to attend school, stay in South Africa, perform educational duties, submit tenders).

- **Definition of Administrative Action:**
  - Decisions made by those with state authority (e.g., expulsion of John, withdrawal of permit for Theodor, suspension of Thami, awarding of tenders).

---

### Administrative Action vs. Executive Action

- **Confusion Among Students:**
  - **Administrative Action:** Performed by organs of state as defined in **s 239 of the Constitution**.
  - **Executive Action:** Excluded from administrative action as defined in **s 1 of the PAJA**; refers to policy-formulating decisions made by ministers (e.g., housing policy).
  - **Implementation of Legislation:** When acting per legislation, ministers act administratively (e.g., execution of the Refugees Act).

---

### Authority and Legal Compliance

- **Authorization to Act:**
  - Action must be permissible as derived from law (e.g., Constitution, specific legislation).

- **Compliance with Legal Requirements:**
  - Actions must adhere to the rights of individuals, ensuring they are lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair:
    - **Lawful:** Must comply with relevant legal sources.
    - **Reasonable:** Decisions must be sound and based on objective facts.
    - **Procedural Fairness:** Subordinate parties must be allowed to present their case.
    - **Right to Written Reasons:** Enshrined in **s 33(2)** of the Constitution; necessary when a decision adversely affects rights.

---

### Just Administrative Action Requirements

1. **Lawfulness:**
   - Comply with constitutional and legal mandates.

2. **Reasonableness:**
   - Decisions must have a logical basis and be reasonable to an outsider.

3. **Procedural Fairness:**
   - Fair procedures must be followed, ensuring impartiality and opportunity for representation.

4. **Written Reasons:**
   - Required for decisions impacting rights negatively.

---

### Control and Correction of Administrative Action

- **Correcting Non-compliant Actions:**
  - Initial internal mechanisms (appeals within the department) should be pursued before judicial intervention.
  - Courts serve as a last resort.

- **Potential Remedies:**
  - Aggrieved parties may seek judicial review or reversal of the administrative decision.

---

### Defined Framework of Administrative Law

- **Definition:**
  - Regulates activities of organs of state and entities with public powers/functions, ensuring procedures and actions comply with the law.

---

### Situations Involving Administrative Law (Examples)
- Planning of townships, provision of public utilities (water/electricity), public health inspector duties, transportation regulations, housing allocation, governmental functions related to public interest.

  - (1) Authority figures and private individuals (e.g., actions affecting rights like education).

### 2.2 The distinction between a general and an individual administrative-law relationship

- **General or Objective Relationship**:
  - Legal rules apply to all individuals within a defined group (e.g., refugees).

  - Cannot be altered by individual decisions of officials.

  - Rules apply specifically to identifiable parties.
  - Created through individual administrative decisions, less affected by new general legislation unless explicitly stated (presumption against retrospectivity).

## LEARNING UNIT 3: The legal subjects of the administrative-law relationship

### 3.1 Definition of “Organ of State”
- **Section 239(b)** broadens the definition of “organ of state”:
  - Includes functionaries or institutions exercising powers or functions under the Constitution or any legislation.
- Difficulty in distinguishing whether an entity acts in public vs. private capacity.
  - **Chirwa v Transnet Ltd**: Langa CJ notes the complex nature of defining "public" powers (2008).
- Essential considerations:
  - (a) Determining if functions are public.
  - (b) Assessing if these functions are in accordance with legislation.
- Focus on the nature of the function, not the individual performing it.
  - Reference: **President of the Republic of South Africa v SARFU** (2000).

### 3.2 Distinction between Private and Public Law
- Traditional private/public law distinctions increasingly blurred.
- Example: Legislation regulating private-law relationships (e.g., leases).
- **Legislation Examples**: 
  - Employment of Educators Act 1998.
  - Prevention of Family Violence Act 1993.
  - Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 2000.

### 3.3 Privatization of Public Functions
- State privatizes functions traditionally public (e.g., transport, telecommunications).
- Entities like **Transnet**, **Telkom**, and **Eskom** exercise public powers under legislation despite not being public administration.

### 3.4 Identifying Organs of State
- Analyze if entities qualify as “organs of state” based on statutory frameworks.
- Examples:
  - **National Soccer League**: Lacks legislative foundation, thus not an organ of state.
  - **SABC Board**: Qualifies as it is established under the Broadcasting Act.
  - **Unisa**: Acts under the Higher Education Act.
  - **Private Schools**: Generally not organs of state, but subject to government legislation.
  - **High Court**: Not considered an organ of state as per Section 239.

### 3.5 Associations and Clubs
- **Voluntary Associations** (e.g., jockey clubs):
  - Not statutory bodies and generally not organs of state.
  - Disciplinary powers derived from internal constitutions.
- **Common-Law Application**:

- **Constitutional Provision**: Parliament must enact legislation for just administrative action.

- **Importance**: Determining if administrative action exists is crucial for the application of the right to just administrative action (s 33, Constitution).  
- **Threshold Requirement**: Recognition of action as administrative is needed for legal application of rights.  
- **Exclusions**: Many actions may resemble administrative action but do not qualify under PAJA.

### 5.3 Classes of Administrative Action  
#### 5.3.1 Three Classes of Conduct  
- **Legislative Administrative Acts**:  
  - Recognizable form, published in official documents (e.g., Government Gazette).  
  - Creates, varies, or ends general relationships; cannot be regulated by decision alone.  
  - Governed by specific rules; delegation of legislative power requires express statutory authority.  
  - Must comply with original Act's framework, avoiding conflict or vagueness.

- **Judicial Administrative Acts**:  
  - Characterized as **quasi-judicial**, resembling court actions.  
  - Conducted by administrative tribunals, e.g., **Films and Publications Appeal Board**.  
  - Not part of judiciary hierarchy; subject to judicial control via appeal/review.

- **Administrative Acts**:  
  - Refers to day-to-day government activities, e.g., licensing, promotions, arrests.  
  - Types include police acts (urgent actions) and discretionary administrative acts (choice in action context).

### 5.4 Discretionary Administrative Acts  
- **Definition**: Discretion involves the choice of action in particular situations.  
- **Types of Discretion**:  
  - **Wide Discretion**: Broad choice, e.g., "in his or her opinion" language.  
  - **Narrow Discretion**: Conditions specified in enabling provision guide discretion.  
- **Legal Boundaries**: Choices must remain lawful, governed by legal frameworks (e.g., referee analogy).

### 5.5 Separation of Powers and Administrative Action  
- **Principle**: Separation of powers divides state authority into legislative, executive, and judicial branches, preventing concentration of power.  
- **Administrative Law Principle of Deference**: Courts respect administrators’ decisions while maintaining the obligation to review actions based on legal standards.  
- **Judicial Control**: Cases like **Department of Transport v Tasima** highlight varying views on the extent of judicial control in administrative actions.  

### 5.6 Conclusion  
- **Significance of Classes**: The classification of administrative action reflects distinct governmental functions, rooted in the separation of powers.

### 5.4 THE LEGAL FORCE OF ADMINISTRATIVE ACTION  
- **Definition**: The legal effect of administrative action in law; aspects include when it takes effect and when it is terminated.

#### 5.4.1 When does administrative action take effect?  
- **Legislative Administrative Acts**: Effective upon promulgation or commencement date.
- **Judicial Administrative Acts**: Effective at the ruling or judgment unless a statute specifies an appeal period.
- **Administrative Acts**: Effective upon notification by publication or individual announcement.  
  - Importance: Determines compliance with deadlines for appeals, objections, etc. (Baxter 1984:367).

#### 5.4.2 Termination of the legal force of administrative action  
- **Causes for Termination**: Repeal, revocation, amendment, lapse of time, withdrawal, or court order.
- **Functus Officio**: The organ of state is “having completed the task” and cannot alter the decision once the matter is finalized.

##### Legislative Administrative Acts  
- Can be repealed or amended at any time, but does not apply retrospectively once rights are acquired.

##### Judicial Administrative Acts  
- Functus officio upon ruling; force of **res iudicata**.

##### Administrative Acts  
- **Invalid Administrative Action**: Action can be rectified if challenged before court; invalidity confirmed in **Merafong City Local Municipality v AngloGold Ashanti Ltd 2017 (2) SA 211 (CC)**.
- **Valid Administrative Action**:
  - **Onerous/Burdensome**: Can be altered at any time.
  - **Beneficial**: Can only be altered if expressly authorized.
  - **Status-Affecting**: Authority is functus officio once the decision is made (e.g., adoption orders).

### LEARNING UNIT 8: The constitutional right to reasonable administrative action  
#### 8.2 THE PRESENT POSITION IN TERMS OF THE 1996 CONSTITUTION AND THE PROVISIONS OF PAJA  

##### 8.2.1 Section 33(1) of the 1996 Constitution  
- Right to reasonable administrative action is constitutionally guaranteed.

##### 8.2.2 PAJA and the right to reasonable administrative action  
- **PAJA** allows individuals to seek judicial review of administrative actions under **Section 6(1)**.  
- Grounds for review must show that an action is so unreasonable that no reasonable person could have taken it (**Section 6(2)(h)**).  
- Fails to address consequences or effects of actions; focuses solely on the reasonableness of the decision itself.  
- **Wednesbury Test** established in **Associated Provincial Picture Houses Ltd v Wednesbury Corporation** [1948] 1 KB 223: Courts can intervene if a decision is so unreasonable that no reasonable authority could reach it.  
- Section 6(2)(h) suggests a reversion to gross unreasonableness, meaning only extreme instances of unreasonableness are reviewable.

##### 8.2.3 The Constitutional Court’s interpretation of the right to reasonable administrative action  
- Case: **Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs** 2004 4 SA 490; 2004 7 BCLR 687 (CC).  
- Court emphasized the importance of reasonableness aligned with **Section 33** of the Constitution.  
- O’Regan J held that administrative actions are reviewable if they are decisions a reasonable decision-maker could not reach.  
- Determining reasonableness involves:
  - Nature of the decision  
  - Identity and expertise of the decision-maker  
  - Relevant factors for the decision  
  - Reasons given for the decision  
  - Nature of competing interests  
  - Impact on affected individuals  

- Distinction between reviews and appeals must be preserved; respect must be shown towards decision-makers within the reasonableness test. Courts cannot simply rubber-stamp decisions due to complexity or decision-maker's identity.

#### 8.3 JUSTIFIABLE ADMINISTRATIVE ACTION IN TERMS OF SECTION 24(D) OF THE INTERIM CONSTITUTION  

##### 8.3.1 Section 24  
- **Section 24(d)** requires justifiable administrative action relating to reasons given when an individual's rights are affected.  
- Absence of “unreasonableness” terminology; emphasizes “justifiable” and its relation to reasons.  
- Justifiable decisions must be rational, coherent, and supported by reason.  
- Rationality requires a balance between affected rights and reasons for decisions.

##### 8.3.2 The courts’ approach to justifiability in section 24(d)  
- Post-1994 case: **Standard Bank of Bophuthatswana Limited v Reynolds** 1995 3 BCLR 305; 1995 3 SA 74 (B) advocated for less stringent tests for unreasonableness.  
- Case: **Kotzé v Minister of Health** 1996 3 BCLR 417 (T) analyzed justifiability, determining that reasons must be factually and legally sound.  
- Case: **Roman v Williams** 1997 9 BCLR 1267 (C); 1998 1 SA 270 (C) highlighted that decisions must be justifiable based on suitability, necessity, and proportionality, thus broadening the judicial review scope.  
- The essence of justifiability is objective substantiation; the decision must align with the requirements of suitability, necessity, and proportionality.

### Public hearing/enquiry
- No absolute right to a public hearing; confidentiality may be necessary in certain instances (e.g., disciplinary matters).
- Balance required between interests of openness and confidentiality.
- Generally, administrative hearings must be open to the public.

### The party must be informed of considerations which count against him or her
- All facts detrimental to a person must be communicated to allow for defense (Loxton v Kenhardt Liquor Licensing Board 1942 AD 275).
- In **Down v Malan 1960 2 SA 734 (A)**, if a party could foresee prejudicial facts, failure to act on that knowledge is attributable to their own negligence.

### Reasons must be given by the administrator for any decisions taken
- Administrators obliged to provide reasons for decisions, though inconsistently applied historically.
- Courts often did not mandate reasons unless statutes allowed recourse (e.g., appeal).
- Failure to provide reasons can lead to adverse inferences (Sigaba v Minister of Defence and Police 1980 3 SA 535 (TkSC); WC Greyling & Erasmus v Johannesburg Local Transportation Board 1982 4 SA 427 (A)).

### The nemo iudex in sua causa rule (“no one should be a judge in his or her own case”)
- Decision-makers must be perceived as impartial (Yates v University of Bophuthatswana 1994 3 SA 815 (BG)).
- Common forms of bias:
  - **Pecuniary interest** (e.g., **Rose v Johannesburg Local Road Transportation Board 1947 4 SA 272 (W)**).
  - **Personal interest** (e.g., **Liebenberg v Brakpan Liquor Licensing Board 1944 WLD 52**).

### At what stage of the decision-making process should procedural fairness be applied?
- Procedural fairness should be observed **before** a decision is taken.

  6. Administrative body cannot rectify the irregularity.  
  7. Internal remedy does not provide adequate protection (e.g., **Msomi v Abrahams 1981**).

## LEARNING UNIT 12: Judicial control of administrative action and remedies in proceedings for judicial review

### 12.1 PRECONDITIONS BEFORE TURNING TO JUDICIAL CONTROL
- **Introduction**
  - Preconditions must be met before judicial challenge.
  - Applications must be within a reasonable period.
  - Appeal only against final administrative decisions.
  - Important precondition: **locus standi** (standing).

- **12.1.1 The applicant must have locus standi (standing)**
  - **Locus Standi Definition**: Legal capacity to bring a matter to court; must have a recognizable interest in the outcome.
  - Pre-1994 Rule: Sufficient personal and direct interest required.
  - Pertinent Case: **Dawnlawn Beleggings (Edms) Bpk v Johannesburg Stock Exchange** 1983 3 SA 344 (W); economic gain sufficient for standing.
  - **Actio Popularis**: Concept allowing public interest actions; any individual can challenge administrative actions.

- **12.1.2 Locus Standi per Section 38 of the 1996 Constitution**
  - **Broadened Scope**: More individuals/groups can seek relief for fundamental rights infringements, includes right to just administrative action.
  - **Section 38 Provisions**:
    - Anyone acting in personal interest.
    - Anyone acting on behalf of another who cannot act.
    - Group member acting in group interest.
    - Public interest actions.
    - Associations acting in interest of members.

### 12.2 THE VARIOUS FORMS OF JUDICIAL CONTROL
- Various judicial avenues for challenging administrative action include:
  - **Statutory appeal**
  - **Judicial review**
  - **Interdict**
  - **Mandamus**
  - **Declaratory order**
  - **Defence in criminal proceedings**

- **12.2.1 Statutory Appeal**
  - Courts' appeal jurisdiction is statutory, not inherent.
  - Appeals allowed only for final decisions/orders.
  - Empowering statute provides appeal provisions.
  - Important Note: Appeals review cases restricted to records, examining merits.

- **12.2.2 Judicial Review**
  - Courts possess inherent review jurisdiction via common law.
  - Subject to review: legality of administrative actions.
  - **Grounds for Judicial Review**:
    - Infringement of Bill of Rights.
    - Non-compliance with validity requirements per **s 6 of PAJA**.
  - Key Focus: Review the legality/validity of decisions, not merits.

- **12.2.3 Interdict**
  - Purpose: Prevent unlawful administrative action.
  - Types: Interim or final interdict.
  - Requirements for granting:
    - Clear legal interest threatened.
    - No other satisfactory remedy.
    - Urgency to prevent irreparable harm.
  - Example: **Die Vereniging van Advokate v Moskeeplein (Edms) Bpk** 1982 3 SA 159 (T).

- **12.2.4 Mandamus**
  - Compels performance of a statutory duty; cannot dictate how power is exercised.
  - Examples:
    - Thuli's liquor license application delay.
    - Asylum application delays, as per **s 6(2)(g) PAJA**.
  - Important: Distinction between mandamus and mandate.

- **12.2.5 Declaratory Order**
  - Used for legal disputes/uncertainty regarding administrative actions.
  - Provides authoritative answers on legality.
  - Example case: **Coin Operated Systems (Pty) Ltd v Johannesburg City Council** 1973 3 SA 856 (W). 

### Conclusion
- Judicial control mechanisms allow individuals like Theodor to seek redress through established legal pathways once administrative channels are exhausted. Understanding locus standi and various forms of judicial remedies is crucial in navigating administrative law effectively.

### 12.2.6 Defence in criminal proceedings
- Validity of administrative action can be challenged as a defence in criminal law.
- Common law allows challenge against criminal offences arising from administrative decisions.
- **Case Example**: *R v Abdurahman* 1950 3 SA 136 (A) - conviction set aside due to unreasonable regulations.
- **Case Reference**: *Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Limited v City of Cape Town* 2004 6 SA 222 (SCA) - allows defensive challenges against unlawful administrative actions.
- Defence only after exhausting domestic remedies (as per *S v Brand* 1973 2 SA 469 (RA)).

### 12.3 Judicial review: grounds for review in terms of Section 6 of PAJA
#### 12.3.1 Introductory remarks
- Judicial review is a form of control as per Section 6 of PAJA.
- **Section 6(1)**: Any person whose rights are directly affected can institute review proceedings.
- **Section 6(2)**: Grounds for objections to administrative actions outlined.

##### Categories of grounds of review:
1. Decision-maker authorization (s 6(2)(a)).
2. Compliance with decision-making procedures (s 6(2)(b)-(e)).
3. Impact and rationality of administrative action (s 6(2)(f)-(i)).

#### 12.3.2 The Decision-Maker (Administrator) (s 6(2)(a)(i)–(iii))
- Grounds related to the administrator:
  - **Ultra vires**: Lack of authority or acting beyond powers.
  - **Unauthorised delegation**: Power delegated without authority.
  - **Nemo iudex in sua causa**: Rule against bias by the administrator.

#### 12.3.3 Manner of Decision Taken (s 6(2)(b)–(e))
- Grounds include:
  - **Formal requirements non-compliance** (s 6(2)(b)).
    - Example: Procedural rules in the Refugees Act.
  - **Procedural unfairness** (s 6(2)(c)).
  - **Materially influenced by an error of law** (s 6(2)(d)).
  - **Not taken under proper considerations** (s 6(2)(e)):
    - Unauthorised purposes, irrelevant considerations, arbitrary action.

#### 12.3.4 The Administrative Action Itself (s 6(2)(f)–(i))
- Grounds for review when:
  - Action contravenes law or is unauthorised (s 6(2)(f)).
  - **Rationality**: Must connect with purpose and information before the administrator.
  - **Failure to take a decision** (s 6(2)(g)).
    - Administrative misleading about delays.
    - **Case Example**: *Nazo v Free State Gambling & Liquor Authority* [2015] ZAFSHC 227.
  - **Unreasonable action** (s 6(2)(h)).
  - **Unconstitutional or unlawful** actions (s 6(2)(i)) - catch-all provision.

### 12.4 Procedure for Judicial Review in terms of PAJA
#### 12.4.1 Which court may review administrative action?
- Courts empowered to review:
  - Constitutional Court (s 167(6)(a)).
  - High Courts and designated magistrates' courts.
- Important amendment: Designated magistrates’ courts may now review administrative actions.

#### 12.4.2 Procedure for Review
- Review must be instituted within 180 days as per **Section 7(1)** of PAJA.
  - From the date of internal remedies conclusion or awareness of action/reasons.
- **Case Example**: *City of Cape Town v Aurecon South Africa (Pty) Ltd* 2017 (4) SA 223 (CC) - 180-day rule clarified.
- Extensions of time may be granted (s 9(1) and (2)) in the interests of justice.

### Additional Notes
- Adherence to procedural rules important to uphold the right to review.
- Legislative intentions aim to balance judicial efficiency with resolving disputes non-judicially.

## Chapter: Earthlife Africa (Cape Town) v Director General: Department of Environmental Affairs

### Page 215-220

#### Legal Principles and Framework

- **ECA Regulations**: No further comments on the final Environmental Impact Report (EIR) are permitted before decisions by competent authorities. 
- **Public Participation**: EIA Regulations provide for full participation in all processes, not limited to the investigation phase. Public participation must also occur in the adjudicative phase (DG’s evaluation).

#### Key Arguments and Judicial Reasoning

- **Respondents’ Position**: Claims that public participation is only essential until the EIR submission; appeals allowed post-decision. 
- **Judicial Rejection**: Public participation continues throughout the evaluation process. Procedural fairness, particularly the **audi rule**, mandates consultation on all relevant new material.

#### Significant Case References

- **Attorney-General, Eastern Cape v Blom** (1988): Established that procedural fairness extends to all stages of decision-making.
- **Huisman v Minister of Local Government**: Dictates fairness in considering new evidence that may affect decisions.
- **De Ville**: Emphasizes requirements for hearings before final decisions, particularly when new evidence arises.

#### Procedural Fairness

- Opportunity for comments on ‘substantially different’ final EIR is deemed necessary for procedural fairness.
- DG's refusal to permit public comment based on a misconception undermines the fairness of the process and invalidates the decision.

#### Assessing the DG's Decision-Making Process

- DG maintained that public comments were not permitted post-EIR submission; this position was characterized as opportunistic.
- Despite being in possession of the final EIR six months prior, the opportunity to comment was rendered meaningless due to the DG's attitude.

#### Conclusions Drawn

- **Legitimacy of a Hearing**: An interested party's right to a hearing implies direct communication with the decision-maker, not just intermediaries (e.g., consultants affiliated with one party). 
- **DG’s Responsibility**: Even with expert input, a decision-maker must consider submissions from all sides adequately; failure to do so renders the decision procedurally unfair.

#### Final Ruling

- The DG’s decision to issue authorization based on an EIR that materially differed from what had previously been submitted is flawed and falls to be set aside. 
- An opportunity for further public submissions prior to a new decision is mandated, consistent with PAJA, ensuring adherence to procedural fairness.

#### Court Order

- **Review and Set Aside**: The decision authorizing Eskom’s pebble bed modular reactor is annulled.
- **Remittance for Reconsideration**: The DG must consider new submissions before making a fresh determination.
- **Costs**: The respondents are ordered to bear the applicant’s legal costs.

### Important Legislation

- **ECA**: Environment Conservation Act 73 of 1989.
- **PAJA**: Promotion of Administrative Justice Act, mandates procedural fairness in administrative actions.

### 15 The Refugees Act, 130 of 1998
- **Date of Assent**: 20 November 1998

#### Purpose
- To implement international standards related to refugees in South Africa.
- To regulate the entry, applications, rights, and obligations of refugees.

#### Preamble
- South Africa's commitment to international refugee conventions:
  - **1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees**
  - **1967 Protocol Relating to the Status of Refugees**
  - **1969 OAU Convention Governing Refugee Problems in Africa**

### Arrangement of Act

#### Chapter 1: Interpretation, Application and Administration of Act
1. **Definitions**:
   - **Abusive Application for Asylum**: Application aimed at circumventing legal proceedings.
   - **Aliens Control Act (1991)**: Act concerning the control of aliens (Act No. 96 of 1991).
   - **Appeal Board**: Refugee Appeal Board (s. 12).
   - **Asylum**: Recognized refugee status.
   - **Asylum Seeker**: Individual seeking refugee recognition.
   - **Asylum Seeker Permit**: Permit under s. 22.
   - **Child**: Individual under 18.
   - **Department**: Department of Home Affairs.
   - **Dependants**: Unmarried children, destitute family members, etc.
   - **Director-General**: Head of the Department.
   - **Fraudulent Application for Asylum**: Based on known falsehoods.
   - **Manifestly Unfounded Application**: Not based on valid grounds.
   - **Minister**: Minister of Home Affairs.
   - **Regulations**: Prescribed by regulation.
   - **Refugee**: Individual granted asylum.
   - **Refugee Reception Office**: Offices established under s. 8(1).
   - **Refugee Reception Officer**: As per s. 8(2).
   - **Refugee Status Determination Officer**: As per s. 8(2).
   - **Rules**: Regulations made by Appeal Board.
   - **Social Group**: A group defined by gender, orientation, disability, etc.
   - **Standing Committee**: Committee for Refugee Affairs (s. 9).
   - **UNHCR**: United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees.

2. **General Prohibition of Refusal of Entry**:
   - No entry refusal if return leads to persecution or threats to life/freedom.

3. **Refugee Status**:
   - Eligibility requires:
     - Well-founded fear of persecution.
     - External aggression or disorder in country of origin.
     - Dependants of qualifying individuals.

4. **Exclusion from Refugee Status**:
   - Excluded if suspected of serious crimes or violating international principles.

5. **Cessation of Refugee Status**:
   - Ceases when:
     - Individual avails themselves of their country's protection.
     - Achieves citizenship in another country.
     - Returns to original country without fear.

6. **Interpretation, Application, and Administration of Act**:
   - Must align with international agreements, including:
     - **UN Convention and Protocol**.
     - **OAU Convention**.
     - **Universal Declaration of Human Rights**.
   - Minister responsible for administration.

### ANNEXURE C: The Refugees Act, 130 of 1998 

## CHAPTER 4: REVIEWS AND APPEALS 

### Review by Standing Committee 
- **Section 25**:
  - Standing Committee reviews decisions by Refugee Status Determination Officers (RSDOs).
  - May invite UNHCR for input, request relevant person’s attendance, conduct inquiries, and summon the applicant for additional information.
  - Decisions:
    - Confirm or set aside decisions.
    - Decide on questions of law per section 24(3)(d).
  - Must inform RSDO of decision promptly.
  - Directives to be given back to RSDO for application reconsideration.

### Appeals to Appeal Board 
- **Section 26**:
  - Appeals lodged by any asylum seeker against RSDO rejections.
  - Appeal Board may confirm, set aside or substitute RSDO decisions.
  - May invite UNHCR input, refer matters back, pursue inquiries, and request applicant appearances.
  - Legal representation allowed upon request.

## CHAPTER 5: RIGHTS AND OBLIGATIONS OF REFUGEES 

### Protection and General Rights of Refugees 
- **Section 27**:
  - Refugee entitlements:
    - Written recognition of status.
    - Full legal protection (Chapter 2 of the Constitution).
    - Immigration permit application after 5 years (Aliens Control Act, 1991) if certified as indefinite refugee.
    - Identity document and South African travel document.
    - Right to employment and access to basic health and education services.

### Rights of Refugees in Respect of Removal from Republic 
- **Section 28**:
  - Removal permissible for national security/public order.
  - Must follow constitutional rights and international law.
  - Dependants may be removed if asylum attempts are failed or rejected.
  - Detainment pending removal is allowed.
  - Order must allow time for approval from another country.

### Restriction of Detention 
- **Section 29**:
  - Detention must be reasonable; exceeds 30 days necessitates review by a High Court judge.
  - Child detention is a last resort and for minimal time.

### Identity Document to Refugee 
- **Section 30**:
  - Identity document must contain key personal details including identity number, name, gender, DOB, country of origin, photo, fingerprints.

### Travel Document to Refugee 
- **Section 31**:
  - Refugee may apply for a travel document.

### Unaccompanied Child and Mentally Disabled Person 
- **Section 32**:
  - Child needing care must go to Children’s Court for asylum assistance.
  - Mentally disabled persons must receive help in asylum applications.

### Dependants of Refugee 
- **Section 33**:
  - Refugee must assist dependants in applying for asylum.
  - Dependants can remain post-termination of dependence conditions.
  - Survivors of refugees can remain in the Republic under certain conditions.
  - Dependants can apply for refugee status.

### Obligations of Refugees 
- **Section 34**:
  - Refugees must abide by the laws of the Republic. 

## CHAPTER 6: SUPPLEMENTARY AND GENERAL PROVISIONS 

### Reception and Accommodation of Asylum Seekers in Event of Mass Influx 
- **Section 35**:
  - Minister may declare groups as refugees.
  - Designation of reception areas for large groups.
  - Appointment of area managers and withdrawal of designations allowed.

### Withdrawal of Refugee Status 
- **Section 36**:
  - Erroneous recognitions due to false information can be retracted.
  - Standing Committee must notify affected persons and allow submissions.
  - After due consideration, recognition may be withdrawn leading to treatment as prohibited persons under the Aliens Control Act.

### Offences and Penalties 
- **Section 37**:
  - Criminal acts related to fraud or permit contraventions punishable by fine/imprisonment (up to 5 years).

### Regulations 
- **Section 38**:
  - Minister may regulate aspects related to asylum seeker influx, referral processes, forms, conditions of stay, interpretation services, and other necessary matters.
  - Regulations on influx must consult provincial Premier.

### Training of Staff 
- **Section 39**:
  - Director-General to ensure proper training for delegated staff under the Act.

### Transitional Arrangements 
- **Section 40**:
  - Existing asylum applicants and recognized refugees before the Act’s commencement are regarded under this Act.

### Short Title and Commencement 
- **Section 41**:
  - This Act may be cited as the Refugee Act, 1998.
