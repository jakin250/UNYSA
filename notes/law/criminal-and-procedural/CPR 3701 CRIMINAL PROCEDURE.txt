## 1.3 CRIMINAL PROCEDURE IN THE CONTEXT OF THE CONSTITUTION
- **Constitution as Supreme Law**: All laws must comply with the Constitution (s 2).
- **Bill of Rights (Chapter 2)**: Protects individual rights in criminal procedure.
  - **Section 8(1)**: Applies to all law and binds all state organs.
  - **Section 7(2)**: State must respect and fulfill rights in the Bill of Rights.
  - **Section 35**: 
    - (1)(a): Right to remain silent.
    - (2)(a): Right to prompt information on reasons for detention.
    - Arrested Persons Rights:
      - (c): Right to a public trial.
      - (e): Right to be present at trial.
      - (h): Presumption of innocence and right to remain silent.
      - (i): Right to challenge evidence.
      - (j): Protection against self-incrimination.
- **Section 36**: Limitation clause; factors for court consideration:
  - (a): Nature of the right.
  - (b): Importance of limitation purpose.
  - (c): Nature & extent of limitation.
  - (d): Relation between limitation and purpose.
  - (e): Less restrictive alternatives.

## 1.4 THE PRESUMPTION OF INNOCENCE IN CRIMINAL PROCEDURE
- **Guarantee of Innocence**: Accused presumed innocent until proven guilty.
- **Factors Affecting Presumption**:
  - **Previous Convictions**: Prohibited in trials (CPA s 211); presiding officers should recuse themselves if previously involved with the accused.
  - **Standard of Proof**: Prosecution bears the burden of proof beyond a reasonable doubt. 
    - Must establish:
      - (a) act (conduct)
      - (b) compliance with crime definition
      - (c) unlawfulness of conduct
      - (d) culpability (intention/negligence).
- **Principle of Legality**: Supports presumption of innocence.

## 1.5 CRIMINAL PROCEDURE IN THE CONTEXT OF THE INQUISITORIAL AND ACCUSATORIAL PROCEDURAL SYSTEMS
- **Historical Roots**: South African procedure influenced by Roman, Roman-Dutch, and English law.
- **Accusatorial System**: 
  - Judge as detached umpire; police as investigators.
  - Prosecution manages evidence and controls trial process.
  - Trial is a contest between prosecution and defense.
- **Inquisitorial System**: 
  - Judge actively conducts proceedings; controls witness questioning.
  - Accused questioned by investigating judge.
- **Hybrid Elements**: South African procedure is mainly accusatorial but includes inquisitorial aspects in specific circumstances (e.g., questioning at plea hearings, guilty pleas).

### Securing the Attendance of the Accused at the Criminal Trial
(Chapter 7 of the handbook)

#### OVERVIEW
- Identification of methods for securing attendance: summons, written notice, indictment, arrest (s 38, CPA).

#### 2.1 HOW IS THE ACCUSED BROUGHT BEFORE COURT IN GENERAL?
- Various methods: **summons, written notice, indictment, arrest**.

#### 2.2 BRINGING THE ACCUSED BEFORE COURT BY WAY OF ARREST

##### 2.2.1 Summons
- Used for summary trials; pre-arrest procedure includes:
  1. Prosecutor drafts charge and hands to clerk (s 54(1)).
  2. Clerk issues summons with charge details (s 54(1)).
  3. Clerk provides summons to process server (s 329).
  4. Service of summons must occur 14 days prior (s 54(3)).

##### 2.2.2 Written Notice to Appear
- Issued by peace officer if probable fine ≤ R2,500 (s 56(1)):
  - Includes accused's details and hearing instructions.
  - Permits guilt admission and fine payment without court appearance.

##### 2.2.3 Indictment
- Used in superior courts; must include:
  - Charge details and defendant's personal information (s 144(1)-(3)).
  - Summary of substantial facts and witness list.
  - Service to accused must be 10 days prior to trial (s 144(4)).

##### 2.2.4 Arrest
- Arrest is severe infringement of rights (s 12(1)(a) & (b), Constitution).
- Preferably with a warrant, barring exceptions (unlawful without).
  
###### 2.2.4.1 Use of Force in Effecting an Arrest
- Strict conditions for force use:
  - Only with resistance to arrest or to prevent fleeing.
  - Never as punishment.
  - **Definition of terms** (s 49 CPA):
    - "Arrestor": authorized to make arrests.
    - "Suspect": reasonably suspected of committing an offence.
    - "Deadly force": likely to cause serious harm/death.

#### 2.3 PROCEDURE AFTER ARREST
- Arrestee must be taken to police station promptly.
- Detention must not exceed 48 hours without being presented to court (Ex parte Prokureur-Generaal, Transvaal 1980 (3) SA 516 (T)).
- First appearance allows for remand or release on bail (Constitution s 35, CPA s 50).

#### 2.4 EXTRADITION
- Extradition ensures accused is brought to foreign state's court.
- Arrest required as per section 40(1)(k) for offenses recognized in SA law.

## 18 Arrest and Force

- Offence must be forcible and serious, involving life-threatening violence or grievous bodily harm.

### Relevant Case Law:
1. **Matlou v Makhubedu 1978 (1) SA 946 (A)** 
   - Justified killing of a fugitive when escape cannot be avoided by other means; oral warning, warning shot, then aiming for legs emphasized.

2. **Govender v Minister of Safety and Security 2001 (4) SA 273 (SCA)**
   - Wide interpretation of reasonableness; considerations include suspect's age, build, and potential for non-violent arrest.

3. **Jooste NO v Minister of Police 1975 (1) SA 394 (E)**
   - Liability of dog handlers if harm to suspect is unjustified; force must be reasonable and necessary.

4. **Ex Parte Minister of Safety and Security and Others: In Re S v Walters and Another 2002 (4) SA 613 (CC)**
   - Key principles of arrest:
     - Aim of arrest is to bring suspected offenders to trial.
     - Arrest not the sole method of achieving this.
     - Cannot be used to punish suspects.
     - Force allowed only if necessary, and least degree required.
     - Shooting a suspect allowed in limited circumstances; must pose a threat of violence.

## 4 LEARNING UNIT 4: Search and seizure (Chapter 9 of the handbook)

### 4.1 INTRODUCTION
- Right to property and privacy threatened by unauthorized searches and seizures.
- Strict legal rules govern searches and seizures; unlawful searches lead to claims for damages.
- Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) contains primary regulations; other laws may provide specific powers.
- Aim: Assess legality of searches concerning constitutional rights such as personal privacy (s 10), freedom from degrading treatment (s 12(1)), and bodily integrity (s 12(2)).

### 4.2 WHICH ARTICLES MAY BE SEIZED DURING SEARCH AND SEIZURE?
- CPA prescribes articles eligible for seizure; not all linked to illegality may be seized.
- Legal protections exist for individuals whose property is investigated.

### 4.3 WHAT ARE THE CONTENTS AND OPERATIONAL FUNCTIONS OF A SEARCH WITH A WARRANT?
- General rule: Searches require a warrant.
- Justices of the peace (including certain police officers) may authorize warrants.
- Warrant must specify objects to be seized; failure to disclose material facts can invalidate the warrant (refer to **Thint and Another v The National Director of Public Prosecutions 2008 (2) SACR 421 (CC)**).

### 4.4 WHAT ARE THE CONTENTS AND OPERATIONAL FUNCTIONS OF A SEARCH WITHOUT A WARRANT?
- Searches without a warrant are restricted to specific circumstances:
  - Consent from the individual.
  - Reasonable belief that a warrant would be issued and time-sensitive.

### 4.5 WHAT HAPPENS TO THE ARTICLES WHICH ARE SEIZED IN THE COURSE OF A SEARCH?
- CPA ss 30-36 govern disposal and forfeiture of seized articles.
- Seized articles kept in police custody; may be transferred to court if needed for proceedings.

### Legal Consequences of Unlawful Actions by Authorities
- **Formal-law consequences**: Exclusionary rule as per **s 35(5) of the Constitution**.
  - Evidence obtained unlawfully must be excluded if its admission undermines trial fairness.
  
- **Substantive-law consequences**: Governed partly by **s 28**.
  - Police officials acting outside search warrant authority, or conducting unauthorized searches, are liable for fines or imprisonment (max six months).
  - Aggrieved individuals may claim compensation for unlawful searches/seizures (s 28(2); compensation claims follow procedural rules in **s 300**).

### Additional Legal Framework
- **s 48 of CPA**: Allows peace officers or authorized persons to enter premises forcibly to make an arrest under set conditions.
  - Must have reasonable suspicion of individual’s presence.
  - Must audibly request entry and state purpose before using force.

### 5.1 THE LEGISLATIVE AND CONSTITUTIONAL UNDERPINNINGS OF PRE-TRIAL RELEASE
#### 5.1.1 Introduction
- **Delay in trial** can result from incomplete investigations or extensive testimonies.
- **Constitutional right**: Release on bail (s 35(1)(f)), contingent on the interests of justice.
- Bail ensures minimal curtailment of accused's rights and balances public safety with individual freedom.
- Key considerations include the necessity for the accused to stand trial vs. preventing public harm.

---

### 5.2 DIFFERENT FORMS OF PRE-TRIAL RELEASE
#### 5.2.1 The Police
- **Judicial function**: Normally, only competent courts can set bail, with exceptions for police bail in minor offences.
- Conditions: First court appearance; cash payments without guarantees or discretionary conditions.
  
#### 5.2.2 The Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP)
- **Prosecution bail**: Applicable to Schedule 7 offences (excludes serious crimes like murder and rape).
- Accepts guarantees and cash payments; conditions may be set by the DPP.
- Re-assessment at first court appearance per *section 60*.

#### 5.2.3 A Court of Law
- **Jurisdictions**:
  - Any court where accused appears can hear bail applications (with specific exceptions following Schedule 6).
  - High Court's inherent jurisdiction allows for bail applications during appeals.
- **Hours of operation**: Section 50(6)(i)(b) restricts bail hearings post-hours; High Courts may waive this.
- **Locus standi**: Identified in sections 65 and 65A.
- **Bail factors**: Consider safety, risk of flight, potential witness tampering, system integrity, public order.
- **Procedural aspects**: Bail applications differ from strict criminal procedures, with relaxed evidentiary rules allowing hearsay.

---

### SECTION 60(4)
- Grounds for bail refusal include risks to public safety, flight risk, witness tampering, criminal justice integrity, and public order disturbances.

---

**Summary of police bail (s 59)**:
- Can be granted by police officials for non-serious offences; requires deposit of specified cash.
- Consults with investigating officer; recognizes provisions must be formalized and communicated.

## 6 LEARNING UNIT 6.1
### The right to be informed and the “plea” stage

#### 6.1 INDICTMENTS AND CHARGE SHEETS
- Indictment (superior court) or charge sheet (lower court) informs parties of trial substance (what, where, when, whom).
- Fundamental right of the accused to be informed per **Constitution, Sections 32 and 35(3)(a)**.
- Refer to **Shabalala v Attorney-General 1995 (12) BCLR 1593 (CC)** for case law on this right.

#### 6.1.1 How is the accused informed of the allegations against him?
- Charge sheets must detail allegations to allow accused to respond.

#### 6.1.2 How are errors or defects in the charge sheet corrected?
- **Section 86**: Allows amendment for material defects.
- **Section 88**: Automatic correction for non-material defects. 
- Amendments should not result in new charges that may prejudice the defense.

#### 6.1.3 How many offences may the state allege against the accused in a single instance?
- **Section 83**: Allows multiple offences in a charge, subject to avoiding duplication of convictions.

#### 6.1.4 May several accused be charged together?
- **Section 156 of the Criminal Procedure Act**: Separate offences at the same time/space may be tried together. 
- Misjoinder can occur if connection is absent (**Makganje 1993 (2) SACR 621 (B)**).

#### 6.1.5 May additional accused persons and charges be added at any stage of the trial?
- Refer to **Sections 157(1) and 81(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act** for provisions on adding charges/accused.

---

### 6.2 HOW IS THE ACCUSED INFORMED OF THE ALLEGATIONS AGAINST HIM?
#### 6.2.1 Introduction
- Prosecutor formalizes charges via indictment/charge sheet (critical for participant awareness).

#### 6.2.2 What are the procedural requirements of a valid charge sheet or indictment?
- Strict compliance necessary for valid charge sheets as per Criminal Procedure Act. 
- Written requests for particulars permissible before evidence presentation (**s. 87; read with s. 85(1)(a)**). 

#### 6.2.3 Is the prosecutor bound by the particulars provided?
- Information is record-bound, and prosecutors must substantiate claims; erroneous particulars may harm state credibility.

---

### 6.3 HOW ARE ERRORS OR DEFECTS IN THE CHARGE SHEET CORRECTED?
- Section 86: Amend charge sheets for significant omissions or errors; does not allow new charge substitution.
- Section 88: Offers automatic correction, defaulting to essential amendments before judgment unless prejudicial.

---

### 6.4 HOW MANY OFFENCES MAY THE STATE ALLEGE AGAINST THE ACCUSED IN A SINGLE INSTANCE?
- Offences may overlap; evaluate duplication risks early to avoid prejudicial consequences for sentencing.
- Principle of **autrefois acquit** applies (previous acquittals prevent further prosecution on same facts).

---

### 6.5 MAY SEVERAL ACCUSED BE CHARGED TOGETHER?
- Joinder is allowed; **Section 156** supports trials for multiple accused based on shared evidence.
- Absence of shared connection may indicate a misjoinder.

---

### 6.6 MAY ADDITIONAL ACCUSED PERSONS AND CHARGES BE ADDED AT ANY STAGE OF THE TRIAL?
- Joinder must occur before evidence presentation concerning that charge; refer to **Sections 157(1) and 81(1)**.

## 1. Amending Charge Sheets (p. 42)
- **Sections 86 and 88**:
  - **Section 86**: Amend charge sheet to add essential allegations without prejudice to the accused.
  - **Section 88**: Allows rectifying charge defects by leading evidence, requiring:
    - Offence must be mentioned in the charge.
    - Charge sheet must embody the offence.
    - Proper evidence must be led.
  - Evidence correction cancelled if defect is raised before judgment.
  - **Section 123**: DPP can convert summary trial into preparatory examination (Bham v Lutge 1949 (3) SA 392 (T)).

## 2. Legal Position on Charges (p. 43-46)
- Accused can be charged with multiple offences.
- **Duplication of convictions**: Prevents multiple convictions for a single culpable act.
- **Right to a fair trial**: Constitutionally enshrined principle (Whitehead 2008 (1) SACR 431 (SCA)).
- Prosecutor discretion on charges.
- **Grobler 1966 (1) SA 507 (A)**: Section 83 pertains to charge formulation, not convictions.
- Duplication analysis depends on:
  1. Single act constituting multiple offences (statutory/common-law).
  2. **Section 336**: Prosecution permitted under either statutory or common law; no double punishment.
  3. Multiple acts nearly simultaneous also constitute multiple offences.
     - **Tests**:
        - **Single intent and continuous transaction**.

## LEARNING UNIT 8: The conduct of the trial

### 1. Separation of Trials
- **Joinder**: Application for separation typically by defense; court discretion is crucial.
- Key considerations for separation:
  - **Prejudice**: Must be probable, not merely possible (case: **Nzuza**).
  - **Admissibility of Evidence**: Evidence admissible against one co-accused but not another influences the decision.
  - **Imputation of Blame**: Shared blame may justify joint trials to ascertain degrees of guilt (case: **Solomon**).
  - **Guilty Pleas**: Trials must be separated if one accused pleads guilty; complete guilty trials before continuing with others (cases: **Pieterson**, **Zonele**, **Somciza**).
  - Right to call a co-accused does not outweigh right against self-incrimination.

### 2. Common Law vs. Statutory Grounds for Separation
- **Common Law**:
  - Jointly charged trials generally cannot be separated post-issue joining.
  
- **Statutory Law (Section 157, Criminal Procedure Act)**:
  - Separation permitted upon application, can be directed at any trial stage.
  - Prosecutors cannot separate trials mainly to use one accused as a witness against others.
  - Trials started anew after separation.

### 3. Court's Discretion to Recall/Examine Witnesses
- See **Section 167** (examine witnesses) and **Section 186** (subpoena witnesses) of the Criminal Procedure Act.

### 4. Closed Trials Circumstances
- **Authority & Reasons**:
  - **Section 153(1)**: Security/public morals threatened → MAY order in camera.
  - **Section 153(2)**: Harm potential to witnesses/accused → MAY testify behind closed doors.
  - **Section 153(3)**: Sexual/extortion-related complaints → MAY request proceedings cleared.
  - **Section 153(3A)**: In sexual offences, attendance limited to necessary persons only.
  - **Section 153(5)**: Child witness considerations → MAY clear court, limit under-18 attendances.
  - **Section 158(2)**: Evidence via electronic media upon request.
  - **Child Justice Act Section 63(5)**: Trials of child offenders closed; permissions may be granted.

### 5. Case Law Overview (Section 174, Criminal Procedure Act)
- Relevant cases: **S v Hudson**, **S v Manona**, **S v Dladla** (1961), **S v Bouwer**, **S v Mpetha**, **S v Shuping**, **S v Zulu**, **S v Phuravhatha**, **S v Lubaxa**, **S v Mafokane**.

### 6. Reopening of State's Case
- State may rebut evidence if new matters arise in the defense's case. The trial court's discretion applies for reopening.

  - Consider potential for diversion and appropriate placement for the child.

- **Timing**:

- **Participants**:
  - Include presiding officer, prosecutor, child, parent, probation officer, diversion service provider (if applicable).

- **Child Care Protections**:
  - No leg irons/handcuffs unless exceptional; separation from adults in custody.

## 1.9 THE DIVERSION PROCESS
- **Definition**: 
  - Diversion is removing a child from formal court processes, relying on restorative justice ideals.

- **Stages of Diversion**:
  1. **Prosecutorial Diversion** (**section 41**):
     - Only for Schedule 1 offences, requiring acknowledgment of responsibility.

  2. **Presiding Officer Diversion** (**section 52**):
     - Criteria: acknowledgment, no undue influence, prima facie case, consent from parties.
     - Involves prosecutor's consultation regarding victim views.

  3. **Trial Diversion**:
     - Possible at any trial stage before state case ends.
     - Requires acknowledgment, bridging consequences for breach of diversion terms.

- **Diversion Levels**:
  - **Level 1**: Minor offences, max duration of 12/24 months based on age (low severity options).
  - **Level 2**: More serious offences, max duration of 24/48 months with stricter options.

## 1.9.1 DIVERSION SUMMARY
- **Schedule Ratings**:
  - **Schedule 1**: Least serious offences (e.g., theft <R2,500).
  - **Schedule 2**: Not trivial but less serious than Schedule 3.
  - **Schedule 3**: Most serious (e.g., murder, robbery).

- **Who May Recommend Diversion**:
  - Prosecutor: Can recommend before preliminary inquiry or state case close.
  - Presiding officer: Final order in child justice court.
  - DPP: May indicate diversion for Schedule 3 offences under exceptional circumstances.

## LEARNING UNIT 1: The Child Justice Act 75 of 2008 (CJA)
### Overview and Purpose
- **Child Justice Act (CJA)**: Aimed at establishing a separate justice system for child offenders, aligned with constitutional rights and international obligations (notably ss 28 and 35).
- **Child-Friendly Processes**: Incorporates mechanisms such as diversion and preliminary inquiries to make the justice process more considerate of child offenders.

### Preliminary Inquiries (Section 47 of CJA)
- Conducted informally by inquiry magistrate, focusing on gathering information and assessing the child's acknowledgment of responsibility.
- **Key Steps**:
  - (1) Explain purpose and procedures to the child.
  - (2) Ascertain acknowledgment of responsibility.
  - (3) Gather relevant documentation (probation assessments, previous convictions).
  - (4) Engage parents or guardians in the process.

### Trial Procedures
- **Child Justice Courts (Sections 63-67)**: Child matters referred here must be handled with jurisdiction under CJA provisions and considering the child's best interests.
- **Plea Process**: Requires informing the child of allegations, rights, and subsequent procedures.
- **Trial Standards**:
  - Fair proceedings tailored to the child’s age.
  - Limiting presence of non-essential attendees.
  - Upholding rights to parental or guardian assistance, unless justified otherwise.

### Parental and Guardian Involvement
- **Mandatory Assistance**: A child must be assisted by a parent or guardian unless they are unavailable, in which case the court can appoint an observer.
- **Failure to Comply**: Procedures are outlined for when compliance with rules concerning parental attendance is not met.

### Postponements and Trial Efficiency
- **Time Limits**: Trials must conclude swiftly, with specified durations for postponements based on the child's custody situation (prison, child care).
- **Diversion Orders**: Child justice court may order diversion before case conclusion, subject to compliance warning.

### Court Flexibility
- **Joint Preliminary Inquiries**: Allowed for co-accused children if beneficial.
- **Measures for Additional Evidence**: Inquiry magistrates can seek further documentation or information needed.

### Legislation and Jurisprudence References
- **Adjustments and Amendments**: CJA extends or modifies provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act as necessary (Sections 63-67).
- **Emphasis on Child's Best Interests**: Throughout all processes, the priority is the welfare and rights of the child.

- **Legislation and Case Law**: Various acts as noted above; pivotal for understanding the legal landscape surrounding child justice.
