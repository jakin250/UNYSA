## 1. Introductory Topics 
### 1.1 Criminal Liability: A Summary

#### 1.1.1 General
- **Criminal liability** requires certain conditions to be met for conviction.
- Principle of legality: Criminal conduct must be recognized by law, independent of societal morals. 

#### 1.1.2 The Four Elements of Criminal Liability
1. **Act or Conduct**
   - Characterized by voluntary acts or omissions.
   - Conduct must be voluntary and within the will/intellect of the accused (e.g., not applicable in cases of sleepwalking).
   - Liability for omissions only if a duty to act exists.

2. **Compliance with the Definitional Elements**
   - Conduct must align with the specific definitional elements of a crime.
   - Defines how different crimes are distinguished (e.g., theft vs. robbery).

3. **Unlawfulness**
   - Conduct must not be justified by law.
   - Justifications may include self-defense, necessity, consent, etc.

4. **Culpability**
   - Focuses on the mental state of the accused at the time of the act.
   - Sub-requirements:
     - **Criminal Capacity**: Ability to comprehend wrongfulness and act accordingly.
     - **Intentional or Negligent Conduct**: Determines either intentional crimes or those based on negligence.

#### 1.1.3 Sequence of Investigation into Presence of Elements
- Investigate requirements in the order:
  1. Conduct (voluntary)
  2. Compliance with definitional elements
  3. Unlawfulness
  4. Culpability
  
- Conclusion on liability is drawn from the lowest (first) requirement up.
- If any requirement is unmet, further inquiry becomes moot (illustrated by the kite retrieval metaphor).

### Chapter 2: Principle of Legality

#### Learning Unit 2: The Principle of Legality as Entwined in the Constitution

**2.1 Background**
- Criminal law must protect individuals against state abuses.
- Principle of legality grounded in fairness and constitutional democracy.

**2.2 The Concept of Legality**
- Law must recognize conduct as criminal for liability to arise.
- A breach of legal rules does not necessarily constitute a crime without clear legal definition.
- Nullum crimen sine lege: No crime without a legal provision, embedded in Section 35(3)(l) of the Constitution.

**2.3 Definition and Contents of the Principle**
**2.3.1 Definition**
- Accused cannot be convicted unless:
  - Conduct recognized as a crime at the time of commission.
  - Clearly defined in legal terms.
  - Defined prior to the act occurring.
  - Requires no broad interpretation of legal definitions.

**2.3.2 Rules Embodied in the Principle**
1. **Ius acceptum**: Crime must be recognized by law; courts cannot create crimes.
2. **Ius praevium**: Recognition of crime must exist at the time of commission.
3. **Ius certum**: Crimes must be clearly formulated.
4. **Ius strictum**: Narrow interpretation of crime definitions required.
5. **Nulla poena sine lege**: Sentences must comply with the same requirements as crimes.

- Principle of legality emphasizes the need for public knowledge of criminal conduct to ensure compliance.
- Reinforces democratic governance by reserving crime definition to elected representatives rather than judiciary.

## 2.4 CONDUCT MUST BE RECOGNISED BY THE LAW AS A CRIME (IUS ACCEPTUM)

- In codified criminal law jurisdictions, only conduct defined as a crime in the criminal code is punishable.
- South African criminal law is not fully codified; crucial crimes like **murder** and **assault** are based on common law.
- The principle of legality mandates that a court can only convict if the conduct is recognized as a crime under common or statutory law.
- **Ius acceptum** means "the law as it has been received," incorporating both common and existing statutory law.
- **Section 35(3)(l)** of the Constitution implies respect for ius acceptum, protecting rights to fair trials and preventing conviction for non-existent crimes.

### 2.4.1 Common-law crimes
- No crime exists if not specified by common law; courts cannot create offenses based on morals (M 1915 CPD 334).
- Constitutional Court emphasized in **Masiya v Director of Public Prosecutions 2007 (2) SACR 435 (CC)** that law reform is a legislative responsibility, not judicial.

### 2.4.2 Statutory crimes
- An Act creating a crime must explicitly state what is a crime and the associated punishment.
- Ambiguity in statute's language favors the accused (Hanid 1950 (2) SA 592 (T)).
- Legal normal (non-criminal rules), criminal norms (criminal conduct), and criminal sanctions (penalties) defined.

## 2.5 CRIMES SHOULD NOT BE CREATED WITH RETROSPECTIVE EFFECT (IUS PRAEVIUM)

- The principle of legality prohibits conviction for an act that was not a crime when committed (ius praevium).
- Retrospective legislation is declared ex post facto, unjustly punishing acts that were legal at the time.
- **Section 35(3)** ensures no conviction for acts not regarded as crimes when committed.
  
## 2.6 CRIMES OUGHT TO BE FORMULATED CLEARLY (IUS CERTUM)

- Criminal norms must be clear; vagueness violates the principle of legality (ius certum).
- Historical example of vague prohibition from Nazi Germany demonstrates dangers of unclear law.
- **Section 35(3)** of the Constitution likely encompasses ius certum, safeguarding accused’s understanding of criminality.

## 2.7 PROVISIONS CREATING CRIMES MUST BE INTERPRETED STRICTLY (IUS STRICTUM)

- **Ius strictum** mandates strict interpretation of crime-creating provisions, focusing on literal meanings.
- Courts must avoid broad interpretations or analogies that expand criminal definitions detrimentally to the accused.
- The **Masiya** case highlights that, even if the definition of rape was amended to include anal penetration, the new definition was only applied prospectively, respecting the accused's rights under the Constitution.
- The Court argued that prior definitions were irrational and arbitrary, extending definitions when necessary to align with constitutional values.

**Chapter 3: Elements of Criminal Liability**

**3.3 The Act as an Element of Criminal Liability**  
- **3.3.1 Definition of Act**  
  - The term "act" refers to both acts and omissions (conduct), used in a non-technical sense.  
  - Context determines whether "act" is used literally or broadly (both commission and omission).  

- **3.3.2 Thoughts Not Punishable**  
  - Mere thoughts or intentions are not punishable.  
  - Criminal liability arises from actions, including attempts, which necessitate an act beyond thoughts or decisions.

- **3.3.3 Act Must Be a Human Act or Omission**  
  - The perpetrator must be a human being.  
  - Animals and inanimate objects cannot be punished; a human can be liable only for crimes facilitated by animals (e.g., Fernandez 1966 (2) SA 259 (A)).  

- **3.3.4 Act or Conduct Must Be Voluntary**  
  - Conduct is punishable only if voluntary, capable of being controlled by the will.  
  - Involuntary actions (e.g., sleepwalking, epilepsy) do not constitute criminal liability.  
  - Voluntary act ≠ willed act; negligent acts can still result in liability.  

- **3.3.4.1 General**  
  - Acts must be voluntary to be criminal; involuntary acts do not qualify.  
  - Distinction between layman’s and jurist’s understanding of "act."

- **3.3.4.2 Factors Excluding Voluntariness of Act**  
  - **a. Absolute Force (vis absoluta)**  
    - Conduct may be involuntary due to absolute force (e.g., coercion by a stronger person; Hercules 1954 (3) SA 826 (A)).  
  - **b. Natural Forces**  
    - Acts propelled by natural forces (e.g., accidents due to hurricanes) are also involuntary.  
  - **c. Automatism**  
    - **i. Meaning**  
      - Behaving mechanically (e.g., reflexes or during sleep) qualifies as automatism.  
      
    - **ii. "Sane" vs. "Insane Automatism"**  
      - "Sane automatism" = absence of voluntary act, with the onus on the state (Trickett 1973 (3) SA 526 (T)).  
      - "Insane automatism" = based on mental illness, with the onus on the accused.  

    - **iii. Difficulty of Defence**  
      - Courts generally scrutinize automatism defenses cautiously; need for expert evidence.  
      
    - **iv. Antecedent Liability**  
      - Accused can be liable if aware of conditions leading to automatism but still engages in risky behavior (e.g., Victor 1943 TPD 77).  

**3.4 Omissions**  
- **3.4.1 Definition of Omissions**  
  - The term "act" also encompasses omissions (failure to act).  
  - Discussion on the liability for omissions follows.

# Study Notes: Legal Duty to Act Positively

## 3.4.1 Legal Duty to Act Positively

### 3.4.1.1 General Rule
- **Legal Duty**: An omission is punishable only if there is a legal duty to act positively (Minister van Polisie v Ewels 1975 (3) SA 590 (A)).
- **Moral vs Legal Duty**: A moral duty differs from a legal one.
- **Community Convictions**: Legal duty arises when the community's legal convictions require action.

### 3.4.1.2 Legal Duty: Specific Instances
- **Statutory Duty**: Legislations impose duties, e.g., filing tax returns (s 61 National Road Traffic Act 93 of 1996), reporting corruption (s 20 Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004), and financial crime (various provisions Financial Intelligence Centre Act 2001).
- **Common Law Duty**: Legal duties may arise from common law, e.g., high treason requires reporting to police.
- **Contractual Duty**: Duties may arise from agreements, e.g., Pittwood (1902) regarding railway gates.
- **Control of Dangerous Objects**: A duty arises for those responsible for dangerous objects (Fernandez 1966 (2) SA 259 (A)).
- **Protective Relationships**: Duties exist in relationships such as parent-child (B 1994 (2) SACR 237 (E)).
- **Omission Following a Positive Act**: Duties arise from previous acts, e.g., omissio per commissionem (e.g., lighting a fire).
- **Incumbency Duty**: Duties arise from positions, e.g., police duty to assist victims (Minister van Polisie v Ewels 1975).
- **Court Orders**: Legal duties can result from court orders, e.g., maintenance payments.

### 3.4.1.3 The State's Duty to Protect Citizens from Violent Crime
- **Constitutional Duty**: Courts recognize the state's duty to protect citizens (Carmichele v Minister of Safety and Security 2001 (4) SA 938 (CC)).
- **Civil Cases**: Recognition of legal duties in civil cases impacts criminal liability.
- **Accountability**: The state's duty is founded on accountability and common-law principles (Minister of Safety and Security v Van Duivenboden 2002 (6) SA 431 (SCA)).

## 3.4.2 The Defence of Impossibility

- **Voluntary Omission**: An omission must be voluntary to incur liability.
  
### Requirements for Impossibility Defence
1. **Positive Duty Requirement**: Legal provision must impose a positive duty (not a prohibition).
2. **Objective Impossibility**: Must be objectively impossible for X to comply (Hoko 1941 SR 211).
3. **Responsibility for Impossibility**: X must not be responsible for the situation causing impossibility (Close Settlement Corporation 1922 AD 194).

### Case References
- **Canestra 1951 (2) SA 317 (A)**: Defense of impossibility rejected due to lack of a positive duty.
- **Leeuw 1975 (1) SA 439 (O)**: Clarifies that mere inconvenience does not constitute impossibility.

#### 4.3 Murder and Culpable Homicide

- **Causation in Death Cases**: 
  - "To cause the death" refers to the act's direct link to death in specific circumstances. 
  - Example cases: **Makali 1950 (1) SA 340 (N)**; **Hartmann 1975 (3) SA 532 (C)**.

---

#### 4.3.3 Principles to Be Applied in Determining Causation

##### 4.3.3.1 Basic Principle

- **Causal Link Requirements**:
  - **Factual Cause**: X's act must be a conditio sine qua non for Y's death (but-for causation).
  - **Legal Cause**: It must be reasonable and fair under policy considerations to deem X's act as the cause of Y's death.

##### 4.3.3.2 Factual Causation – Conditio Sine Qua Non

- **Definition**: An act is a conditio sine qua non if the prohibited result (Y's death) does not occur without it.
- **Test Application**: Courts must consider if Y's death would have happened had X's act not occurred. 
- **Case Law Support**: Referenced: **Makali 1950 (1) SA 340 (N)**, **Mokoena 1979 (1) PH H 13 (A)**, **Minister van Polisie v Skosana 1977 (1) SA 31 (A)**.
  
##### 4.3.3.3 Legal Causation General

- **Need for Limitation**: The broad range of factual causes necessitates a second criterion for legal causation to exclude irrelevant factors.
- **Specific Tests**: Courts utilize various tests (proximate cause, adequate cause, novus actus interveniens) based on equity considerations.

##### 4.3.3.4 Theories of Legal Causation

- **Individualisation Theories**: Focus on the most operative cause. Limited support in contemporary law; seen in **Daniëls 1983 (3) SA 275 (A)**.
- **Theory of Adequate Causation**: 
  - Definition: An act is a legal cause if it typically leads to the result in normal circumstances.
  - Example: Striking a match naturally leads to a fire, unlike merely calling a dog.
- **Novus Actus Interveniens**: 
  - Definition: An intervening act that breaks the causal chain (e.g., a third party's act).
  - **Legal Definition**: Must be unexpected, abnormal, or unusual; deviates from the normal course of events (S v Counter 2003 (1) SACR 143 (SCA)).

---

#### 4.3.4 Courts' Approach to Legal Causation

- **Policy Considerations**: Courts do not prefer one specific theory for all cases; they consider what is reasonable, fair, and just (Daniëls 1983, Mokgethi 1990).
- **Usage of Multiple Theories**: Courts may apply varied theories based on context to arrive at just outcomes.

---

#### 4.3.5 Own View – Theory of Adequate Causation Preferable

- **Recommendation**: Prefer the theory of adequate causation for legal assessments of causation.
- **Critiques of Individualisation Theories**: Highlighted in **Daniëls**, emphasizing the unpredictability of consequences not naturally expected from the act.

  - **Legal Cause**: Z's actions = novus actus preventing X's liability.

- **Third Set of Facts (subsequent event)**:
  - Outcome = novus actus; X's actions not legal cause of Y’s death.

## 4.3.7 Examples from decisions

### 4.3.7.1 Assisted suicide – the Grotjohn decision
- **Issue**: X encourages Y's suicide; Y commits suicide.
- **Outcome**: Grotjohn (1970) clarified that voluntary act of Y does not negate causal link.
- **Legal Outcome**: Causal link exists if Y's act aligns with X's intentions.

### 4.3.7.2 The Daniëls decision 
- **Facts**: X shoots Y; Z subsequently shoots Y.
- **Judgment**:
  - **Majority View**: X’s act = factual/legal cause due to Z's actions being non-intervening.
  - **Minority View**: Trengove JA sees Z's shot as novus actus.

### 4.3.7.3 The Mokgethi decision
- **Facts**: X shoots bank teller (Y), leading to complications resulting in Y's death.
- **Judgment**: 
  - Factual causation present; legal causation absent due to Y's failure to follow medical advice.

### 4.3.7.4 Negligent medical treatment – the Tembani decision
- **Facts**: X shoots Y with negligent medical treatment leading to Y's death.
- **Judgment**: 
  - Factual causation confirmed; negligence in treatment does not exempt X from liability if original wound posed ongoing danger.

## 4.3.8 Causation: a summary
- **Causation Rules**:
  1. Causal link requires both factual & legal causes.
  2. Factual Cause: X's act as conditio sine qua non for Y's death.
  3. Legal Cause: Policy considerations must justify linking X's action to Y's death.
  4. Theories used include individualization, adequate causation, and novus actus interveniens; not exclusive.

- Four cardinal requirements for crime liability:  
  1. Act or conduct  
  2. Compliance with definitional elements  
  3. Unlawfulness  
  4. Culpability  
- Current focus: unlawfulness.

---

### 5.2 THE MEANING OF "UNLAWFULNESS"  
#### 5.2.1 General  
- Compliance with definitional elements does not ensure liability.  
- Unlawfulness is a further requirement: an act can be lawful or unlawful beyond just meeting definitional elements.  

#### 5.2.2 Acts that comply with the definitional elements are not necessarily unlawful  
- Compliance examples:  
  1. **Murder**: Killing in self-defence is lawful.  
  2. **Assault**: Medical procedures with consent are lawful.  
  3. **Speeding**: Emergency transport can be lawful despite breaking speed limit **(Pretorius 1975 (2) SA 85 (SWA))**.  

#### 5.2.3 Content of unlawfulness  
1. **Grounds of justification**:  
   - An act may not be unlawful if justified by reasons like self-defence, necessity, or consent.  
   - Possible limits to grounds exist (e.g., exceeding limits is unlawful).  

2. **Legal convictions of society**:  
   - Conduct is unlawful if it conflicts with boni mores (good morals) or societal legal convictions **(Clark v Hurst 1992 (4) SA 630 (D))**.  
   - Bill of Rights values (e.g., dignity, equality) are crucial considerations.  
   - A systematic inquiry must differentiate between provisional unlawfulness and absolute unlawfulness.

#### 5.2.4 Unlawfulness distinguished from culpability  
- Unlawfulness is evaluated without considering the actor's state of mind; culpability is subsequently assessed.  

---

### 5.3 PRIVATE DEFENCE  
#### 5.3.1 Definition of private defence  
- Private defence allows lawful force to repel an unlawful attack threatening protected interests, provided the defence is necessary, directed at the attacker, and proportional.  
- Private defence applies as a broader concept than just self-defence; it includes defence of others.  

#### 5.3.2 Requirements of the attack  
1. The attack must be unlawful:  
   - No private defence against lawful acts (e.g. resisting lawful arrest).
   - Prearranged duels do not qualify for private defence, as seen in **Jansen 1983 (3) SA 534 (NC)**, where the act was part of an unlawful attack.  
   - An attack's culpability is irrelevant; private defence can apply against non-culpable acts.

## 6.3.4 Requirements for a valid plea of consent (pp. 374-380)
### Consent Requirements:
1. **Voluntary Consent**
   - Must be free of coercion (case: **Ex parte Minister of Justice**).
   - Submission ≠ consent (case: **Volschenk 1968 (2) PH H283 (D)**).
2. **Minimum Mental Abilities**
   - Must understand nature and consequences of the act (cases: **C 1952 (4) SA 117 (O)**; **K 1958 (3) SA 420 (A)**).
3. **Awareness of True Facts**
   - Must know facts relevant to the act (cases: **Flattery 1877**; **Williams 1923**).
4. **Express or Tacit Consent**
   - No qualitative difference; both types valid.
5. **Timing**
   - Consent must be given before the act.
6. **By the Complainant**
   - Typically requires the complainant's consent; exceptions exist (e.g., parental consent for minors).

## 6.4 Presumed Consent (pp. 381-383)
- Justification if Y's consent cannot be obtained but reasonable grounds exist to believe Y would consent.
- Examples:
  - Ambulance driver transporting unconscious Y (presumed consent).
  - Surgeon performing emergency surgery without explicit consent.

  1. Order from a person in lawful authority.
  2. Duty to obey the order.
  3. Harm caused must be no more than necessary.

  - **s 12: Freedom/security of person**
  - **s 28(1)(d): Right of children to protection from maltreatment**.

### 6.7.3 Parents’ Right to Impose Corporal Punishment

  - Reasonable chastisement defence inconsistent with children’s constitutional rights.
  - Ban on corporal punishment does not impede parental care.
  - Less restrictive means for discipline available. Appeal dismissed.

### 7.2 THE REQUIREMENT OF CULPABILITY IN GENERAL
#### 7.2.1 Introduction
- Criminal liability requires a culpable state of mind alongside the act and unlawfulness.
- Culpability = Grounds for legal reproach; assessed based on the individual's mental state.
- Exceptions: Mentally ill individuals or children cannot be blamed if unable to act lawfully.

#### 7.2.2 Culpability and unlawfulness
- Culpability assessed after establishing unlawfulness; cannot blame lawful conduct.
- Criteria for unlawfulness are universal, while culpability considers the individual’s characteristics.
- Grounds of justification exclude unlawfulness; culpability may be excluded by criminal capacity or mistake.

#### 7.2.3 Terminology
- Common terms: *Mens rea* (culpability) and "fault"; preferred term is "culpability".

#### 7.2.4 Criminal capacity and forms of culpability
- Criminal capacity = mental ability to appreciate wrongdoing and act accordingly.
- Two forms of culpability: intention and negligence; possession of criminal capacity doesn’t imply culpability without these.

#### 7.2.5 The principle of contemporaneity
- Culpability must coincide with the unlawful act; no crime if culpability arises before or after the act.
- **Example:** X unintentionally kills Y; lacks intention at the moment of the act.

- **Case Reference:** *Masilela 1968 (2) SA 558 (A)* – confirmation of a single course of conduct for culpability.

#### 7.2.6 Classifications regarding culpability
- Culpability is multi-faceted; assessed in various study units:
  - Study unit 8: Mental illness and youth exclusions.
  - Study units 9/10: Intention.
  - Study unit 11: Negligence.
  - Study unit 12: Intoxication and provocation.
  - Study unit 13: Instances disregarding culpability.

### 7.3 CRIMINAL CAPACITY
#### 7.3.1 Definition
- Criminal capacity requires:
  1. Understanding of wrongful acts.
  2. Ability to act according to that understanding.
- Lack of either = no criminal liability.

#### 7.3.2 Criminal capacity vs. intention
- Foundation for culpability; investigation into criminal capacity precedes intention.
- Distinction: Criminal capacity = mental abilities; intention = state of mind.

#### 7.3.3 Two psychological legs of the test
- Two components of criminal capacity:
  1. **Cognitive Function:** Ability to distinguish right from wrong.
  2. **Conative Function:** Ability to control behavior per one's insights (self-control).
  
#### 7.3.4 Defences excluding criminal capacity
- **Particular Defences:**
  - Mental illness (Criminal Procedure Act, Sections 77-79).
  - Youthful age (Child Justice Act, Section 7(1)).
  
- **General Defence:** Non-pathological criminal incapacity not limited to specific mental characteristics; may exclude criminal capacity.

## 7.4 THE DEFENCE OF NON-PATHOLOGICAL CRIMINAL INCAPACITY
### 7.4.1 General
- General defence of criminal incapacity distinct from mental illness and youth.
- **Laubscher 1988 (1) SA 163 (A)**: Introduced "non-pathological criminal incapacity" to exclude mental illness from section 78 of the Criminal Procedure Act.
- No proof of mental illness required; brief period of incapacity is sufficient (e.g., emotional collapse).
- Interpretation of deficiencies in criminal apprehension and control during the act is crucial.

### 7.4.2 The Law Before 2002
- If incapacity due to mental illness or youth, those specific defences apply.
- Non-pathological criminal incapacity applies when neither factor is present; focuses on ability to appreciate and act accordingly.
- Possible causes: emotional collapse, provocation, intoxication, stress, or fear.
- The **state must prove capacity; X lays foundation** for defence, ideally needing expert testimony (not essential).
- Key cases: **Campher 1987 (1) SA 940 (A) & Wiid 1990 (1) SACR 561 (A)**.

### 7.4.3 The Judgment in Eadie
- **Eadie 2002 (1) SACR 663 (SCA)**: Challenged existence of non-pathological criminal incapacity.
- Facts: Accused acted violently post-alcohol and provocation, resulted in murder conviction.
- Court equated non-pathological incapacity with **sane automatism**, emphasizing the voluntary nature of actions.
- Established precedent that provocation-related claims might align with automatism, complicating non-pathological claims.

### 7.4.4 The Present Law
- Following **Eadie**, success in non-pathological incapacity arguments is unlikely; must introduce doubt about voluntariness.
- Courts will evaluate claims against common human behavioral understanding.
- **Navsa JA**: Critiques subjective claims; suggests merger of policy and legal principles.
- **Snyman**: Suggests Eadie promotes partial liability rather than full acquittal in provocation cases.
- Current stance: non-pathological criminal incapacity still viable for causes outside provocation and emotional stress.

- **mens rea**: Guilty mind; culpability requirement.

7. Non-pathological criminal incapacity involves emotional stress, provocation, shock, etc.
8. **Eadie**: Merged the defenses of provocation with sane automatism; prompts limited success in non-pathological claims.

## 8.2 MENTAL ILLNESS
### 8.2.1 Introduction
- Criminal capacity exclusions include mental illness noted in **section 78** of the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**.
- Previously used **M'Naghten rules**; regulation shifted to statutory framework post-1977.

### 8.2.2 Contents of Section 78(1)
- Defines incapacity: 
  - If accused suffers from mental illness at the time of act/omission, incapable of:
    - a. Appreciating wrongfulness
    - b. Acting in accordance with that appreciation
- "Shall not be criminally responsible" equates to "shall lack criminal capacity".

### 8.2.3 Analysis of Section 78(1)
- Two legs of test:
  - **A**: Pathological leg
  - **B**: Psychological leg
- Both legs must be satisfied for defence; must show causal connection.

### 8.2.4 Mental Illness or Mental Defect
1. **Definition**: Refers to pathological disturbances; does not include temporary conditions (e.g., intoxication).
2. **Expert Testimony**: Courts require psychiatric evidence for diagnosis.
3. **Origin**: Not necessary for mental illness origin to be psychological; can be organic.
4. **Duration**: Both permanent and temporary illnesses are relevant; lucid intervals invalidate defence if X was sane at act.
5. **Intoxication**: Chronic effects may lead to recognized mental illnesses like **delirium tremens** (Holliday 1924 AD 250).
6. **Distinction**: Mental defect denotes permanently low intellect; mental illness is typically later onset and variable.

### 8.2.5 Psychological Leg of Test
- Focus: Determines whether mental illness impairs mental abilities.
- **Cognitive Faculty**: Ability to discern right from wrong (B(i)).
- **Conative Faculty**: Ability to act on that insight (B(ii)). This aspect is crucial for most cases.
- Lack of self-control need not stem from an "irresistible impulse", it can manifest gradually.
- **Case Reference**: **Kavin 1978 (2) SA 731 (W)** highlights gradual disintegration vs. impulsive actions.

### 8.2.6 Onus of Proof
- **Section 78(1A)**: Presumption against mental illness; burden of proving rests with the party alleging the illness.

### 8.2.7 Verdict
- If successful, court finds X **not guilty** due to mental illness or mental defect (s 78(6)).
- Possible orders:
  - Admission to treatment facility
  - Conditional discharge
  - Unconditional discharge
- Serious cases may trigger additional measures under the **Mental Health Care Act 17 of 2002**.

### 8.2.8 Diminished Responsibility or Capacity
- **Section 78(7)**: Recognizes diminished capacity due to mental illness; may influence sentencing.
- Criminal capacity exists but is diminished; psychological factors may mitigate punishment.

### 8.2.9 Mental Abnormality at the Time of Trial
- Distinction between:
  - Claim of mental abnormality at the commission of the act
  - Claim of mental abnormality at trial
- Courts cannot proceed with trial for a mentally abnormal defendant; procedural matters referred to in related courses.

##### 9.1 BACKGROUND
- **Culpability**: Based on criminal capacity and culpability forms (intention or negligence).

##### 9.2 THE TWO ELEMENTS OF INTENTION
- **Cognitive Element**:
  - Awareness of:
    - The act/nature.
    - Definitional elements.
    - Unlawfulness.
- **Conative Element**:
  - Willful direction toward a specific act/result. Transition from daydreaming to intentionality.

##### 9.3 DEFINITION OF INTENTION
- An action is intentional if:
  - The actor wills the act/result knowingly.
  - Awareness encompasses the act, its contextual definitional elements, and unlawfulness.

##### 9.4 THE DIFFERENT FORMS OF INTENTION
- Three forms: 
  - **Direct Intention** (dolus directus)
  - **Indirect Intention** (dolus indirectus)
  - **Dolus Eventualis**
- Intention suffices if any form is present; no single form is exclusive to crimes.

##### 9.4.1 DIRECT INTENTION (dolus directus)
- **Definition**: Act is executed with the aim of causing the forbidden result.

#### 9.4.2 Indirect intention (dolus indirectus)
- **Definition**: 
  - Person acts with indirect intention if the forbidden result is not the main aim but is foreseen as an inevitable consequence of achieving the main aim.

#### 9.4.3 Dolus eventualis
- **Definition**:
  - Act with dolus eventualis if:
    1. Subjectively foresees the possibility of the forbidden result.
    2. Reconciles with this possibility (often expressed as being reckless).
- Important keywords: **foresees**, **possibility**, **reconciles**.
  
- **Examples**:
  1. **Kewelram 1922 AD 213**: X sets fire to merchandise to claim insurance, foreseeing damage to the building.
  2. **Wilhelm Tell scenario**: X aims for an apple on Y's head, foresees the possibility of hitting Y instead. If he reconciles, he has dolus eventualis.
  3. **Jolly 1923 AD 176**: X sabotages a train, foresees potential deaths and reconciles with the risk.

- Court Considerations:
  - Foreseen possibility, and reconciled with it.
  - Example of **X shooting game**: foresees potential harm but does not reconcile leads to culpable homicide.
  - **Humphreys 2013 (2) SACR 1 (SCA)**: Court ruled X foresaw but did not reconcile, leading to a conviction for culpable homicide.

#### 9.5 The Test for Intention is Subjective
- **Subjective assessment**: Focus on the accused’s actual thoughts.
- Avoid using an objective reasonable person standard.
  
#### 9.6 Proof of Intention – Direct or Indirect
- **Direct evidence**: Admission of intent during confession.
- **Indirect proof**: Inferences drawn from conduct and circumstances.
  
- **Example**: Stabbing incident analyzed through behavior and circumstances leads to inferred intent.

#### 9.7 Knowledge, as an Element of Intention, Must Cover All Requirements of Crime
- **Intention elements**:
  - Knowledge must refer to:
    1. The act.
    2. Definitional circumstances.
    3. Unlawfulness.
- Example: **Common-law perjury** requires knowledge of making a false declaration in legal proceedings. Lacking awareness of any element negates intention.

- **Graphical concept**: Intention must relate to all crime elements.

### 9.8 Intention Directed at the Circumstances Included in the Definitional Elements
- Intention requires knowledge of circumstances included in the definitional elements.

- Knowledge of unlawfulness reflects X’s awareness that conduct is prohibited by law.

### 9.10 The Distinction Between Motive and Intention
- Intention must not be confused with motive (Peverett 1940 AD 213).
- Motive is immaterial to establishing intention; relevant for punishment severity.
- Intention exists regardless of whether X's motive is perceived as laudable (Hartmann 1975 (3) SA 532 (C)).
- Even without desire to cause an unlawful result, intention exists (Hibbert 1979 (4) SA 717 (D) 722).

## Chapter 10: Intention II: Mistake
### 10.1 Mistake Nullifies Intention
- Intention requires awareness of:
  - the act,
  - the definitional circumstances,
  - the unlawfulness.
- Absence of awareness/mistake negates intention.

### 10.2 Mistake Need Not Be Reasonable
- Determining a mistake’s existence is subjective regarding X’s mindset.
- Comparisons to reasonable person standards are not applicable, as the test for intention is inherently subjective (Sam 1980 (4) SA 289 (T)).
- Personal characteristics may influence judgment about intention.

### 10.3 Mistake Must Be Material
- Not all wrong impressions qualify as a defense against intention.
- Mistakes must pertain to critical elements of the crime—act, definitional elements, unlawfulness.
- Mistake about the object of an act (error in objecto) does not confer automatic reduction of liability.

**Chapter 10: Mistake and Aberratio Ictus in Criminal Law**

**10.1 Error in Objecto**  
- Error in objecto excludes intention when it relates to elements of the crime.  
- **Murder Elements:** Unlawful intentional causing of death of another person.  
- Example: Shooting a human while believing it's an animal (mistake excludes intention).  
- Negligence may still apply if a reasonable person would have foreseen the true object.  
- **Culpable Homicide:** May result from negligent acts.

**10.2 Material vs. Non-Material Mistakes**  
- **Material Mistake:** Concerns the crime's definitional elements. E.g., shooting someone thinking it’s a baboon (intention is absent).  
- **Non-Material Mistake:** Incorrect identity of the victim does not negate intention. E.g., intending to kill John but killing Peter instead (guilty of murder).  
- Mistakes about identity do not impact the intention regarding murder.

**10.4 Mistake Relating to the Chain of Causation**  
- Mistakes about how harm occurs can occur in materially defined crimes (e.g., murder).  
- **Examples:**  
  - A pushes a victim, intending drowning; victim dies differently (manner differs).  
  - A misses target; victim dies from unforeseen effects (shock).  
- **Goosen Case (1989):** Recognized that such mistakes could exclude intention if the causal chain differed materially from what was envisioned.  

**10.5 Aberratio Ictus Does Not Constitute a Mistake**  
- **Definition:** Aberratio ictus - missing the intended target due to lack of skill.  
- **Examples:**  
  - Bullet ricochets, hitting an unintended target.  
  - Javelin thrown at intended target strikes another individual.  
  - Poisoned apple eaten by a different person.

**10.5.2 Legal Approaches to Aberratio Ictus**  
- **Transferred Culpability Approach:** Intention to kill is transferred to the actual victim.  
- **Concrete-Figure Approach:** Intention evaluated based on whether the shooter foresaw the alternative victim being hit.  
- South African law favors the concrete-figure approach emphasizing subjective intention.

**10.5.4 Judging Aberratio Ictus Situations**  
- The court ruling in **Mtshiza 1970 (3) SA 747 (A)** supports the concrete-figure approach.  
- Guilt established regarding the intended victim (attempted murder). For the unintended victim:  
  - Guilt arises if intention to harm the actual victim was present (dolus eventualis).  
  - Absence of foresight regarding the unintended victim negates murder. 

**Legislation & Cases Referenced**  
- Goosen 1989 (4) SA 1013 (A)  
- Lungile 1999 (2) SACR 597 (A)  
- Bernardus 1965 (3) SA 287 (A)  
- Mtshiza 1970 (3) SA 747 (A)

### 10.5.4 Aberratio ictus

- **Definition**: Aberratio ictus refers to a situation where the injury occurs to a different person than the one intended.
- **Key Principle**: Intent (dolus) and negligence are pivotal in determining culpability.
- **Culpable Homicide**: If intention to kill is absent, and X should have foreseen the possibility of causing death, this leads to culpable homicide.

### 10.5.5 Aberratio ictus and error in objecto – factual situations

- **Distinction**: Aberratio ictus is not equated with error in objecto (mistake regarding the object).
- **Error in Objecto**: X believes she is targeting one object but is mistaken. This may not negate intent if the action fulfills definitional elements.

  1. X shoots thinking it is a horse but injures another horse – **error in objecto**.
  2. X mistakenly believes she is shooting at a horse but hits a donkey – **error in objecto**.
  3. X targets a horse but hits a human being – may negate murder if a reasonable person would foresee potential harm, leading to culpable homicide.
  4. X aims at a horse; the bullet hits Z behind it – **aberratio ictus**; court investigates for intention or negligence.
  5. X shoots at Y but hits Z – **aberratio ictus**; test for intention and negligence applies.
  6. X’s shot hits a car instead of a person – **aberratio ictus**; intent for property damage is assessed based on foreseeability.

- **Cases**: Tissen 1979 (4) SA 293 (T) and Raisa 1979 (4) SA 541 (O) illustrate the application of these principles.

### 10.6 Mistake Relating to Unlawfulness

- **General Principle**: Awareness of unlawfulness must inform X's intent.
- **Knowledge Requirement**: To establish culpability, X must recognize the unlawfulness (refer to **Campher 1987 (1) SA 940 (A)** and **Collett 1991 (2) SA 854 (A)**).

#### 10.6.1 Mistake Relating to a Ground of Justification

- **Putative Private Defence**: If X mistakenly believes she is acting in self-defense, the unlawful act may not constitute murder if lacking awareness of unlawfulness (e.g., **Joshua 2003 (1) SACR 1 (SCA)**).
- **Example**: X kills husband believing him to be a burglar; lack of awareness negates murder charge.

#### 10.6.2 Mistake of Law

- **General Insight**: Ignorance of the law is primarily not a defense.
- **Historical Context**: In South African law, prior to 1977, ignorance was not an excuse. In 1977, **De Blom 1977 (3) SA 513 (A)** changed this approach, holding ignorance of law could be a valid defense depending on circumstances.
- **Current Law**: If X’s lack of knowledge means she lacked dolus, this can nullify culpability.

### Conclusion

- In the application of laws regarding culpability, both intention and awareness of unlawfulness are key determinants. Distinguishing between types of mistakes is crucial in legal outcomes, particularly when addressing situations like aberratio ictus.

## 11.5 DISCUSSION OF THE DEFINITION OF NEGLIGENCE

### 11.5.1 Negligence: Result vs. Circumstance
- **Divisions of Crimes:** 
  - Formally defined crimes: forbids specific acts/omissions regardless of results.
  - Materially defined crimes: forbidden conduct that causes specific conditions/results.
- **Culpable Homicide:**
  - Defined as unlawful, negligent causing of another's death.
  - Only common-law crime requiring negligence, not intention.
- **Statutory Crimes:** 
  - Examples: Negligent driving (s 63(1) National Road Traffic Act 93 of 1996), Unlawful possession of firearms (s 3 Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000).
- **Focus on Result:** 
  - Discussion primarily on negligence causing a result; briefly address negligence related to circumstances later.

### 11.5.2 The Concept of the Reasonable Person
- **Definition:** 
  - Fictitious person representing objective standard of reasonable conduct.
- **Terminology:** 
  - Historically known as "reasonable man," replaced to reflect gender neutrality.
- **Characteristics:**
  - Ordinary, average person; not excessively cautious or reckless.
  - Objective criterion; subjective personal traits not considered.
  - May err in judgement; mere incorrect decisions aren't necessarily negligent.

### 11.5.3 Reasonable Foreseeability
- **Key Component of Negligence:**
  - Assesss whether a reasonable person foresaw the existence of the circumstance/result.
- **Foreseeability vs. Likelihood:** 
  - Must foresee possibility, not likelihood (Herschell v Mrupe 1954 (3) SA 464 (A) 471).
- **Contextual Assessment:** 
  - Evaluate negligence in light of current circumstances (example: heavy rain impacting visibility).
- **Culpable Homicide Focus:** 
  - Assess foreseeability of death as a result of conduct, not just injury (Bernardus 1965 (3) SA 287 (A) 296).

### 11.5.4 Taking Steps to Avoid Result
- **Second Leg of Negligence:**
  - Reasonable person would take steps to prevent known risks.
- **Not Always Applicable:** 
  - Distinction where risks are minor, costs of prevention high, or benefits unbalanced.

### 11.5.5 Differences Between X's Conduct and Reasonable Person
- **Conclusive Rule:**
  - Conduct is negligent if it varies from how a reasonable person would act or foresee.

### 11.5.6 Negligence Regarding Circumstance
- **Focus on Formally Defined Crimes:**
  - Example: *Mnisi 1996 (1) SACR 496 (T)* - negligent firearm possession requires proving reasonable foreseeability of possession circumstance.
  
## 11.6 SUBJECTIVE FACTORS 
- **General Rule:** 
  - Negligence determined by reasonable person standard; exceptions include:
  1. **Children:** 
     - Test adjusted to reasonable child standard.
  2. **Experts:** 
     - Evaluate based on reasonable expert standards (Van Schoor 1948 (4) SA 349 (C) 350).
  3. **Superior Knowledge:**
     - Courts consider X's superior knowledge exceeding that of a reasonable person (Mahlalela 1966 (1) SA 226 (A) 229).

## 2. The "lenient" and "unyielding" approach to voluntary intoxication

- **Two schools of thought**:
  - *Unyielding approach*: Intoxication cannot excuse criminal conduct; punished equally to sober individuals.
  - *Lenient approach*: Intoxication may result in lack of intention or capacity, possibly exempting from liability.
  
- **Legal history**:
  - Originally, voluntary intoxication was no defense, only mitigated punishment (unyielding approach).
  - Shift towards lenient approach began post-1981, notably after *Chretien 1981 (1) SA 1097 (A)*, leading to fears of too many acquittals, prompting 1988 legislation.

## 12.5 DEVELOPMENT OF THE DEFENCE OF VOLUNTARY INTOXICATION

### 12.5.1 The law before 1981

- Intoxication was never a complete defense, only possibly mitigating punishment.
- *Specific intent theory*: Crimes categorized into "specific intent" (e.g., murder) and "ordinary intent".
  - Intoxication could negate "specific intent," leading to conviction of lesser crimes (e.g., culpable homicide).
- **Criticism**: Assumes ability to form "ordinary intent" while lacking "specific intent".

### 12.5.2 The law after 1981 – the decision in Chretien and the rules enunciated therein

- *Chretien 1981 (1) SA 1097 (A)* altered legal perspective:
  - X drove intoxicated, causing injuries; acquitted due to lack of intent stemming from intoxication.
- **Key legal points from *Chretien*:
  1. Severe intoxication resulting in involuntary muscular movements negates liability.
  2. Excessive intoxication can entirely negate criminal capacity.
  3. Rejection of *specific intent theory*; intoxication can negate intent, impacting lesser crimes.
  4. Caution against lightly inferring intoxication as involuntary or lacking capacity due to implications for justice.

- Possible effects of intoxication:
  1. Exclusion of voluntary act.
  2. Exclusion of criminal capacity.
  3. Exclusion of intention.

### 12.5.3 The crime created in section 1 of Act 1 of 1988

1. **Reason for legislation**: React to *Chretien*'s allowance of intoxication as a complete defense, societal demand for accountability.
   
2. **Degrees of intoxication**:
   - *Category (a)*: Intoxication excludes intention but not capacity.
   - *Category (b)*: Intoxication excludes capacity but not voluntary action.
   - *Category (c)*: Intoxication leads to involuntary acts.

3. **Wording of section 1**:
   - Addresses liability when faculties impaired leading to unlawful acts while acknowledging intoxication.
   
4. **Effect of section 1 on Chretien**:
   - Section 1 addresses (b) and (c) categories, not (a).
   - *Chretien* can’t be convicted under section 1 due to retained defense concerning intention.

5. **Simplified version of contents of section**:
   - *Chretien* established intoxication can be a defense under categories (a), (b), (c).
   - Section 1 limits liability to categories (b) and (c) only, requiring provisions for convicting those falling under intoxication effects.

## 12.5 INTOXICATION AND CRIMINAL LIABILITY

- **Legal Principle**: If X commits an act amounting to a crime but is intoxicated and lacks criminal capacity, the court (Chretien judgment) must find X not guilty of the original crime but guilty of contravention of section 1(1) of Act 1 of 1988.
- **Implication**: Court can impose the same punishment for contravention as if convicted of the original crime, preventing unpunished exit.

### 12.5.1 Requirements of Statutory Crime (Section 1(1))

- **Group A - Consumption Circumstances**:
  - A1: Consumption by X
  - A2: "Any substance" that impairs faculties
  - A3: Impairment leading to lack of criminal capacity
  - A4: X knows the substance has such effects

- **Group B - Act Commission Circumstances**:
  - B1: Commission of a prohibited act under penalty
  - B2: Lack of criminal capacity at the time of commission
  - B3: Absence of criminal liability due to lack of capacity
  
### 12.6 Discussion of Elements

- Applies to impairment from both alcohol and drugs (any substance).
- Conviction requires intent to consume with knowledge of effects (Element A4).
- Assumed voluntary consumption is necessary for application.
- Unique aspect: state must prove lack of criminal capacity for section 1(1) when generally it must prove presence of capacity.
- **Case Example**: Maarohanye may not be convicted under section 1 if original charge lacks proven lack of capacity (attempted murder acquittal).

### 12.6.1 Key Case - Ramdass 2017 (1) SACR 30 (KZD)

- X, intoxicated at Y's death.
- Reasonable doubt existed regarding X's criminal capacity; thus, acquittance of murder and potential conviction for contravention was analyzed.
- Prosecution challenges in proving lack of capacity while acquitted.
  
## 12.7 INTOXICATION AND CULPABLE HOMICIDE

- X can be acquitted of murder due to intoxication but found guilty of culpable homicide if criminal capacity exists and negligence is proven (objective standard).

## 12.8 EFFECT OF INTOXICATION ON PUNISHMENT

- Intoxication can yield leniency in sentencing if guilty of the charged crime, but not applicable where intoxication is an element (e.g., driving under influence).
- Can aggravate punishment under certain circumstances of prior knowledge of intoxication-induced aggression.

## 12.9 PROVOCATION AND CRIMINAL LIABILITY

- Provocation does not excuse murder but can mitigate punishment; reduces negligence in culpable homicide only if a reasonable person would similarly react.
- Does not permit lesser offense in cases of assault with intent to grievous bodily harm or complete acquittal in common assault.

### GLOSSARY

- **Actio libera in causa**: Intentionally consuming substances to have courage for committing a crime.

### SUMMARY OF INTOXICATION'S EFFECT ON CRIMINAL LIABILITY

- **Automatism due to Intoxication**: Not guilty of the charged crime, must be convicted under section 1 of Act 1 of 1988.
- **Lack of Criminal Capacity**: Same as above.
- **Lack of Intention**: Not guilty of original charge; potential culpable homicide conviction exists only if criminal capacity is found.

### 14.1 BACKGROUND  
- Criminals often act with accomplices; the focus is on multilateral participation in crime.  
- E.g., in a robbery scenario (X as the robber; Z as the lookout), can Z be liable?  

### 14.2 INTRODUCTION  
#### 14.2.1 Classification of Persons Involved in a Crime  
- "Persons involved in a crime" is a collective term; not a legal classification.  
- Participants: those who further the crime.  
- Non-participants: do not aid the crime; e.g., accessories after the fact, who assist after the crime is committed.  

#### 14.2.2 Definitions of a Perpetrator and an Accomplice  
- **Perpetrator:**  
  1. Complies with all liability requirements of the crime's definition, OR  
  2. Acted with others; liability imputed via common purpose.  
- **Accomplice:**  
  1. Does not meet all liability requirements, AND  
  2. Does not have conduct imputed via common purpose but unlawfully furthers another's crime.  

#### 14.2.3 Distinction Between Perpetrator and Accomplice Explained  
- To classify someone as a perpetrator, assess crime definition and the person's actions, state of mind, and characteristics.  
- **Example:** Murder defined as unlawfully and intentionally causing death.  

### 14.3 PERPETRATORS  
#### 14.3.1 Co-perpetrators: Unnecessary to Identify Principal Perpetrator  
- No legal rule limits to one principal perpetrator; all may be liable.  
- Co-perpetrators: each acts in compliance with crime’s definition.  
- Example: A and B can both be liable for murder regardless of their individual contributions.  

#### 14.3.2 Co-perpetrators: Difference Between Direct and Indirect Perpetrator Irrelevant  
- **Direct Perpetrator:** Commits the crime; **Indirect Perpetrator:** Uses another to commit the crime.  
- Liability for murder remains equivalent; both direct and indirect perpetrators equally liable.

#### 14.3.3 Being a Perpetrator of Murder in Terms of General Principles of Liability  
- Liability established through general principles; all involved must meet the definition of crime.  
- Example: X1 (shooter), X2 (lookout), X3 (driver) all liable if they further the murder.

#### 14.3.4 Being a Perpetrator of Murder by Virtue of the Doctrine of Common Purpose  
- Common purpose allows imputation of liability; actions of one can impose liability on others involved.

#### 14.3.5 Joining In  
- Participation can occur at various stages; assessing the actions at the moment of joining in is crucial for liability.  

#### 14.3.6 Principles Relating to Common Purpose  
- Key principles that govern how participants can be held jointly liable in the criminal act.  

### Summary  
- Understanding participation in crime involves clear definitions and distinctions between types of involvement.

**14.3.4 Being a perpetrator of murder by virtue of the doctrine of common purpose**

*820 (Criminal Law 232-233; Reader 114-142)*

---

**14.3.4.1 General**

- Causal contribution to murder by multiple people is challenging to establish.
- Doctrine of common purpose facilitates convictions when causation is indeterminate.
- The underlying principle: Acts of each participant in a shared criminal purpose are imputed to others.
- Liability based on shared intention; individual actions do not need direct causation to death.
- Reference: *Malinga 1963 (1) SA 692 (A) 694*.
- Preference for using general liability rules when possible to avoid inequitable convictions.

---

**14.3.4.2 Proof of existence of common purpose**

- Proven by:
  - Express or implied prior agreement (difficult to establish as most conspiracies are secret).
  - Active participation in a common criminal design, as seen in *Thebus 2003 (2) SACR 319 (CC) 336*.

---

**14.3.4.3 Necessity of Common Purpose Doctrine**

- Essential to hold participants accountable even when individual actions cannot be definitively linked to a crime.
- Example: A group throws stones at a victim, making causation hard to establish.
- Without this doctrine, collective criminal behavior would evade justice.

---

**14.3.4.4 Judgment in Safatsa**

- Reference: *Safatsa 1988 (1) SA 868 (A)* - key case illustrating the doctrine.
- Appellants participated in a communal attack culminating in murder.
- Court ruling affirmed conviction based on common purpose sans causal links needed.

---

**14.3.4.5 Active association as proof of participation**

- Active association can substantiate common purpose.
- Requirements for conviction without agreement:
  - Present at crime scene.
  - Awareness of the attack.
  - Intent to join in.
  - Manifested action supporting the group.
  - Intention to kill or contribute to death.
- Passive spectators are not liable (*Motaung 1990 (4) SA 485 (A)).

---

**14.3.4.6 Liability on the basis of active association - constitutional validity**

- Reference: *Thebus 2003 (2) SACR 319 (CC)* challenged active association liability.
- Arguments on rights to dignity, freedom, and fair trial rejected.
- Court supports the doctrine for its effectiveness in addressing collective criminality.

---

**14.3.4.7 Common purpose and dolus eventualis**

- Common purpose doesn't require direct intention to kill; *dolus eventualis* suffices.
- Requires foresight of death possibilities; case specifics dictate the presence of intent.
- Example case: *Mambo 2006 (2) SACR 563 (SCA)* - established that shared intent in robbery does not automatically infer intent to kill.
- *Molimi 2006 (2) SACR 8 (SCA)* emphasizes non-imputable conduct diverging from common purpose.

### 14.3.4.7 Principles of Common Purpose
- **Dolus Directus and Dolus Eventualis**  
  - Direct intent: knew actions would occur.  
  - Foreseeing potential actions and accepting that possibility (eventual intent).

- **Case Summary: X1, X2, and Z's Robbery**  
  - Co-conspirators planned a robbery (Clicks store) with information from X1.  
  - Events escalated to violence with fatal consequences (security guard and hostage murdered).  
  - X1, X2, and Z were convicted on seven counts, including murder and robbery.

- **Supreme Court of Appeal Ruling**  
  - Common purpose acknowledged.  
  - Foreseeability of criminal acts: attempted murder of the store employee was foreseeable (dolus eventualis).  
  - Kidnapping and murder of the hostage not foreseeable; X1 and X2 acquitted on these charges.  

---

### 14.3.4.8 Dissociation from the Common Purpose
1. **Withdrawal Requirements**  
   - Clear intention necessary for effective withdrawal.  
   - Motivation must not stem from fear or escape.

2. **Positive Act Required**  
   - Mere passivity does not equal withdrawal link.  

3. **Factors Determining Effective Withdrawal**  
   - Participation extent, crime progress, disengagement timing, steps taken to prevent crime.  

4. **Timing of Withdrawal**  
   - Must occur before "execution commencement" to be valid.

5. **Voluntary Nature of Withdrawal**  
   - Must be voluntary for negative liability.

---

### 14.3.5 Joining In
- **Definition**: Individual joining the assault after lethal injury inflicted on the victim (Z's actions post-X’s wound).

- **Key Points**:  
  - If Z's actions hastened Y's death, he is guilty of murder; if not, he's a joiner-in.  
  - Joining-in is not applicable if Y is already dead or if there’s prior conspiracy.

- **Case Reference**: *Motaung 1990 (4) SA 485 (A)*  
  - Joiner-in cannot be convicted of murder; only attempted murder is applicable as per retrospective liability principles.

---

### 14.3.6 Important Principles Related to Common Purpose
1. **Common Purpose Definition**  
   - Acts performed under common purpose are imputed to co-participants.

2. **Causation in Crime**  
   - Imputed conduct includes causing a result.

3. **Variations in Conduct**  
   - Unforeseen actions by group members not imputed unless known or foreseen.

4. **Proof of Association**  
   - Evidence of prior agreement or active involvement validates common purpose.

5. **Timing for Liability**  
   - Active association for murder claims must occur before fatal injuries.

6. **Dissociation Impact**  
   - Withdrawal can lead to negative liability under certain conditions. 

---

### Summary
1. **Participant vs. Non-participant**:  
   - Participants further crime; non-participants do not (accessories post factum).

2. **Perpetrators vs. Accomplices**:  
   - Perpetrators fulfill crime definition; accomplices further crimes without fulfilling the definition.

3. **Perpetrator Grounds**:  
   - Defined by legal causation or common purpose.

4. **Common Purpose and Joiner-in**:  
   - Refer to principles listed in 14.3.6 for liability regarding joiner-ins.

## Chapter 15: Accomplices and Accessories After the Fact

### 15.3 Accessories After the Fact

- If multiple individuals dispose of a corpse, all can be accessories after the fact.
- Those who did not commit the murder but assisted in the aftermath are also accessories.
- **Case Reference**: *Jonathan* 1987 (1) SA 633 (A) upheld the "rule in Gani's case," acknowledging exceptions to general accessory rules.

#### 15.3.4 Reason for Existence Questionable

- Accessory after the fact overlaps with obstructing justice.
- The necessity of the crime is debatable; both terms can often describe similar behaviors.
- **Case Reference**: See *Gani* supra 220A; *Pakane* 2008 (1) SACR 518 (SCA).

### Summary

1. Definition of accomplice established in previous units.
2. Conduct of an accomplice is to further another's crime (includes assistance, advice, encouragement).
3. Accomplice must further crime unlawfully and intentionally.
4. Accomplice requires presence of a perpetrator.
5. Accessory after the fact does not further the crime; provides post-crime assistance.
6. Liability as an accessory to the act must be intentional aid to a perpetrator.
7. Accessory liability is secondary, relying on another's primary crime.
8. Gani's case presents an exception where one can't be an accessory after the fact to their own crime.

## Chapter 16: Attempt, Conspiracy, and Incitement

### 16.1 Background

- Individuals can be guilty of crimes not completed: attempts, conspiracies, and incitements.
- These forms are known as inchoate or anticipatory crimes, aimed at preventing completed crimes.
- Law punishes anticipatory actions to maintain order and enable police intervention against crime preparations.

### 16.2 Attempt

#### 16.2.1 General

- Attempts to commit common-law crimes are punishable by common law.
- Section 15(1) of Act 27 of 1914, replaced by s 18(1) of Act 17 of 1956, confirms that attempts at statutory crimes are also punishable.

#### 16.2.2 Definition of Rules Relating to Attempt

1. Liability arises if conduct, intended to commit a crime, unlawfully reaches execution commencement.
2. Attempt is punishable even if impossible, given belief in factual circumstances.

#### 16.2.3 Four Different Types of Attempt

1. **Completed Attempt**: Intention and actions complete, but crime not achieved (e.g., missed gunshot).
2. **Interrupted Attempt**: Actions executed but interrupted (e.g., preparing to commit arson).
3. **Attempt to Commit the Impossible**: Actions cannot result in desired outcome (e.g., mistaken belief in poison).
4. **Voluntary Withdrawal**: Actions taken, but decision to cease before completion (e.g., discarding poisoned food).

### 16.2.4 Completed Attempt

- Generally, guilty if actions are fully executed intending to commit the crime but fail.
- **Examples**: Firing at someone and missing; submitting an abusive letter but intercepted.

### 16.2.5 Interrupted Attempt

- Majority of reported cases involve this type, where distinguishing whether actions constitute a punishable attempt can be complex.

## 16.2.5 Attempt
### 16.2.5.1 General Principles of Attempt
- Mere intention to commit a crime is not punishable.
- Liability arises from committing an act (outward conduct).
- Not all outward conduct qualifies as a punishable attempt.
- Distinction between stages of conduct:
  - **Stage 1**: Acts of preparation (no liability; e.g., buying a knife).
  - **Stage 2**: Acts of execution/consummation (guilty; e.g., attacking with a knife).
  - **Stage 3**: Completed crime (guilty of the crime).

### 16.2.5.2 Rule for Interrupted Attempt
- Courts distinguish:
  - **Acts of preparation** (no attempt).
  - **Acts of execution** (guilty of attempt).
- Application is complex due to vagueness of "preparation" and "consummation".

### 16.2.5.3 Case Law Examples
- **Sharpe 1903 TS 868**: Confirmed differentiation between acts of preparation and consummation.
- **Schoombie 1945 AD 541**: Confirmed conviction of attempted arson despite interruption.
- **Croucamp 1949 (1) SA 377 (A)**: Preparation without attempt (inspecting stolen goods).

## 16.2.6 Attempt to Commit the Impossible
### 16.2.6.1 Approaches
- **Objective Test**: Focus on facts (X not guilty if impossible to commit).
- **Subjective Test**: Consider X's state of mind (X guilty based on belief).

### 16.2.6.2 Davies Case
- **Davies 1956 (3) SA 52 (A)**: Established subjective test; X guilty of attempted abortion despite the foetus being dead.
- Legal impossibility and subjective belief determine liability in impossible attempts.

### 16.2.6.3 "Putative Crimes"
- Mistakes of law (e.g., believing adultery is a crime) result in no punishable attempt.
- **Key Distinction**: Davies rule applies to factual mistakes, not legal ones.

## 16.2.7 Voluntary Withdrawal
- No punishable attempt if withdrawal occurs before commencement of consummation.
- **Hlatwayo 1933 TPD 441**: Conviction for attempted murder despite change of mind post-commencement.
- Courts consistently rule change of heart after commencement does not exclude liability.

## 16.2.8 Intention
- Necessary for liability; presence of intention, including dolus eventualis.
- **S v Nyalungu 2013 2 SACR 99 (T)**: Convicted based on intention to transmit HIV despite extraneous factors.
- **Culpable Homicide**: No attempt possible (negligence vs. intention).

### Consideration of Forms of Attempt
- Examples of ineffective attempts:
  - Attempting to steal from an empty pocket.
  - Attempting to break into a safe with a broken screwdriver.

### 16.3 Conspiracy
- **Statutory Crime**:  
  - Governed by **Section 18(2)(a) of Riotous Assemblies Act 17 of 1956**.  
  - Criminalizes conspiracy to commit any crime.

- **Definition**:  
  - Agreement between two or more persons to commit a crime (Moumbaris 1974 (1) SA 681 (T)).

- **Key Points**:  
  - Completed upon agreement; no proof of further steps required (Alexander 1965 (2) SA 818 (C)).  
  - May be tacit or express (B 1956 (3) SA 363 (EC)).  
  - Parties need not agree on the exact method (Adams 1959 (1) SA 646 (Sp C)).  
  - A "meeting of the minds" required (Cooper 1976 (2) SA 875 (T)).  
  - Communication not required; joining organization implies conspiracy if aware of unlawful aims (Moumbaris supra 687).

- **Intention**:  
  - Includes intention to conspire and to commit a crime.

- **Punishment**:  
  - Same as for actual crime; generally, less severe (s 18(2)(a)).

---

### 16.4 Incitement
- **Statutory Crime**:  
  - Governed by **Section 18(2)(b) of Riotous Assemblies Act 17 of 1956**.  
  - Criminalizes incitement to commit crimes.

- **Definition**:  
  - Inciting, instigating, or procuring another to commit an offense.

- **Key Points**:  
  - Charged only if the actual crime was not committed (Khoza 1973 (4) SA 23 (O)).  
  - No requirement for persuasion; incitement can occur even in cases of police traps (Nkosiyana 1966 (4) SA 655 (A)).  
  - Conduct of inciter is primary, not capability of persuasion by the incitee.  
  - Incitement can be explicit or implicit.  
  - If not known to incitee, may lead to attempt of incitement.

- **Punishment**:  
  - Same as for the actual crime; generally, less severe (s 18(2)(b)).

---

- **Forms**:
  - Completed attempt.

  - Voluntary withdrawal.
- **Intention Requirement**: Necessary for conviction.

#### Conspiracy
- **Definition**: Agreement to commit a crime.
- **Completion**: Upon agreement.
- **Intention**: To conspire and further the crime.

#### Incitement
- **Definition**: Encouraging another to commit a crime.
- **Charge**: Only if actual crime not committed.
- **Persuasion**: Not required for conviction.

## 979 Not Guilty* of Crime Charged, But Guilty of Contravening s 1 of Act 1 of 1998
- **Legal Principle:** A defendant may be found guilty of a lesser offense under specific statutory provisions even if not guilty of the primary charge.

- **General Rule:** Intoxication may negate capacity to form intent but does not absolve liability for all actions.

## 981 If Charged with Crime Requiring Intent: Result or Circumstances Not Foreseen
- **Legal Outcome:** If intent cannot be established due to unforeseen results, considerations for liability may shift.

- **Implication of Mistake:** A reasonable mistake can negate the requisite intent for a crime.
