## LEARNING UNIT 1: Crimes against the state
### 1.1 BACKGROUND
- Common-law crimes: High treason, sedition.
- Limited focus on **terrorism** and **public violence** due to their prevalence.

### 1.2 TERRORISM
#### 1.2.1 Introduction
- Statutory offence under **Protection of Constitutional Democracy against Terrorist and Related Activities Act 33 of 2004**.
- Objectives: Prevent terror acts, protect civilians, bring terrorists to justice, prohibit South Africa as a haven for terrorism, and international cooperation against terrorism.

#### 1.2.2 Definition
- **Terrorist activities** defined broadly in **section 1** of the Act, including:
  - Violence by any means.
  - Exposure to harmful substances.
  - Endangerment or serious bodily harm causing death.
  - Risk to public health or safety.
  - Destruction of property or environmental damage.
  - Disruption of essential services/system.
  - Major economic loss.
  - Creation of public emergencies or insurrections.

- **Case Reference**: *S v Okah [2018] ZACC 3* showcases extraterritorial harm related to terrorism.

#### 1.2.3 Elements of the crime
- **Act**: Specific conduct defined by the Act.
- **Unlawfulness**: Conduct must be unlawful.
- **Specific Intention**: 
  - Aimed at threatening national unity/territorial integrity.
  - Causing public insecurity or terror.
  - Compelling or intimidating individuals/government/public.

- Purpose: Advancement of political, religious, ideological, or philosophical motives.
- Knowledge of core definition elements is vital; memorization of exhaustive details is unnecessary.

### 1.3 PUBLIC VIOLENCE
#### 1.3.1 Definition
- Defined in terms of collective unlawful acts causing public disorder.

#### 1.3.2 Elements of the crime
- Requires unlawful conduct in a collective context.
  
#### 1.3.3 The object or interest protected
- Protects public order and safety.

#### 1.3.4 Joint action
- Requires participation in concerted conduct by multiple persons.

#### 1.3.5 Examples of conduct
- Rioting, mob violence.

#### 1.3.6 Serious proportions
- Must involve significant disturbance or danger to the public.

#### 1.3.7 Unlawfulness
- Conduct must violate legal norms.

#### 1.3.8 Intent
- Requires culpable intent connected to the collective action.

# PART A: CRIMES AGAINST THE STATE AND THE ADMINISTRATION OF JUSTICE

## 1.2 TERRORISM

### 1.2.4 The Object or Interest Protected
- Safety and security of the Republic, its institutions, and people.

### 1.2.5 The Act
- Prohibited conduct includes:
  - Use of violence
  - Release of toxic substances
  - Endangering life/physical integrity
  - Serious health and safety risks to the public
  - Property damage
  - Serious interference with services
  - Economic destabilization
  - Creation of public emergencies or insurgencies

### 1.2.6 Unlawfulness
- Compulsion may justify an act.
- Acts forming part of protests/dissent, without intent to harm, are excluded (Constitution ss 10, 23(2)(c)).

### 1.2.7 Intention
- Requires specific intention with two components:
  - **First Component**:
    - Threaten the Republic's unity and territorial integrity.
    - Intimidate the public or segments regarding security.
    - Unduly compel or coerce action/inaction from entities.
  - **Second Component**:
    - Act must advance a political, religious, ideological, or philosophical motive.
- **Dolus eventualis** is a sufficient form of intention.
- Objective test applies for inferring intention from conduct and circumstances.

### Additional Offences (s 11-13)
- Harbouring/concealment of terrorists (s 11).
- Failure to report suspected terrorists (s 12).
- "Hoaxes" related offences (s 13).

## 1.3 PUBLIC VIOLENCE

### 1.3.1 Definition
- Unlawful, intentional act by multiple persons intended to disturb public peace/order violently.

### 1.3.2 Elements of the Crime
1. An act
2. Performed by a number of persons
3. Assumes serious proportions
4. Unlawful
5. Intentional, focused on disturbing peace or infringing rights

### 1.3.3 Object or Interest Protected
- Protection of public peace and order.

### 1.3.4 Joint Action
- Committed by multiple persons acting in concert.
- Number varies with context; intent must be collective.

### 1.3.5 Examples of Conduct
- Faction fights, resistance to police, rioting, coercion of workers, disruption of meetings.

### 1.3.6 Serious Proportions
- Disturbance must threaten safety of non-participants.
- Factors include:
  - Number involved
  - Time, place, duration
  - Cause, status of participants
  - Use of weapons, injuries, damage

### 1.3.7 Unlawfulness
- Actions must be unlawful; participation may be justified under specific circumstances (e.g., compulsion, private defense).

### 1.3.8 Intent
- Must be aware of collective action's nature and participate intentionally.

## SUMMARY
- **Terrorism**: Involves violence, endangerment, economic destabilization, unlawful acts with specific intent (two components).
- **Public Violence**: Joint action that disturbs peace, significant intent required, no necessity for prior planning.

- **Protected Interest:** Public peace and order.
- **Group Requirement:** Must involve multiple people acting with a common purpose.
- **Violence Requirement:** Acts must have violence or threats of violence.

## Learning Unit 2: Crimes against the Administration of Justice

### 2.1 Perjury at Common Law
#### 2.1.1 Definition
- Unlawful, intentional false declaration made under oath during legal proceedings.

#### 2.1.2 Elements of the Crime
- **Declaration Made**
- **False Declaration**
- **Under Oath or Equivalent**
- **In Legal Proceedings**
- **Unlawful Nature**
- **Intentionality**

#### 2.1.3 False Declaration
- **Objectively False:** Requires objective falsity, unlike English law which allows subjective falsity.
- **Form:** Can be oral or written (affidavit).
- **Express or Implied:** Implied falsehoods must be a necessary implication based on trial evidence (e.g., *Vallabh (1911)*).

#### 2.1.4 Under Oath
- Must be under oath or equivalent form (affirmation or warning).
- Relevant provisions: Sections 162-164 of the Criminal Procedure Act.
- **Authority Requirement:** Oath administrant must have the authority.

#### 2.1.5 In the Course of a Legal Proceeding
- **Legal Context:** False declaration must be made during a legal proceeding.
- **Admin. Tribunal Exclusion:** Extrajudicial statements not count as perjury (*Carse (1967)*).
- **Prospective Use:** Statements must foresee potential use in trials (*Beukman (1950)*).

#### 2.1.6 Unlawfulness
- Acknowledgement of falsity post-declaration not an excuse.

#### 2.1.7 Intent
- Must know or foresee falsehood; negligence is insufficient (*Mokwena (1984)*).

### 2.2 Statutory Perjury
#### 2.2.1 Definition
- Making two conflicting declarations under separate oaths or equivalents.

#### 2.2.2 Elements of the Crime
- **Two Declarations**
- **Conflicting Nature**
- **Under Two Different Oaths or Equivalents**
- **Unlawful and Intentional Manner**

#### 2.2.3 Content of Section
- Criminal Procedure Act Section 319(3) outlines the crime and conditions for conviction without proving which statement was false.

#### 2.2.4 Two Statements under Oath 
- **Form Irrelevance:** Oral or written; legal proceeding context not mandatory.
- **Substitutes for Oath Permissible:** Includes affirmations.

### 2.3 Defeating or Obstructing the Course of Justice
- **Definition:** Actions meant to disrupt legal processes.
- **Elements:** Must involve unlawful interference with judicial functions.
- **Examples of Conduct:** E.g., bribery, false testimony.
- **Intention Requirement:** Must intend to obstruct or defeat justice.

### 2.4 Contempt of Court
#### 2.4.1 Definition
- Actions that disrespect the authority or dignity of the court.

#### 2.4.2 Elements of the Crime
- Must involve conduct that undermines court processes.

#### 2.4.3 Unusual Characteristics
- Distinctive in nature as it addresses court authority.

#### 2.4.4 Reasons for Existence
- Maintain order and respect for legal processes.

#### 2.4.5 Acts
- Includes disruptive behavior during proceedings.

#### 2.4.6 Unlawfulness
- Conduct must be unlawful to constitute contempt.

#### 2.4.7 Intent
- Must intend to undermine court authority.

#### 2.4.8 Administration of Justice by Courts
- Key for upholding the judicial system's integrity.
  
#### 2.4.9 Examples of the Crime
- **Contempt in Facie Curiae:** Actions within the presence of the court.
- **Contempt Ex Facie Curiae:** Actions outside immediate court proceedings.

# LEARNING UNIT 2: Crimes Against the Administration of Justice

## 2.2 PERJURY
### 2.2.5 The Statements Conflict with Each Other
- State must prove conflict between statements (Mahomed supra 442).
- In **Ramdas 1994 (2) SACR 37 (A)**, clear conflict required; statements must be mutually exclusive.
- Distinction: 
  - **Perjury**: ONE false declaration under oath in legal proceeding.
  - **Statutory Perjury**: TWO conflicting statements under oath on TWO occasions. 
  
### 2.2.6 Onus on Accused
- Accused must prove belief in the truth of each statement on a balance of probability.
- This reverse onus may be unconstitutional, conflicting with **section 35(3)(h) of the Constitution**.

## 2.3 DEFEATING OR OBSTRUCTING THE COURSE OF JUSTICE
### 2.3.1 Definition
- Crime consists of unlawful and intentional conduct defeating or obstructing the administration of justice.

### 2.3.2 Elements of the Crime
- (1) Any act,
- (2) Defeats or obstructs justice,
- (3) Unlawfully,
- (4) Intentionally.

### 2.3.3 Defeating or Obstructing Justice
- Overlaps with other crimes (e.g., contempt of court, perjury).
- Important to differentiate "defeating" (requires proven defeat of justice) from "obstructing".

### 2.3.4 Examples of Conduct
- Unlawful inducement of false testimony,
- Refusal to testify,
- Providing false information to authorities,
- Tampering with court documents.
- Example: **Naidoo 1977 2 SA 123 (N)** (flashing lights warning of speed traps).

### 2.3.5 Possibility of Court Case Ensuing
- Crime can occur without a pending case if a real court case could result (e.g., false police complaints).

### 2.3.6 Intention
- Accused must foresee the potential for their actions to obstruct justice.

### 2.3.7 Attempt to Defeat or Obstruct Justice
- False information intended to mislead authorities can be charged as an attempt, even if not acted upon.

## 2.4 CONTEMPT OF COURT
### 2.4.1 Definition
- Unlawful and intentional violation of judicial dignity or publication intending to influence pending judicial proceedings.

### 2.4.2 Elements of the Crime
- (1) Violation of judicial dignity or publication concerning proceedings,
- (2) Unlawful,
- (3) Intentional.

### 2.4.3 Unusual Characteristics
- Acts can vary significantly and some may even be treated civilly, dependent on prosecution.

### 2.4.4 Reasons for the Existence of the Crime
- Aimed at maintaining the integrity of the judicial system (Van Niekerk 1972 (3) SA 711 (A) 720).

### 2.4.5 Acts
- **Contempt in Facie Curiae**: Direct acts in court.
  - Magistrates can convict summarily (Ngcemu 1964 (3) SA 665 (N)).
- **Contempt Ex Facie Curiae**: Actions outside the court's presence (e.g., scandalizing the court).

### 2.4.6 Unlawfulness
- Privileged statements by Legislative Assembly members do not constitute contempt.
- Fair comment on judicial outcomes is permissible; public debate is encouraged.

### 2.4.7 Intent
- Generally requires intention, but negligence applicable in media contexts involving pending cases.

### 2.4.8 Administration of Justice
- Offending language must target judicial officers or court integrity, not administrative actions.

### 2.4.9 Examples of the Crime
#### 2.4.9.1 Contempt in Facie Curiae
- E.g., shouting, acting inebriated, loud conversations.
- Must establish intent; trivial breaches may not suffice (e.g., **Nyalanbisa 1993 (1) SACR 172 (Tk)** ).

## LEARNING UNIT 2: Crimes Against the Administration of Justice

### 2.4.9.2 Contempt ex facie curiae

- **Definition**: Conduct constituting contempt ex facie curiae occurs outside the court and includes:
  - Pretending to be an officer of the court (e.g., advocate, attorney).
  - Intentionally obstructing an officer of the court (e.g., a messenger).
  - Bribing or attempting to bribe judicial officers, legal representatives, or witnesses.
  - Deliberately failing to appear as a summoned witness.
  - Publishing commentary on pending cases.
  - Scandalizing the court.
  - Failing to comply with a court order.

#### Commentary on Pending Cases
- **Definition**: Publishing information or commentary that may influence the outcome of a case still sub iudice.
- **Press Restrictions**: Legal to report trial evidence but not outside information or personal opinions until case concluded.
- **Reason**: Prevent "trial by newspaper"; judges should not be swayed by external commentary.
- **Sub iudice Duration**: From commencement (summons/arrest) to final judicial conclusion, including appeals.
- **Influence Test**: Wide; includes publications that may influence even if not directly received or believed by the court (*Van Niekerk 1972 (3) SA 711 (A) 724*).

### 2.4.9.3 Modern Applicability
- **Emerging Issues**: Challenges to contempt ex facie curiae in light of modern media environment.
- **Case Example**: 
  - *S v Pistorius (CC113/2013) [2014] ZAGPPHC 793 (12 September 2014)* decision on broadcasting trials balanced constitutional rights.
  
#### Liability of a Newspaper Editor
- **Legal Information**: Must not prejudice the administration of justice.
- **Definition of Unlawful Publication**: Publication causing real risk of substantial prejudice.
- **Case Reference**: *Midi Television (Pty) Ltd t/a E-TV v Director of Public Prosecutions (Western Cape) 2007 (2) SACR 493 (SCA)* - press freedom can cause justice prejudice.

#### Culpability in Publications
- **Editor vs. Reporter**: 
  - Editor (Z): Negligence sufficient for criminal liability (*Harber 1988 (3) SA 396 (A)*).
  - Reporter (X): Requires proof of intention.
  
#### Notable Exceptions
- Only two forms of culpability in common law don't require intent: contempt and culpable homicide.

### 2.4.10 Scandalising the Court
- **Definition**: Publication likely to discredit judges or the judicial system.
- **Relevance of Evidence**: Actual disrepute of the administration of justice not required; mere tendency suffices.
- **Case Reference**: *S v Moila 2005 (2) SACR 517 (T)* - convictions upheld based on malice.

### 2.4.11 Failure to Comply with a Court Order
- **Definition**: Deliberate failure to comply leads to contempt.
- **Common Practice**: Typically, not criminally prosecuted; civil applications favored.
- **Applicable Cases**: 
  - *Knott v Tuck 1968 (2) SA 495 (D)* - civil contempt.
  - *Beyers 1968 (3) SA 70 (A)* - Attorney general may charge non-compliance.
- **Intent Requirement**: Essential—showing deliberate action against court orders (*S v Samuels 2016 (2) SACR 298 (WCC)*).

---

### Summary
1. **Perjury**: Objective false declaration, must be under oath and within legal proceedings.
2. **Defeating/Obstructing Justice**: Distinction made between defeat and obstruction; not required to have a pending case.
3. **Contempt of Court**: Subcategories include in facie curiae and ex facie curiae with varying culpability standards based on the offender (intention vs. negligence).
4. **Scandalising Court**: Involves any allegations capable of damaging the judge or judicial system.
5. **Compliance with Court Orders**: Noncompliance is criminal when done with intent, even in civil proceedings.

## 3.2 Corruption (Criminal Law 401–417)

### 3.2.1 General
- **Corruption** undermines moral values and trust in authorities, fostering organized crime.  
- Originated as "bribery" (common law), now governed by:  
  - **Corruption Act 94 of 1992** (replaced in 2004 by)  
  - **Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004**  

### 3.2.2 General and Specific Crimes of Corruption
- **2004 Act** establishes:  
  - A broad general offence   
  - Specific criminalized corrupt activities  
- Legislative style: repetition of elements of general offence in specific offences to aid applicability.

### 3.2.3 General Crime of Corruption: Definition in the Act
- **Section 3** outlines general offence:  
  - Acceptance or offering of gratification (benefit to oneself or another)  
  - Inducement to act in a manner that is:  
    - Illegal, dishonest, unauthorized, or biased  
    - Abusive of authority or breach of trust  
    - Aimed at achieving unjustified results  

### 3.2.4 Shortened Definition of the General Crime of Corruption
- Simplified elements:  
  - Accepting or giving gratification to act illegally.

### 3.2.5 Corruption by Giver and Recipient
- Distinction:  
  - **Corruption by Giver (X)**: Offers gratification  
  - **Corruption by Recipient (Y)**: Accepts gratification  
- Both parties commit corruption; applicable definitions differentiate conduct of giver and recipient.  

### 3.2.6 General Crime of Corruption: Crime Committed by the Recipient
#### 3.2.6.1 Elements of the Crime
1. Acceptance by Y  
2. Of gratification  
3. Induced action  
4. Unlawfulness  
5. Intention  

#### 3.2.6.2 The Acceptance (Element of an Act)
- **Technical meaning of "accept"**:  
  - Includes agreeing to accept or offering to receive gratification.  
- No distinction between conspiracy and main crime; acceptance can be indirect.

#### 3.2.6.3 The Gratification
- Definition includes:  
  - Money, gifts, loans, property, avoidance of loss/penalty, employment benefits, favors, rights, privileges.  

#### 3.2.6.4 Element of Inducement
- Acceptance must aim to induce action (illegal, dishonest, unauthorized).  
- Aims include:  
  1. Illegal exercise of legal duties  
  2. Misuse/selling of information  
  3. Abuse of authority/trust  
  4. Achieving unjustified results  

#### 3.2.6.5 Unlawfulness
- Unlawfulness implied in definition; conduct must violate moral/legal standards.  
- Examples of lawful conduct:  
  1. Acts under compulsion  
  2. Police traps  
  3. Acceptable tips or small gifts  

#### 3.2.6.6 Intention
- Intention (not negligence) required; includes knowledge of act and its unlawfulness.  
- **Dolus eventualis** applicable — knowing possibility of corrupt acts counts as intention.

### 3.2.7 Corruption by the Giver
#### 3.2.7.1 General
- Mirror to corruption by recipient, focusing on giving rather than accepting.  

#### 3.2.7.2 Elements of the Crime
1. Giving by X  
2. Of gratification  
3. To induce Y to act  
4. Unlawfulness  
5. Intention  

#### 3.2.7.3 The Giving of Gratification
- **Technical meaning**: Includes agreement to give or offering to give gratification.

## 3.4.3 Drug Dealing and Possession (pp. 55-56)

### 3.4.3.1 Act of Dealing 
- Possession for personal use leads to conviction for possession, not dealing (Solomon; Jackson).

### 3.4.3.3 The Drug
- Offence tied to possession of dependence-producing substances as defined in the Act.

### 3.4.3.4 Unlawfulness
- Unlawfulness may be negated by necessity, e.g., coercion or medical prescriptions (section 5 of the Act).

### 3.4.3.5 Intention
- Intention required: X must know substance is regulated, their act constitutes dealing, and it is unlawful.

#### Driving as Voluntary Act
- Driving must be a **voluntary act**; involuntary acts (automatism, epilepsy) do not constitute an offence.
- **Intoxication** may lead to involuntary conduct; however, **actio libera in causa** does not apply to drunken driving.

#### Case Analyses
- **Rooyen 1968 (1) SA 641 (T)**: Control over vehicle movements constitutes "driving," regardless of how the vehicle moves.
- **Ekstraal 1981 (4) S 406 (C)**: Control from the steering wheel of a towed vehicle constitutes "driving."
- **Makhubela 1981 (4) SA 210 (B)**: "Drive" refers to licensed operators; attempting to drive is not prosecutable.

### Vehicle Definition
- **Vehicle**: Device for travel on wheels/crawler tracks, excludes rail-only transport.
- Broad definition may include bicycles (**Gertse 1972 (3) SA 59 (C)**; **Van Wyk 1965 (2) SA 158 (C)**).
  
### Public Road Definition
- **Public Road**: Any thoroughfare or place commonly used by the public or to which they have access.
  - Includes verges, bridges, and connected structures.
- **Criteria for qualification**:
  1. Commonly used by the public.
  2. Public or section has a right of access (**Dillon 1983 (4) SA 877 (N)**).

### Speed Limit
- Established by **section 59(1) NRTA** and **Regulation 426 RTA 29 of 1989**:
  1. Urban areas: **60 km/h**
  2. Outside urban: **100 km/h**
  3. Freeways: **120 km/h**
- The State must prove speed limit breach; accuracy of timing device is critical (Meaker 1998 (2) SACR 73 (W)).

### Unlawfulness
- Certain authorized drivers (emergency vehicles) allowed to exceed speed limits under **section 60 NRTA**.
- **Example Case**: **S v Groep 2002 (1) SACR 538 (E)** - Negligent driving conviction for disregard of road users’ safety.
  
### Culpability
- Discussed as **strict liability**; reasonable mistake may be raised in speed limit violations (Burchell 795–796).

### Reckless or Negligent Driving
#### Definition
- **Section 63 NRTA**: Prohibits reckless or negligent driving on public roads.

#### Elements of Reckless Driving
1. **Driving**
2. **Vehicle**
3. **Public Road**
4. **Recklessly**
5. **Unlawfulness**
6. **Culpability**

- **Act** involves reckless driving defined as wilful disregard for safety.
- **Case law**: Recklessness includes inconsiderate driving (e.g., **Van Zyl 1969 (1) SA 553 (A)**).

### Unlawfulness in Reckless Driving
- May be justified by necessity or official capacity. 

### Culpability in Reckless Driving
- Intention (dolus eventualis) and gross negligence considered.

#### Negligent Driving
- Also prohibited under **section 63(1) NRTA**; protects public safety.
  
#### Elements of Negligent Driving
1. **Driving**
2. **Vehicle**
3. **Public Road**
4. **Negligently**
5. **Unlawfulness**
6. **Culpability**

- Defined by failing the reasonable standard of care (Kruger v Coetzee 1966 (2) SA 428 (A)).

### Culpability in Negligent Driving
- Convictions can extend to serious crimes like murder (e.g., **Humphreys 2013 (2) SACR 1 (SCA)**, **S v Qeqe 2012 (2) SACR 41 (ECG)**).

#### 4.2.2 Rape

##### 4.2.2.1 Definition
- **Section 3 (Sexual Offences Act)**: 
  - Defines rape: Unlawful and intentional sexual penetration of another without consent.
  - **Elements**:
    - (a) Sexual penetration
    - (b) Absence of consent
    - (c) Unlawfulness
    - (d) Intention

##### 4.2.2.2 The Act
- **Prohibited Act**: Involves sexual penetration without consent.
  - **Definition of Sexual Penetration** includes:
    - (i) Genital organs into or beyond genital organs, anus, or mouth.
    - (ii) Other body parts into or beyond genital organs or anus.
    - (iii) Any object into or beyond genital organs or anus.
    - (iv) Animal's genital organs into mouth.
- **Genital Organs**: Includes male/female organs and surgically constructed ones.
- **Materially Defined Crime**: Focus on the situation created (sexual penetration) rather than just the act itself.
  
##### 4.2.2.3 Unlawfulness
- Absence of consent is definitional, not just a justification element.
- **Justification Defense**: If compelled by a third party, could potentially exclude unlawfulness.

##### 4.2.2.4 Intention
- **Specific Requirement**: Knowledge of absence of consent required for conviction.
- **Types of Intention**: 
  - Dolus eventualis suffices—awareness of possible lack of consent.

#### 4.2.3 Compelled Rape

##### 4.2.3.1 Definition
- **Section 4 (Sexual Offences Act)**: 
  - Defines compelled rape as compelling a third person to penetrate another without consent.

    - (b) To penetrate another
    - (c) Absence of consent from third party
    - (d) Absence of consent from complainant
    - (e) Unlawfulness
    - (f) Intention

##### 4.2.3.2 The Act
- **Act Defined**: Compelling a third person to act against the will of both parties involved.
- **Legal Change**: Shifts liability from accomplice (common law) to direct perpetrator for compelling rape.

##### 4.2.3.3 Unlawfulness
- **Potential Exclusions**: If X is compelled to compel Z to act, this may exclude unlawfulness.

#### 4.7.2 Consensual Sexual Penetration
- **Definition (s 15)**

- **Elements:**
  - (a) Sexual penetration act
  - (b) Child 12-16 years
  - (c) Unlawfulness  
  - (d) Intention  
  - **Notes:**
    - Below 12 years = rape; above 12, consent irrelevant.
- **Case Law:**
  - Sheldon-Lakey 2016 (2) SACR 632 (NWM): Consent defense rejected; no age verification.

#### 4.7.2.1 Cases on Constitutionality
- **Teddy Bear Clinic v Minister of Justice (ZACC/2013)**
  - Constitutionality of criminalizing consensual acts between 12-16 years challenged.

  - Respondents lacked evidence proving protection against sexual risks; provisions declared unconstitutional.

    - (b) Aged 16-17, age difference ≤ 2 years.

- Intention not stated in the crime's definition; negligence can suffice.

# Learning Unit 5: Crimes Against Life and Potential Life

## 5.1 Background
- Focus on crimes against life: **murder** and **culpable homicide**.

## 5.2 Murder (Criminal Law 437–441)

### 5.2.1 Definition
- **Murder**: Unlawful and intentional causing of death of another human being.

### 5.2.2 Elements of the Crime
- Elements required for murder:
  1. Causing of death.
  2. Of another person.
  3. Unlawfully.
  4. Intentionally.
- New focus: Requirement for “another person.”

### 5.2.3 “Another Person”
- Must be another living human being; suicide is not a crime.
- Assisting suicide: May lead to murder conviction for the assistant if unlawfully causing death.
- **Infants**: Presumed alive if proven to have breathed upon birth (s 239(1) Criminal Procedure Act).
- Case Reference: **Mshumpa 2008 (1) SA 126 (E)** – discusses living human being requirement.

### Case Note:
- If X intends to kill Y but Y is deceased, charge is **attempted murder** (not murder).

### Mercy Killing and Assisted Suicide
- **Euthanasia**: Doctor aiding in death may be charged with murder or culpable homicide based on circumstances.
- Legal precedent: **Ex parte Die Minister van Justisie: In re S v Grotjohn 1970 (2) SA 355 (A)** discusses assisted suicide and implications on constitutional rights (e.g., dignity).

## 5.3 Culpable Homicide (Criminal Law 442–443)

### 5.3.1 Definition
- **Culpable Homicide**: Unlawful, negligent causing of death of another human being.

### 5.3.2 Elements of the Crime
- Elements required for culpable homicide:
  1. Causing of death.
  2. Of another person.
  3. Unlawfully.
  4. Negligently.

### 5.3.3 Negligence
- Difference from murder: `Intention` (murder) vs `negligence` (culpable homicide).
- **Test for Negligence**:
  - Reasonable person foresaw risk of death.
  - Took steps to guard against risk.
  - X's conduct deviated from reasonable person's conduct.

### Case Reference:
- **S v Botha 2019 (1) SACR 127 (SCA)**: Actions led to conviction change from murder to culpable homicide due to lack of awareness of unlawfulness.

## Summary
1. **Murder**: Unlawful and intentional death of another; **Culpable Homicide**: Unlawful and negligent death of another.
2. Requirement for both: "another living person" must be killed.

1. Burchell, *Principles of Criminal Law* (2016), pp. 577–586.
2. Kemp et al., *Criminal Law in South Africa* (2018), pp. 315–321.

# LEARNING UNIT 6: Crimes Against Bodily Integrity

## 6.1 ASSAULT  
(Criminal Law 447–454; Reader 12–15)

### 6.1.1 Definition
- **Assault**: Unlawful and intentional application of force to another person or inducing fear of immediate application of force.

### 6.1.2 Elements of the Crime
- **Application of Force**: Direct or indirect.
- **Unlawfulness**: Absence of justification.
- **Intention**: Requires intent to apply force or inspire fear.

### 6.1.3 Historical Overview
- Assault evolved from a comprehensive crime (iniuria) into an independent crime under English influence. 
- Iniuria regarding physical integrity = assault.

### 6.1.4 Ways to Commit Assault
- **Direct Application of Force**: Common understanding of assault (e.g., punching).
- **Indirect Application of Force**: Actions causing physical harm without direct contact (e.g., setting a dog on someone). 
- **Inspiring Fear**: Causing an individual to believe immediate force will be applied.

### 6.1.5 Application of Force – Direct
- Common method; includes any physical contact, irrespective of pain.

### 6.1.6 Application of Force – Indirect
- Actions infringing physical integrity. Cases:
  - *Marx 1962 (1) SA 848 (N)*: Force leading to sickness.
  - *A 1993 (1) SA 600 (A)*: Forced ingestion leading to assault conviction.

### 6.1.7 Inspiring Fear of Immediate Force
- No physical contact required.
- Conditions:
  - Threat of violence must be immediate and unlawful.
  - Victim must believe in the threat's sincerity and that the threatener can act.

### 6.1.8 Unlawfulness
- Conduct must lack justification (defenses include private defense, official capacity, consent).

### 6.1.9 Intention
- Intention forms: Direct, indirect, dolus eventualis.
- Must foresee potential harm and awareness of unlawfulness.
- Intoxication might exclude intention (Chretien 1981 (1) SA 1097 (A)).

### 6.1.10 Attempt
- Recognized that attempted assault is possible (fear may not be induced despite near assault).

### 6.1.11 Assault with Intent to Do Grievous Bodily Harm  
(Criminal Law 453–454)

#### 6.1.11.1 Definition
- Unlawful application of force or induction of fear with specific intent to cause grievous bodily harm.

#### 6.1.11.2 Elements of Crime
- Same as general assault but requires specific intent to inflict grievous bodily harm.

#### 6.1.11.3 Intent to Do Harm
- Specific intent required; actual infliction of harm is irrelevant to liability (*Dube 1991 2 SACR 419 (ZS) 424*). Dolus eventualis suffices.

#### 6.1.12 Assault with intent to commit another crime
- **Definition**: Assault intending to commit a further crime (e.g., rape, robbery, murder).
- **Separate Offences**: Each further crime constitutes a separate offence.
- **Requirements**: Intent to commit the further crime is necessary; aligns with attempts (e.g., attempted rape).

#### 6.2 POINTING OF A FIREARM
##### 6.2.1 Definition
- **Legislation**: **Section 120(6) of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000**: Pointing a firearm or a look-alike firearm at a person without good reason is an offence.

##### 6.2.2 Elements of the offence
1. Pointing of
2. A firearm or similar object
3. At any person
4. Unlawfully
5. Intentionally

##### 6.2.3 The act: "To point ... at"
- **Conviction Requirements**: No need to prove if a shot was fired or if the weapon was loaded.
- **Interpretation of "point at"**: 
  - **Narrow Interpretation**: Points at Y where a bullet would hit if discharged (adopted in **Van Zyl 1993 (1) SACR 338 (C)**).
  - **Broader Interpretation**: Directing towards Y in a manner that arouses fear of being hit (adopted in **Hans 1998 (2) SACR 406 (E)**).

##### 6.2.4 A firearm, et cetera
- **Definition of firearms**: Includes any device designed to propel a bullet.

##### 6.2.5 "Any other person"
- **Requirement**: Must point at a person; pointing at animals is insufficient for conviction.

##### 6.2.6 Unlawfulness
- **Justification**: No criminal liability if pointing due to grounds like self-defence.

##### 6.2.7 Intention
- **Culpability Requirement**: Intention is necessary; mere negligence is insufficient.
- **Application**: Must know that handling an object described in the Act and that pointing at another person is unlawful.

#### SUMMARY
- **Assault**:
  - Definition includes application of force or inspiring fear.
  - Grounds for justification: private defence, official duty, etc.
  - Intention required for culpability, not negligence.

- **Assault with the intent to do grievous bodily harm**:
  - All requirements for ordinary assault apply, plus intent.
  - Actual harm inflicted is not necessary for liability.

- **Assault with intent to commit another crime**:
  - Requires intention to commit an additional crime.

- **Pointing of a firearm**:
  - Definitions provided in legislation.
  - Broad interpretation of "pointing" preferred for wider protection against fear.
  - Intention is required for the offence.

### 7.2.6 Infringement may lead to both civil and criminal actions

2. Not limited to sexual impropriety.
3. X or Y can be either gender.

- **Objective Element**: Conduct must offend a reasonable person's standards.

#### 7.2.7.3 Examples of infringement of dignity

## 7.2 CRIMES AGAINST PRIVACY

### 7.2.8 Violation of dignity or privacy must be serious
- **Legal Standard**: Violations must be serious; trivial insults do not qualify (Seweya 2004 (1) SACR 387 (T)).
- Contextual Factors for Seriousness:
  - Ages of parties involved (young vs. mature)
  - Gender considerations (Van Meer 1923 OPD 77)
  - Nature of act (indecent exposure, "Peeping Toms")
  - Relationship between parties (stranger vs. intimate)
  - Persistence of conduct (e.g., rude staring)
  - Publicity (greater harm with wider audience exposure)
  - Sexual impropriety (unwanted sexual advances viewed seriously)
  - Public standing of Y (offensive comments to public officials may constitute greater contempt, Momberg case).

### 7.2.9 Unlawfulness
- **Justifying Grounds**:
  - **Necessity**: E.g., fleeing a fire (even if naked).
  - **Consent**: Y's permission for unwanted observation.
  - **Official Capacity**: Actions by police under duty.

### 7.2.10 Intention
- Must be intentional; negligence does not suffice.
- X must be aware that actions violate Y's dignity or privacy and recognize lack of justification.

## 7.3 CRIMINAL DEFAMATION (Criminal Law 467–469)

### 7.3.1 Definition
- Criminal defamation: unlawful, intentional publication harming someone’s reputation.

### 7.3.2 Elements of the crime
1. Publication
2. Defamatory allegation concerning another
3. Unlawfulness
4. Intentionality

### 7.3.3 Publication
- "Publication" means dissemination to third parties (can be oral or written).
- Allegations solely to Y may constitute crimen iniuria if injurious.

### 7.3.4 Defamatory allegation
- Defamatory words must incite hatred, contempt, or ridicule.
- X must intend to harm Y's reputation actively.
- Seriousness is a requirement for defamation (Hoho 2009 (1) SACR 276 (SCA)).
- **Constitutionality**: Freedom of expression is not absolute; protects dignity. 

### 7.3.5 Intent
- Must intend to defame (Motsepe 2015 (2) SACR 125 (GP): reliance on truth negated intent).

## 7.4 ABDUCTION (Criminal Law 395–399)

### 7.4.1 Definition
- Abduction: unlawful, intentional removal of an unmarried minor without consent, intending marriage or sexual intercourse.

### 7.4.2 Origin and character of the crime
- Historically aimed at protecting parental authority over minors; relevance has evolved but still penalizes seduction of minors.

### 7.4.3 Legal interest protected
- Interests of parents/guardians: control over minors and marriage consent.

### 7.4.4 Elements of the crime
1. Removal 
2. Unmarried minor
3. From parents' control 
4. Intent for marriage/sexual intercourse 
5. No parental consent 
6. Unlawfulness 
7. Intent

### 7.4.5 Removal
- Removal need not be forcible; agreement is sufficient (Jorgenson 1935 EDL 219).

### 7.4.6 Person removed
- Minor defined as under 18 (s 17 of the Children’s Act 38 of 2005).

### 7.4.7 Removal from parental control
- Must be without parental consent; minors' consent is immaterial.

### 7.4.8 Intention to marry or engage sexually
- Must aim to remove minor for substantial duration aiming for marriage/sexual intercourse.

### 7.4.9 Without parental consent
- Consent of parents is critical; Y's consent alone does not absolve X.

### 7.4.10 Unlawfulness
- Must lack justification.

### 7.4.11 Intention
- Intention must encompass all crime elements; must be aware of minor status and non-consent of parents (Churchill 1959 (2) SA 575 (A)).

## 7.5 KIDNAPPING (Criminal Law 471–474)
### 7.5.1 Definition
- Kidnapping: Unlawful and intentional deprivation of a person's freedom of movement or parental control over a child under 18.

### 7.5.2 Elements of the crime
1. Deprivation
2. Freedom of movement (or parental control)
3. Unlawfulness
4. Intentionally

### 7.5.3 Character of the crime
- Protects freedom of movement and parental/custodian control over minors.
- Important features:
  - Parents cannot commit this crime regarding their own child.
  - Forcible removal not required.
  - Duration of deprivation is irrelevant.
  - Motive (e.g., ransom) not essential for liability; may influence sentencing.
  - Unlawfulness may be negated if acting in official capacity or with consent of an adult.
  - Intention requires knowledge of unlawfulness.

## GLOSSARY
- **dignitas**: dignity

## SUMMARY
### Crimen Iniuria
1. Definition: Protects dignity and privacy.
2. Dignity expressed as "mental tranquillity," "self-respect," or "feeling of chastity."
3. Infringement of dignity requires awareness and feeling of degradation by Y.
4. Examples include indecent exposure and vulgar abuse.
5. Privacy infringement examples: Peeping Toms, unauthorized listening devices, confidential communications.
6. Seriousness is required for qualifying as crimen iniuria.
7. Culpability: intention.

### Criminal Defamation
1. Definition: Protects a person's reputation.
2. Harm requires publication to someone other than Y.
3. Defenses: truth, public benefit, fair comment, privilege.
4. Intention to harm Y's reputation and knowledge of unlawfulness required.

### Abduction
1. Seven elements identified (not elaborated here).

### Kidnapping
1. Definition: Similar to above; protects freedom or parental control.
2. Ransom demand not required for crime, but may lead to extortion charge.
3. Childstealing as a form of kidnapping.
4. No need for physical removal; concealing or imprisoning is sufficient.
5. Permanent deprivation not required.

# LEARNING UNIT 8: Theft

## 8.1 DEFINITION
- Theft: unlawful, intentional appropriation of movable, corporeal property.
  - Property can belong to:
    1. Another and in their possession.
    2. Another but in the perpetrator’s possession.
    3. The perpetrator but in another's possession, where the other has a superior right of possession.
- Intention to permanently deprive the rightful possessor is necessary.

## 8.2 GENERAL CHARACTERISTICS OF THE CRIME
- Definition drawn from Roman-Dutch law due to absence of specific legislation.
- Includes:
  - Appropriation of property in another's possession.
  - Appropriation of property already in the perpetrator's possession (embezzlement).
  - Unlawful re-possession of own property (arrogation of possession).

## 8.3 LATIN TERMINOLOGY
- Furtum: theft.
- Contrectatio: act of theft, but should be understood as "appropriation."
- Res: object of theft.
- Animus furandi: intention to steal.
- Invito domino: without owner's consent (unlawfulness).

## 8.4 DIFFERENT FORMS OF THEFT
- Common forms include:
  1. Removal of property from another's possession.
  2. Embezzlement.
  3. Arrogation of possession (taking back one's own property from lawful possession).

## 8.5 FOUR BASIC REQUIREMENTS
1. Act of appropriation.
2. Specific type of property.
3. Unlawfulness of act.
4. Intention (to appropriate).

## 8.6 THEFT IN THE FORM OF THE REMOVAL OF PROPERTY
### 8.6.1 Act of appropriation
- Defined by:
  1. Deprivation of lawful owner.
  2. Exercise of ownership rights by the perpetrator.

#### 8.6.1.1 Components of appropriation
- Negative: exclusion of the rightful owner.
- Positive: explicit exercise of ownership by the perpetrator.

#### 8.6.1.2 Attempted vs. Completed Theft
- Distinction based on control over property at the time of intervention.
- If appropriation is not completed (e.g., apprehended before control is obtained), only attempted theft is relevant.

#### 8.6.1.3 Shoplifting Cases
- Caught before exiting self-service shop can lead to completed theft determination; however, depends on effective control assessment.

### 8.6.2 The property
- Must comply with:
  1. Be movable (cannot steal immovable property).
  2. Be corporeal (cannot steal abstract concepts).
  3. Be available in commerce.
  4. Must belong to someone else, barring arrogation of possession.

### 8.6.3 Unlawfulness
- Consent of the owner negates unlawfulness (invito domino).
  
### 8.6.4 Intention
- Requires intent; theft cannot be committed negligently. Must encompass all elements of the crime.

## 8.6 Requirements for Intention in Theft

- **Basic Requirements**
  - Act of appropriation
  - Property requirement
  - Requirement of unlawfulness

### 8.6.4 Intention with Respect to the Property

- **Definition**
  - X must know the property is movable, belongs to someone else, or is possessable by another.
  
- **Key Case: Rantsane 1973 (4) SA 380 (O)**
  - X believed property was res derelictae, lacked intention to steal.
  
- **Understanding Res Nullius/Res Derelictae**
  - Misbelief negates theft intention.
  
### 8.6.4.2 Intention with Respect to Unlawfulness

- **Requirement**
  - X must be aware of the unlawfulness of her actions.
  
- **Key Case: Slabbert 1941 OPD 109**
  - X believed Y would consent; lacked unlawful awareness.

### 8.6.4.3 Intention with Respect to the Act (Intention to Appropriate)

- **Definition of Appropriation**
  - Positive: Exercising ownership rights.
  - Negative: Excluding owner’s rights.
  
- **Components of Intention to Appropriate**
  - (1) Intention to exercise ownership rights.
  - (2) Intention to exclude the owner.

- **Intention to Permanently Deprive**
  - Cannot intend only temporary deprivation.
  - **Example**: Taking a car only for a joyride does not constitute theft.
  
- **Historical Context**
  - Roman and Roman-Dutch law did not require permanent deprivation.
  - **Key Case: Sibiya 1955 (4) SA 274 (A)**
    - Confirmed necessity of permanent deprivation for theft conviction.

### 8.6.4.3(c) Intention to Appropriate Includes Permanent Deprivation

- **Clarification**
  - Emphasis on general intention to appropriate rather than solely on permanent deprivation.

## 8.7 Embezzlement

- **Definition**
  - Theft by appropriating property already in X's possession.
  
- **Acts Constituting Embezzlement:**
  - Consuming, selling, donating, exchanging property.

- **Example of Finder's Conduct**
  - Failure to return lost property can lead to theft.

- **Key Case: Director of Public Prosecutions, Western Cape v Parker 2015 (2) SACR 109 (SCA)**
  - VAT vendor misappropriating funds held not guilty under common theft.

## 8.8 Arrogation of Possession (Furtum Possessionis)

- **Definition**
  - Owner steals own property from someone with a prevailing right to possess.

- **Examples**
  - X takes property from a secured position against the agreement.
  
- **Key Cases:**
  - Roberts 1936 (1) PH H2 (C) - Convicted for theft of car in garage.
  - Janoo 1959 (3) SA 107 (A) - Guilty for removing goods without appropriate rights.

## 8.9 Theft of Money

- **Context**
  - Money theft includes incorporeal property like bank credit.
  
- **Key Case: Verwey 1968 (4) SA 682 (A)**
  - Actions reducing the owner's bank credit constitute theft.

- **Mechanisms of Theft**
  - No tangible coins or notes required; economic effect is paramount.

- **Theft as a Continuing Crime**  
  - Theft continues as long as the stolen property remains in possession of the thief.  
  - Perpetrators and accessories after the fact treated equally in theft.

#### Glossary
- **animus furandi**: Intention to steal.  
- **contrectatio**: Act of stealing (handling or touching of property).  
- **delictum continuum**: Continuing crime.  
- **furtum**: Theft.  
- **furtum possessionis**: Theft of possession of property.  
- **furtum usus**: Theft of use (not applicable in current law).  
- **invito domino**: Without the owner's consent.  
- **lucri faciendi gratia**: Intention to gain advantage.  
- **res**: Thing or property.  
- **res communes**: Things belonging to everyone.  
- **res derelictae**: Abandoned things.  
- **res in commercio**: Things capable of commercial exchange.  
- **res nullius**: Things belonging to nobody.  

#### Summary  
1. **Definition of Theft**: See definition above.  
2. **Act of Stealing**: Appropriating property by depriving the owner and exercising rights of an owner.  
3. **Forms of Theft**:
   - Removal of property (property in owner's possession).
   - Embezzlement (property in perpetrator's possession).
4. **Self-Theft**: Can commit theft of one's own property if it is in possession of someone with a superior right.  
5. **Property Requirements**:
   - Must be movable.
   - Must be corporeal and capable of commerce.  
   - Must not be res communes, derelictae, or nullius.  
   - Belongs to someone else unless arrogation of possession applies.
6. **Unlawfulness**: Act must be without permission from the possessor.  
7. **Intention for Theft**:
   - Must relate to act, nature of property, unlawfulness, and permanent deprivation.  
   - Temporary use and return does not constitute theft.
8. **Theft Types**:
   - Embezzlement: Appropriation of property already in perpetrator's possession.
   - Arrogation of possession: Owner removes own property from lawful possessor.

# LEARNING UNIT 9: Robbery and Receiving Stolen Property

## 9.1 Robbery

### 9.1.6 Causal Link between Violence and Acquisition of Property
- Causal link required between violence/threats and property acquisition; absence leads to no robbery charge.
- **Case Reference**: *Pachai 1962 (4) SA 146 (T)*: Threats did not secure property due to prior police trap; resulting in attempted robbery, not robbery.

### 9.1.7 The "Bag-and Cellphone-Snatching Cases"
- Snatching handbag/cellphone can meet robbery criteria even without victim resistance.
- **Case Reference**: *Sithole 1981 (1) SA 1186 (N)*: Force used to overcome victim's grip on handbag constituted robbery.
- **Case Reference**: *Salmans 2006 (1) SACR 333 (C)*: Any force, however slight, against the victim suffices for robbery; grabbing effects dispossession.
- **Case Reference**: *Hlatwayo 1980 (3) SA 425 (O)*: Resistance by victim indicates robbery.

### 9.1.8 Property Need Not Be on the Victim's Person or in His Presence
- **Case Reference**: *Ex parte Minister van Justisie: In re S v Seekoei 1984 (4) SA 690 (A)*: Robbery found even when victim’s property was two kilometers away.

## 9.2 Receiving Stolen Property

### 9.2.1 Definition
- Crime defined as unlawfully and intentionally receiving stolen property with knowledge of its status as stolen.

### 9.2.2 Elements of the Crime
1. Receiving
2. Stolen goods
3. Unlawfully
4. Intentionally (knowledge of stolen status)

### 9.2.3 Stolen Property
- Applicable only to movable corporeal property; receiving proceeds does not constitute the crime (*Augustine 1986 3 SA 294 (C)*).

### 9.2.4 Receiving the Property
- Involves possession; mere negotiation or inspection without intent to possess does not constitute crime.

### 9.2.5 Unlawfulness
- Must be unlawful; consent or intent to return property negates criminal charge.

### 9.2.6 Culpability
- Must know property is stolen; dolus eventualis sufficient if aware of such possibility and chooses to receive.

## SUMMARY
1. **Robbery**: Theft by violence (actual or threatened); requires causal link between violence and property acquisition.
2. **Theft Not Robbery**: If violence follows theft, implies separate charges (assault and theft).
3. **Bag Snatching**: Snatching entails robbery if force is used, regardless of victim's resistance.
4. **Property Presence**: No requirement for victim's property to be physically present during violence for robbery to occur.
5. **Receiving Stolen Property**: Criminal for knowingly receiving stolen goods; overlaps with theft.

# 10.1 FRAUD
(Criminal Law 523–532; Reader 38–41)

## 10.1.1 Definition
- Fraud: unlawful, intentional misrepresentation causing actual or potential prejudice.

## 10.1.2 Elements of the crime
1. **Misrepresentation**
2. **Causes or may cause prejudice**
3. **Unlawful**
4. **Intentional**

## 10.1.3 The Misrepresentation
- Misrepresentation: Deception through falsehood; conduct representing non-existent facts.
  - **Forms of Misrepresentation**:
    - Written, verbal, or conduct (e.g., nodding).
    - Express or implied representations.
      - Examples: Implicit representation of payment intent when purchasing on credit (Persotam 1938 AD 92).
      - **Case Reference**: Mdantile 2011 (2) SACR 142 – Misrepresentation by conduct confirmed fraud despite lack of oral statement.
- Misrepresentation by commission or omission.
  - **Legal duty to disclose**: May arise from statute (e.g., Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, s. 137(a)) or natural duty.
- **Case References**:
  - Larkins: Omitting key facts about salary led to fraud conviction.
  - Yengeni: Failure to disclose benefits to Parliament can lead to fraud charges.
  - Tshopo: Failing to disclose relationship in tender applications constituted omission misrepresentation.

## 10.1.4 The Prejudice
### 10.1.4.1 General
- Prejudice refers to harm resulting from misrepresentation. Mere lying is not fraud; tangible harm must occur.

### 10.1.4.2 Types of Prejudice
- **Actual vs. Potential**: 
  - Both types support fraud conviction; potential is sufficient.
  - **Legal Standards**:
    - Prejudice implies risk of harm.
    - Prejudice need not be financial or direct to the victim.
  - **Case Reference**: Mngqibisa 2008 (1) SACR 92 – Potential prejudice upheld even if claim not paid.
  - **Noteworthy Cases**: Dyonta 1935 AD 52; Ndwambi 2016 (2) SACR 195(SCA).

### 10.1.4.3 Nature of Prejudice
- Prejudice can be proprietary (related to material possessions) or non-proprietary (affecting broader interests).
  - **Examples** of Non-Proprietary Prejudice:
    - Cheating in exams (Thabetha 1948).
    - Submitting forged documents (Jass 1965).
    - Misrepresentation to authorities (Heyne).

## 10.1.5 Unlawfulness
- Fraud inherently involves unlawful deception; awareness of falsehood is no defense.

## 10.1.6 Intent
- Must cover all components of fraud.
  - Intent to misrepresent: Knowledge of falsity.
  - Intent for prejudice: Awareness that harm may arise from misrepresentation.
- **Distinction**: Intent to deceive (belief in falsehood) vs. intent to defraud (inducing harmful actions).
- **Case Reference**: Gardener 2011 (1) SACR 570 (SCA) – Directors withheld information leading to fraud.

## 10.1.7 Attempt
- General consensus is no attempted fraud due to potential prejudice sufficing for fraud. 
- However, **Appellate Division** (Heyne 1956) allows attempted fraud where misrepresentation is not yet disclosed or received.

# Learning Unit 10: Fraud and Related Crimes

## 10.1 Definitions and Context
- **Fraud**: Intentional misrepresentation causing actual or potential prejudice.
- **Theft by False Pretences**: Obtaining property via misrepresentation with consent induced by the fraud.

## 10.2 Forgery and Uttering (Criminal Law 532–535)
### 10.2.1 Definition
- **Forgery**: Making a false document to the detriment of another.
- **Uttering**: Passing off a false document to another’s prejudice.

### 10.2.2 Elements of the Crime
1. Making a false document (forgery).
2. Passing off a false document.
3. Actual or potential prejudice.
4. Unlawfulness.
5. Intention.

### 10.2.3 General Character of the Crime
- Forgery is a type of fraud; completed upon document falsification, while fraud is complete when misrepresentation becomes known.
- Uttering occurs when the falsified document is used by the forger or others.

### 10.2.4 Falsification of a Document
- Broad interpretation of "document," including:
  - Testimonials
  - Cheques, Receipts, Promissory notes, Bonds, Educational qualifications.
- A document is false if it misrepresents its nature or contains false information. 

### 10.2.5 Passing off a False Document
- Must be communicated as genuine.
- Attempted uttering occurs if the document does not reach its intended recipient.

## 10.3 Theft by False Pretences (Criminal Law 535–537)
### 10.3.1 Definition
- Theft by false pretences occurs if one unlawfully obtains another's property via misrepresentation.

### 10.3.2 Elements of the Crime
1. Misrepresentation by X to Y.
2. Actual harm to Y (property loss).
3. Causal connection between misrepresentation and prejudice.
4. Appropriation of property by X.
5. Unlawfulness.
6. Intention.

### 10.3.3 General Character of the Crime
- Involves fraud followed by theft, where consent obtained through misrepresentation is not considered valid.

## Summary of Key Principles
### Fraud
1. Definition revolves around misrepresentation of facts.
2. May be verbal, written, or through conduct; includes both acts of omission and commission.
3. Prejudice can be actual or potential and of various natures.

### Forgery and Uttering
- Forgery constitutes misrepresentation through falsified documents.
- Uttering involves involving another party with the forged document, which can lead to criminal charges.

### Theft by False Pretences
- Always involves an element of fraud and invalid consent leading to theft.

### LEARNING UNIT 11: Crimes relating to damage to property

**11.2.4 Damage**  
- Damage includes total/partial property destruction, loss, and injury to property.
- Damage includes alteration that incurs costs for restoration (Bowden 1957 (3) SA 148 (T)).

**11.2.5 Unlawfulness**  
- Justifications for unlawful injury:  
  1. Statutory provisions (Oosthuizen 1974 (1) SA 435 (C)).  
  2. Necessity (self-defense against animals, Jaffet 1962 (2) PH H220 (R)).  
  3. Official capacity (e.g., police actions).  
  4. Consent from property owner.

**11.2.6 Intention**  
- Crime committed intentionally (referred to as "malicious" injury).
- Malicious motive is irrelevant (Mnyanda 1973 (4) SA 603 (N) 605).
- Graffiti as property damage:  
  - Removal costs determine damage—subjective view on art vs vandalism.
  - Criminalized in **Section 44 of the General Law Further Amendment Act 93 of 1964**; artistic value not a defense.
  - Rulings affirm graffiti as property damage (Bowden 1957 (3) SA 148 (T)).

**11.3 ARSON**  
- A form of malicious injury to property.

**11.3.1 Definition**  
- Arson defined as unlawfully and intentionally setting fire to:  
  (a) immovable property belonging to another;  
  (b) own immovable insured property, to claim insurance value.

**11.3.2 Elements of the crime**  
- Elements include:  
  1. Setting fire to.  
  2. Immovable property.  
  3. Unlawful act.  
  4. Intention to commit.

**11.3.3 The property**  
- Property must be corporeal, immovable.
- Insured property burn for insurance claim also constitutes fraud.

**11.3.4 Setting fire to**  
- Crime completes upon setting fire; attempts only if caught pre-ignition.

**SUMMARY**  
- Malicious injury to property includes:  
  1. Definition = property harm.  
  2. Property can be movable/immovable.  
  3. Tampering that incurs cost indicates damage.  
  4. Intention required; evil motive not essential.  
- Specifics on arson:  
  5. Definition of arson confirmed.  
  6. Arson only concerning immovable property.  
  7. Intention to damage required, causing patrimonial harm.

### LEARNING UNIT 12: Housebreaking with the intent to commit a crime

**12.1 DEFINITION**  
- Housebreaking defined as unlawfully/intentionally breaking into a building/structure with intent to commit a crime.

**12.2 ELEMENTS OF THE CRIME**  
- Elements comprise:  
  1. Breaking and entering.  
  2. Building/structure.  
  3. Unlawfulness.  
  4. Intent.

**12.3 BREAKING AND ENTERING**  
- Composed of:  
  1. Breaking into structure.  
  2. Entering.

**12.3.1 Breaking**  
- Not always requires damage but displacing an obstacle that bars entry suffices (Ngobeza 1992 (1) SACR 610 (T)).
- Examples:  
  - Physical breaking or merely opening a closed door/window qualifies.
  - Acts like pushing aside curtains (not part of building) do not.

**12.3.2 Entering**  
- Mere breaking insufficient; entry defined as inserting any body part/instrument into building (Maruma 1955 (3) SA 561 (O)).
- Attempted entry results in attempted housebreaking conviction (Mavela 2008 (2) SACR 608 (Ck)).

**12.4 BUILDING OR STRUCTURE**  
- Structure must be for human habitation/storage; immovability is key in certain contexts (De Wet and Swanepoel; Snyman).
- Structures qualifying for the crime include:  
  - House, storeroom, factory, business premises.  
- Non-qualifiers include:  
  - Motorcar, railway truck, fowl run, tent for storage (Abrahams 1998 (2) SACR 655 (C)).

# Chapter 12: Housebreaking with the Intent to Commit a Crime

## 12.5 UNLAWFULNESS
- Breaking and entering must be unlawful.
- Not considered a crime if:
  - Entering one's own house.
  - Shared room with consent.
  - Permission from the owner (e.g., domestic worker).  
  - **Case**: *Mashinga 1976 (2) PH H210 (A)*.

## 12.6 INTENTION
- Two distinct components of intention required for housebreaking:
  1. Intention to unlawfully break into and enter the house/structure.
     - Intention absent if X believes she is breaking into her own house or with owner’s consent.
  2. Intention to commit another crime inside.
     - Mere housebreaking without this intention does not constitute the crime.
     - Potentially punishable as malicious injury to property.
     - The intended crime must differ from housebreaking.
     - Charges of housebreaking with the intent to commit an unknown crime are permitted.
     - **Legislation**: *s 95(12) read with ss 262 and 263 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977*.

## Summary
1. Definition of the crime of housebreaking involves both (a) housebreaking and (b) entering a structure.
2. "Breaking" = removal/displacement of an obstacle that bars entry.
3. "Entering" is complete once any part of the body or instrument breaches an entry point with intent to control contents.
4. Applicable to any structure for human habitation/storage of property.
5. Required intention:
   - (a) Unlawful breaking and entering.
   - (b) Intention to commit another crime inside.
